# Woodworking & DIY Crafting Basics

## Wood Types
- **Softwood** (from conifers): pine, fir, cedar — cheaper, easier to work, less durable, good for construction/furniture
  - Pine: most common for DIY, knots can be problematic (knowing or they fall out)
  - Cedar: naturally rot-resistant, good for outdoor, aromatic
- **Hardwood** (from deciduous trees): oak, maple, walnut, cherry, mahogany — more expensive, harder, durable, beautiful grain
  - Oak: strong, open grain, heavy — classic furniture. Red oak (more pink, porous) vs white oak (water-resistant, used for boats/barrels)
  - Walnut: rich dark color, straight grain, excellent for fine furniture
  - Maple: very hard, light color, great for cutting boards, butcher blocks
  - Cherry: warm reddish-brown that darkens with age, beautiful grain
- **Plywood**: layers of wood veneer glued together — stable, resists warping, large sheets
  - Baltic birch: high quality, many thin plys, no voids, strong
  - MDF (medium-density fiberboard): smooth, cheap, heavy, not water resistant, paints well
  - Particle board: cheapest, weakest, disintegrates with water — avoid for anything that needs to last

## Essential Power Tools
- **Circular saw**: most versatile — cross cuts (across grain), rip cuts (with grain), bevel cuts. Clamp a straight edge for guide
  - Blade: combination blade (general purpose), rip blade (with grain), crosscut blade (across grain). More teeth = smoother cut
- **Jigsaw**: curved cuts, cutouts, plunge cuts. Go slow in curves, use fine blade for clean cut
- **Drill/driver**: drill (holes) + driver (screws). Impact driver: more torque for driving screws — essential for deck building, furniture assembly
  - Bits: twist bits (wood/metal), spade bits (large holes), Forstner (clean flat-bottom holes), hole saw (large circles)
  - Clutch: set to appropriate torque to prevent stripping screws
- **Random orbital sander**: leaves no swirl marks (unlike regular orbital) — start coarse (80 grit), progress to fine (220)
  - Dust collection: attach vacuum or use dust bag — wear mask anyway
- **Miter saw** (chop saw): accurate crosscuts, angles, and miters — essential for trim work, picture frames
  - Sliding compound miter saw: can cut wider boards. Double bevel: tilt left and right without flipping
- **Table saw**: straight rip cuts, dado cuts (grooves), precision joinery — most dangerous tool, respect it
  - SawStop: detects skin contact → stops blade instantly ($1000+ but lives saved)
  - NEVER remove riving knife (prevents kickback) — safety guard stays on for most cuts

## Joinery Basics
- **Butt joint**: simplest — glue + screw/nail — weakest (end grain to face grain doesn't glue well)
  - Reinforce with pocket screws (Kreg jig), dowels, or biscuits
- **Pocket hole**: angled hole + self-tapping screw → strong, hidden, easy — great for beginner furniture
  - Requires pocket hole jig (Kreg is standard). Clamp material, drill pocket, drive screw
- **Dado/groove**: slot cut across/across grain — shelf in bookcase, drawer bottom
  - Cut with table saw (dado blade) or router
- **Mortise and tenon**: peg (tenon) fits in hole (mortise) — one of strongest joints — traditional chairs, tables, doors
  - Can cut by hand (chisel + saw) or with router/drill press + chisel
- **Dovetail**: interlocking wedges — extremely strong, prevents pulling apart — hallmark of fine craftsmanship
  - Drawers (will not fall apart), boxes. Hand-cut (expert) or router jig
- **Half-lap**: remove half thickness from each piece, overlap — strong, flat surface
- **Box joint** (finger joint): like dovetail but rectangular — strong, decorative, easier to make (table saw + jig or router)
- **Dowels**: glue + wooden dowel in aligned holes — strong if done right, alignment critical (dowel jig helps)

## Finishing
- **Preparation**: sand progressively (80→120→180→220 grit), clean dust (tack cloth), fill defects (wood filler)
  - Sand with the grain, not across. Final sand by hand (orbital leaves swirls at 220)
- **Stain**: color only, no protection — apply with rag, wipe excess, let dry. Gel stain easier for vertical surfaces
  - Test on scrap first — wood species dramatically affects color result
  - Conditioner (pre-stain) needed for blotchy woods (pine, cherry, maple)
- **Polyurethane**: clear protective coat (most common) — oil-based (amber, durable, smelly) vs water-based (clear, less durable, cleans up with water)
  - Apply thin coats, lightly sand 320 grit between coats, 3 coats minimum
  - Wipe-on poly: thin with mineral spirits, apply with rag — easier, no brush marks, but more coats needed
- **Danish oil / Tung oil / Teak oil**: penetrates wood, brings out grain, matte finish — less protection than poly but easier (wipe on, wipe off)
- **Paint**: prime first (especially bare wood, MDF), multiple thin coats > one thick coat, sand between coats with 220 grit
  - Cabinet/furniture paint: enamel, chalk paint, milk paint — use appropriate brush (synthetic for latex, natural for oil)
- **Wax**: paste wax (e.g., Minwax) over other finishes for extra protection + sheen — not protective on its own

## Workshop Safety
- **Eye protection**: always — safety glasses minimum, goggles for overhead work
- **Hearing protection**: for any power tool running >5 minutes (damage is cumulative and permanent)
  - Electronic muffs: amplify conversation, cut out loud noise — best compromise
- **Dust protection**: N95 mask minimum for sanding, MDF, any dust-creating work. Silicosis risk from hardwoods, MDF formaldehyde
  - Shop vac + dust collector for tools — lungs don't regenerate like skin
- **Loose clothing**: roll up sleeves, tie back long hair, remove jewelry — all can get caught in spinning tools
- **Table saw**: never stand in line with blade (kickback path). Use push sticks for narrow cuts. Riving knife stays on
  - Kickback: piece thrown back at you at high speed — most common table saw injury
- **General**: disconnect power before changing blades/bits. Sharp tools are safer than dull (less force needed). No gloves near spinning blades (can get pulled in). First aid kit in shop

## Beginner Projects
- **Cutting board**: hardwood strips glued together, sanded, oiled — simple, useful, teaches glue-up + sanding + finishing
- **Photo frame**: miter cuts (45°), glue + nail or spline — teaches angles, joinery
- **Simple bookshelf**: plywood box with dados for shelves — teaches pocket holes, squaring, finishing
- **Nightstand**: box on legs with drawer — teaches drawer construction, joinery
- **Shop stool**: 2x4s, pocket screws — projects that make projects easier
- **Design resources**: SketchUp (free web version), Pinterest (find plans), Ana White (free furniture plans), YouTube (every technique has tutorial)
