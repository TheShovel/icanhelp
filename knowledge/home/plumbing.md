# Plumbing Basics for Homeowners

## How Your Home Plumbing Works
- **Water supply**: main line from street (public or well) → water meter → main shutoff (KNOW WHERE THIS IS! Usually at curb in box or in basement/crawl space near front wall). Cold water splits to each fixture, hot water goes to water heater first → hot water lines to each fixture
  - Main shutoff location: at water meter (curb) — need special key tool ~$10 for water meter box; at house (basement or crawl). LABEL IT. Everyone in household should know location
- **Drain-waste-vent (DWV) system**: gravity drainage (pipes slope 1/4" per foot minimum). Water flows down through traps (P-trap under each fixture = water seal prevents sewer gas from entering. If fixture unused for long time, trap dries out = sewer smell — pour water down to refill)
  - Vent pipes (extend through roof) let air into system → prevents vacuum that would suck water out of traps. If you hear gurgling when draining: vent is probably clogged
  - Septic vs sewer: rural = septic tank (treat on property — pump every 3-5 years, $300-600). City = municipal sewer (monthly fee, maintain line from house to street — homeowner typically responsible for lateral to property line)
- **Water pressure**: standard home: 40-80 PSI. Below 40 = poor flow. Above 80 = strain on fixtures, leaks. Install pressure reducing valve (PRV) if >80 PSI. Test: $10 gauge screws onto outdoor spigot
- **Water heater**: tank (30-80 gallons, lasts 10-15 years, natural gas (recovery faster) or electric (cheaper but slower) or heat pump/hybrid (most efficient)). Tankless/on-demand (endless hot water, more efficient, higher upfront cost. Requires larger gas line)
  - Temperature: 120°F recommended (prevents scalding, saves energy, reduces mineral buildup). Above 120°F = scald risk (especially children + elderly). Below 120°F = bacteria risk (legionella)
  - Maintenance: drain 1-2 gallons yearly (remove sediment — sediment layer at bottom reduces efficiency, can cause rumbling sounds). Test TPR valve (temperature + pressure relief — safety valve. Test yearly: lift lever briefly — should release water then stop). Replace anode rod every 3-5 years (sacrificial metal rod prevents tank rust — if rod gone, tank is next. Lot of sediment buildup and a hard-water life shortened)
  - Expansion tank: required in closed systems (check valve on main line). Absorbs pressure increase when water heats up — without it, TPR valve may drip constantly or water heater may fail early

## Common Problems & Fixes

### Clogged Drain
1. **Kitchen sink**: plunger first (seal over drain with wet cloth over the other side). Remove trap (bucket underneath, unscrew slip nuts). Dishwasher connection may have food buildup inside hose — disconnect and snake it. If still clogged: snake/auger (hand-crank or drill attachment — push through drain line, rotate to break up clog)
   - NEVER use chemical drain cleaner on standing water—acid sits in pipe, damages pipe. Drano/Liquid Plumber = caustic. Use as LAST RESORT or not at all slowly into very small amount of water with fan on for ventilation + goggles. They damage pipes and traps with repeated use
   - Prevent: drain strainer (catch food), don't pour grease down sink (pour into can, dispose in trash), monthly hot water flush + baking soda/vinegar
2. **Bathroom sink**: clog usually in P-trap (hair, soap, toothpaste). Remove trap, clean out. If still clogged: zip-it tool ($3 plastic stick with barbs — pulls hair out of drain). Works better than chemical for bathroom sink clogs
3. **Shower/tub**: hair at drain cover. Remove cover, pull out hair. Zip-it tool. If still clogged: remove overflow plate, feed snake through overflow, fish for hair. Or chemical drain cleaner (if nothing else works — clear standing water, pour, wait 15-30 min, flush with HOT water)
4. **Toilet**: plunger (firm seal, push slowly then pull sharply — don't plunge violently, can crack bowl). If plunger doesn't work: closet auger (toilet snake — curved to navigate toilet trap. Crank into bowl, past the trap, pull back). Remove toilet (last resort: shut off water, flush remaining, sponge out, unbolt from floor, lift — heavy! — replace wax ring, re-bolt, reinstall)
5. **Main drain (house)** (all fixtures slow/backing up): call plumber — usually needs professional snake/power auger. May be tree roots in sewer line. $200-500 to snake, $3,000-6,000+ to replace pipe with pipe bursting/burrowing

### Running Toilet
- **Most common cause**: flapper not sealing. Replace flapper ($5-10 at hardware store, universal, easy. Turn off water, flush to empty tank, unhook old flapper, take to store to match — some have adjusters or are made for specific flush valve)
  - Chain can be too short (flapper can't close: the rubber flapper worn (torn, warped, soft), just replace it) or too long (can't seal). Adjust chain length — leave a little slack, not much
- **Water level too high**: adjust float — screw on fill valve turns, or pinch clip to slide float down. If float won't go down: fill valve is saturated with water = replace entire fill valve ($15-20, universal)
- **Phantom flush** (fills randomly, no one used it): flapper leaking slowly (mildew + mineral deposits on flapper). Replace flapper. Or cracked overflow tube — replace fill valve
- **Handle sticks**: clean/lubricate or replace handle ($8, universal)

### Leaky Faucet
- **Compression (two handles)**: worn rubber washer at stem end. Turn off water, remove handle, unscrew stem, replace washer (bring old to hardware store — size matters). 5/16" and 3/8" common sizes
- **Cartridge (single handle, shift up/down or rotating)**: remove handle, pull cartridge (pliers, wiggle), replace with exact match. BRAND MATTERS. Take old cartridge to hardware store — many brands (Moen, Delta, Kohler, Price Pfister) have proprietary cartridges. Delta makes a specific "ball" style for some single handles
- **Ceramic disc (modern single handle, smooth quarter-turn on/off)**: clean or replace cartridge. Mineral deposits cause leaks. Remove cartridge, rinse, reinstall. If leaking persists: replace
- **Dripping from spout**: usually worn O-rings or cartridge. Dripping under sink at base of faucet: worn O-rings where spout meets body. Loose handles: tighten screw under decorative cap (pry cap off with flathead, tighten screw)

### Low Water Pressure
- **One fixture**: clean aerator (unscrew faucet tip, disassemble, soak in vinegar 1 hour, scrub with toothbrush). Mineral buildup blocks aerator screen. If all fixtures affected: call water company or check PRV (pressure reducing valve)
- Shutoff valves under sink (small lever/knob) may be partially closed. Check they're fully open
- Galvanized steel pipes (pre-1960s houses): interior corrodes over time = restricted flow. Replace with copper or PEX

## Tools Every Homeowner Needs
- **Plunger**: toilet plunger (accordion/folded, seal fits toilet). Sink/tub plunger (flat bottom). Have BOTH (toilet plunger is awkward for sink; sink plunger doesn't seal toilet)
- **Pipe wrench**: adjustable, 10-14" or 18". For tightening/removing metal pipes (shutoffs, gas lines), not chrome. Use with cheater bar = leverage + dangerous
- **Basin wrench**: reaches faucet nuts in tight spaces under sink. Indispensable for faucet replacement — long handle with pivoting jaw
- **Tubing cutter**: clean copper pipe cuts for repair. Ends square, prevents leaks. Deburr inside of cut after cutting
- **Propane torch**: for soldering copper pipes (sweating joints). MAP gas = hotter, faster. Flux, solder, wire brush for cleaning. NEW: push-fit connections (SharkBite) for emergencies/PEX without soldering
- **Auger/snake**: hand-crank or electric for drains. Hand-crank = fine for sinks + showers. Electric for main drains. Flat "toilet snake" curved to navigate bowl — won't scratch inside of toilet
- **Adjustable wrenches**: 6" + 10". For hex nuts on supply lines, shutoffs. Channel-lock pliers (two sizes) for larger nuts, slip joint for many types
- **Teflon tape (PTFE)**: seal threaded pipe connections. Wrap 3-5 turns clockwise ("as you tighten"). NOT for flared fittings (compression, which use ferrule). NOT for gas lines (use yellow Teflon/pipe dope for gas). Pipe dope (Rectorseal) in addition or instead for heavy-duty threads
- **PEX tools**: PEX cutter (ratcheting — cut clean), PEX crimp tool + rings (for copper/brass fittings — pull the heads of the ring to seal pipe onto fitting). PEX expansion tool (for Uponor — expands pipe, fitting inserted, pipe shrinks tight. More expensive tool, better for ease-of-install. Most DIYers use crimp rings)

## Pipe Types
- **Copper**: lasts 50-80 years, expensive, requires soldering (or push-fit/SharkBite). Used for water supply lines. Type M (thin wall, most common — residential), Type L (thicker, commercial), Type K (thickest, underground). L recommended for main lines
- **PEX (cross-linked polyethylene)**: flexible plastic, freeze-resistant (expands slightly instead of bursting), easy DIY, cheaper than copper — 30-50 year lifespan. Colors: red (hot), blue (cold), white (either). Can run long continuous lengths, fewer joints = fewer leaks
  - Connects via: crimp rings + copper fittings (cheapest/tool ~$40) or expansion rings (Uponor/Wirsbo — $100+ tool). PEX A (PEX/Uponor) = most flexible (burst resistant, freeze tolerant) vs PEX B (crimp) = stiffer, tighter coil. PEX A available in a coil and kink-resistant
- **PVC**: white/gray rigid plastic. For DWV (drain waste vent), irrigation, not for hot water (softens). Joints with primer + solvent cement. Schedule 40 (standard) vs Schedule 80 (thicker, stronger)
- **CPVC**: light yellow/tan, for hot+cold supply. Like PVC but rated for hot water. Less common now than PEX. Joints with solvent cement
- **Galvanized steel**: older homes (pre-1960s) — interior rusts, restricts flow, bad for health (lead in old solder + zinc). Replace with copper or PEX. Zinc coating fails = rust flakes in water
