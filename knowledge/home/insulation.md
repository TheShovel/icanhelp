# Home Insulation & Ventilation

Types, R-values, installation, and air sealing for energy efficiency.

## Insulation Types
- **Fiberglass batts**: Pre-cut panels (16" or 24" width for standard stud/joist spacing). R-13 (2x4 wall), R-19 (2x6 wall), R-30 (attic). Cheapest DIY option. Must be cut precisely around obstructions. Irritating to skin/lungs
- **Fiberglass loose-fill**: Blown-in for attics, hard-to-reach spaces — requires rental blower. Better coverage in irregular spaces. Settles ~10% over time
- **Rock wool (mineral wool)**: Better fire resistance, water repellent, sound damping. Denser than fiberglass. More expensive. Roxul/ Rockwool brand. Doesn't irritate skin as much
- **Spray foam**:
  - Open-cell: Low density, R-3.5-4 per inch, vapor permeable, cheaper. Good for interior walls, noise reduction
  - Closed-cell: High density, R-6-7 per inch, vapor barrier, structural strength (adds racking resistance). For rim joists, crawl spaces, exterior walls (especially in humid climates). DIY kits (Froth-Pak) vs professional
- **Rigid foam boards**:
  - EPS (Expanded Polystyrene): White beadboard, R-4 per inch, cheap. Good for below-grade (can get wet)
  - XPS (Extruded Polystyrene): Blue/pink/purple, R-5 per inch, closed-cell, better moisture resistance. For basement walls
  - Polyiso: Foil-faced, R-6-6.5 per inch, highest per inch. Efficiency drops in cold (<20°F). For exterior continuous insulation
- **Cellulose**: Recycled newspaper treated with borates (fire, pest resistance). Blown-in. R-3.5 per inch. Good thermal performance, better air sealing than fiberglass. Settles ~15-20%

## R-Values (Recommended)
- **Attic**: R-49 to R-60 (16-20" fiberglass or 15-18" cellulose)
- **Walls**: R-13 to R-21 (2x4 walls R-13 batts, 2x6 walls R-19-R-21)
- **Floors** (above unheated space): R-25 to R-30
- **Crawl space walls**: R-10 to R-15 rigid foam (conditioned crawl space) or R-19 for floor insulation
- **Basement walls**: R-10 to R-15 rigid foam (interior or exterior)
- **Slab edge**: R-5 to R-10 (2" rigid foam vertical at slab perimeter)

## Air Sealing
- **Biggest leaks**: Attic hatch (cover with insulated box + weatherstrip), rim joists (seal with rigid foam + spray foam), plumbing/electrical penetrations (caulk or foam), recessed lights (IC-rated covers), windows (weatherstripping, caulk), doors (sweeps, weatherstripping), sill plate (foam gasket between concrete and wood)
- **Materials**: Caulk (acrylic latex for cracks under 1/4", polyurethane/ silicone for larger gaps), spray foam (expandable — Great Stuff: window/door foam for non-expanding, regular foam for gaps, fire block for around penetrations), weatherstripping (V-strip for sides of doors/windows, foam tape for irregular gaps, door sweeps for bottom), acoustic sealant (for large gaps, stays flexible)
- **Blower door test**: Professional test — depressurizes house to measure total air leakage. Target: < 3 ACH50 (air changes per hour at 50Pa) for existing homes, < 1 for new construction Passive House
- **Thermal bypass**: Air moving through gaps bypasses insulation — worst in attics (fluffy insulation doesn't stop air movement, only slows conductive heat). Air seal before adding insulation

## Ventilation
- **Attic**: Ridge vent + soffit vents (continuous air flow from soffit to ridge). No gable vents when ridge vent used (short-circuits airflow). Soffit baffles keep insulation from blocking soffit vents. Attic should NOT be sealed — needs to breathe
- **Bathroom fans**: Vent outside (not to attic). 50 CFM minimum, 1 CFM per sq ft of room preferred. Quiet fans rated <1 sone. Humidity-sensing switch optional
- **Range hood**: Kitchen ventilation — 100 CFM minimum, 1 CFM per 100 BTU for gas stove (400 CFM+). Ducted (outside) vs ductless (recirculates through charcoal filter). Ducted strongly preferred for moisture/grease/CO removal
- **HRV/ERV**: Heat Recovery Ventilator (HRV) — exchanges heat between outgoing stale air and incoming fresh air. Energy Recovery Ventilator (ERV) also transfers humidity. Essential for tight houses (blows air change per hour). Balanced ventilation — 60-80% efficiency
- **Whole house fan**: Attic-mounted, pulls air from open windows through house into attic, exhausts through attic vents. Great for cooling in temperate climates. Use only when outdoor air is cool + dry
- **Crawl space**: 1 sq ft vent per 150 sq ft crawl space (for vented). Encapsulated (sealed + insulated + dehumidified) better for energy and moisture. Dehumidifier recommended for sealed crawl spaces

## Moisture Control
- **Vapor barriers**: In cold climates (class I/II vapor retarder on warm-in-winter side — interior). In hot-humid climates (exterior). For mixed climates, class III vapor retarder (latex paint) or smart vapor retarder (varies permeability with humidity — MemBrain)
- **Condensation issues**: Warm moist air hitting cold surface → water → mold/ rot. Prevent by keeping warm interior air away from cold surfaces (air sealing) or keeping cold surfaces warm enough (insulation)
- **Dew point**: Temperature at which air saturates and condenses. In walls, dew point should be in or near insulation, not inside wall cavity — determined by insulation R-value placement relative to temperature gradient

## Installation Tips
- **Batts**: Full depth, no compression (compression reduces R-value). Cut slightly oversized and friction-fit. Split around wiring — don't compress behind. No gaps
- **Blown-in**: Rental blower needed. Use baffles for cathedral ceilings. Netting or fabric for vertical walls. Density matters — too fluffy = less R-value. Target 3-4 lbs/cu ft for cellulose
- **Rigid foam**: Tape seams (foil tape or house wrap tape). For basement: glue to concrete with construction adhesive + fastener (Tapcon masonry screws with large washer). XPS or EPS for below-grade
- **Spray foam**: Temperature and humidity affect curing. Professional application for large areas. DIY kits: shake vigorously, apply in thin passes (too thick = overheating, delamination). PPE required (isocyanates sensitize respiratory system)
