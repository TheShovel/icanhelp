# Drywall and Plaster

## Drywall Basics

- **Drywall types:** Standard (white/ivory) for general rooms. Moisture-resistant (green board) for bathrooms, basements — not waterproof, just mold-resistant. Mold/mildew-resistant (purple board) — better moisture core. Fire-rated (Type X, 5/8") for garages, furnace rooms, between units. Blue board for veneer plaster. Sound-dampening (double-layer or proprietary).
- **Thicknesses:** 1/4" (curved walls, over old surfaces), 3/8" (limited use), 1/2" (standard walls, ceiling 16" OC), 5/8" (ceilings 24" OC, fire-rated, stiffer). Use 5/8" on garage ceiling attached to living space above.
- **Screwing schedule:** Walls — 8" OC on ceilings, 12" OC on walls, 16" OC in corners. Screw 3/8" from edge (avoid breaking paper). Slightly dimple surface without breaking paper. Use drywall screw gun with depth-sensitive clutch, or drill with dimpler bit.
- **Cutting:** Score face paper with utility knife, snap back (clean break), cut back paper. Cutouts (outlets): measure from nearest panel edge, mark, cut with drywall saw, or use rotozip/router for production work. Keyhole saw for rough openings.

## Taping and Mudding (Coat System)

- **Compound types:** All-purpose (pre-mixed, coats 1–3) — some shrinking. Taping (drying-type, low shrink). Topping (for final coat, easier sanding). Hot mud (powder, sets chemically in 20–90 min) — no shrink, good for patches/fast work. Lightweight compound reduces dust.
- **Coat 1 — Tape:** Embed paper tape (or fiberglass mesh) in thin bedding coat (~1/8"). Paper tape adds strength (tear-resistant). Fiberglass mesh self-adheres but requires full fill (cracks may telegraph through paper). Press tape tight with 6" knife, remove excess mud. Let dry 24 hr.
- **Coat 2 — Fill:** Apply wider 8–10" knife over tape. Feather edges. Fill fastener dimples. Let dry.
- **Coat 3 — Final skim:** 12" knife, thin compound, feather edges 12–16" wide. Dimples. Minimal sanding if done well. Spot prime before painting.
- **Mud mixing:** Add water for pre-mixed joint compound (thin to mayonnaise consistency) — stir drill with paddle. Don't over-mix (adds air bubbles). Hot mud — mix only as much as you can use before set time.
- **Inside corners:** Paper tape folded (crease with fingers). Apply mud to both sides, press tape into corner with corner trowel or 4" knife. Coat each side separately. Outside corners use metal or paper-faced metal corner bead.
- **Butt joints (factory edges):** Pre-fill with setting-type compound to minimize hump. Feather wide (12"–14" each side). Butt joints are the hardest to hide — plan sheet layout to stagger them away from lighting.

## Patching Holes

- **Small holes (<6"):** Cut patch from scrap drywall slightly larger than hole. Trace hole on patch, cut along inside. Apply hot mud around hole opening, insert patch (with backing strip or drywall clips), mud over tape. Mesh tape over seam.
- **Medium holes (6"–12"):** Cut hole into clean rectangle. Install wood/metal backing strips screwed into existing drywall. Cut patch to fit, screw into backers, tape and mud.
- **Large holes (damage >12"):** Cut back to nearest studs. Remove damaged section. Nail new blocking between studs if needed. Cut full sheet to fit, screw to studs, tape all seams.
- **Old work (butterfly / California patch):** Cut scrap drywall 2" larger than hole each side. Cut back-paper score line 1" in from edge, remove face paper from outer strip (leave face paper on center). Apply mud to opening edges, insert patch, mud over paper edges.

## Corner Bead

- **Metal corner bead:** Nail or screw through pre-punched holes. Most durable, but can dent. Cut to length with aviation snips. Use corner trowel or 8" knife to mud one side at a time. Slight reveal (1/8") prevents spider cracks.
- **Vinyl/plastic corner bead:** Nail or staple. Flexible — good for curved walls. Can split under impact.
- **Paper-faced metal bead:** Pre-folded, staples to corner, adds strength, minimal mud needed. Best for interior corners visible under strong light.
- **No-coat / flex bead:** Pre-dimpled edge creates stop for mud — no need to feather. Fast install, forgiving, but not as strong.
- **Bullnose bead:** Corner radius ~1/2". Requires full coat coverage. Use 3 coats with 8" knife.

## Sanding Techniques

- **Pole sander (screen or sandpaper):** 80–120 grit for initial. Use fine (150–180) for finish. Switch to wet sanding for dust-free — damp sponge in circular motion.
- **Avoid over-sanding:** Cut paper = weak joint. Stop when mud surface transitions from glossy to matte at edges. Feather edge is key.
- **Dust control:** Sanding attachment for shop vac (vac-boom). Enclose room with plastic. Use drywall sanding sponge for hard-to-reach. Wear respirator (N95 minimum, P100 better). Silica dust is a carcinogen.
- **Sponge finishing:** Damp sponge (not wet) along dried mud — smooths ridges without dust. Rinse sponge frequently.

## Plaster Repair (Lath and Plaster)

- **Lath and plaster construction:** Wood or metal lath strips nailed to studs, with three-coat plaster (scratch ~1/4", brown ~3/8", finish ~1/8"). Harder, more soundproof than drywall but brittle.
- **Hairline cracks:** V-groove with utility knife or crack widener. Fill with patching plaster (durabond, Structolite, or hot mud). Embed fiberglass mesh tape. Skim-coat entire wall area if many cracks.
- **Small holes:** Clean loose plaster. Apply setting-type compound (durabond) in layers — fill halfway, add mesh tape, finish flush. Use bonding agent (Plaster-Weld or similar) on old plaster/dust before applying patch.
- **Loose plaster (bulging from lath):** Key has broken — plaster no longer bonded to lath. Re-attach with plaster washers (large galvanized washers + drywall screws) through to lath/studs. Drill holes first to avoid cracking more. Then patch screw holes.
- **Large damaged areas:** Remove loose plaster back to sound material, exposing lath. If lath intact: apply scratch + brown coats (use Structolite for brown). If lath rotted: cut back to studs, install new drywall flush to old plaster surface, tape transition (consider 5/8" drywall + plaster finish coat over top).
- **Skim coating over damaged/cracked plaster:** Clean surface, apply bonding agent, apply 1/8" setting compound in 2–3 coats. Alternative: 1/2" drywall over existing plaster (fur out electrical boxes/trim as needed).

## Texture Matching

- **Orange peel (fine stipple):** Spray texture in can (small repairs) or hopper gun + compressor for larger areas. Thin compound to paint-like consistency. Practice on cardboard to match nozzle tip and air pressure. Alternative: use texture brush and stipple.
- **Knockdown (splattered, flattened):** Apply splatter with hopper gun (larger drops than orange peel). Let dry ~10–20 min (until no longer shiny). Gently drag wide knife (12" or skimming blade) across in single direction to flatten peaks. Avoid overworking — creates smooth spots.
- **Skip trowel / Spanish lace:** Thick mud applied with trowel or wide knife in random pattern. Drag trowel lightly to "skip" and expose drywall. Practice is critical — more additive than subtractive.
- **Slap brush / stomp:** Mud applied with brush (stipple brush) and stomped or twisted to create pattern. Common on ceilings. Use thinned compound and texture brush.
- **Popcorn / acoustic texture:** Ceiling-specific. Spray mixed popcorn texture (pre-mix or add polystyrene beads). May contain asbestos (pre-1978) — test before sanding. Removal: scrape after wetting with water sprayer (check for asbestos first). Current code limits popcorn use due to fire hazard.
- **Smooth finish (Level 5):** Skim coat entire wall with thin topping compound, then sand smooth. Required under high-gloss paint or strong side lighting. Most labor-intensive.
- **Texturing best practices:** Prime wall first (different absorption = uneven texture). Mask floors/walls thoroughly. Practice on scrap. Multiple thin passes better than one heavy. Keep texture compound consistent.
