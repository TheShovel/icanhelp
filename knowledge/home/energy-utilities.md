# Energy & Utilities Management

## Electricity Bill Breakdown
- **Supply vs delivery**: supply/generation (the electricity itself, ~40-60% of bill — competitive market where available). Delivery/transmission + distribution (wires, poles, transformers, maintenance — regulated, ~40-60%). In deregulated states: choose your supplier, but delivery stays with utility
  - Understand your bill: kWh (kilowatt-hours) = actual energy usage. kW (demand) = peak usage. Delivery charges: based on how much you use when the grid is stressed. Fixed monthly fee: $10-30 for meter + connection
- **Time-of-use rates**: cheaper at night (off-peak), expensive 4-9 PM (peak). Shift usage: run dishwasher/dryer overnight, charge EV at night, pre-cool house before peak. Potential savings: 10-30%. TOU plans require behavioral changes; if you can't shift load, you may pay more than flat rate
- **Deregulation**: some states allow choosing supplier (not delivery provider). Compare rates (¢/kWh for generation). Beware: variable rates (can spike), teaser rates (jump after 3-6 months), early termination fees. Fixed rate for 12-36 months = predictable. In regulated states (most SE US, NW, parts of Midwest): monopoly utility controls generation + delivery. Compare power companies: if available, third-party suppliers (they procure energy, utility still delivers). Check: renewable energy content (some suppliers offer 100% green)
- **Average usage**: 886 kWh/month (US average 2021). Range: 500-2,000 depending on home size, climate, occupants. 1,000 sq ft apt ~500-800 kWh. 2,500 sq ft home ~1,200-1,800. Heat pump / AC biggest user (30-50% of total). Water heater 12-15%. Refrigerator 5%. Lighting 5-10%. Electronics 10%

## Natural Gas Bill
- **Main uses**: space heating (50-70% of gas bill in cold months). Water heating (20-30%). Cooking (5-10%). Drying clothes (5-10%)
- **Therms vs CCF**: gas measured in therms (100,000 BTUs). 1 CCF (100 cubic ft) ≈ 1 therm. Average: 50-150 therms/month in winter for northern homes. Summer: 10-30 therms (just water heat, stove, maybe pool/spa)
- **Energy content**: 1 therm of natural gas = 29.3 kWh of electricity. At $1/therm vs $0.12/kWh: gas = $0.03/kWh equivalent vs $0.12/kWh (gas 4x cheaper per unit heat). But gas heating appliances less efficient than electric heat pumps? A heat pump (COP 3) = $0.04/kWh vs gas $1/therm → gas still slightly cheaper in most US markets. HP at efficient COP 3-4 = competitive with $1.20/therm gas
- **Cost savings**: lower thermostat (1°F saves ~3%). Seal leaks. Insulate ducts, water pipes, attic floor. Upgrade furnace from 80% AFUE to 95% = 15% gas reduction. Tankless water heater: ~30% less gas than tank

## Water & Sewer Bill
- **Usage**: average US family ~12,000 gallons/month (300 gallons/day/person). Breakdown: showers 20%, toilets 30%, faucets 15%, clothes washer 15%, leaks 10%, other 10%
- **Sewer vs water**: sewer typically 50-100% of water charge (based on water usage assumption most water goes down drain). Some utilities: winter averaging (base sewer on winter usage, when outdoor use zero). Leak adjustment: some utilities forgive excess usage from detected leak (with proof of repair)
- **Water-saving ROI**: low-flow showerhead ($10, savings $50-100/yr). WaterSense toilet ($150-500, 30% toilet water savings = 11,000 gal/yr ≈ $100 saving). Faucet aerators ($2, save 30-50% at that faucet). Leak detection: 10% of homes have leak >90 gal/day. Fix dripping faucet ($10, saves $30-50/yr if hot water). Replace old washing machine with HE front-load: saves 30-50 gal/load

## Solar Panels
- **Types**: monocrystalline (most efficient, 20-23%, black, expensive). Polycrystalline (16-18%, blue, cheaper, less efficient). Thin-film (amorphous Si/ CdTe/ CIGS, 10-18%, flexible, less efficient, large area needed). Most residential: monocrystalline (REC, SunPower, QCELLS, Silfab, Panasonic)
- **System size**: typical 5-10 kW for home (requirement: 10-20 panels × 400W each). Roof area needed: ~400 sq ft for 6 kW. Annual production: ~1,200-1,500 kWh per kW installed per year (varies by location, orientation, tilt). In AZ/CA: 1,500-1,800 kWh/kW. In Pacific NW/Northeast: 1,000-1,200. In cloudy Seattle: 900-1,000
  - Panel wattage 2024: 400-450W typical (residential). Panel degradation: ~0.5%/year, 80-90% output after 25 years (warranty: 25 yr for 80-85% production usually)
- **Cost**: $2.50-3.50/watt installed (after 30% federal tax credit). 6 kW system: $15-21k before tax credit, $10.5-14.7k after. Payback: 5-12 years. Tesla solar: $2.50/watt, but service issues. Sunrun: leases (avoid unless no tax liability — lease = third party owns panels, you pay them less than utility, no upfront cost but less long-term benefit). Cash purchase > loan > lease. Best return: cash or low-interest solar loan. "Solar loans": 10-15 year term
- **Net metering**: excess solar energy exported to grid → credit on bill. Full retail (1 kWh export = 1 kWh credit) in some states (MA, NY, UT, OK, parts of others). Reduced (avoided cost) in others: utility pays wholesale rate (2-4¢/kWh) vs retail 10-20¢. State policies vary widely. Without net metering: solar costs more, battery storage more valuable. NEM 3.0 (California 2023): reduced solar export credit to ~8¢/kWh — battery storage essential for payoff. NEM 2.0 (old CA): 1:1 retail net metering
- **Battery storage**: Tesla Powerwall 3 ($9-12k, 13.5 kWh usable, 10 yr warranty). Enphase IQ Battery 10T ($8-12k). LG Chem RESU ($7-10k). Purpose: backup power + store solar for self-consumption (maximize ROI when net metering is poor). Backup load: power critical circuits (lights, fridge, well pump, furnace fan, modem) not whole house. Whole home backup: 2-3 Powerwalls ($20k+). Best for: expensive electricity, poor net metering, frequent power outages, time-of-use rates (discharge during peak, charge from solar/off-peak)
  - ROI calculation: $10k battery, daily arbitrage 10 kWh × $0.20/kWh × 365 = $730/yr = 13.7 yr payback. With TOU rates (peak $0.50, off-peak $0.10): charge at $0.10, discharge at $0.50 = $0.40/kWh spread × 10 kWh × 365 = $1,460/yr = 7 yr payback

## Generator
- **Portable**: 5,000-10,000W, $500-2,000. Runs on gasoline (noisy, 10-20 hr runtime). Extension cords to individual appliances. Manual transfer switch: $500-1,000 installed (avoids backfeeding through house). NEVER operate indoors or in garage (CO kills)
- **Standby**: 10,000-22,000W, $3,000-8,000 + installation $2,000-5,000. Automatic transfer switch (turns on within 10-30 sec of outage). Runs on natural gas or propane (fuel from utility line — no refueling needed during outage). No extension cords, connected to house circuits. Better: covers whole house or essential circuits. Rougher: maintenance (oil changes, battery, exercise weekly). Lasts ~15-20 years with maintenance
- **Inverter generator** (Honda, Yamaha): clean power for sensitive electronics (no harmonic distortion: sine wave vs modified sine wave). Quieter, more fuel-efficient. Smaller capacity (2,000-7,000W). For CPAP, laptops, and sensitive electronics: inverter = must. For power tools/refrigerator: regular generator fine. Parallel kit: connect two inverters for 2x power
- **Whole-house load calculation**: sum wattage of lights + appliances + well pump + AC (multiply starting surge × 2-3 for motor loads). Typical: 10-12 kW generator for essential circuits (well pump, furnace fan, fridge, sump, lights). 20-22 kW for most/all house. Major loads: AC 3-5 kW running (starting surge 6-15 kW). Well pump: 1-2 kW running (surge 3-5 kW). Microwave: 1-1.5 kW
