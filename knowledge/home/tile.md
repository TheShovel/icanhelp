# Tile Work

## Tile Types

- **Ceramic tile:** Clay-based, glazed (color in top layer) or unglazed. Body porcelain if water absorption <0.5%. PEI rating 1–5 (1 = wall only, 5 = heavy commercial). Glazed ceramic is stain-resistant. Durable but can chip if body is soft. Cost: $1–$5/sqft installed.
- **Porcelain tile:** Denser, harder, lower water absorption than ceramic. Through-body color (color runs through tile) — good for cuts/edges. Full-body porcelain for high-traffic. Rectified edges allow tight grout lines (1/16"). Can be polished, matte, textured. Cost: $3–$15/sqft.
- **Natural stone tile:** Marble (soft, etches from acid — use sealer), granite (very hard, low maintenance), travertine (porous, needs filled/unfilled options), slate (split-face texture, variable thickness), limestone (soft, high maintenance, avoid kitchens/baths). All require sealing. Cost varies widely.
- **Glass tile:** Translucent/colored, non-porous, stain-proof. Prone to cracking if substrate flexes. Use white thinset behind translucent glass. Difficult to cut — need wet saw with glass blade or score/snap cutter for mosaics. Cost: $7–$30/sqft.
- **Mosaic tile:** Small format (2×2" or mesh-mounted). Sheet-mounted for faster install. Use on shower floors for slip resistance. Level substrate carefully — small tiles telegraph imperfections.
- **Porcelain tile vs LVT/LVP:** Tile is harder, cooler, lower maintenance long-term. LVP quieter/warmer underfoot, easier to install, but less durable and lower resale value.

## Underlayment / Substrate Prep

- **Cement board (CBU):** 1/4" (walls) or 1/2" (floors). Cut with carbide scorer or grinder. Screw every 6"–8" with special cement board screws. Tape joints with alkali-resistant mesh tape and thinset. Do NOT use drywall screws or paper tape.
- **Uncoupling membrane (Ditra, RedGard uncoupling mat):** Decouples tile from subfloor movement. Roll-on crack isolation membrane for small cracks. Ditra on plywood subfloor before tile — waterproofs and uncouples. Must be embedded in thinset — trowel both sides.
- **Plywood subfloor:** Must be L/360 deflection rating for tile. Double-layer plywood (min 1-1/8" total) recommended. No OSB directly under tile long-term (potential swelling). Use cement board or uncoupling membrane over plywood.
- **Concrete subfloor:** Must cure minimum 28 days. Check moisture — use calcium chloride test (<3 lbs/1000 sqft/24 hr for impervious tile, <5 for stone). Use crack isolation membrane over control joints. Don't tile directly over expansion joints.
- **WarmWire / radiant heat:** Embed in self-leveling underlayment or thinset. Check compatibility with deflection. Use uncoupling membrane over.
- **Subfloor flatness:** Must be flat within 1/4" in 10' for large format tile, 1/8" in 6' for smaller tile. Use self-leveling compound (Ardex, LevelQuik) on uneven concrete. Self-leveler requires primer first.

## Layout Planning

- **Center lines:** Find room center (+ perpendicular lines). Dry-lay tiles from center to check cuts at walls. Adjust starting point so cut tiles at both ends are >½ tile width. Avoid sliver cuts (<2") at walls.
- **Offset patterns:** Running bond (brick pattern) requires ≥33% overlap for wood-look planks. Herringbone, diagonal (45°), basketweave, hopscotch (5-spot, use 2 tile sizes). Modular patterns need specific ratios (e.g., 8×8 + 8×16 + 16×16).
- **Shower layout:** Start with whole tile at ceiling or floor? Typically full tile at bottom (waste at top behind shower head). Accent strip / niche: center on eye level (48"–54" finished height). Niche: waterproof membrane fully integrated with pan liner.
- **Grout line size:** Rectified porcelain = 1/16"–1/8". Calibrated stone = 3/16"–1/4". Subway tiles = 1/8". Match grout line to tile tolerances — non-rectified requires thicker lines.
- **Curb/saddle layout:** Tile top with same floor tile (or contrast). Curb height: 2" minimum above drain. Slope top toward shower, or slightly level with slight drain slope.

## Cutting Tools

- **Snap cutter (manual rail cutter):** Score + snap. Best for straight cuts on ceramic and some porcelain. Fast, no water/dust. Cuts up to 24" or 48" depending on model. Do NOT use for natural stone or very hard porcelain.
- **Wet saw (tile saw):** Blades — continuous rim (smooth cuts, ceramic/porcelain), turbo rim (fast, for stone/resin bond), segmented rim (aggressive for granite). Replace blade when cutting slows. Water tray cools blade + suppresses dust. Use for L-cuts, angles, and hard tiles.
- **Angle grinder with diamond blade:** For plunge cuts, outlet cutouts, notches. Use wet (water bottle) for dust control or dry diamond blade. Grinder skills take practice — cut from back for cleaner edge.
- **Tile nippers:** Chip away small pieces (curves, irregular cuts). Score cut line with cutter first. Nippers used for shaping.
- **Rod saw / hacksaw with diamond wire:** For intricate curves in ceramic tiles. Slow but precise.
- **Drill bits:** Carbide-tipped or diamond core bits. Use diamond holesaw for large holes (faucets). Start at slow speed, keep wet.

## Thinset vs Mastic

- **Thinset (cement-based):** For floors, showers, large format, natural stone. Types: Modified (latex added, for plywood / cement board) — polymer provides flex and bond. Unmodified (for porcelain — use on concrete, or under Ditra). Large format tile requires LHT (large heavy tile) thinset with anti-sag properties.
- **Mastic (pre-mixed acrylic):** For wall tile only — small format ceramic (≤8×8"). Do NOT use wet areas (showers, pools) — mastic breaks down when wet. No drying shrinkage faster to use. Not suitable for stone, large tile, floors.
- **Epoxy thinset:** Chemical-resistant, fully waterproof. Used in commercial kitchens, pools, steam showers. Difficult to work with — high strength, fast setup. Expensive.
- **Mix consistency:** "Peanut butter" — stiff enough to hold trowel notch without slumping. Mix with drill + paddle. Let slake 5–10 min for modified thinset (re-hydrate polymers). Do not over-mix.
- **Trowel size:** Small format (<6"): 1/4"×3/16" notch. Wall tile (6–12"): 1/4"×1/4". Floor (12–16"): 1/4"×3/8". Large format (>16"): 1/2"×1/2" or 3/4"×3/4". Back-butter tiles >12" for full coverage. Check coverage after setting — minimum 80% dry area, 95% wet/shower.

## Grout Types

- **Sanded grout:** For gaps >1/8". Sand prevents cracking. Can scratch polished stone — use unsanded or epoxy on soft stone. Available in hundreds of colors.
- **Unsanded grout:** For gaps ≤1/8". Smooth, works with polished stone. Can shrink/crack in wide joints. Used for glass tile (sand scratches).
- **Epoxy grout (e.g., Spectralock, Fusion Pro):** Stain-proof, no sealing, flexible, waterproof. Harder to install — tight working time, residue cleanup must be precise. Expensive but ideal for kitchen counters, wet areas, high-traffic floors. No color variation between batches.
- **Furan grout:** Black chemical-resistant epoxy — industrial use only. Rare.
- **Grout consistency:** Stiff enough to pack joints fully. Wipe diagonally across joints with damp sponge (don't wash out joint). Clean haze after 20–30 min with microfiber. If grout smears or water runs milky — you're washing too much out.
- **Grout cure time:** 24–72 hr before walking on (epoxy 24 hr, cementitious longer). Wait 7 days before sealing cementitious grout.

## Sealing

- **Penetrating sealer:** Absorbs into stone/grout, repels water/oil. Use on natural stone, unglazed tile, cementitious grout. Glazed ceramic doesn't need sealing. Reapply annually (high-traffic) or every 2–3 yr.
- **Enhancing sealer:** Darkens/wets out stone color like water effect. Seals simultaneously. Test on scrap first.
- **Topical sealer:** Forms surface film (enhanced gloss). Not recommended for floors — wears unevenly, can peel. Use only for decorative non-traffic surfaces.
- **Grout sealer:** Spray-on or wipe-on. Usually penetrating. Some modern color-seal grout is pre-sealed. Recoat when water beads no longer form on grout.
- **Epoxy grout:** Does NOT need sealing.

## Tile Repair

- **Cracked tile removal:** Score grout around tile with grout saw or oscillating tool. Drill holes in center (several), then chip outward with hammer and cold chisel. Remove pieces. Clean out old thinset. Install new tile with matching thinset. Grout when set.
- **Matching replacement tile:** Keep original boxes. If discontinued — check attic/garage/crawlspace (original leftovers), buy from salvage stores, use similar tile under cabinet or move fixture.
- **Loose tile (hollow sound):** Check grout — if grout cracked or missing, water may have loosened thinset. Remove tile if loose, scrape old thinset, re-stick. For small areas: inject epoxy through drilled hole in grout line. Clamp with weight until set.
- **Broken grout:** Grind out with grout saw or dremel. Re-apply matching grout. If matching impossible, re-grout entire floor or use colorant/stain over old grout.
- **Cracked grout near walls:** Use caulk (sanded/color-matched silicone) at change of plane (wall-to-floor, wall-to-wall corners). Never grout plane changes.
