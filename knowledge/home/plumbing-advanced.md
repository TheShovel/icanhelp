# Home Plumbing Repairs (Advanced)

## Pipe Materials & When to Use

### Copper
- **Types**: Type M (thin wall, residential water supply), Type L (thicker, commercial/underground), Type K (thickest, underground/service). M is fine for above-ground interior — use L for underground or exposed
- **Joining**: solder (sweat) — clean pipe + fitting with emery cloth, apply flux (acid paste, prevents oxidation and helps solder flow), heat with torch (propane or MAPP gas), apply solder (lead-free 95/5 tin/antimony). When joint reaches temp, solder melts into gap via capillary action — touch at the gap edge, not the flame. Let cool naturally
  - Push-fit (SharkBite): expensive fittings ($5-15 each) but no torch, no flux, no solder. Great for repairs in tight spaces. Must fully insert pipe (measure depth mark). Can be removed with disconnect tool. Brass body with stainless steel teeth and EPDM O-ring. Rated for underground burial (wrap in foam + silicone grease if underground). NOT for behind walls? Actually they're reliable enough for walls with proper installation — 25-year warranty
  - Crimp (ProPress): tool $1,000+ (rental $100/day). Faster than solder, no fire risk, good for tight spaces. Used in commercial settings. Electrical crimp tool + mechanical press. Acceptable behind walls

### PEX
- **Types**: PEX-A (Uponor/Wirsbo — expander method, most flexible, kink-resistant, freeze-tolerant, connects with expansion rings: stretches pipe over fitting, then shrinks to fit. Best for cold climates — expands, less burst risk). PEX-B (crimp ring/ clamp method — uses copper crimp rings or stainless clamps. Stiffer, less expensive, more common for DIY). PEX-C (similar performance to B, different manufacturing — electronic crosslinking, slightly stiffer)
  - PEX-A expander: tool (~$100-250), rings (plastic — slide onto pipe, stretch, insert fitting, shrink to form seal). Expensive tool but permanent + high reliability
  - PEX-B crimp: tool (~$40-60), copper crimp rings or stainless cinch clamps. Easiest DIY. Most home centers sell this system
- **Pros**: flexible (bend radius ~6x diameter), fewer fittings (fewer leak points), freeze-resistant (expands 2x before burst, copper bursts at 1/3 expansion), cheaper than copper, easier install. Color-coded (red=hot, blue=cold, white=any)
- **Cons**: UV sensitive (cannot be exposed to sunlight — degrades), some concern about chemical leaching (minimal in modern PEX, approved for potable water throughout US/EU). Rodents may chew on it (like any plastic). Can't be used for outdoor exposed runs

### Push-Fit Accessories
- **SharkBite fittings**: brass body, stainless grab ring (holds pipe), EPDM O-ring (seals). Push pipe in until it bottoms out on the internal stop. Disconnect with plastic removal tool (or C-shaped orange tool). Compatible with copper, PEX, CPVC, PE-RT. **NOT** compatible with PE (polyethylene irrigation tubing) — black roll pipe with no markings. Max temp: 200°F at 80 PSI. 25-year warranty when installed on clean, properly cut pipe (square cut + deburred). Can be buried underground (wrap in foam insulation + silicone + or electrical tape to prevent corrosion)

### PVC & CPVC
- **PVC** (white/gray): Schedule 40 (standard DWV — drain waste vent). Schedule 80 (thicker, higher pressure — for industrial, not typically needed in home). For pressure applications, use NSF-PW certified pipe + fittings (have pressure rating; not all DWV PVC is pressure-rated). Maximum temp: 140°F (softens at higher temps). Cannot be used for hot water
  - Solvent welding: primer (purple: clean + soften pipe), then cement (apply to both pipe + fitting, push and twist 1/4 turn, hold 30 sec). Purple primer is required by code in many areas (identifies that primer was used). Use clear if purple visible looks ugly, but must still use primer
- **CPVC** (tan/off-white): rated for hot + cold supply (up to 200°F). Uses different solvent cement (yellow/green — CPVC-specific). More expensive than PVC. Less common than PEX for new construction. Common in areas with corrosive water (pH extremes that corrode copper). More brittle than PEX

## Advanced Repairs

### Replacing a Shutoff Valve
- **Under sink**: shut off main water. Unscrew old compression valve (loose the compression nut and ring — the ferrule compresses onto pipe, loosening by turning nut counter. If old ferrule stuck on pipe: remove carefully with hacksaw/pipe cutter, score cut through ferrule without cutting pipe, then pop off with screwdriver). Replace with new quarter-turn ball valve (far more reliable than multi-turn) — compression or push-fit. Better: ball valve with quarter-turn handle (on/off quickly, no stem wear). Avoid old multi-turn gate valves
  - Multi-turn valves: washer inside degrades over time → drip. Quarter-turn ball valve: Teflon ball seals against seats, last longer, open/close quickly

### Fixing Low Water Pressure
- **One fixture**: clean aerator (unscrew, soak in vinegar, clean debris). Check shutoff valve (fully open). Clean supply line (disconnect from fixture, check screened washer at connection). Remove showerhead, soak in vinegar if mineral deposits
- **Whole house**: check pressure regulator (PRV) — can adjust nut on top to boost pressure slightly (+5-10 PSI per turn?). Typically 40-80 PSI standard. Most PRVs are adjustable: loosen lock nut, turn adjusting screw clockwise to increase. Be aware: increase can cause water hammer (water rushing, pipes bang)
  - Old galvanized steel pipes: interior rust restricts flow. Replace with PEX/copper. Pipe diameter: older homes often 1/2" lines throughout when current code requires 3/4" main + 1/2" branches. Can upgrade to 3/4" at main

### Water Hammer
- **Cause**: fast-closing valve (washing machine, dishwasher, ice maker) suddenly stops water → pressure wave rattles pipes. Not just noise — can cause pipe damage over time (loose joints, cracks)
- **Fix**: install water hammer arrestor (precharged air chamber) at each fast-closing appliance. $15-20 each, threaded onto washing machine outlet box. Old method: vertical pipe stub (air chamber). Over time air dissolves into water (waterlogged) — no longer works. Modern piston/ bladder arrestors don't waterlog
  - Also: secure loose pipes with pipe clamps (add felt between pipe and clamp to prevent noise conduction). Air chambers in old houses: drain entire system, air will refill chamber (temporary fix)

### Sump Pump
- **Types**: submersible (in pit, quieter, longer life, more expensive) vs pedestal (motor above pit, cheaper, louder, shorter life). Submersible preferred for finished basements
  - Horsepower: 1/3 HP (standard basement, 1,500-3,000 gph), 1/2 HP (larger pits or deeper water), 3/4-1HP (heavy water/ high head). GPH rating varies by head height: rated at 0' lift (free flow) vs 10' lift (typical actual output ~1,000-2,000 gph)
- **Battery backup**: required if power goes out during heavy rain (when you need sump most). Water-powered backup (uses city water pressure to siphon — expensive to run, but works indefinitely without electricity). Battery backup units: $200-400, includes built-in charger, battery lasts 5-7 years, runs pump for hours depending on load
  - Check valve on discharge line: prevents water flowing back into pit when pump stops. Must be above floor level (or visible) for easy service. Dual float switches: tethered (long life, but can get stuck) vs vertical (narrow pits, less hang-up)
- **Maintenance**: check pit monthly — remove debris (gravel, sand, rocks can jam impeller). Clean intake screen. Pour bucket of water in to test activation. Replace pump every 5-10 years before it fails (don't wait for failure) — basement flood = $10k+ damage

### Garbage Disposal
- **Jam**: turn off power (unplug or use wall switch). Use hex key (Allen wrench) in center hole under unit — rotate back and forth to free impeller. Then press reset button on bottom. If still stuck: use flashlight to check for foreign objects (glass, metal, silverware)
  - NEVER put hand inside. Use tongs or pliers. Unplug before reaching in (most have no wall switch disconnect)
- **Leak**: check from top (sink flange — tighten with pipe wrench on basket? Usually tighten from top, counterclockwise; new plumber's putty under flange if still leak). Check hoses (dishwasher connection — tighten clamp). Check unit body (crack = replace). Replace disposal ($100-200) is simple: twist/pry locking ring, swap, reinstall

### Tankless Water Heater
- **Benefits**: endless hot water (up to rated GPM — typically 2-4 showers at once depending on model). Uses less energy (no standby heat loss. Electric tankless: 95-98% efficient? Actually, electric is near 100% efficient at point of use; gas 82-94% EF depending on unit). Saves space (wall-mounted). Longer life (20+ years vs 10-15 tank)
- **Cons**: high upfront cost ($800-2,000 + installation). Gas ones need larger gas pipe (3/4" vs 1/2") and adequate BTUs (199,000 BTU typical — may need gas line upgrade from meter, $$$). Electric ones need massive electrical capacity (100+ amps of 240V — ~$2,000 for panel upgrade plus new wires). If current system doesn't have capacity, tankless may be very expensive to install
- **Flow rate**: measure simultaneous hot water demand (2 showers at 2.0 GPM each + dishwasher 1.5 = 5.5 GPM). Choose unit rated for that flow at your incoming water temperature (cold winter = larger unit needed). Groundwater temp varies: 70°F in Florida, 40°F in Minnesota → tankless must heat water up 90° vs 55° difference — halves available GPM
- **Maintenance**: descale annually (vinegar/descaling solution through flush kit. Most come with isolation valves for flush). In hard water areas: more frequent descaling. Nylon hose connections for flush kit: connect to unit's hot + cold valves, submerge intake hose in descaling solution bucket, output hose back to bucket, recirculate with pump for 45 min. Missing annual flush = heat exchanger failure = expensive repair

### Water Softener
- **How it works**: ion exchange — resin beads swap calcium/magnesium (hardness) for sodium/potassium. Salt brine regenerates beads every 2-14 days (depending on usage). Regeneration flushes hard minerals down drain. Capacity measured in grains: typical 30,000-80,000 grain capacity for residential
- **When needed**: hard water (>7 grains per gallon — test strip). Signs: soap scum, white deposits on fixtures, scaling on water heater (reduces efficiency, lifespan), dry skin/hair. Most water across western + central US is hard
  - Soft water: better for appliances, less soap needed, no scaling. Some dislike slippery feel (from lack of minerals — not soap residue, actual feeling difference)
- **Installation**: before water heater + after any outdoor spigots (landscaping doesn't need softened water). Bypass valve required for service. Drain line (softener backwash water to floor drain or standpipe). Brine tank: salt pellets (solar or evaporated salt ~$10 per 40lb bag). Potassium chloride (sodium-free alternative, more expensive, for low-sodium diets)

## Toilet Repair (Advanced)

### Replacing Wax Ring (Toilet Leaking at Base)
1. Shut off water, flush empty, sponge out remaining water
2. Remove toilet (unbolt from floor — plastic caps pop off, 9/16" nut, disconnect water line). Tilt carefully, set on old towels/trash bag
3. Scrape old wax from flange (floor) + toilet horn. Wear gloves. Remove old wax — if old ring stuck in pieces, scrape off
4. Install new wax ring (plus rubber/felt flange extender if tile floor is higher than flange — common issue after bathroom renovation). Some plumbers prefer one wax ring with integral plastic flange horn (better seal)
  - Wax ring alternatives: wax-free rubber seal (SaniSeal, Fluidmaster Better Than Wax) — fits uneven floors, compressible, can reposition toilet after setting (impossible with wax). Works well, less forgiving if flange is recessed
5. Set toilet: new bolts (T-bolts into flange). Lower toilet straight down over bolts, push down firmly, twist slightly to seat wring (rock front to back, not side to side to avoid cracking). Don't rock. Tighten nuts evenly (alternating sides) until snug — overtightening = crack porcelain
6. Reconnect water, test for leaks at base. Caulk around base (leave back gap for leak detection — you DON'T caulk completely around, only 3 sides: both sides + front, leave back uncaulked so you see water trail if wax fails)

### Toilet Flush Mechanism (Fill Valve)
- Replace fill valve (Fluidmaster 400A universal, ~$10). Shut water, flush, disconnect water line, loosen lock nut on tank interior, pull old valve out. Adjust height (mark at 1" below overflow tube). Connect new valve: tighten lock nut, connect water supply, adjust float (water level 1/2" below overflow tube top)
