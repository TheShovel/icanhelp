# Home Canning & Preserving

## Equipment
- **Water bath canner**: large pot with rack (holds jars submerged). For high-acid foods only (pH <4.6): fruit, jam, jelly, pickles, tomatoes (with added acid), chutney, vinegar-based sauces. $20-40. Can substitute: large stockpot with rack or towel on bottom
- **Pressure canner**: for low-acid foods (pH >4.6): vegetables, meat, poultry, fish, beans, soup, stock. MUST use pressure canner (NOT pressure cooker — cookers smaller, don't maintain steady pressure, not safe for canning). Dial gauge (must be calibrated yearly) vs weighted gauge (self-regulating, more reliable). $100-400
  - DO NOT use electric pressure cookers (Instant Pot) for canning — not safe (don't maintain correct pressure + temperature consistently for long enough). Only USDA-approved pressure canners with proper pressure gauge
- **Jars**: Mason jars (Ball, Kerr, Bernardin, Weck). Regular mouth (for jams, pickles) vs wide mouth (for whole vegetables, meat, soups). Sizes: 4oz, 8oz (jelly/jam), 16oz/pint, 32oz/quart. DON'T reuse commercial jars (pasta sauce, mayo — not tempered for home canning, crack under heat + pressure)
- **Lids**: 2-piece — flat (sealing surface, single-use) + band (reusable, screw ring). Tattler reusable lids (plastic + rubber gasket — expensive upfront, lasts years, but seal different, user preference). Flat lid compound: must be brand new for proper seal. Old lids: DO NOT reuse (seal failure guaranteed)
- **Tools**: jar lifter (rubber grips — essential, $8). Funnel (wide mouth, regular mouth). Bubble remover / headspace tool (plastic or wooden, non-metallic — metal can scratch glass in jar). Lid lifter (magnetic wand to lift lids from hot water). Kitchen towels, timer, labeled permanent marker

## Science of Canning
- **Botulism**: Clostridium botulinum bacteria produces deadly toxin (fatal in minute doses). Spores: heat-resistant. Require: low acid (pH>4.6), anaerobic (vacuum sealed), low salt/sugar. ONLY pressure canning reaches 240-250°F to kill spores. Botulism toxin: destroyed by boiling 10 min, but not by cooking — so boiling canned low-acid food 10 min kills any possible toxin (safety measure)
  - DON'T taste test if canning low-acid food! If suspect spoilage (bulging lid, off odor, foam, spurt upon opening): DO NOT eat, do NOT taste. Wrap jar in plastic bag, discard in outdoor trash, wash all surfaces with bleach. If store-bought can bulges, return or discard (but botulism common in improper home canning only in very rare events)
- **Acid**: high-acid foods inhibit botulism — only need water bath (212°F). Test pH: need <4.6 for safe water bath. Tomatoes: pH varies (4.7-5.0) — MUST add acid (lemon juice: 1 tbsp per pint, 2 tbsp per quart OR citric acid: 1/4 tsp per pint, 1/2 tsp per quart). Cider vinegar (5% acidity) for pickles
- **Headspace**: 1/4" for jams/jellies, 1/2" for fruits/pickles/tomatoes, 1" for low-acid vegetables, meats — necessary for expansion + vacuum

## Processing Steps
1. **Prep**: wash jars (hot soapy water, keep hot). Simmer lids (180°F, NOT boiling — damages sealant). Prepare food per recipe
2. **Fill**: use funnel, leave proper headspace. Remove bubbles (run bubble remover around inside edge), wipe rims clean (vinegar on paper towel removes residue, ensures seal), center lid, apply band fingertip-tight
3. **Process water bath**: fully cover jars with water (1-2" above). Bring to boil, start timer. Don't boil too hard (agitates inside jars). Keep covered
4. **Process pressure canner**: vent steam 10 min (to expel air), then pressurize to correct PSI (dial gauge: 11 PSI for up to 1,000 ft, weighted gauge: 10 PSI / 15 PSI above 1,000 ft). Maintain steady pressure. Start timer after pressure reached
   - At altitude: increase processing time for water bath (2% per 1,000 ft). Pressure canning: increase pressure, not time (15 PSI for >1,000 ft if using weighted gauge, or adjust dial gauge to altitude)
5. **Cool**: turn off heat, let pressure release naturally (pressure canner, 0 PSI, wait 10 min remove jars). Remove jars, place on towel (not directly on cold surface), leave undisturbed 12-24 hours. Don't tighten bands, don't invert jars (sugar can break seal)
6. **Test seals**: press center of lid (should not flex). Remove bands, try to lift lid off by edges. Store without bands (to prevent trapped moisture → rust). Label with contents + date. Wipe jars with damp cloth to remove residue
7. **Storage**: cool, dark, dry (60-75°F). Don't store above oven or in direct sunlight (heat degrades quality). Best used within 1 year (safe indefinitely but quality declines). Home canned food can last years if stored properly

## What to NOT Can
- **Dairy**: milk, cream, cheese (unsafe, low acid, protein breaks down, separates. Soft cheese = botulism risk. Hard cheese: texture changes, cheese curdle). No safety-tested recipes for pressure canning dairy
- **Flour/thickener**: cornstarch, flour, arrowroot (interferes with heat penetration, leads to under-processing, rice + pasta same issue — starch absorbs liquid, expands, jar may not come up to safe temp, will also be mushy). Use ClearJel (modified cornstarch, heat-stable, USDA approved for canning) for pie fillings. Not all starches are equal
- **Eggs**: protein breaks down, texture = rubber. Safety risk (low acid, anaerobic. botulism risk)
- **Pumpkin butter/purée**: density prevents heat penetration. Sugar pumpkin purée: NOT safe for water bath (low acid + dense). Must pressure can cubed pumpkin only (not mashed)
- **Fats/oils**: canning in oil (garlic in oil = botulism risk! Must be refrigerated and used within 2 weeks, not shelf stable). Use: herbs in olive oil = refrigerate only. Meats may be canned plain (without flour, starchy bags)
- **Lettuce, leafy greens** become slimy, inedible. Not safe — too dense for heat penetration
- **Pureed vegetables**: too thick for proper heat penetration (corn, winter squash, pumpkin, beans, peas). Cube vegetables, process in liquid

## Troubleshooting
- **Seal failure**: lid didn't seal (concave click, not flex. If unsealed: re-process within 24 hrs with new lid (same processing time). If >24 hr: refrigerate and use within 1 week. Cause: not enough headspace, food residue on rim, overfilled, band too tight (air can't escape)
- **Siphoning** (liquid loss): too high pressure fluctuation, filled too full, not vented long enough for pressure canner. Can be from: heat too high, rapid temperature change. Food still safe — top up with boiling liquid before storage? No, if seal holds, food safe (just less liquid)
- **Floating food**: overfilled, air bubbles not released, food too light (fruit in syrup). Pack food tighter. Not a safety concern
- **Discoloration**: iron or sulfur from water/minerals. Darkening of fruit from oxidation (some fruits darken). Not a safety issue. Add lemon juice / citric acid to light colored fruit if desired
- **Mold**: seal failure (air got in). Discard entire jar, contents, avoid smelling. DO NOT scoop mold off — spores have penetrated entire jar. Food safety: discard
- **Fermentation**: liquid cloudy + fizzy = fermentation (jar wasn't sealed properly, yeast got in). Discard (except if you meant to ferment — that's different: pickles/spoiled? Check recipe. If you opened jars to atmosphere or under-processing. Usually discard)
