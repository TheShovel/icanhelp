# Home Fire Safety

## Smoke Alarms
Install smoke alarms inside bedrooms, outside sleeping areas, and on every level. Test monthly. Replace batteries when needed and replace the whole alarm according to its date label, often around 10 years.

## Carbon Monoxide Alarms
Use CO alarms near sleeping areas and on each level if the home has fuel-burning appliances, fireplace, attached garage, or generator risk. CO is odorless and can be deadly. If an alarm sounds, leave the building and call emergency services.

## Escape Plan
Every household needs two ways out of each sleeping area when possible. Pick an outside meeting place. Practice the route, especially with children, older adults, guests, and anyone who may need help evacuating.

## Kitchen Fires
Stay near active cooking. Keep towels, paper, packaging, and sleeves away from burners. For small grease fires, turn off heat and cover with a metal lid or baking sheet. Do not use water on grease fires.

## Extinguishers
Use an ABC-rated extinguisher for general home use. Remember PASS: Pull pin, Aim at base, Squeeze handle, Sweep side to side. If the fire is spreading, smoke is heavy, or exit is not clear, leave instead of fighting it.

## Electrical Fire Prevention
Do not run cords under rugs, overload power strips, or use damaged cords. Large heat-producing appliances should plug directly into wall outlets. Warm outlets, flickering lights, burning smells, or frequent breaker trips need attention.

## Heating Safety
Keep space heaters away from bedding, curtains, furniture, and pets. Use heaters with tip-over shutoff. Clean chimneys and vents as recommended. Never use ovens, grills, or outdoor heaters for indoor heating.

## Generators and Grills
Run generators, charcoal grills, and gas grills outdoors far from windows, doors, and vents. Garages and covered patios are not safe for generator operation. Carbon monoxide can enter the home quickly.

## After a Fire
Do not re-enter until authorities say it is safe. Photograph damage, contact insurance or landlord, save receipts, and avoid turning utilities back on without clearance if wiring, gas, or structure may be damaged.
