# Pool Maintenance

## Sanitizer Systems

- **Chlorine (tablets/granular/liquid):** Most common. Tablets (trichlor) dissolve slowly in skimmer/feeder — stabilizes pH down but raises cyanuric acid (CYA). Liquid chlorine (sodium hypochlorite) is fast-acting, raises pH, no CYA buildup. Granular (dichlor or calcium hypochlorite) for shocking or small doses. Cost: ~$100–$300/yr for tablets.
- **Salt water chlorinators (SWG):** Salt (NaCl) ~3000–4000 ppm passes through electrolytic cell producing free chlorine. Softer feel, less chemical handling. Cell replacement $300–$700 every 3–5 yr. Salt doesn't evaporate — only add when draining/backwashing. Still need CYA stabilizer. Corrosion risk for stone/lighting if salt migrates.
- **Bromine:** Better for hot tubs/spas — less odor, stable at higher temps. More expensive than chlorine. Degrades in sunlight (store indoors). Use sodium bromide + oxidizer (non-chlorine shock) to activate. Can use bromine tablets in a floater.
- **Biguanide/PHMB (Baquacil):** Non-halogen, no smell, no bleach effect. Expensive, prone to pink algae/slime, can't use standard test kits. Converting to chlorine requires draining.

## Water Chemistry Balancing

| Parameter | Ideal Range | Why It Matters |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| pH | 7.4–7.6 | Low = corrosive/eye sting; high = poor sanitizer efficacy, scaling |
| Total Alkalinity (TA) | 80–120 ppm | Buffer for pH; low = pH bounce; high = pH drift up |
| Calcium Hardness (CH) | 200–400 ppm | Low = etching plaster/finishes; high = scaling |
| Cyanuric Acid (CYA) | 30–50 ppm (outdoor) | Stabilizer for chlorine — slows UV degradation above 50 ppm causes chlorine lock |
| Free Chlorine (FC) | 1–3 ppm (7.5% of CYA for SWG) | Active sanitizer; must maintain CYA ratio to avoid algae |
| Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) | <1500 ppm | High TDS reduces sanitizer effectiveness, indicates need to drain |
- **Adjusting pH:** Muriatic acid (lowers) or sodium carbonate/soda ash (raises). Add slowly to deep end with pump running.
- **Adjusting TA:** Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) raises TA; muriatic acid lowers both pH and TA.
- **CYA management:** Added via cyanuric acid powder in skimmer sock. Only reduced by draining/backwashing.

## Shocking / Oxidizing

- **Calcium hypochlorite (cal-hypo):** Granular shock — 65–75% available chlorine. Adds calcium. Do NOT mix with trichlor tablets or add via same feeder (explosive gas).
- **Sodium dichlor (dichlor):** pH-neutral shock (stabilized). Adds CYA. Good for regular shocking.
- **Lithium hypochlorite:** Fast-dissolving, no calcium/CYA addition. Most expensive. Good for vinyl/ fiberglass pools.
- **Non-chlorine shock (potassium monopersulfate):** Oxidizes contaminants without raising chlorine. Use before heavy bather load, after rain, or weekly — allows swimming in ~15 min.
- **Shock schedule:** Every 1–2 weeks (more in heavy use/heat). Target breakpoint chlorination: FC to 10–30 ppm temporarily to break chloramines (combined chlorine <0.5 ppm).

## Filter Types

- **Sand filter:** Media = #20 silica sand. Catch particles down to ~20–40 microns. Backwash when pressure rises 8–10 psi above clean pressure. Sand replacement every 5–7 yr. Pros: low maintenance, cheap. Cons: least fine filtration, wastes water on backwash.
- **Cartridge filter:** Pleated polyester cartridge. Catches 10–15 microns. No backwash — remove and hose off every 1–3 months. Replace cartridge yearly. Pros: fine filtration, water-efficient, no multiport valve. Cons: cartridges cost $30–$100 each.
- **DE (Diatomaceous Earth) filter:** Grids coated with DE powder. Catches 2–5 microns (best filtration). Backwash + recharge with fresh DE. Full teardown/cleaning 1–2×/yr. Pros: clearest water. Cons: most complex, DE powder must be disposed of properly (don't send to sewer in some areas), expensive grids can tear.

## Vacuuming and Circulation

- **Manual vacuum:** Attach to skimmer or dedicated suction port. Use telescopic pole + vacuum head + hose. Slow, steady overlapping passes. Start shallow.
- **Automatic pool cleaner:** Suction-side (uses skimmer suction, cheap, but increases filter load). Pressure-side (uses return line, needs dedicated booster pump for best results). Robotic (self-contained with pump/filter bag — most efficient, no pump strain, ~$500–$1500). Best for in-ground pools.
- **Pump run time:** 8–12 hr/day in summer, 4–6 hr in winter (if not closed). Rule: one full turnover (pool volume ÷ pump flow rate) per 24 hr. Run during off-peak electric rates if possible.
- **Skimmer baskets and pump strainer:** Empty skimmer basket weekly (more in fall). Check pump basket before each run — debris in impeller reduces flow.

## Winterizing

- **Above-ground pools:** Lower water level below skimmer. Disconnect pump/filter, drain lines, blow out return lines. Cover with heavy-duty winter cover (keep leaves out). Add winterizing chemicals (anti-algae, low-temp enzyme).
- **In-ground pools:** Drain filter and pump. Blow out return lines with shop vac/compressor. Add antifreeze (pool-grade, non-toxic) to lines only if risk of trapped water. Install expansion plugs in returns. Lower water 6" below skimmer. Winter cover (mesh vs solid — mesh drains through, solid needs pump to remove rain). Keep some water in pool to handle hydrostatic pressure.
- **Pool pill / floating winter chlorinator:** Optional slow-release desiccant + algaecide. Don't rely solely on it.
- **Opening in spring:** Remove cover slowly (don't dump debris into pool). Refill to normal level. Reinstall pump/filter. Shock heavily + run 24 hr. Test and balance. Vacuum dead algae/debris.

## Common Problems

- **Green/cloudy algae:** Green = free-floating or wall-clinging (chlorine deficiency, filter running too few hours, high pH, low CYA). Treatment: brush walls, shock to 10–20 ppm FC, run pump 24 hr, vacuum dead algae to waste. Mustard algae (yellow) — more resistant, requires high chlorine + algaecide. Black algae (blue-green spots) — deep roots, scrub with steel brush, treat with high chlorine + copper-based algaecide.
- **Cloudy water (no algae):** Poor filtration (check filter/pump runtime). Imbalanced chemistry (high pH, high TA, high calcium). Fine particles — use clarifier (flocculant clumps small particles for filter) or flocculant (sinks to bottom, vacuum to waste). Also check phosphates (food for algae) — >500 ppb may need phosphate remover.
- **Metal stains (brown/rust/blue-green):** Brown/red = iron (from well water or corroded heater). Blue/green = copper (from algaecides or corroded heat exchanger). Remove: lower pH to 7.2, add ascorbic acid (vitamin C) or stain-specific sequestrant. Prevent with metal sequestrant when filling.
- **Calcium scale (white crusty deposits):** High calcium hardness + high pH. Remove with pumice stone or scale remover. Prevent by maintaining CH <400 ppm and pH <7.8.
- **Chlorine lock:** High CYA (>100 ppm) renders chlorine ineffective. Only cure: partial drain/refill to lower CYA to 30–50 ppm. Test CYA monthly.
- **Pump won't prime:** Air leak on suction side (check lid O-ring, drain plug, skimmer weir). Water level too low. Clogged impeller. Blocked skimmer line.
- **Heater issues:** Low flow/restricted flow triggers pressure switch shutoff. Dirty heat exchanger scales up in high calcium water. Gas heater: check gas pressure/ignitor. Heat pump: clean coils, ensure airflow.
