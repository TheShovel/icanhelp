# Driveways and Paving

## Driveway Material Types

- **Asphalt:** Petroleum-based (hot mix or cold patch). Flexible — handles minor ground movement. Dark color absorbs heat (snow melt). Lifespan 15–25 yr with sealcoating every 3–5 yr. Best for cold climates (flexes with freeze/thaw). Cost: ~$3–$7/sqft installed. Requires resurfacing (overlay) after cracking becomes widespread.
- **Concrete (plain or reinforced):** Rigid — more crack-prone than asphalt if subgrade moves. Control joints every 10–12 ft (tooled or saw-cut). Lifespan 25–40 yr. Lighter color = less heat absorption. Stamped/stained options for decorative. Cost: ~$5–$12/sqft. Requires sealing to protect from deicing salts and staining.
- **Pavers (interlocking concrete, brick, stone):** Individual units on sand base. Most durable long-term — flex with ground movement, easy to replace damaged pavers (no demolition). Requires edge restraint and joint sand (polymeric sand prevents weed growth). Lifespan 30+ yr if base prepared properly. Cost: $10–$25/sqft. Weeds/ants in joints. Snow removal difficult (plow catches edges).
- **Gravel / crushed stone:** Cheapest option (~$1–$3/sqft). Requires containment edge (timber, steel, concrete strip). Ruts easily with heavy use. Hard to shovel snow. Class 5 or 3/4" minus with fines (compacts well). Top surface of decorative pea gravel (doesn't compact — displaces under tires). Needs annual replenishment.
- **Concrete vs asphalt key difference:** Concrete needs proper joint placement and curing. Asphalt needs compacted subbase and sealcoating. Concrete lighter colored so cooler. Asphalt flexible so better on unstable subgrade. Pavers most labor-intensive but pretty and maintainable.

## Base Preparation

- **Subgrade (native soil):** Must be stable (no soft spots, organic matter removed). Cut/ fill to correct grade. Compact with plate compactor or roller. If soil is clay / high water table: install geotextile fabric to separate base from subgrade + thicker base layer (8–12").
- **Base course:** Crushed stone (3/4" minus, or Class 5 / ABC (aggregate base course)). Compact in 4" lifts (thin layers) with vibratory roller or plate compactor. Total base depth: 4" for light vehicle, 6–8" for standard driveway, 10"+ for heavy trucks. Base width = paved width + at least 6" each side.
- **Sand bedding (for pavers):** 1"–1.5" of concrete sand over compacted base. Screed flat (screed pipes guide depth). Do NOT compact sand — spread loose, lay pavers, then compact into sand.
- **Stone dust / fines bound base (for gravel driveway):** 6–8" of 3/4" minus with >10% fines. Compact until hard surface. Adding water helps bind fines. Top with 2–3" of clean 3/4" gravel (no dust — won't track).
- **Proof rolling:** Drive loaded truck over prepared base. Soft spots = dig out, replace with new base material. No rutting under loaded truck.

## Drainage and Slope

- **Cross slope (crown):** 2% (1/4" per ft) from center to edges for concrete/asphalt. Paver surface same. Crown prevents standing water = less frost heave damage and longer life.
- **Longitudinal slope (along driveway):** Minimum 1% (1/8" per ft) to drain toward street or low point. Steep slopes (>10%) risk washout — add speed bumps/broken grade or concrete gutter at low point.
- **French drain / trench drain:** If driveway slopes toward house or garage: install trench drain across driveway just before garage apron. Tie into drainage pipe to daylight/storm sewer. Concrete forms for trench — cast iron or plastic grates (load-rated for vehicles).
- **Swales alongside driveway:** Direct water away from pavement base. 2% slope min away from pavement edge. Downspouts must discharge 5 ft+ from driveway.
- **Driveway apron (curb cut):** Public right-of-way — permit required for curb cut and approach slab. Usually thickened concrete (6") to handle street crossing. Municipal inspection required.

## Concrete Finishing Techniques

- **Pour and finish:** After concrete placed, strike off with screed (2×4 or aluminum). Bull float to level and bring cream. Wait until bleed water evaporates. Edge with edger, cut joints with groover (every slab width equal, 1/4 depth of slab).
- **Broom finish:** Drag broom perpendicular to travel direction after floating. Depth of broom texture varies (light for garage, medium for drive). Adds skid resistance. Do when surface is still semi-soft — not too early (clogs broom) or late (broom won't texture).
- **Stamped concrete:** Fresh concrete imprinted with stamp mats (stone, slate, brick, wood patterns). Requires colored hardener (dry shake) + release agent. Stamp in sections. Control joints cut after stamping (or use deco joints in pattern). Sealed with clear sealer (re-coat every 2–3 yr). Cost: ~$10–$18/sqft.
- **Exposed aggregate:** Surface retarder sprayed after finishing then washed off next day exposing stone. Slippery — use only for decorative, not primary driveway unless fine aggregate. Sealed to prevent stone pop-out.
- **Curing:** Concrete must stay moist 7 days minimum for full strength. Options: wet curing (soaking/spraying), curing compound (membrane-forming sealer sprayed on), plastic sheeting. Cold weather: insulated blankets. Do NOT let concrete freeze within first 48 hr.
- **Control joint timing:** Fresh concrete: tooled joints after bleed water gone. Hardened: wet saw within 4–12 hr of finishing (depending on temperature) — prevents random cracks. Saw too early (ravels edges) vs too late (cracks already formed).
- **Expansion joints:** Pre-formed asphalt-impregnated fiber strips. Installed where driveway meets garage slab, sidewalks, or at midpoints of long runs (>30'). Compressible — allows thermal movement. Also at house foundation (never attach driveway to foundation — use expansion joint).

## Sealing and Maintenance

- **Asphalt sealer (coal tar or asphalt emulsion):** Apply every 3–5 yr. Coal tar more durable but environmental concerns (some states ban). Emulsion is water-based, less durable. Apply with squeegee on clean, crack-free surface. One coat (for moderate wear) or two coats (first for fill, second for seal). Dry 24 hr.
- **Concrete sealer:** Penetrating silane/siloxane (clear, keeps breathe, preserves look) — prevents salt/water damage. Acrylic film-former (adds gloss, wear-prone) — use for decorative stamped concrete. Apply every 2–5 yr depending on exposure.
- **Paver sealing:** Polymeric sand between joints — activates with water (hardens). Seal pavers with penetrating sealer to protect color and prevent weed growth. Re-sand joints as needed (sand washes out). Seal every 3–5 yr.
- **Gravel driveway maintenance:** Regrade yearly (drag box blade behind tractor or hand rake). Add fresh gravel every 2–3 yr. Fill potholes as they appear. If base becomes too deep in soft spots, dig out to firm subgrade, add geotextile, compact new base.
- **Snow removal:** Asphalt: snow shovel or plow (metal blades will gouge if not careful — use rubber cutting edge). Concrete: avoid deicing salts (damage concrete -> spalling). Use sand for traction. Plow carefully — don't drop plow edge on joints.

## Crack Repair

- **Asphalt crack repair:** Hairline cracks: fill with cold pour crack filler (rubberized) from jug. Use backer rod for cracks >1/2" wide. For alligator cracking (multiple small cracks): patch area wide (remove loose asphalt, fill with cold patch, compact). Larger cracked areas -> sawcut and remove, patch with hot mix or cold patch.
- **Concrete crack repair:** Hairline cracks: epoxy injection (structural) or polyurethane foam (flexible, water stop). For non-structural: rout crack V-shape (angle grinder), fill with concrete patching compound. For wider cracks/ spalling: remove affected area, apply bonding adhesive, patch with vinyl-reinforced concrete. For cracks >1/4": chisel and fill with concrete repair caulk (sanded or unsanded).
- **Paver crack / uneven paver:** Remove damaged paver (use pry bar or paver puller). Replace with new paver (keep spares). For settled pavers: lift, add/reduce sand base, re-level. Tamped with rubber mallet. Top with polymeric sand.
- **Gravel pothole:** Remove debris. Fill with 3/4" minus, compact in layers. Top with same gravel. If pothole keeps returning: excavate deeper, install geotextile, add large crushed rock base (2–3"), then final gravel.

## Resurfacing vs Replacement

- **Resurfacing (asphalt overlay):** Requires existing pavement structurally sound, with only surface cracks/alligatoring. Mill or clean surface, apply 1.5–2" new asphalt overlay. Life: 8–12 yr. Cost: ~50% of full replacement.
- **Resurfacing (concrete overlay):** Thin cement-based overlay (polymer-modified) troweled or stamped over existing concrete. Requires sound base with no major settling. Life: 5–10 yr. Cheaper than replacement but surface can delaminate if base unstable.
- **Replace entirely:** Needed when base compromised (deep cracks, settled areas, extensive alligatoring — base failure). Full depth removal: demo old driveway, remove/ replace base, pour new. Cost: full replacement is 2–3x resurface, but lasts full lifespan.
- **Indicators for replacement:** Cracks >1/4" throughout. Pavement sections separated vertically (settlement). Repeated potholes same location (subgrade failure). Extensive cracking beyond repair (>20% of surface).
