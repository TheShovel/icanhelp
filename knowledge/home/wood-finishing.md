# Wood Finishing & Refinishing

## Wood Types & Grain
- **Open grain** (oak, ash, walnut, mahogany): visible pores. Need grain filler for smooth finish (optional for rustic). Apply paste wood filler before staining if you want smooth, glass-like finish. Stain color: darker stain accentuates grain pattern
- **Closed grain** (maple, birch, cherry, pine): fine, tight pores. Less grain filler needed. Maple can be difficult to stain evenly (prone to blotching — use pre-stain conditioner)
- **Figured wood**: curly (tiger), bird's eye, quilted, flame — grain patterns that reflect light. French polish or clear lacquer to enhance figure. Avoid dark stains that hide figure

## Sanding
- **Grits**: coarse 40-60 (shape, material removal), medium 80-120 (smooth), fine 150-220 (final sanding), very fine 320-600 (between coats). Don't skip grits (jump more than 50% = remaining scratches). Always sand in direction of grain, never across — cross-grain scratches visible through finish
- **Sanding between coats**: 320-400 grit (light scuff, not through to wood). Use sanding block (flat surface, not fingers — fingers cause uneven sanding). Tack cloth (sticky cheesecloth) to remove dust before finishing. Mineral spirits on rag (reveals sanding scratches = way to spot-check before finish)
- **Dust-free sanding**: vacuum attachment on sander. Tack cloth after sanding. Wipe with mineral spirits (raised grain will show bumps if not sealed). Denatured alcohol for cleaning before finishing

## Stains
- **Oil-based stain**: penetrates deep, longer working time, more even, strong odor, slower drying (6-24 hrs). Clean up with mineral spirits. Most forgiving — rag on, wipe excess. Longer open time lets you blend + avoid lap marks
- **Water-based stain**: low odor, fast drying, water cleanup. Raises grain (must sand after). Best for: quick projects, latex/water-based poly topcoat compatibility. Fast drying can lead to lap marks (overlap streaks)
- **Gel stain**: thick, sits on surface, doesn't penetrate much. Good for: vertical surfaces, non-wood (MDF, metal, laminate, previous finishes without stripping). More even on blotchy woods (pine, birch). Can't wipe off completely — workable time short
- **Pre-stain conditioner**: essential for blotchy woods (pine, cherry, birch, maple, poplar, alder). Apply before stain, let sit 15 min, wipe excess, stain within 2 hrs. Seals pores for even stain absorption. "Blotching" = stain absorbs unevenly creating dark patches in dense grain

## Clear Topcoats

### Polyurethane
- **Oil-based poly**: amber tint, durable, self-leveling, slow drying (6-8 hrs recoat), strong odor. Most durable for floors, furniture, high-traffic. Cures harder, builds thicker coats. Dries to glossy; between coats: sand with 320 grit. Longer open coat = matches oil-based stain better — can use same thinner
- **Water-based poly**: clear (no yellowing), dries fast (2-4 hrs recoat), low odor, less durable (but improved modern formulas). Good for: light woods (maple, birch), white/ painted furniture, kitchen cabinets. Better hardness than oil? Most modern water-based have polyurethane resins, comparable for many uses. Polyurethane: oil-based vs water-based — not quite as hard as oil-based for floors but similar for furniture. Pros: no odor, fast recoat, brush+water cleanup
- **Application**: thin coats (3-5) > thick coats (1-2). Brush: high-quality synthetic brush (water-based) or natural bristle (oil). Foam brush (OK for small flat surfaces). Wipe-on poly (thin with mineral spirits, apply with rag, easier, slower, more coats needed 5-6). Spray for best finish
- **Troubleshooting**: bubbles (stir gently, don't shake; thin coats; use a foam brush or add retarder). Dust nibs (better environment: tent, wet floor, or spray booth). Orange peel (thinned too much or too thick). Runs (too heavy coat). Fish eye (contamination from silicone/wax — clean surface with mineral spirits first). Hazing (water-based on oil stain: let oil stain dry 72 hrs+)

### Varnish
- **Spar varnish**: for outdoor (UV resistant, flexible — expands/contracts with wood in weather). Oil-based, has UV blockers, durable. Dries hard but stays somewhat flexible. Needs multiple coats, recoat every 1-2 years on exterior. NOT food-safe (toxic) unless specified
- **Shellac**: natural (beetle secretion), food-safe, fast drying, warm amber. Not waterproof — alcohol dissolves it (spilled drink). Good for: undercoats, sealing knots (less bleed-through), French polish (beautiful hand-rubbed finish). Cut with denatured alcohol. Shelf life: 1 year (liquid shellac has limited shelf life, flakes last indefinitely)
- **Lacquer**: fast-drying, durable, spray application. Professional finish for furniture (conversion varnish = catalyzed lacquer, thick, hot, solvent-based, hard). Clear and durable. Needs spraying (HVLP) — brush strokes show. Flammable (spray booth). Best for: high-quality cabinets, fine furniture. Nitrocellulose lacquer: classic guitar finish, yellows over years, chips (but can be touched up). Pre-catalyzed lacquer: good shelf life, durable, spray-on

### Wax
- **Paste wax** (Minwax, Briwax, Renaissance Wax): soft sheen, protects against moisture, not very durable. Works best over another finish (put wax over poly for softer she + hand feel). Apply with rag, let dry 15 min, buff. Reapply 1-2x/year. Not great for dining tables (heat + moisture damage). Renaissance Wax = museum conservation (reversible, protects metal + wood)
- **Wood wax** vs **butcher block conditioner** (mineral oil + beeswax): food-safe for cutting boards

## Refinishing Furniture
1. **Strip**: chemical stripper (Citristrip = less toxic, slower; Klean-Strip = stronger, faster. Methyl chloride = most effective but hazardous requires PPE + ventilation). Apply with brush, let sit 15-30 min, scrape with plastic putty knife, wipe with mineral spirits. Heat gun (for paint removal — careful not to scorch wood, work in hot gun at low speed method). Sanding: start 80 grit, progress to 220. Remove ALL old finish before sanding (residue clogs sandpaper)
2. **Repair**: fill cracks (wood filler, epoxy for large gaps). Glue loose joints (wood glue + clamp). Replace broken parts (cut new piece, shape to match). Wood filler vs epoxy: wood filler = for small holes, dries fast, won't stain well; epoxy = gap-filling, structural, sandable, paintable, stain with gel stain
3. **Sand**: 80 → 120 → 180 → 220. Dust-free finish sanding. Wet sand (with mineral spirits or water) = finer final finish + reveals grain direction
4. **Stain** (optional): apply with rag, wipe excess, let dry 24 hrs. Test color on hidden area first. Oil-based stain: min 24 hr dry before topcoat. Water-based stain: ready in 2-4 hrs
5. **Topcoat**: 3-5 coats polyurethane (oil or water-based). Sand 320 grit between coats. Let cure 7 days before heavy use. For painted finish: prime (BIN shellac for stain blocking), 2-3 coats latex enamel/water-based poly, sand between coats, high durability finish
6. **Final polish**: steel wool 0000 + wax for smooth satin sheen. Rubbing compound for gloss furniture polish. Hand-rubbed look: pumice + oil (for high gloss) — layer oil + reduce pumice grit progressively
