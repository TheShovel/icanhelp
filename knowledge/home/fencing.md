# Fencing

## Wood Fencing

- **Cedar:** Naturally rot/insect-resistant. Takes stain/paint well. Grades: Clear (knot-free, expensive), Knotty (rustic, cheaper). Lifespan 15–25 yr with maintenance (stain/seal every 3–5 yr). UV-gray if untreated. Western Red Cedar most common.
- **Pressure-treated (PT) pine/ fir:** Chemical preservative (ACQ or MCA) resists rot/termites. Standard for posts (even under cedar fences). Green tint when new, weathers gray. Warps/cracks more than cedar. Cardboard wrapped to prevent sap/staining. Lasts 18–22 yr.
- **Composite (Trex, Fiberon, etc):** Recycled wood fiber + plastic (HDPE or PVC). No rot, no painting/staining (color through). Warp-resistant. More expensive up-front (~30–50% more than wood). Can sag if not properly gated/supported. Heat expansion — leave gaps.
- **Board-on-board (B.O.B.):** Vertical pickets on both sides of stringers — privacy. Shadowbox (alternating sides) allows some airflow. Solid privacy (tight pickets).
- **Post & rail (ranch fence):** 3–4 horizontal rails. No privacy — property boundary, livestock. Typically PT pine or cedar.
- **Pickets:** Flat top, dog-eared, scalloped, pointed (gothic), spear-top. Standard spacing: 2"–5" apart for semi-privacy.
- **Stringers (horizontal rails):** 2×4 pressure-treated. Notch posts for inset or mount on face. 3-rail for 6' fence (top, middle, bottom). Galvanized bracket systems simplify install.

## Chain Link Fence

- **Components:** Fabric (9ga–11ga — lower gauge = thinner, 11ga standard residential). Posts: terminal (corner, gate, end) and line posts (10' OC max). Tension bands, stretch bar, tie wires. Top rail runs through post loop caps.
- **Height:** 3–4 ft (decorative/pet), 5–6 ft (security/privacy). Slats (vinyl or aluminum) can be woven in for privacy (adds wind load).
- **Fabric installation:** Stretch fabric tight — use come-along (ratchet puller) or fence stretcher bar. Attach tension bands to terminal posts. Tie wire every 2" to top rail, every 12" to line posts.
- **Pros:** Cheapest fence material (~$15–$25/linear ft installed). Low maintenance. Durable (galvanized lasts 15–20 yr, vinyl coated lasts 20+). Transparent (won't block views/wind).
- **Cons:** No privacy (unless slatted). Not decorative. Climbing hazard. Can rust at cuts (touch up with galvanizing spray).

## Vinyl Fence

- **PVC construction:** UV-stabilized, impact-modified. Colors white, tan, gray, beige — has some color variation. Hollow or foam-filled (more rigid). Lighter than wood.
- **Styles:** Privacy (flat or scalloped pickets), semi-privacy (spaced pickets), ranch (rails, post caps), ornamental (simulated wrought iron).
- **Installation:** Posts set in concrete (24"–36" deep). Panels slide into post channels. Leave expansion gaps (vinyl expands in heat). Some brands interlock with hidden brackets.
- **Pros:** Zero maintenance (no paint/stain). Won't rot, warp, or insect-damage. Easy to clean (hose). Lifetime warranty common. Good wind resistance (if post spacing followed).
- **Cons:** Can yellow/embrittle in extreme UV over 15+ yr. Cracks in severe cold (-20°F). Repairs: full panel replacement (can't patch). Expensive (~$25–$45/ft installed). Not as strong as wood — can flex under snow/debris load.
- **Gate:** Needs metal reinforcement inside hollow post. Sagging is common with vinyl gates — use torsion spring / concrete post anchor.

## Wrought Iron / Aluminum Fencing

- **Wrought iron (historic):** Heavy, rust-prone (needs painting every 3–5 yr). Real wrought iron no longer common — mostly steel. Expensive installation. Ornamental scrolls add cost.
- **Aluminum (modern alternative):** Lighter, rust-proof (powder-coated). Looks similar to wrought iron. Spear-top or flat-top pickets. More economical (~$20–$40/ft installed). Used for pool safety, front yard boundaries, commercial. Welded or slip-fit sections.
- **Installation:** Posts set in concrete (24" min depth). Panels bolt to posts. Some systems have adjustable brackets for slopes (racking). Aluminum can dent from impact.
- **Gates:** Typically double-drive (10–16 ft wide). Weld hinges, drop rod on latch side. Self-closing hinges for pool safety (required by code). Locking latch.
- **Pros:** Opens property views. Highest security for anti-climb. Low maintenance (aluminum). Lasts 30+ years.
- **Cons:** No privacy. Climbing risk unless finials added. Painting required for steel/iron. Child-safe only with narrow picket spacing (≤4").

## Post Setting

- **Concrete setting:** Dig hole (dig width = 3× post width). Depth: 1/3 post length above ground + 6" gravel base. 8' fence = ~3' deep hole minimum (frost line). Mix concrete (Quikrete/ SRW). Plumb post in both directions, brace until set (24 hr). Above-ground concrete mounded 2" above grade to shed water. Backfill: concrete flush with ground (for confined space) or 4" gravel cap on concrete.
- **Gravel setting (alternative):** No concrete — tamped crushed stone/gravel around post. Allows water drainage, prevents rot longer. Recommended for wood posts in wet areas. Grab strength is less — use for gates? Gate posts need concrete — gravel can shift.
- **Post anchors / brackets:** Galvanized steel bracket set in concrete before fence install. Holds wood post above ground (prevents rot). Used for decks and elevated fences. Post sits in bracket — easier replacement.
- **Concrete vs gravel:** Concrete: stronger hold, heavier (more stable), but traps water against wood post (rot). Gravel: drains well, easier to replace, but less lateral strength. Compromise: concrete collar with gravel backfill base.

## Gate Installation

- **Gate width:** 3–4 ft walk gate. 10–16 ft double driveway gate. Wood gates sag less with diagonal brace (compression: bottom hinge to top latch).
- **Hinges:** Strap hinges for wood (heavy). Butt hinges for light duty. Self-closing hinges for pool safety (code). Bolted through post (lag screws into post).
- **Latch + catch:** Gravity latch, spring-loaded (auto close), cane bolt (fixes gate open). Padlock hasp for security. Some handles have built-in lock.
- **Driveway gates:** Slide or swing? Swing requires clearance, slide needs header beam. Slide rails on ground — clear snow. Automatic openers require electrical run. MH 3/4 hp for residential sliding gates.
- **Gate sag prevention:** Diagonal brace (compression — hinge to latch), tension cable, spring-loaded wheels on large gates. Use 4×4 or stronger for gate posts.

## Repair Tips

- **Leaning post:** Dig around post (or dig new hole adjacent). Cut at ground level and install new post alongside, connected with Simpson strong-tie or through-bolts. For concrete-set posts: demo concrete with sledge/chipping hammer — easier to dig new adjacent post.
- **Broken picket:** Wood — pry off and replace, nail or screw into stringer. Pre-stain/paint replacement to match. Vinyl — remove by sliding panel out if possible, or cut and replace full panel section.
- **Gate dragging:** Check hinge screws tight. Dig out sag at latch side? Adjust hinges (loosen, shift, retighten). Add wheel on dragging corner. Replace wooden gate with metal frame gate if persistent.
- **Rotted post bottom:** Saw off rot above damaged area. Splice new post section with galvanized metal post saver bracket (Simpson PBS). Concrete in new section.
- **Rust on iron/aluminum:** Wire brush, apply rust converter, prime, repaint. For aluminum: touch up powder coat with matching paint (sanding first).

## HOA / Permits / Regulations

- **HOA restrictions:** Submit application with fence plan, materials, color. Typically max height 4 ft front yard, 6 ft rear/side. No chain link in front yards (many HOAs). Approval process 30–60 days. Fines for unapproved fences.
- **Permits required:** Most municipalities for >6 ft or masonry. Setback from property line required (0"–36" depending on code). Corner lot visibility triangle (no obstruction above 3 ft at intersection). Egress windows — fence must have gate for fire access.
- **Property line:** Survey before digging. Never build on neighbor's side without written agreement (boundary line fence laws vary by state — good neighbor policy / adverse possession risk). Confirm property pins placement.

## Wind Load Considerations

- **Solid fences (privacy) act as sails:** Wind loading on 6' solid fence = ~25 lbs/sqft at 100 mph (code). Use 6×6 posts at 6'–8' OC in high wind zones. Smaller panels break wind better: semi-solid or spaced pickets reduce load.
- **Post depth minimum:** 30"–36" in moderate wind, 42"–48" in high sustained wind zones (coastal, plains). Larger diameter posts (5×5 or 6×6) reduce flex.
- **Gravel vs concrete for wind:** Concrete provides more weight holding lateral loads. Gravel allows flex but may loosen over time.
- **Post spacing:** Reduce from 8' to 6' OC for high wind areas. Install 2×6 top rail (or 2×4 flat) to distribute wind load.
- **Vinyl wind rating:** Check manufacturer max wind speed. Some vinyl fences rated only to 90 mph — not appropriate for hurricane zones.
