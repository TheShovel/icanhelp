# Plumbing Code & Permits

## Why Permits Matter
- **Code compliance**: plumbing code (IPC or UPC depending on state) ensures safe, functional, non-contaminated water systems. Backflow prevention (prevent sewage/ cross-connection from entering drinking water). Proper venting (prevent sewer gas in home). Drain slope (prevent clogs). Building code also ensures fire safety (penetrations fire-stopped) + structural safety (notches too large in joists)
  - International Plumbing Code (IPC) used by most US states (US has two main plumbing codes: IPC and UPC/Uniform Plumbing Code — both based on model codes). Uniform Plumbing Code (UPC): used primarily in Western US (California, Arizona, Oregon, Washington, Nevada, Texas, Colorado, Utah, Idaho, Alaska, Hawaii, Montana, Wyoming? Actually mostly West Coast + mountain states)
- **Permits needed for**: water heater replacement (gas: $100-300, electric: $50-150). New bathroom addition. Moving plumbing fixtures. Adding new circuits? No, that's electrical. For plumbing: sewer line replacement. Repiping house. Gas line. Permit exempt: replacing faucet, fixing leak, clearing clog, replacing toilet, sink drain repair (same location). Always check local requirements (towns vary)
- **Consequences of no permit**: insurance may deny claim (fire from unpermitted plumbing, water damage from faulty work). Home sale issues (buyer's inspection finds unpermitted work). Fines (stop work order, city can fine per day, $500-1000+). Safety: improperly vented gas = CO poisoning. Cross-connections: backflow into potable water line = sickness

## Drain piping
- **Drain slope**: 1/4" per foot (minimum 1/8" for 3"+ pipe). Too much slope: water runs too fast, leaves solids behind (scouring too aggressive?). Too little: water sits, clogs form. P-trap required for every fixture (water seal: 2-4" deep minimum — prevents sewer gas). Trap arm: from trap outlet to vent. Maximum length before vent: 30" for 1.25" pipe, 60" for 2" pipe. S-traps prohibited (siphon traps self-siphon — water seal pulled under certain conditions, sewer gas enters)
- **Venting**: each fixture trap must have vent (air to prevent siphoning). Vent pipe extends up through roof (or connect to main vent). Revent (individual vent) connects trap to existing vent. Wet vent (drain also serves as vent for upstream fixture). Absent vent = gurgling sounds, slow drain, sewer smell. Island sink venting: loop vent under counter (air admittance valve/ AAV on island = Studor vent — one-way valve that opens to let air in but doesn't let sewer gas out). AAV: code varies by state (not allowed everywhere for long runs)
- **Cleanouts**: required at each change of direction >45°, at building base/foot of stack, every 100 ft for straight run. Cleanout plug: accessible, same size as pipe. Cleanouts at main drain + every 50-75 ft for sewer. Solvent weld vs mechanical cleanout plugs

## Water piping
- **Pipe sizing**: fixture unit method (calculate total FU on each run, size accordingly). Main line: 3/4" minimum (1" for large homes). Branch to fixtures: 1/2" to single fixture, 3/4" to manifold. Maximum water velocity: 8 ft/sec (less to prevent water hammer + noise)
- **Shutoff valves**: at each fixture (under sink, behind toilet, at water heater, main shutoff at house entry). Ball valves preferred over gate valves (gate valve stem corrodes shut). Grade: full port ball valve = full bore, no restriction. Main shutoff: 1/4-turn ball valve (preferred — quicker, more reliable than gate valve)
- **Backflow prevention**: hose bibs (outdoor spigots) need vacuum breaker/ atmospheric vacuum breaker (prevents water from garden hose siphoning back into house). Irrigation system needs reduced pressure zone (RPZ) or pressure vacuum breaker (PVB) — depends on hazard level. Double check valve assembly (DC): for low hazard
- **Water hammer arrestor**: required near quick-closing valves (washing machine, dishwasher, ice maker). Washing machine outlet box often has built-in arrestors? After-market arrestor threads onto washing machine supply box. Prevents pipe banging when valve closes quickly

## Gas piping
- **Materials**: black iron pipe (standard for natural gas/propane). CSST (corrugated stainless steel tubing — flexible, easier install, $50-100 per 100 ft, specific grounding/bonding requirements to prevent lightning arc damage — require special grounding, yellow jacket. If not bonded correctly: gas leak + fire risk during lightning strike)
- **Sizing**: BTU calculations (based on total load, pipe length, pressure drop). Typical: 1/2" good for water heater (40k BTU), furnace (60-100k BTU). 3/4" for range (50k BTU) + dryer (22k BTU) or longer runs. 1" for full house. Pressure: natural gas typically 0.25 PSI (7" water column), propane 11" water column across LP appliances. Low pressure system 7-14" WC. Higher pressure (2 PSI) with regulator at each appliance possible
- **Gas valve**: accessible, clearly marked "on/off" (gas rating stamped on body). Sediment trap (drip leg) required at each appliance (vertical pipe stub to catch debris + moisture before appliance). Flexible gas connector (stainless steel corrugated, 3-6 ft maximum, yellow for gas) at appliance connection
- **Testing**: pressure test with air (minimum 15 PSI for 30 min for low-pressure system, or 10-15 PSI, depending on code). All joints checked with soap bubbles/snoop liquid. Gas permit: almost always required for any new gas work (including water heater)

## Sewer & Septic
- **Connection to city sewer**: 4" minimum pipe, slope 1/4" per foot. Cleanout at property line + near house. Sewer line inspection: camera by plumber before purchase (tree roots, offset joints, bellies, cracks — $500-800). Sewer line replacement: $3,000-30,000 depending on length, depth, access. Trenchless (pipe bursting, pipe relining/Cured-in-place pipe/CIPP) avoids digging up yard: $80-150/ft vs $50-100/ft for open trench
  - Sewer scope: video inspection, recommended before buying any old house. Cracks, belly = water pools, solids build up. Roots = need cleaning, possible repair in future
- **Septic system**: tank (1,000-1,500 gallon, concrete/plastic/fiberglass) + drain field (leach field — perforated pipes in gravel). Tank: pump out every 3-5 years ($300-600). Water treatment? Don't: flush wipes (even "flushable"), grease, feminine products, chemicals (antibacterial soaps, bleach, drain cleaner). Do: maintain bacteria (no massive bleach/chemicals). Septic system lifespan: 20-40 years with proper maintenance. Drain field replacement: $5,000-15,000 (if it fails — saturated soil, surfacing sewage, backups into house). Failing septic = expensive!
  - Mound system: for high water table / poor soil — elevated drain field with sand fill + pump. Expensive $20k-40k. Aerobic treatment unit (ATU): adds oxygen to decompose waste (for small lots in poor soil). Most expensive
