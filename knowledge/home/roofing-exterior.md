# Roofing & Exterior

## Roofing Materials

### Asphalt Shingles
- **Most common** (80% of US homes): cheap ($100-150/sq), easy to install, 20-30 year lifespan. Types: 3-tab (flat, entry-level, 20 yr, cheapest, less wind resistance). Architectural/dimensional (thicker, dimensional look, 30 yr, better wind resistance, most popular). Premium/luxury (look like slate/wood, 40+ yr, thicker, heavier, expensive)
  - "Square" = 100 sq ft. Roof area = square footage × pitch factor (8:12 roof = 1.2 factor). 2,000 sq ft house with 8:12 roof = 2,400 sq ft roof = 24 squares. Shingles per square: 3 bundles per square (architectural), 2-3 bundles per square? Standard 3-tab: 3 bundles = 1 square. Architectural: 3-4 bundles per square depending on brand
  - Underlayment: felt paper (15lb or 30lb) or synthetic (lighter, stronger, better UV resistance). Ice + water shield: required on eaves (6 ft up) in cold climates (prevents ice dam leaks). Starter strip (starter row at eaves + rakes instead of cutting regular shingles). Ridge cap: pre-tabbed ridge shingles for peaks
  - Ventilation: intake (soffit vents) + exhaust (ridge vent). Balanced: 1 sq ft per 300 sq ft attic (50% intake + 50% exhaust). Unbalanced = moisture buildup, ice dams, shingle overheating, warping sheathing. Include in every roof replacement
- **Metal roofing**: steel (40-70 yr, $350-900/sq). Aluminum (coastal, 50+ yr, $500-900/sq). Copper (100+ yr, $1,500+/sq, patinas green, premium). Standing seam (hidden fasteners, best, expansion clips needed = expensive). Corrugated (exposed fasteners, cheaper, leak risk at each fastener)
- **Slate**: 100+ year lifespan, expensive ($1,000-2,500/sq), heavy (requires reinforced roof structure), requires specialist. Natural stone, splits into thin sheets. Best for historic homes
- **Tile**: clay (Spanish mission style, 50-100 yr, $600-1,200/sq). Concrete tile (durable, heavy 900 lb/sq, cheaper than clay). Requires sturdy roof structure. Common in SW US, Florida, Mediterranean climates

### Common Problems
- **Leaks**: often at flashing (valleys, chimneys, vents, skylights, wall intersections), not shingles themselves. Drip edge water overlaps. Ice dams (warm attic melts snow, refreezes at cold eaves → water dams and backs up under shingles). Fix: improved attic insulation + ventilation, ice + water shield at eaves, heat cables
- **Missing/cracked shingles**: wind damage (replace individual shingles, don't need whole roof). Curling (age, poor ventilation, poor quality). Granule loss → can predict age/replacement soon
- **Algae/moss**: dark streaks from blue-green algae (Gloeocapsa magma). Fix: zinc/copper strip at ridge (rain releases ions, kills algae). Bleach wash (dilute, not high pressure — pressure wash damages shingle granules)
- **Lichens on north slopes** — normal; organic growth removal by hand

## Gutters
- **Types**: K-style (most common — flat bottom, decorative front). Half-round (round tubes, traditional, more expensive). Seamless (formed on site from coil, fewer leaks, better than sectional). Sectional (pre-cut sections, cheaper, joints leak over time). Seamless gutters: prefer (install one long piece, fewer joints — costs slightly more, dramatically fewer leaks)
- **Material**: aluminum (most common, light, rust-proof, affordable, 20-30 yr). Copper (expensive but lasts 50+ yrs, beautiful patina over time). Galvanized steel (strong, rust eventually, heavy). Vinyl (cheapest, brittle in cold/freeze-thaw, 5-10 yr, UV degrades)
  - Gutter guards: mesh/screen (keep leaves out, $5-15/lin ft, reduce cleaning frequency). Foam inserts (cheap, trap debris, not recommended). Micro-mesh (best — fine mesh bonds to water flow, blocks even small debris, $20-40/ln ft). Clean gutters: only if trees overhang roof; factor into roof cost: $100-300 per cleaning
- **Downspouts**: 2x3" (standard, 5,500 sq ft roof capacity per downspout?? At 1"/hr rain,1,200 sq ft roof = 750 gal = one downspout needed per 350-500 sq ft. One downspout needed per ~400 sq ft roof). Extend 4-6 ft from foundation (splash block or underground drain). Drain away from house: most foundation damage from water at base. Extension required in most codes

## Siding
- **Vinyl**: cheapest ($3-8/sq ft installed), low maintenance, but can crack in cold, fade over time, looks cheap. Hollow-backed vs insulated (with foam backing R-2-4?). Not environmentally friendly (PVC). Some neighborhoods ban for lack of authenticity
- **Fiber cement** (HardiePlank): durable (50+ yr), fire-resistant, resistant to rot + termites, better appearance, $8-15/sq ft, heavy, harder to install = more labor. Requires painting every 10-15 yr. Most popular premium siding
- **Wood**: cedar shingles/shakes (natural R-value, beautiful, high maintenance, $$$, needs painting/staining every 3-5 years). Beveled/ lap siding (clapboard). T1-11 (plywood sheets with grooves, cheap, looks cheap). Wood performs well in dry climates, poor in humid
- **Engineered wood** (LP SmartSide): OSB with resin + zinc borate (rot/termite resistant). Painted. Cheaper than fiber cement, easier to install, less durable (still 30 yr)

## Decks
- **Wood**: pressure-treated pine (cheapest, $15-25/sq ft, needs staining/sealing every 2-3 yr, can warp/crack/splinter. Best value: PT pine). Cedar/redwood (natural rot resistance, lighter, ages to gray, $5-10/sq ft more). Ipe/Brazilian hardwood (hard, durable, expensive, need special fasteners — pre-drill), 40yr+)
- **Composite** (Trex, TimberTech): recycled wood + plastic, low maintenance (never needs staining), $25-40/sq ft, expensive initially, can get hot in sun (dark colors hotter, but lighter = cooler). Scratches show. Cannot refinish. Mold/ mildew on some brands. Hidden fasteners vs visible screws
- **PVC** (Azek): all plastic, no maintenance, coolest surface, most expensive ($30-50/sq ft), scratches more than composite? Not all brands — some PVC planks softer than composite. Good for high-moisture (pool deck)
- **Railing**: wood (maintenance, requires staining), metal (most durable, low-maintenance, $20-50/lin ft vs wood), composite (like deck boards). Cable railing (modern, unobstructed views, expensive, need tensioning system). Pickets spaced ≤4" to comply with code
- **Foundation**: concrete piers (minimum 48" deep — below frost line). 6x6 posts, joists 16" o.c. (on center). Ledger board attached to house (with proper flashing — high risk area for water penetration). Deck framing: permits almost always required (safety critical). For composite: joist spacing ≤16" o.c. to avoid deflection
