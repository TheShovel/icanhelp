# Home Energy Efficiency

## Insulation
- **R-value**: thermal resistance per inch (higher = better). Recommended attic: R-49 to R-60 (15-20" fiberglass), walls: R-13 to R-21 (depending on climate zone). Floor over unheated space: R-25 to R-30. In northern zones: up to R-60 attic. Inspect attic insulation: look for coverage gaps, level of insulation (compare to joist height)
  - Types: fiberglass batt (cheap, DIY-friendly, needs careful installation — gaps compress R-value by 50%. Don't compress, cut perfectly around obstructions). Blown-in cellulose (best for attics — fills gaps, R-3.7 per inch, fire-resistant with borate treatment. Settles 10-20%. Can DIY rental blower). Spray foam (closed cell = highest R-6-7 per inch, air seal + vapor barrier. Open cell = R-3.5, cheaper less vapor barrier. Professional install expensive. seals leaks, best air barrier). Rigid foam (blue/pink board. XPS: R-5 per inch, good for basement walls below grade. Polyiso: R-6-7 per inch, higher)
  - Attic hatch: weatherstrip + insulated cover (foam box over hatch). Recessed lights (IC-rated fixtures only for insulation contact; non-IC = fire hazard with insulation covering them = need 3" clearance)
- **Air sealing**: caulk (acrylic latex for small gaps, silicone for exterior/humid areas). Spray foam (Great Stuff — small cans for gaps <1", large gaps fill with backer rod + caulk). Weatherstrip (doors, windows, attic hatches). Can lights: IC-rated covers. Rim joist (band joist in basement): spray foam or rigid foam + caulk (major air leak around sill plate)
  - Test: blower door (professional — depressurizes house, find leaks with smoke pencil/infrared). DIY: light incense stick near windows, doors, outlets, pipes — if smoke blows sideways = draft
- **Windows**: single-pane = R-1 (terrible). Double-pane = R-2-4 (standard). Triple-pane = R-5-7 (best, expensive). Low-E coating (reflects infrared = heat stays in winter, out summer). Gas fill (argon/krypton between panes slows convection better than air). Vinyl frame (best value), wood (insulates well, needs maintenance), fiberglass (most stable, most expensive), aluminum (least efficient — conducts heat/cold). Storm windows: cheaper than replacement, comparable improvement

## HVAC
- **Heat pump**: moves heat instead of creating it (like AC in reverse). 2-4x more efficient than resistance electric. Ducted (central) or ductless (mini-split). Cold climate heat pumps (Mitsubishi HyperHeat, Fujitsu XLTH) work down to -13°F or lower, still provide COP = efficient ~1.5-2.0 at -15°F. SEER2 (cooling efficiency, higher = better). HSPF2 (heating efficiency). For heat pumps: HSPF2 ≥ 7.5-8.2 for Energy Star. Current min SEER2: 15 SEER2 south, 14 SEER2 north (DOE 2023). Geothermal / ground-source heat pump: much more efficient (use constant 55°F ground temp) but $12,000-30,000 installed (10-year payback, 25-year lifespan, tax credits 30% under Inflation Reduction Act)
  - Heat pump water heater: 2-3x efficient than electric resistance. $1,000-2,000 unit + install. Hybrid mode: heat pump + resistance elements for high demand (heat pump alone may take longer to recover). Cooling effect: extracts heat from surrounding air = cools basement (good in summer, cold in winter). Best located in unconditioned basement/garage. $100-200/yr of savings for typical family. Tax credits 30% (up to $2,000)
- **Furnace**: gas furnace efficiency = AFUE. 80% (standard, oldest pre-90s 60-75%). 90-95% (high efficiency, condensing furnace — second heat exchanger condenses exhaust gas for extra heat extraction, vented through PVC pipe not metal chimney, drains acidic water). 96-98.5% (top tier condensing). Replace 80% with 95%+ = 15-20% gas savings. Important: seal + insulate ducts (up to 30% heat loss through uninsulated ducts in attic/crawlspace, leaky ducts waste more)
- **Programmable/smart thermostat**: saves 10-23% on HVAC (Nest, Ecobee, Honeywell). 7-day schedule, geofencing (away mode with presence detection), Energy Star Smart Home certification, integration with home automation. Setbacks: 10-15°F lower at night/when away. 7-10°F setback for 8 hours = 5-15% savings. Heat pumps: don't use large setbacks (needs aux/emergency heat to raise temp fast, electricity expensive). For heat pumps: set 2-4°F setback max, use "smart recovery" (learns how long to reheat)
- **Maintenance**: change filters every 1-3 months (MERV 8-11 standard. 5-16 MERV rating. 1" filters, 4-5" media filter = less resistance + longer life. Higher MERV = more resistance = more blower energy. Balance filtration + energy cost). MERV 13+ for allergy season — but can restrict airflow. Annual tune-up (check refrigerant charge, clean coils, test safety controls). Clean outdoor AC unit coils (spray with hose, keep debris away)

## Water Heating
- **Types**: tank (30-80 gal, gas (Nat gas typical 30-50 gal) or electric, 10-15 yr). Tankless/on-demand (endless hot water, 20+ yr, 30-50% energy savings). Heat pump hybrid (2-3x efficient than electric tank). Solar thermal (pre-heats water with roof panels, used with conventional backup)
- **Settings**: 120°F recommended (anti-scald + energy savings). Each 10°F reduction = 3-5% energy savings. Above 125°F = scald risk (children + elderly). Below 115°F = legionella risk. Vacation: "vacation" setting (minimum temp to prevent freezing/ bacteria)
- **Efficiency**: EF (Energy Factor, 0-1, higher = better). UEF (Uniform Energy Factor, 2020 standard): gas tank 0.60-0.70, electric resistance ~0.90, heat pump 3+ UEF. Tankless: 0.96-0.98 EF
- **Insulate pipes**: first 6 feet of hot + cold water pipe from water heater (pre-heat water faster, hotter water at tap). Pre-cut foam pipe insulation ($0.50/ft). For electric water heater: new insulation blanket (R-11 or more) only on older poorly insulated tanks (modern tanks well-insulated, covering blanket over electric element access may void warranty)
- **Low-flow fixtures**: 1.5 GPM showerhead (standard 2.5 GPM — 40% reduction saves 2,900 gallons/year). Faucet aerators (1.0-1.5 GPM). Flow restrictors: simple to install, pay back in weeks. Shower: 2.5 GPM heads standard, can reduce to 1.5 GPM without noticeable difference

## Appliances & Electronics
- **ENERGY STAR**: certified efficient models (20-50% less energy than non-certified). Highest efficiency: "Most Efficient" designation (top 10% within ENERGY STAR). Refrigerator (2000: ~500 kWh/yr, 2024: ~300-400 kWh/yr). Top-freezer most efficient, then bottom, then side-by-side. Through-the-door ice maker adds ~20% energy consumption. Clothes washer (HE front-load uses 30-50% less water, 50% less energy than top-load). 2,200 kWh/yr for a new vs older model refrigerator: difference of $100/yr
- **Phantom loads**: electronics draw power when "off" (standby). Average home: 5-10% of electricity bill from standby (an estimated 100W average 24/7 = 876 kWh/yr = $120/yr at 14¢/kWh). Kill-A-Watt meter: measures device's consumption. Slayers: advanced power strips (cut power to peripherals when main device off — TV with gaming console/streaming/soundbar all on one strip). Remove plug from small chargers not in use (they still draw ~0.1-0.5W)
- **LED bulbs**: 90% less energy than incandescent, last 15-25x longer (15,000-25,000 hours vs 1,000h vs CFL 10,000h). Cost: $2-10/bulb (payback 6-12 months). Replace most-used lights first (kitchen, living room, outdoor). Dimmable: check box (not all LEDs are dimmable). Color: warm white 2700-3000K for living spaces, daylight 4000-5000K for task areas

## Whole-House Approach
- **Home energy audit**: professional ($400-600). Blower door test (measure air leakage, ACH50 = air changes per hour at 50 Pascals. Good: <5 ACH50. Leaky old home: 10-20+. Passive house: <0.6 ACH50). Infrared camera (find insulation gaps, thermal bridging, moisture issues). Duct blaster test (measure duct leakage). Report + prioritized recommendations
  - DIY audit: check attic insulation depth, feel for drafts around windows/doors, inspect duct insulation, check for light gaps around outlets (put foam gaskets behind switch plates)
- **Cost-effectiveness order**: free (turn down thermostat). Low cost (<$100: LED bulbs, weatherstripping, programmable thermostat, outlet gaskets, furnace filter change, low-flow showerheads). Medium ($100-1,000: attic insulation, duct sealing, pipe insulation, smart thermostat). High ($1,000-5,000: heat pump water heater, energy-efficient windows (if existing single-pane, otherwise long payback), solar panels (if good roof))
- **Tax credits (Inflation Reduction Act, 2024)**: 30% for geothermal heat pumps, solar panels, battery storage. Up to $2,000 for heat pump. Up to $1,200/year for heat pump water heater, windows, doors, insulation. Up to $600 for heat pump HVAC (heat pump is separate from A/C, heat pump for HVAC = same equipment as heat pump for water heating? No, two different credits: heat pump water heater up to $2,000, heat pump for HVAC up to $600? The max combined $2,000 for heat pumps total)
