# Foundations and Basements

## Foundation Types

- **Slab-on-Grade:** Concrete slab poured directly on prepared subgrade (with or without thickened edge). Common in warm climates (no frost line concerns). Crawlspace below — not accessible. Pros: low cost, no basement moisture issues, pest-resistant. Cons: plumbing in slab (harder to repair), no under-floor storage, less energy-efficient (no thermal mass buffer). Requires 4" min slab thickness, reinforced with wire mesh or rebar.
- **Crawl Space:** Foundation walls (CMU block or poured concrete) with shallow interior space (18"–48" tall). Access via crawl door. Pros: access to plumbing/electrical, can encapsulate improved energy/ moisture. Cons: pest entry common, prone to moisture/ mold if not encapsulated, cold floors above. Vapor barrier (6–12 mil polyethylene) on dirt floor required by most codes. Ventilated or sealed (encapsulated preferred in damp climates).
- **Basement:** Full-height below-grade space (7'+). Finished or unfinished. Poured concrete walls or CMU (concrete masonry units). Pros: square footage, mechanical room / utility space, storm shelter potential. Cons: water intrusion risk, expensive waterproofing, egress windows required for habitable spaces. Foundation depth determined by frost line (36"–48" typical in cold climates).
- **Pier and Beam (Post and Pier):** Concrete piers poured to frost line, with wood beams carrying floor structure. Common in older homes, hillside builds, flood zones. Pros: flood-resistant, good drainage, accessible crawlspace. Cons: wood rot risk at beam-to-pier connection, requires good termite protection, can feel bouncy.
- **Basement vs crawl vs slab at build time:** Slab cheapest (~$8–$12/sqft), crawl $10–$15/sqft, basement $15–$30/sqft. Basement ROI 70–75% nationally (higher in cold climates where it's expected). For new construction in cold climate: basement is standard.

## Common Foundation Problems

- **Vertical cracks (hairline to 1/4"):** Caused by concrete shrinkage (normal settling first year). Usually cosmetic. If >1/4" wide or accompanied by sticking doors/windows → monitor for movement. Most DIY patchable.
- **Diagonal cracks (wider at top or bottom):** Differential settlement — one corner of foundation sinking. Can indicate soil issue (expansive clay, poor compaction, water erosion). Monitor with crack gauge over 6 months. Movement >1/8" continuous → structural engineer consult.
- **Horizontal cracks (in block or poured walls):** Soil pressure pushing against wall — serious structural issue. Indicates inadequate drainage or backfill compaction. On CMU walls: horizontal crack often means wall is bowing inward. Steel I-beam repair or carbon fiber straps needed urgently.
- **Stair-step cracks (block foundation):** Follow mortar joints diagonally. Settlement or lateral pressure. Fill with mortar or epoxy — but if continuing movement, structural repair needed.
- **Settling / sinking (foundation movement):** Signs: doors/windows stick, gaps around window frames, sloping floors, corner cracks radiating from doors. Caused by soil consolidation, tree roots pulling moisture, poor compaction. Underpinning required (steel piers or concrete push piers driven to stable soil).
- **Bowing / leaning walls:** Lateral soil pressure (expansive clay, hydrostatic pressure from poor drainage, frost heave). Repair options: carbon fiber straps (wall facing, cosmetic/restraint, not for significant movement), wall anchors (steel plates inside wall connected to deadman anchors in yard — installed outside, tightened to straighten), steel I-beam reinforcement (welded to footing and floor joists).
- **Water intrusion (leaks, seepage, damp walls):** Most common basement problem. From: hydrostatic pressure, downspouts discharging near foundation, poor grading, failed window wells, clogged perimeter drain tile. Must address water source before any interior finish.

## Waterproofing

- **Interior drain tile (French drain interior):** Channel cut into slab along perimeter, 4" perforated pipe installed, covered with gravel, concrete patch. Pipes gravity-feed to sump pump pit (if below sewer line). Collects water that enters through wall/floor joint. Best for existing water issues where exterior excavation impractical. Does NOT prevent water contact with walls — only mitigates interior flood.
- **Exterior drain tile (exterior waterproofing):** Excavate around foundation to footing. Install perforated pipe at footing level, wrap in filter fabric, backfill with gravel. Apply waterproof membrane to exterior wall (bitumen, rubberized asphalt, bentonite panel, or dimple board). Membrane must extend from footing to 6" above grade. Properly sloped pipe (1/4" per 10 ft) to daylight or sump. Best protection but expensive ($100–$300 per linear foot). Required for finished basements.
- **Sump pump:** Required when interior/exterior drain tile collects water below sewer line. Types: Pedestal (motor above basin, cheaper, easier service) vs Submersible (motor inside sealed basin, quieter, longer-lasting). Backup: battery-powered backup sump pump (Water Commander, Basement Watchdog) — essential. Secondary AC pump for high volume. Alarm for high water. Maintenance: test monthly, clean intake screen, check discharge line for freeze (discharge 5+ ft from foundation).
- **Sealants (interior, crack repair):** Hydraulic cement for active leaks (sets in 3–5 min under pressure). Epoxy injection for structural crack repair (bonds crack, restores strength). Polyurethane foam injection (flexible, stops water through expansion when wet — for non-structural cracks). Silane/siloxane sealers (penetrating, prevent damp wall). Do NOT rely solely on sealants for foundation walls actively bowing or with hydrostatic pressure.
- **Window well drainage:** Liner required (corrugated plastic or metal). Drainage at base (gravel + drain pipe tied to exterior drain tile) — if missing, water fills well and leaks under window. Window well covers (polycarbonate dome) prevent rain accumulation.
- **Exterior grading (drainage slope):** Grade must slope **away** from foundation minimum 6" in first 10 feet (5% slope). If not possible, install swale or French drain to direct water away. Downspout extenders (minimum 5' from foundation, better 10'). Splash blocks direct water away. Gutters must be clean / free-flowing.
- **Perimeter drainage:** If exterior grade is flat or negative: install French drain at perimeter (surface or buried trench with pipe + gravel). Combined with dry well (if soil percolates) or daylight pipe.

## Crack Repair Methods

- **Epoxy injection (structural repair):** For non-moving cracks (dry, clean). Inject low-viscosity epoxy under pressure — restores original tensile strength. Steps: route crack (V-groove), install injection ports every 6–8", seal between ports with paste, inject epoxy from bottom port upward, hold pressure until gelation. Use for foundation walls, beams. For dry cracks 1/16"–1/4".
- **Polyurethane foam injection (water stop):** For active water leaks (damp or wet cracks). Single-component or two-component foam expands on contact with water (hydrophilic). Stops leaks by filling crack and sealing under pressure. Steps: drill holes along crack (6–8" intervals) at 45° angle, install injection nipples, inject foam until it exits adjacent hole. Flexible — moves with foundation. For wet cracks only (not structural).
- **Hydraulic cement (temporary active leak plug):** Fast-setting cement powder. Mix with water, force into wet crack (must be actively weeping). Expands as sets. Holds back moderate hydrostatic pressure. Do not use as permanent solution — brittle, won't bond structurally.
- **Carbon fiber crack repair (settled crack):** Carbon fiber fabric + epoxy applied across crack. Prevents further widening (strong in tension). For horizontal/twisting cracks in block/poured walls. Much shallower than steel beams (takes less interior space). Must be applied to clean, sound wall.

## Grading for Drainage

- **Grade slope minimum:** 5% (6" vertical drop in first 10 ft). Optimal: 8–10%. Use transit level or string line to measure. Retaining wall may be needed near property line if slope exceeds grade.
- **Swale:** Shallow, grass-lined channel that directs surface water around or through property. 2–3% slope minimum. 2' minimum width at bottom. Can carry storm water to street/storm drain. Must not drain onto neighbor's property (legal requirement).
- **French drain (trench + pipe + gravel):** For areas where surface water puddles. Dig trench 18"–24" deep, 6"–12" wide. Line with filter fabric. 4" perforated pipe (slotted side down or solid for long runs). Cover with 3/4" clean stone. Wrap fabric over top, cover with soil.
- **Downspout drainage:** Extend gutter downspouts at least 5 ft (10 ft ideal) from foundation using solid pipe (not corrugated extenders which clog). Bury pipe in shallow trench (12" deep) and route to daylight or dry well. Use flexible downspout connectors at house connection for minor movement.
- **Dry well:** Trench or pit filled with stone (3–4 ft deep, 3+ ft wide). Collects roof runoff via pipe. Allows water to percolate into ground slowly. Only for soil with good percolation. Overflow path to daylight needed. Not for high water table areas.
- **Rain garden:** Shallow planted depression with native water-tolerant plants. Filters runoff, allows infiltration. Location: 10+ ft from foundation. Size: ~20–30% of roof area draining to it. Overflow to yard/swale.
- **Landscaping drainage solutions:** Downspout pop-up emitter (buried pipe ends at pop-up valve at lower grade — discharges when water pressure opens). Curtain drain (shallow, surface-level trench, no pipe, just gravel + decorative). Retaining wall drainage — include weep holes at base + perforated pipe behind wall.
