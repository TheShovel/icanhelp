# Deck Building

Planning, materials, construction, and maintenance of wooden and composite decks.

## Planning & Design
- **Permits**: Most jurisdictions require permit for decks >30" above grade. Inspection at key stages (footings, framing, final). Check local building codes — requires setbacks (usually 5-10 ft from property line)
- **Size & location**: Connect to house via ledger board (attached to house rim joist). Free-standing (not attached) for complex foundation situations. Steps to grade: 1 step minimum. Multiple levels for sloping yards
- **Sun/shade**: Overhead? Pergola, retractable awning, shade sail. Orientation: south-facing = hottest (afternoon sun). Consider existing trees (shade from deciduous trees in summer, sun in winter)
- **Load requirements**: Live load 40 psf (people, furniture, snow). Dead load 10-15 psf (materials). For heavy snow loads, closer joist spacing or larger beams

## Materials
- **Pressure-treated lumber**: Most common, least expensive (#2 grade or better). Rated for ground contact (for posts/ beams) or above ground. ACA or ACQ treated (safe for pets/plants after dry). Warps, cracks, splinters as it dries. Must stain/seal every 1-2 years
- **Cedar/Redwood**: Naturally rot-resistant, beautiful grain, ages gray (if left unfinished). Softer than PT, dents easily. More expensive ($2-3x PT). Weaker span ratings
- **Composite (Trex, TimberTech)**: Wood fiber + plastic. No staining, no splinters, works like wood but doesn't rot. Gets hot in sun (surface temp 30°F+ higher than wood in direct sun). Darker colors hotter. Scratches show. ~2x cost of PT but less maintenance over life
- **PVC (Azek)**: Pure PVC — entirely waterproof, won't rot/ warp. Hollow or cellular core. Cooler than composite in sun (lighter colors reflect). Can't paint well. More expensive than composite. Needs proper ventilation (expands/contracts more)
- **Hardware**: Galvanized or stainless steel (PT wood corrodes regular steel). Joist hangers (code-required for joist-beam connections). Simpson Strong-Tie is standard brand. Structural screws (LedgerLOK, GRK) for ledger attachment (not lag bolts in modern codes)

## Foundation
- **Frost depth**: Below frost line (typically 24-48" depending on climate). Footings must go below to prevent heaving. Concrete piers, sonotubes (cardboard forms), or precast deck blocks (only for ground-level decks)
- **Footing size**: Minimum 12" diameter (24" for corner posts on large decks). Concrete (3000+ PSI). Rebar (vertical rebar in pier for reinforcement in seismic zones)
- **Post base**: Standoff connector elevates wood post 1" above concrete (prevents rot from standing water). Simpson ABA66Z or similar
- **Posts**: 4x4 (max 8 ft high) or 6x6 (max 14 ft high). Pressure treated #1 or select structural. Notch top for beam support or use post cap connector

## Framing
- **Ledger board**: 2x8 or 2x10 pressure treated, bolted to house rim joist. Remove siding, install flashing (Z-flashing over ledger top, gap with spacers for drainage). Lag bolts or structural screws 16" OC staggered. Never attach to brick veneer (can't support — needs stand-off or free-standing design)
- **Beams**: Doubled 2x8, 2x10, 2x12 (span table dependent). Laminated on both sides of posts. 2x10 beam spans max ~10-12 ft
- **Joists**: 12" or 16" OC (closer = stiffer feel, required for composite because more flexible than wood). Max span: 2x8 ~10 ft, 2x10 ~13 ft, 2x12 ~16 ft (varies by species, grade, spacing)
- **Joist hangers**: Required for all joist-to-beam connections. Nail with hanger nails (not regular — double shear required per hanger specs)
- **Blocking**: Between joists at mid-span prevents twisting / rotation. Required every 8 ft or at span center for deck boards longer than 8 ft
- **Cantilever**: Joists can extend past beam up to 1/4 joist span (2.5 ft for 10 ft span). Check code — some jurisdictions limit to 2 ft

## Decking
- **Spacing**: 1/8-1/4" gap between boards for drainage (PT), or specified gap for composite (usually 1/8"). Composite buckets have hidden fasteners (Trex Hideaway, Cortex) for screw-free surface
- **Fasteners**: Hidden (clip systems) for composite, face screw or nail for wood. Screws better than nails (won't work loose). Stainless or coated for PT (galvanic corrosion with ACQ treatment)
- **Pattern**: Straight (parallel to house) — simplest, fastest. Diagonal (45°) — more elegant but 15-20% more waste, more framing needed. Picture frame (border boards perpendicular around perimeter)
- **Expansion**: Leave gap between deck boards and house (1/2"). Composite expands more than wood — need proper gaps

## Stairs & Railings
- **Stair math**: Total rise / 7" = number of risers (round up). Then total rise / risers = actual riser height (max 7 3/4"). Run (tread depth) minimum 10". Stringer spacing max 36" for 2x12 treated
- **Railings**: Required if deck >30" above grade (some codes 24"). Height: 36" minimum. Baluster spacing: 4" max (child safety — 4" sphere should not pass through). Glass railing: tempered or laminated, 1/4" min, frame or frameless with clamps
- **Top rail**: 2x6 or 2x4 (serves as handrail + structural). Composite railing systems pre-assembled (easier, more expensive)

## Waterproofing & Under-deck
- **Under-deck drainage**: For decks over usable space — catch rainwater between joists, direct to gutter. Systems: Trex RainEscape, Zip-Up. Allows dry storage or living space below
- **Flashing**: Critical at house attachment — step flashing against siding, Z-flashing above ledger. Code requirement: flashing directed water away from house

## Maintenance
- **Annual inspection**: Check ledger bolts (tighten as needed), railings (wiggle test), posts (for rot at ground), deck boards (loose screws), joist hangers (corrosion)
- **Cleaning**: Pressure washer (careful — can damage wood, use wide tip, <1500 PSI, keep moving). Deck cleaner (oxygen bleach like OxiClean — kills mold/mildew without harming plants). Before staining: clean + brighten (oxalic acid — restore natural color)
- **Staining**: For PT — semi-transparent stain (allows grain to show). Solid stain (hides wood, more like paint, peels over time). Oil-based > water-based for longevity. Apply when wood dry (<20% moisture). Every 2-3 years. Composite: no staining needed (just clean with soap + water)
