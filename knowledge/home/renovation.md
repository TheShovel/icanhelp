# Home Renovation & Remodeling

## Planning a Renovation
- **Budget**: always add 15-25% contingency (unexpected issues found during construction: old wiring, asbestos, water damage, rotting subfloor, structural surprises)
  - Typical costs: kitchen $20K-50K (minor) to $50K-150K (major). Bathroom $10K-30K. Basement finishing $20K-60K. Roof replacement $8K-15K. Flooring $2-10/sq ft installed. Painting interior: $1.5-4/sq ft
  - Payback at resale: kitchen (60-80%), bathroom (55-70%), basement (50-75%), deck (50-70%), roof (50-60%), siding (50-75%), window replacement (50-70%), garage door replacement (90%+ if simple)
  - Never pay 100% upfront to contractor. Typical: 10-30% deposit, progress payments, 10% retainage at final completion
- **Permits**: required for structural, electrical, plumbing, major mechanical. Always pull permits. Problems with unpermitted work: insurance denial (fire from unpermitted electrical = claim denied), home sale issues (buyer/bank orders repair), safety hazards
  - Typical permit costs $200-2,000 depending on scope + municipality. What triggers permit: new wiring, new walls, roof replacement, HVAC, moving plumbing, adding a bathroom. Cosmetic (paint, flooring, cabinets) usually doesn't
- **Contractor selection**: get 3+ quotes, check license + insurance (general liability $1M+, workers comp), check references (call recent clients, look at finished work), get written contract with scope, payment schedule, timeline. Don't pay large deposits. Verify lien waiver at each payment (protects you if contractor doesn't pay subs). Check online for complaints at state licensing board

## Demolition & Prep
- **Safety**: wear PPE (mask/N95 for dust, goggles, gloves, hard hat for overhead demo, earplugs for loud tools). Test for lead paint (home built before 1978 — lead paint common, must follow EPA Renovation, Repair and Painting Rule/RRP: certified renovator, containment, HEPA vacuum cleanup. Lead dust harmful especially to children). Test for asbestos (9x9 floor tile, old pipe insulation, popcorn ceilings pre-1980, vermiculite attic insulation Zonolite brand. Must be tested by certified lab before disturbing — abatement $500-5,000+) . Turn off power/water/gas to area being demolished. Know where pipes/wires run in walls
  - Asbestos: mesothelioma (cancer from inhaled fibers). Do NOT disturb or cut — test first, professional abatement if found. Friable (crumbles in hand) = high risk. Some can be encapsulated (encased in new material) instead of removed
- **Dumpster**: 10-20 yard dumpster ($300-600 + dump fees). Separate: trash, construction waste, recyclables (metal, cardboard). Hazardous waste (old paint, chemicals, batteries) = take to special facility
  - Bagster: small bag from Home Depot for small jobs ($30 bag + $150-200 pickup) vs roll-off dumpster for bigger

## Drywall
- **Installation**: hanging (screw drywall to studs every 12" on ceilings to prevent sag, 16" on walls — use 5/8" for ceilings (fire rated for garages between house+garage), 1/2" for walls). Taping (paper tape for corners and flat joints — stronger than mesh; mesh tape for cracks/large gaps). Mudding/compound (pre-mixed all-purpose compound for taping, lightweight topping compound for final coats)
  - Technique: 3 coats — bed coat with tape, fill/smooth coat, skim/finish coat. Sand between coats (180-220 grit). Dust = ALWAYS wear mask — silica from joint compound dust dangerous (silicosis). Vacuum with HEPA vacuum after sanding. Use pole sander for large areas, hand sanding block for corners
  - Patch small holes: California patch (cut paper + drywall patch, mud over). Old work patch: cut hole square, screw in old-work clip to adjacent stud, patch with new piece + mud

## Flooring

### Hardwood
- **Solid**: 3/4" thick, can be sanded + refinished 3-5 times over life (30-100 years). Widths: strip 2 1/4", plank 3-5", wide plank 6"+. Must be nailed to subfloor. Cannot install below grade (basement = moisture warps). Acclimate 3-7 days in room before install
  - Engineered: plywood base + hardwood veneer (1/8" to 1/4" thick). Can sand 1-2 times. Can be installed below grade, over radiant heat, glue/nail/float. Wider planks possible. More dimensionally stable than solid for humidity changes
  - Finish: site-finished (stain + polyurethane on site — custom color, durable, smelly, 2-3 days dry time per coat, dust) vs prefinished (factory-finished — consistent, durable aluminum oxide coating, less site work, beveled edges possible). Water-based poly (clear, less odor, dries fast) vs oil-based (amber, durable, longer odor)

### Laminate & LVP
- **Laminate**: HDF core + printed image layer + clear wear layer. Floating click-lock. Cheaper ($1-4/sq ft). Good for DIY, easier. Lasts 10-20 years. Can't refinish. Susceptible to moisture (swells)
  - Laminate quality: AC rating (AC3 = residential, AC4/5 = commercial). Thickness (8-12mm better). Pad attached vs separate. Waterproof laminate now exists (WPC/SGP core)
- **Luxury Vinyl Plank (LVP)**: vinyl (PVC) core + printed layer + clear wear layer. Waterproof. 100% waterproof — great for basements, bathrooms, kitchens. Thinner than laminate (4-8mm). Warmer underfoot. Click lock or glue down. Lasts 15-25 years. More expensive than basic laminate
- **Tile**: ceramic (glazed, cheaper, softer) vs porcelain (harder, less porous, better for floors, through-body color. Grade 4-5 rating for floor use). Rectified (precision cut edges = smaller grout lines 1/8") vs non-rectified
  - Large format tile (12x24, 24x48) = fewer grout lines, more modern. Install requires flat subfloor and anti-fracture membrane. Grout: sanded (for joints >1/8", stronger) vs unsanded (for <1/8"). Epoxy grout (stain-proof, harder to install, more expensive. Worth it for kitchen/bath)
  - Cutting tile: wet saw (best cuts, rental $50/day). Manual snap cutter for straight cuts. Angle grinder with diamond blade for curves. Tile nippers for small irregular cuts. Bullnose: use schluter edge trim instead (metal J-profile)
  - Heated floors: electric radiant heat mats (SunTouch, Schluter Ditra Heat) under tile — very nice in bathrooms. Costs $100-200 for a bathroom plus the thermostat. Run on dedicated circuit. Pour self-leveling over mats before tiling if needed

## Painting (Interior)
- **Tools**: roller (9/16" nap for smooth walls, 1/2" for textured walls, 3/4" for heavy texture). Angled brush (2-3" for cut-ins). Painter's tape (FrogTape yellow = best for clean lines, blue = standard for clean removal). Extension pole (5-8 ft). Paint tray + liners. Drop cloths (canvas > plastic for walk-on — canvas absorb spills; plastic = slippery + doesn't absorb)
  - Technique: cut in edges first (brush along trim, ceiling, corners), then roll (W or M pattern -> fill in, then backroll without reloading for even distribution). 2 coats always (primer + 1 color coat if color is on new drywall/ bare wall, or 2 color coats over existing). Remove tape while paint is slightly wet (peels cleaner). Don't overload roller = drips
- **Types**: flat (no shine — hides wall imperfections. Living rooms, adult bedrooms. But harder to clean). Eggshell (low satin, washable — most popular for walls, good for wide areas). Satin (moderate sheen, easy to clean, bathrooms, kids' rooms, trim). Semi-gloss (shiny, scrubbable, trim + doors + cabinets). High-gloss (very shiny, best for cabinets + trim exposed to moisture)
  - Primer: required for new drywall (PVA primer), bare wood, drastic color changes, stains, or if painting over glossy paint (without sanding). Tinted primer near your finish color = fewer topcoats

## Trim & Molding
- **Crown molding**: at ceiling-wall joint. Inside corners cope joint (not miter — coped: cut shape of profile on one piece, fits over profile of adjoining piece. Miters on outside corners). Spring angle (38°/45° most common. Most crowns = 38° spring for 45° cuts; 45° = installs at 45° from wall. Always check). Cut upside down on miter saw (flat on saw fence — rotated to installed position)
- **Baseboard**: MDF (cheap, smooth, no knots. Can swell if wet. Use primed MDF). Pine (easy to work, knots may bleed through without primer — use shellac primer or BIN). Primed finger-joint (more stable than MDF?)
  - Inside corners: cope joint (stronger, hides gaps as house settles). Outside corners: miter. Caulk top gap (flexible acrylic latex caulk, not silicone for painting). Nail every 16" with 18ga brad nailer or 15ga finish nailer. Set nails below surface, fill with wood putty, sand + paint. Use shoe molding to cover gap at floor

## Kitchen Remodel
- **Cabinet refacing**: keep existing boxes, replace doors + drawer fronts + veneer sides = 30-50% of full replacement cost. Good if boxes are solid and you like the layout. Repaint: degrease, sand (TSP or degreaser first), prime (shellac for stains), paint (enamel or cabinet paint with hardener). Spraying = best finish but overspray — spray booth if no appliances
- **Countertops**: laminate ($15-30/sq ft installed, cheap, durable, can't repair). Quartz ($50-100/sq ft, durable, consistent color, no sealing needed. "Engineered stone", 90% quartz mineral + resin). Granite ($40-80/sq ft, natural, heat resistant, needs sealing. Seal annually). Butcher block ($20-40/sq ft, warm, scratchable. Oil frequently). Marble ($60-150/sq ft, beautiful, stains + etches easily — not for messy cooks). Solid surface (Corian $40-60/sq ft, seamless, repairable, feels warm). Porcelain slab/Corian porcelain slab ($50-80+. Large format, durable, outdoor)
- **Backsplash**: subway tile (classic, cheap), mosaics (glass, stone, ceramic), large format porcelain slab (seamless). Materials: ceramic, glass, stainless steel, stone. Thickness: standard subway 3"x6" x 1/4". Get thin-set, grout (epoxy best for kitchen — resists stains), caulking at corners + countertop junction (silicone, color-matched). Seal stone tiles before + after

## Bathroom Remodel
- **Wet area waterproofing**: cement board (Durock, HardieBacker) + liquid membrane (RedGard, Hydro Ban) or Schluter Kerdi membrane (sheet membrane, thinset + fleece. New standard in tile showers). Pan: pre-formed foam (Schluter Kerdi tray, $200-400, slope built-in, easy) vs traditional mortar bed (custom slope, more skill, cheaper, heavier. Mortar bed = 1/4" per ft slope). Curb or curbless (barrier-free — needs linear drain, slope + trench drain). Waterproof behind all: corners + niches sealed with Kerdi band/flashing or liquid membrane
  - Waterproofing failure: most common bathroom renovation failure mode. Worth paying extra for
- **Shower fixtures**: rough-in valve (back in wall, pressure balance or thermostatic). Trim kits. Rainshowe head + handheld. Body sprays. Thermostatic valve eliminates temperature fluctuations. Valve depth: for tile + waterproofing (minimum depth behind finish). Access panel on back side in case of failure
- **Toilet**: rough-in distance (wall to center of flange — standard 12", also 10", 14"). Flushing: gravity vs pressure-assist (Toto). Bowl shape: round (shorter, good for small bath. 16-17" front of bowl to tank) vs elongated (more comfortable, needs space. 18-19"). Height: comfort height/chair height (17-19" seat — better for aging). 1.28 gpf low flow standard. Best: Toto Drake II, American Standard Champion 4
- **Ventilation**: exhaust fan (vented to OUTSIDE, not into attic). Humidity sensor switch (automatic). Size: 50 CFM minimum, 1 CFM per sq ft of bathroom. 8 cfm needed for water heater room also. Fan should move air from bathroom → attic/outdoor. Run duct to roof or wall. 4" rigid metal duct (not flexible foil — flexible restricts air and can sag)
