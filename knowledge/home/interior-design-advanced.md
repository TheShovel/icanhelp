# Interior Design Styles (Advanced)

## Design Movements & Eras
- **Arts & Crafts (1880-1910)**: reaction against industrialization — handcrafted, natural materials, honesty of construction. William Morris (UK), Stickley (US). Key features: exposed joinery, oak, stained glass, earthy colors, handmade tiles. Morris: "Have nothing in your houses that you do not know to be useful, or believe to be beautiful"
- **Art Nouveau (1890-1910)**: organic, flowing curves, natural forms (vines, flowers, insects). Whiplash curves, asymmetrical. Gaudi (Barcelona — Casa Batlló, Parc Güell, Sagrada Familia. His work is architecture + furniture: all custom). Horta (Belgium). Mucha (graphics, posters). Tiffany lamps (stained glass lampshades)
- **Art Deco (1920s-1930s)**: geometric, symmetrical, luxurious — zigzags, sunbursts, chevron patterns, exotic materials (ivory, ebony, lacquer, sharkskin, chrome, glass block). Streamline Moderne (aerodynamic curves — like ocean liner design applied to buildings/trains/toasters. Chrysler Building, Rockefeller Center. Empire State Building spire = art deco). Iconic: Chrysler Building spire, Radio City Music Hall, Miami Beach pastel deco hotels
- **Bauhaus (1919-1933)**: "form follows function" — minimal, industrial materials, geometric, no ornament. Walter Gropius, Marcel Breuer (Wassily chair — tubular steel), Mies van der Rohe (Barcelona chair — "less is more"). Ludwig Mies van der Rohe (Barcelona Pavilion, Farnsworth House). The school itself was influential despite only existing 14 years
- **Mid-Century Modern (1945-1965)**: clean lines, organic curves, integration with nature, new materials (plywood, plastic, fiberglass). Charles + Ray Eames (Eames lounge chair, La Chaise, fiberglass shell chairs), Harry Bertoia (wire mesh Diamond chair), Arne Jacobsen (Egg chair, Swan chair). Case Study Houses (California: Stahl House/CSH#22 by Pierre Koenig, Eames House by Charles Eames). Minimalist yet warm, contrast of natural + synthetic
- **Postmodern (1970s-1990)**: reaction against modernism's seriousness — color, irony, historical references, Pop art influences. Memphis Group (Ettore Sottsass — Carlton cabinet — bright colors, asymmetrical, playful, "pop art"). Philippe Starck (Juicy Salif lemon squeezer, Louis Ghost chair). Formica + laminate + postmodern furniture is common in postmodern design
- **Scandinavian modern (1950s-present)**: minimal, functional, affordable for all. Light woods (birch, beech, pine), clean lines, cozy (hygge/lagom — balanced, not minimalist to excess). IKEA = democratic design (affordable for many. Democratic design at IKEA: form, function, quality, sustainability, low price — five dimensions). Alvar Aalto (Finland — bentwood furniture, organic shapes). Hans Wegner (Wishbone chair, Shell chair). Arne Jacobsen (Series 7 chair). Finn Juhl, Poul Henningsen (PH lamp series: glare-free, multiple shades)

## Color Palettes by Style
- **Scandinavian**: white walls, pale wood floors, black accents, muted pastels (pale pink, sage, dusty blue), warm grays. Contrast with texture (wool throws, sheepskin, linen)
- **Bohemian**: jewel tones (deep teal, burgundy, mustard, purple, magenta), warm neutrals, lots of pattern mixing (ikat, kilim, suzani, batik), global textiles. Layering rugs, pillows, curtains. Eclectic maximalism — more is more
- **Industrial**: dark grays, black, brown, warm metallics (copper, brass), exposed brick red, concrete gray. Rusty metals, aged leather
- **Farmhouse**: whites, creams, soft grays, warm wood tones, pale blue, sage green. Shiplap (horizontal wood planks — also beadboard, board and batten wall treatment). Apron-front farmhouse sink
- **Minimalist**: white, off-white, beige, gray, black accents. Monochromatic palette with strategic color pop. Japanese minimalism (wabi-sabi = beauty of imperfection. Kintsugi: gold repair of broken pottery — celebrates flaws. Ma: negative space. Shibui: simple, subtle, unobtrusive)

## Textiles & Materials
- **Natural fibers**: linen (wrinkles naturally, breathes, cool — casual elegance). Cotton (versatile, comfortable, easy care). Wool (warm, durable, stain-resistant, good for rugs + upholstery). Silk (luxurious, delicate, temperature regulating — needs special cleaning). Hemp (durable, eco-friendly, textured). Jute (coarse, natural, good for rugs — but sheds)
- **Performance fabrics**: Crypton (stain-proof, liquid barrier, antimicrobial). Sunbrella (fade-resistant, outdoor fabric, acrylic solution-dyed — lasts 5-10 years). Microfiber (polyester/nylon blend, stain-resistant, affordable, soft). Olefin (polypropylene, stain-resistant, UV-resistant, good for outdoors). Teflon coating (stain repellant applied to any fabric as after-treatment)
- **Leather types**: full-grain (top quality, natural marks, patinas beautifully — develops character over time). Top-grain (sanded, more uniform, less durable than full-grain). Bonded/ genuine (reconstituted leather scraps — low quality, peels, avoid if possible). Nubuck (sanded full-grain, velvety, delicate, stains easily). Aniline (dyed but not coated, shows natural grain, absorbs spills instantly). Semi-aniline (coated, more stain-resistant, uniform color, slightly less "natural" feel)

## Window Treatments
- **Curtains by length**: apron (just below window sill — casual, good for kitchens). Floor-length (standard — hem 1/4-1/2" above floor if no rug under. Modern clean). Break (hem touches floor slightly — softer look; similar to pants at hem). Puddle (extra length pools on floor — formal, opulent, dusty — not practical for high-traffic)
  - Stackback width: when open, curtain should stack back onto wall without blocking glass. Minimum: rod extends 8-12" past window on each side. For tall windows: use two panels, operated separately. For layered: sheers close to glass + decorative panels outside (sheer translucent vs opaque)
- **Blinds**: Roman (fabric folds up — soft shades — classic. Flat vs hobbled/looped). Roller (modern, clean, motorized options with smart home control). Cellular/honeycomb (energy-efficient — traps air in cells, good insulation value. Light-filtering vs room-darkening). Wood (natural warmth, heavier, need wide slats for aesthetic). Faux wood (PVC/composite, moisture-resistant, good for bathrooms/kitchens). Venetian/metal (cheap, modern, but noisy)
- **Hardware**: rods (curtain rod diameter: 1-1.5" for standard, 2-3" for heavy drapes). Finials (decorative ends — ball, square, spear). Rings (clip rings = easiest, goes through rod + attaches clip directly to fabric. Pinch pleat headers = traditional, more formal. Grommet top = modern, easy to slide). Mounting: inside mount (within window frame, clean, shows off trim, not for doors?). Outside mount (above frame, makes window appear larger, hides imperfect drywall. Extends beyond 2-4" each side). Rings size 1-2" for rod thickness

## Space Planning & Layout
- **Traffic flow**: define primary + secondary paths. Main walkways: 36-48" minimum (48" for wheelchair accessible). Secondary: 24-36". Behind furniture clearance: 30-36" for sitting, 12-18" for walking behind sofa
- **Seating arrangement**: face each other for conversation (not all facing TV). Distance between coffee table and sofa: 14-18". Between chairs: 42-48" for conversation. Side table reachable from every seat (armrest-to-table: 4-8")
- **TV viewing**: distance = screen size × 1.5-2.5 (for 65" TV: 8-13 ft). Center of TV at seated eye level (42" from floor for average — measure your seated eye height). Above fireplace = too high (neck strain). Tilting mount helps (no chin tuck at 55°+ if seated 10'+, but still bad)
- **Rug sizing**: all furniture legs on rug = traditional, formal. Front legs only = contemporary, common in living rooms. No furniture on rug = rug too small. Sizes: living room 8'x10' minimum for most, 9'x12' better (or even 10x14 for large). Dining: 24-30" beyond table edge on each side for chair pull-out clearance. Bedroom: 2' under nightstands, extend 18-24" beyond bed. Runner: hung 4" past end of hallway

## Lighting Design
- **Layers (3 types)**: ambient (general illumination — overhead, chandelier, track lighting, recessed). Task (focused — reading lamp, under-cabinet kitchen, bathroom vanity mirror). Accent (highlight features — art light, picture light, uplighting, spotlights on architectural details). Good lighting uses all 3 layers separately dimmed
- **Light temperature**: 2700K (warm white — cozy, living rooms, bedrooms). 3000K-3500K (soft white — transitional, kitchens, baths — clean). 4000K (neutral — task areas, garages). 5000K+ (daylight — commercial, garages, jewelry stores — harsh, avoid in homes unless for dedicated work). Mixing temps in same space = jarring. Stay within 300K variation per room
- **Fixture placement**: chandelier over dining table — 1/2 to 2/3 width of table. Bottom of fixture 30-36" above table surface. Pendant over kitchen island — one fixture every 24-30" for linear island, centered over island. Height 30-36" above counter. Kitchen island pendants: if island 6'+ long, use 2-3 pendants equally spaced. For single pendants, center 12"
- **Art lighting**: picture light 2-4" above frame, angle to center of art. Track heads can be positioned 30° from wall. Color temp: 2700-3000K for art, avoid UV (damage over time). Gallery/ museum spec: 100-150 lux on art surface, color temp 3000K exact, limited UV. For home: LED picture lights. Avoid: halogen (hot, UV)

## Wall Finishes
- **Paint finishes**: flat/matte (no shine, hides imperfections, hardest to clean. Good for ceilings, low-traffic walls, adult bedrooms). Eggshell (low sheen, washable — most popular for walls. Hides minor blemishes well). Satin (medium sheen, durable, scrubbable — good for bathrooms, kitchens, kids' rooms, laundry). Semi-gloss (high sheen, very durable, moisture-proof — trim, doors, cabinets, bathrooms, high-traffic). Gloss (extremely shiny, most durable — for cabinets, furniture, accent details)
- **Wallpaper**: peel-and-stick (rental-friendly, temporary, less forgiving on imperfect walls. Can be repositioned). Traditional paste (permanent, more durable, larger selection, requires skill). Grasscloth (natural fibers — textured, adds warmth, delicate, seams visible, hard to clean). Mural (scenic/photo wallpaper — one wall statement, can be removable). Material choices: vinyl (durable, washable, great for kitchens/baths). Non-woven (breathable, strippable, dimensionally stable — best of all). Paper (traditional, harder to clean)
  - Where to wallpaper: accent wall (behind bed, dining room). Ceiling (fifth wall — powder room, entry). Small rooms (powder room, hallway, closet — makes them feel intentional). Entire room for maximalist/cozy look (traditional drawing room, library, study)
