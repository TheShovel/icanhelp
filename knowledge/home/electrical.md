# Basic Home Electrical

## Safety First
- **NEVER** work on live circuits — turn off power at breaker and test with multimeter/ non-contact voltage tester before touching ANY wire
  - Non-contact voltage tester (NCVT): cheap ($15), essential — beeps/flashes near live wires. Test it on a known live circuit first to confirm it works
  - Multimeter: more reliable than NCVT, can verify 0V
- One hand in pocket when working on anything that could be live (reduces risk of current across chest)
- Use rubber-soled shoes, stand on dry surface
- Never use aluminum wiring (homes built 1965-1973) — requires special connectors, needs electrician
- Know where your main breaker is, know how to shut off ALL power
- GFCI outlets (with TEST/RESET buttons) required: bathrooms, kitchens, garages, basements, outdoors, crawlspaces — they save lives

## Circuit Breaker Panel
- **Main breaker**: shuts off ALL power to house (typically 100-200 amp)
- **Branch breakers**: 15-20 amp for general lighting/receptacles, 30-50 amp for appliances (dryer, stove, water heater, AC)
  - 15A breaker = max 1800W (120V × 15A). 20A = 2400W
  - Kitchen circuits: at least two 20-amp circuits for countertop appliances
  - Arc Fault Circuit Interrupter (AFCI): required in bedrooms, living areas (detects dangerous arcing)
- **Double-pole breakers**: 240V circuits (large appliances, EV chargers) — take two slots, handle the two 120V hot wires
- **If breaker trips**: unplug everything on that circuit, reset breaker, plug back one by one to find problem
  - If breaker won't reset or trips immediately: short circuit or ground fault — call electrician
  - If breaker trips after high load (microwave + hair dryer on same circuit): overload — move devices to other circuits

## Outlets & Switches
- **Outlet wiring**: black wire (hot/bronze screw), white wire (neutral/silver screw), bare/green wire (ground/green screw)
  - Hot = carries electricity TO device. Neutral = returns it. Ground = safety path for fault current
  - Receptacle orientation: ground pin DOWN (standard, safer if plug partially pulled out)
- **Pigtail**: short wire joining multiple wires together with a wire nut — never rely on outlet itself to pass current to other outlets
  - Recommended: pigtail hot, neutral, and ground so only one wire per screw on the outlet
- **Switches**: single pole (one switch controls light), 3-way (two switches control same light — hallway, stairs), 4-way (three+ switches)
  - Dimmer switch: rated for LED/smart bulbs (not all dimmers work with all LEDs — check compatibility)
  - Black screw on 3-way switch = common (traveler wires = brass screws)
- **Replacing outlet**: match wires to same terminals as old outlet. Strip wire 5/8" (use correct stripping notch), form loop clockwise around screw (screw tightens loop), tighten firmly

## Wiring a Light Fixture
1. Turn off breaker and verify power is off
2. Connect fixture wires: fixture black to house black, white to white, ground to ground (or connect to box if metal)
3. Wire nut connections: strip ~3/4", twist wires together clockwise with pliers, screw on wire nut, gently tug to verify secure
4. Tuck wires neatly into box (fold back and forth, don't cram), attach fixture, install bulbs
5. Turn breaker on, test
- **Weight**: heavy fixtures MUST be attached to junction box or ceiling joist, not just drywall — use appropriate mounting bracket
  - Ceiling fan rated box required for ceiling fans (standard boxes aren't strong enough)

## Light Bulbs
- **LED**: most efficient (90% less energy than incandescent), longest life (15-25 years), range of color temps, slightly more expensive upfront
  - Lumens (brightness): 800 = 60W replacement, 1100 = 75W replacement, 1600 = 100W replacement
  - Color temperature: 2700K (warm white = cozy, living/bedroom), 3000K (soft white = kitchen/bath), 4000K (neutral = task lighting), 5000K (daylight = garage, reading)
  - CRI (Color Rendering Index): 90+ = accurate colors (important for makeup, art studios)
  - Dimmable: NOT all LEDs are dimmable — check packaging, need compatible dimmer switch (many older dimmers don't work with LEDs, causes flickering)
- **Incandescent**: being phased out (inefficient, short life), warm light — banned for general lighting in US (2023, some exceptions)
- **CFL** (compact fluorescent): better than incandescent but contains mercury (special disposal), slow to warm up — being replaced by LEDs
- **Smart bulbs**: WiFi/ Bluetooth/Zigbee controlled, color-changing, dimming, scheduling — need hub for Zigbee (Philips Hue), WiFi bulbs work standalone (but can crowd network)

## Running New Cable (Basics)
- **Romex** (NM-B): non-metallic sheathed cable — #14 for 15A circuits, #12 for 20A, #10 for 30A
  - Buying: 14/2 = 14 gauge, 2 conductors + ground (for lights). 12/2 = 12 gauge (outlets). 12/3 = 12 gauge, 3 conductors (for 3-way switches)
- **Stapling**: secure cable every 4-6 ft, within 12" of box. Don't overtighten staple (can damage insulation)
- **Drilling through studs**: center of stud (not within 1-1/4" of edge — protect from drywall screws). Put nail plate over stud where cable passes through
- **Box fill**: can't pack too many wires into too small box — code limits based on box volume and wire count (counts: each conductor = 1, each device = 2, grounds = 1, clamps = 1)
- If in doubt: PULL PERMIT and have work INSPECTED (required for new circuits, additions, major changes)

## When to Call an Electrician
- **Call**: new circuits, panel upgrades (or anything involving inside panel beyond flipping breakers), running new cable through finished walls, service entrance (from meter to house), any work that needs permit + inspection, aluminum wiring work
- **DIY**: replace outlet/switch, replace light fixture (same location), install dimmer, replace ceiling fan with same type, wire a thermostat
- **ALWAYS electrician**: flickering lights throughout house, breaker that keeps tripping with no overload, buzzing from panel or outlets, outlets warm to touch, shock when plugging in, aluminum wiring
