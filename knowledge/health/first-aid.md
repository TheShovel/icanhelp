# Basic First Aid & Safety

## Emergency Numbers
- **911** — US emergency (police, fire, ambulance)
- **112** — EU emergency (works globally on GSM phones)
- **Poison Control (US)**: 1-800-222-1222
- **Non-emergency police**: call local station

## When to Call 911
- Unconscious or unresponsive
- Chest pain or difficulty breathing
- Severe bleeding that won't stop
- Choking (can't cough/speak/breath)
- Severe allergic reaction (swelling face, trouble breathing)
- Stroke signs: F.A.S.T. — Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call
- Seizure lasting > 5 minutes
- Major burns, deep wounds, or head trauma

## Cuts & Scrapes
1. Stop bleeding: apply direct pressure with clean cloth, elevate if possible
2. Clean: rinse with cool water, mild soap around wound (not in it)
3. Apply antibiotic ointment
4. Cover with sterile bandage
5. Change daily, keep clean
6. Signs of infection: redness spreading, warmth, pus, fever, red streaks

## Burns
- **First degree** (red, no blisters): cool water 10 min, aloe vera, ibuprofen
- **Second degree** (blisters): cool water, don't pop blisters, sterile gauze, seek medical if large
- **Third degree** (white/charred, no pain): call 911, cover loosely, don't remove stuck clothing
- Never apply ice directly, butter, or toothpaste

## Choking (Heimlich Maneuver)
- Ask "Are you choking?" (if they can speak/cough — encourage coughing)
- Stand behind, arms around waist
- Make fist above navel, grab with other hand
- Thrust inward and upward (J-shaped motion)
- Repeat until object dislodged or person goes unconscious
- If unconscious: call 911, start CPR, check mouth for object

## CPR (Adult)
1. Call 911 first
2. Push hard and fast in center of chest (100-120 compressions/min)
3. Compress at least 2 inches deep
4. Let chest fully recoil between compressions
5. 30 compressions, then 2 rescue breaths (if trained)
6. Continue until EMS arrives or person recovers
- Beat: "Stayin' Alive" by Bee Gees (~100 bpm)

## Allergic Reaction (Anaphylaxis)
- Symptoms: hives, swelling face/tongue, trouble breathing, wheezing, dizziness, vomiting
- Treatment: epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) into outer thigh (through clothing)
- Call 911 immediately after epinephrine (can wear off)
- Lay person flat, elevate legs if dizzy
- Second dose may be needed after 5-15 min if no improvement

## Poisoning
- Call poison control immediately
- Do NOT induce vomiting unless instructed
- Save the substance/container for identification
- If person is unconscious or having seizure: call 911
- For chemical on skin: remove contaminated clothing, rinse with water 15 min
- For chemical in eyes: flush with lukewarm water 15 min

## Sprains & Fractures
- **RICE**: Rest, Ice (20 min on/off), Compression (elastic bandage), Elevation
- Signs of fracture: deformity, inability to bear weight, severe pain, swelling, bruising
- Splint: immobilize joint above and below injury with rigid object (board, rolled magazine)
- Seek medical for suspected fracture or severe sprain

## Heat Exhaustion vs Heat Stroke
- **Heat exhaustion**: heavy sweating, cool/clammy skin, headache, nausea, dizziness, weak pulse
  - Move to cool place, drink water, cool compresses
- **Heat stroke** (emergency): hot/red/dry skin, confusion, unconsciousness, high body temp
  - Call 911, cool rapidly (ice packs on neck/armpits/groin), do NOT give fluids if unconscious

## First Aid Kit Essentials
- Sterile gauze pads (various sizes)
- Adhesive bandages (assorted)
- Medical tape
- Antiseptic wipes / hydrogen peroxide
- Antibiotic ointment
- Hydrocortisone cream (rash, itching)
- Elastic bandage (ACE wrap)
- Triangular bandage (sling)
- Scissors, tweezers
- Instant cold pack
- Nitrile gloves
- CPR mask
- Pain reliever (ibuprofen, acetaminophen)
- Antihistamine (Benadryl)
- Burn cream / aloe vera gel
- Eye wash
- First aid manual
