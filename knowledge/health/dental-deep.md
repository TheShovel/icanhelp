# Oral Hygiene & Dental Health (Advanced)

## Tooth Anatomy
- **Crown**: visible part — enamel (hardest substance in human body, 96% hydroxyapatite), dentin (softer, yellow, contains nerve endings), pulp (blood vessels + nerves)
- **Root**: below gum line — cementum (covers root), periodontal ligament (connects tooth to bone — shock absorber, holds tooth in socket), alveolar bone (tooth socket). Normally covered by gum tissue
  - Root exposure (recession) = gum tissue recedes → dentin exposed → sensitivity. Causes: brushing too hard (mechanical abrasion), periodontal disease, genetics, thin tissue biotype

## Caries (Cavities) Process
- **Demineralization**: acid from bacteria (Streptococcus mutans, Lactobacillus) dissolves enamel — pH below 5.5 causes net mineral loss. Acids come from bacterial fermentation of fermentable carbohydrates (sugar — sucrose most cariogenic because bacteria use it to make sticky glucan matrix that adheres to tooth)
  - Time: demineralization starts within 20 minutes after sugar exposure. Saliva neutralizes acid in ~30-60 minutes. Frequent sugar exposure = constant acid attack
- **Remineralization**: saliva + fluoride + calcium + phosphate rebuild enamel. Fluoride: forms fluorapatite (stronger, more acid-resistant than hydroxyapatite). Requires low pH + fluoride presence to work. Takes ~2-3 hours between acid attacks
  - Xylitol: bacteria can't metabolize xylitol (5-carbon sugar alcohol) — reduces S. mutans counts, stimulates saliva. Gum with xylitol: 2-3 pieces/day after meals. Essential for: high caries risk, dry mouth, frequent snacking

## Gum Disease (Periodontal Disease)
- **Gingivitis**: reversible — plaque biofilm accumulation → inflammation. Gums bleed when brushing/flossing (healthy gums don't bleed). NO bone loss. Treatment: professional cleaning + improved home care. Bleeding = sign of active inflammation, not trauma
- **Periodontitis**: irreversible — inflammation extends below gum line → bacteria colonize subgingival pocket → immune response destroys attachment (gum + bone). Pockets >3mm = periodontitis. Bone loss: cannot be regenerated without surgical grafting. Risk factors: smoking (major — pack/day = 5x risk), diabetes (poorly controlled = 3x risk), genetics (6% population have aggressive periodontitis), stress, certain medications, poor oral hygiene
  - Stages: mild (1-2mm bone loss), moderate (3-4mm), severe (5mm+). Treatment: scaling + root planing (deep cleaning — local anesthetic, deep scaling of root surfaces), periodic maintenance (every 3-4 months), surgery for advanced cases (flap surgery to reduce pocket depth, bone grafting, guided tissue regeneration). AREDS? No — periodontitis also linked to poor wound healing: uncontrolled diabetics have 3-4x more periodontitis than non-diabetic

## Tooth Wear
- **Abrasion**: mechanical wear from brushing too hard (horizontal scrubbing), toothpicks, pipe smoking, biting nails, holding objects with teeth. Pattern: V-shaped notch at gum line (cervical abrasion). Prevention: soft brush, correct technique (small circles, 45° angle at gum line, 2 min, not "sawing" back and forth)
- **Erosion**: acid dissolution (not bacterial) — from dietary acids (soda, citrus juice, wine, sour candies, fruit juice), gastric acid (acid reflux/gERD, bulimia/vomiting). Pattern: cupping on chewing surfaces, smooth appearance, loss of enamel on palatal surfaces of upper front teeth (from vomiting). Prevention: don't brush after acid exposure (enamel softens from acid → brushing brushes it away!). Rinse with water or baking soda water, wait 30-60 min before brushing
  - Most erosive: soda (high acidity + sugar + phosphoric/citric acid). Lemon water sipping throughout day = constant acid on teeth. Use straw to minimize contact
- **Attrition**: tooth-to-tooth wear (grinding/clenching). Pattern: flat, even wear on biting surfaces. Bruxism: often stress-related, sleep bruxism common (~10% adults). Also side effect of some antidepressants (SSRIs). Nightguard (custom from dentist > OTC boil-and-bite) = protects teeth. Botox for severe clenching (masseter muscle). Complication: TMJ pain, headache, abfraction (cracked enamel at gum line from flexing under clenching forces)

## Dental Materials
- **Amalgam** (silver filling): silver + tin + copper + mercury (50% mercury). Very durable (10-15 years). Expands slightly over time (seals better against leakage). Controversy: mercury vapor released during chewing (very low amount, generally considered safe by ADA/WHO except for pregnant women and children as precaution). Being phased out in some countries (EU, Sweden, Norway banned). Less aesthetic
- **Composite resin** (tooth-colored filling): bonded directly to tooth. Aesthetic, removes less tooth structure (no need for mechanical retention). More technique-sensitive. Lifespan 5-10 years (shorter than amalgam). Polymerization shrinkage (2-5%) = stress at tooth interface, microleakage, recurrent decay. Newer bulk-fill composites reduce this
  - Placement: etch (37% phosphoric acid), prime, bond, layer composite (max 2mm per layer), cure with blue light (20-40 sec each layer), shape + polish
- **Porcelain**: crowns, veneers, inlays, onlays. Laboratory fabricated (milled in CAD/CAM or hand-layered on die). Aesthetic, strong (but can fracture). Zirconia (white ceramic, very strong, monolithic — no layering). Lithium disilicate (e.max): translucent, strong, aesthetic. Porcelain fused to metal (PFM): strong, less aesthetic (metal shows at gum over time). Full porcelain (feldspathic, layered) = most aesthetic but most fragile, best for anterior
- **Crown types**: gold (most durable, best fit, expensive, aesthetic disadvantage). Porcelain-fused-to-metal (good durability, fair aesthetics, gum darkening over time). Full porcelain/e.max (best aesthetics, medium durability, good for front). Zirconia (very strong, best for back, slightly opaque). Implant crown (PFM or porcelain attached to titanium abutment screw, needs healthy bone)

## Oral Hygiene Tools (Advanced)
- **Manual toothbrush**: soft bristles (hard bristles = gum recession + root abrasion, ADA only certifies soft). Small head (easier access to back teeth). Replace every 3 months (when bristles splay). Technique: 45° angle at gum line, small circular motions, 2 min timer, don't rinse mouth after spitting (leave toothpaste's fluoride to work). Floss BEFORE brushing (loosens debris, then fluoride from toothpaste penetrates between teeth)
- **Electric toothbrush**: Sonicare (sonic vibration 31,000 strokes/min, fluid dynamics cleaning beyond bristle tips, good for braces). Oral-B (oscillating-rotating, more scrubbing action, individual tufts easier for around gumline). Both are more effective than manual for most people (30% less plaque, 50% less gingivitis over time vs. manual brushing). Advantage: timer + pressure sensor (prevents brushing too hard). Philips Sonicare, Oral-B iO, Burst, Quip (basic but accessible)
  - Pressure sensor: light indicates too much force. Most people brush too hard (gum recession = irreversible). If you see bristles splay within 2 weeks, you're pressing too hard
- **Interdental brushes**: more effective than floss for wide spaces. Sizes 0-6 (0.5-2mm). Rubber picks (GUM Soft-Picks) for beginners. Brushes: pass through gently, poke 1-2 times each space. Hurts at first if gums inflamed + new to it
- **Water flosser** (Waterpik): water pressurized between teeth + below gum line (10-90 PSI). Good for braces, implants, bridges, gingivitis reduction, anyone who struggles with floss. ADA accept: only Waterpik brand clinically shown. For heavy plaque: 3-5 sec per gap, 90-100 PSI. Not a floss replacement (doesn't remove sticky plaque as well as string floss, but reduces gingivitis more than string floss in some studies)
- **Tongue scraper**: reduces bad breath (VSCs — volatile sulfur compounds) more than brushing tongue (scraper removes tongue coating more effectively than toothbrush). Stainless steel or plastic (copper may have extra antimicrobial effect). Scrape from back to front 3-5x, rinse, clean
- **Fluoride varnish**: professional application (at dental cleaning, by dentist/hygienist). 2-5% sodium fluoride (22,600 ppm). Stays on teeth hours, continuous fluoride release. More effective than OTC rinse for high-risk patients (dry mouth, root cavities, braces). For patients at high risk: 2-4x/year. Prescription high-fluoride toothpaste (Prevident 5000 ppm): OTC maximum 1500 ppm (in US most "extra strength" is 1100-1500). Prevident has 5000 ppm = 4x standard. For high decay risk

## Dental Procedures
- **Root canal (endodontic therapy)**: remove infected/damaged pulp, clean + shape canal, fill with gutta-percha (rubber), seal. Local anesthetic (most root canals are painless with modern anesthesia — reputation for pain comes from before anesthetic or emergency infection). Severely infected tooth may be difficult to numb (use extra lidocaine + sweetener solution or refer to endodontist). After: crown recommended (back tooth is weaker after root canal). Success rate: 90-95% for endodontist
- **Extraction**: simple (visible tooth, loosen with elevators, remove with forceps) vs surgical (bone removal, tooth sectioning, impacted wisdom teeth). Wisdom tooth: impacted (under gum/bone). Common issues: pericoronitis infection around partially erupted wisdom tooth. Upper wisdom teeth usually easier. Surgical extractions may require osseous surgery (cutting tooth into pieces for removal)
- **Implant**: titanium post surgically placed in jawbone → osseointegration (3-6 months) → abutment + crown. Success rate 95%+ for healthy non-smokers. Replace missing tooth without grinding adjacent teeth (unlike bridge). Cost: $3,000-6,000 per implant (including crown). Bone graft if insufficient bone (extra $500-3,000). Sinus lift for upper back teeth (if sinus floor too low). All-on-4: implants for full arch replacement (4 implants strategically placed per arch). No need for denture adhesives. Requires adequate jaw height + general health for implant surgery
- **Teeth whitening**: OTC strips (10% hydrogen peroxide, 6-14% carbamide peroxide, works but less concentration, takes longer, poorer fit to teeth → less effective). Custom take-home trays (dentist-made, complete fit, 10-20% carbamide peroxide gel, 1-2 weeks, works best for healthy teeth). In-office (35% hydrogen peroxide + light/heat — $500-1,000, immediate result, but potentially more sensitivity). Sensitivity: common (use sensitive toothpaste 2 weeks before). Works best on yellow teeth (brown/gray stains less responsive). NOT effective on: crowns, veneers, fillings, composites (restorations don't respond to bleaching). Overuse: damage enamel (excessive frequency/percentage)
