# Child Development (Ages 0-18)

## Infancy (0-12 months)
- **Physical**: gains ~5-7 oz/week, grows ~1" per month in first 6 months
  - 2 months: lifts head, follows objects, smiles socially
  - 4 months: rolls over, reaches for objects, laughs
  - 6 months: sits with support, transfers objects hand-to-hand, babbles
  - 9 months: crawls, pulls to stand, pincer grasp (thumb + finger), says mama/dada
  - 12 months: stands alone, may walk, says 1-2 words, waves bye-bye
- **Attachment**: secure attachment formed through consistent, responsive care — crying gets warm response = "I am safe and loved"
  - Rooming-in, babywearing, skin-to-skin, responsive feeding all support bonding
- **Language**: talk, read, sing to baby constantly — "parentese" (exaggerated, slow, high-pitched) helps language development
- **Play**: peekaboo (object permanence), rattles, mirrors, textured toys, tummy time
- **Screen time**: NONE (except video calls) — real interaction > screens for brain development

## Toddler (1-3 years)
- **Physical**: walking → running → climbing → jumping. Fine motor: scribbling, stacking blocks, using spoon
  - 18 months: walks, scribbles, says 10+ words, points to body parts
  - 2 years: runs, kicks ball, builds tower of 6 blocks, 2-3 word sentences, follows 2-step instructions
  - 3 years: pedals tricycle, copies circle, 3-4 word sentences, knows name/age, parallel play → associative play
- **Cognitive**: object permanence fully developed, beginning symbolic thinking (pretend play)
  - Cause and effect understanding, simple problem-solving (getting toy from under couch)
  - "Terrible twos" = normal assertiveness, emerging autonomy, limited language + emotional regulation
- **Social-emotional**: parallel play (plays alongside not with), beginning empathy, strong preferences, separation anxiety peaks then declines
  - Temperament is inborn — some easygoing, some intense, some slow-to-warm-up — adjust expectations accordingly
- **Language explosion**: from ~50 words at 18 months to ~1000 words at 3 years
  - Read every day (same books on repeat is fine — repetition is how they learn)
  - Narrate your day (descriptive language: "I'm chopping the orange carrot into small round pieces")
- **Discipline**: redirection, natural consequences, limited choices ("red cup or blue cup?"), consistency
  - Time-in (staying with child while they calm) > time-out (isolation) — teaches emotional regulation
  - No spanking — linked to aggression, mental health problems, weaker parent-child bond

## Preschool (3-5 years)
- **Physical**: hops, skips, catches ball, draws person with head + legs, cuts with scissors, buttons, brushes teeth
  - 4: climbs well, throws overhand, pumps on swing, draws square, uses fork
  - 5: skips, walks backward, uses toilet independently, ties knots, writes some letters
- **Cognitive**: magical thinking, vivid imagination, imaginary friends (normal), beginning understanding of time
  - Curious about everything — endless "why" questions (answer simply, turn it back: "what do you think?")
- **Social-emotional**: cooperative play begins, sharing is hard (developmentally normal), tests boundaries
  - Developing theory of mind — begins to understand others have different thoughts/feelings
  - Fears (dark, monsters, separation) — validate, don't dismiss ("that must be scary. I'm here to keep you safe")
- **Readiness for school**: can follow 3-step instructions, manage bathroom independently, separate from parent for 3 hours, basic peer interaction
- **Screen time**: 1 hour/day max, high-quality content (Sesame Street, Bluey), co-watch and talk about it

## School Age (6-12 years)
- **Physical**: slower, steady growth (2-3"/year, 5-7 lbs/year). Losing baby teeth. Coordination improves dramatically
  - 6-8: running, jumping, throwing with more accuracy. 8-10: team sports possible. 10-12: puberty may begin (girls earlier than boys)
- **Cognitive**: concrete operational stage — logical thinking, understanding cause/effect, can reverse mental operations (math)
  - Reading: learning to read (K-1) → reading to learn (3+). Math: addition/subtraction → multiplication/division → fractions/decimals
  - Executive function develops: planning, organizing, self-monitoring, impulse control (not fully developed until 25+!)
- **Social-emotional**: friendships based on shared interests and trust (not just proximity). Peer influence grows
  - Understanding rules, fairness, competition. Some experience bullying — talk about it
  - Industry vs inferiority (Erikson): need to feel competent at something (academic, athletic, creative, social)
  - Help them find their "thing" — but don't overschedule (need free play time)
- **School**: homework routine, organization strategies, growth mindset ("yet" — "I can't do it YET")
  - Communication with teacher important — advocate when needed, don't solve every problem for them
- **Chores**: build responsibility and competence — age-appropriate (set table, feed pet, make bed, sort laundry)
- **Screen time**: consistent limits, monitor content, talk about online safety, no phones alone at night in bedroom

## Adolescence (13-18 years)
- **Physical**: rapid growth (puberty). Girls: 10-14. Boys: 12-16. Hormones, body changes, sexuality
  - Sleep phase delay — natural bedtime shifts later (melatonin releases later), but school starts early → chronic sleep deprivation
  - Physical activity important — 60 min/day. Many quit sports in teen years — find some movement they enjoy
- **Cognitive**: formal operational stage — abstract thinking, hypothetical reasoning, metacognition (thinking about thinking)
  - Can understand complex concepts, philosophy, sarcasm, metaphor fully
  - Frontal lobe still developing — impulse control, long-term planning, emotional regulation are NOT fully developed
  - This explains risky behavior + intense emotions — it's neurological, not moral failing
- **Social-emotional**: identity formation (Erikson: identity vs role confusion). "Who am I?"
  - Peer group becomes primary reference — status, belonging, acceptance
  - Romantic/sexual relationships begin — need accurate sex education (abstinence-only does NOT reduce teen pregnancy)
  - Mood swings, heightened sensitivity, conflict with parents — separation-individuation process
- **Parenting teens**: autonomy with appropriate limits, listen more than lecture, choose battles
  - "I'm disappointed" > "You're grounded" for most things (they care about your opinion still)
  - Stay connected: family dinners, drives in car, ask open questions ("what was the best/worst part of today?")
  - Keep talking about safety: driving (leading cause of death), substances, sex, online safety, consent
- **Mental health**: 1 in 5 teens has anxiety or depression — rates increasing (social media, academic pressure, uncertain future)
  - Signs: withdrawal, grades dropping, sleep changes, appetite changes, irritability, loss of interest
  - Get professional help if concerned — early intervention matters
- **Substances**: don't panic about experimentation, do talk honestly about risks (not scare tactics which backfire)
  - Most common: alcohol, cannabis, nicotine/vaping. Vaping is epidemic — highly addictive, harms developing brain
  - Signs of problem: using alone, before school, stealing money, personality change, withdrawal without it
- **Social media**: linked to anxiety, depression, poor body image (especially for girls)
  - Set boundaries: no phones in bedroom overnight, social media breaks, talk about comparison trap and algorithmic manipulation
  - Watch for: cyberbullying, FOMO, sleep disruption, unrealistic body standards, radicalization
- **College/career readiness**: not everyone needs 4-year college — trades are excellent careers (plumbers, electricians earn well)
  - Gap year is OK, community college is smart, starting at university and changing path is fine
  - Skills for adulthood: budgeting, cooking, laundry, time management, communication
