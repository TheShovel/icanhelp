# Eating Disorders

Overview of eating disorders — diagnosis, treatment, and support.

## Types
- **Anorexia Nervosa**: Restriction of energy intake relative to needs → significantly low body weight, intense fear of gaining weight/ becoming fat, disturbance in body weight/shape perception. Two subtypes: restricting type and binge-eating/purging type
- **Bulimia Nervosa**: Recurrent binge eating (large amount in discreet time + sense of loss of control) followed by compensatory behaviors (vomiting, laxatives, fasting, excessive exercise). Both episodes ≥1x/week for 3 months. Self-evaluation unduly influenced by shape/weight
- **Binge-Eating Disorder (BED)**: Recurrent binge eating without compensatory behaviors. Marked distress about bingeing. ≥1x/week for 3 months. No purging
- **OSFED (Other Specified)**: Atypical anorexia, purging disorder, night eating syndrome — clinically significant but don't meet full criteria
- **Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder (ARFID)**: Avoidance based on sensory sensitivity, fear of negative consequences (choking, vomiting), or low interest in eating — no body image disturbance

## Warning Signs
- **Behavioral**: Preoccupation with food/calories/dieting, eating in secret, rigid food rules (good/bad foods), ritualistic eating, frequent weigh-ins, excessive exercise, wearing baggy clothes to hide body
- **Physical**: Significant weight changes, dizziness/fainting, cold intolerance, lanugo (fine hair on body), dental erosion (vomiting), calloused knuckles (Russell's sign from inducing vomiting), hair loss, brittle nails, amenorrhea (lost period), electrolyte imbalances
- **Psychological**: Intense fear of weight gain, distorted body image ("I feel fat"), guilt/shame after eating, mood swings, irritability, social withdrawal

## Medical Complications
- **Anorexia**: Bradycardia (slow heart rate), hypotension, orthostatic HR changes, osteoporosis, anemia, renal failure, refeeding syndrome (dangerous electrolyte shifts when restarting nutrition)
- **Bulimia**: Electrolyte imbalances (hypokalemia from vomiting — can cause cardiac arrest), esophageal tears (Mallory-Weiss), gastric rupture, dental enamel erosion, parotid gland swelling, calloused knuckles from induced vomiting
- **BED**: Obesity-related complications (T2 diabetes, hypertension, sleep apnea), metabolic syndrome, increased mortality risk

## Treatment
- **Medical stabilization**: Weight restoration for anorexia (usually inpatient if BMI <15), electrolyte monitoring, refeeding protocol (slow calories to prevent refeeding syndrome)
- **Psychotherapy**: 
  - FBT (Family-Based Treatment) for adolescents / anorexia — parents refeed, child regains autonomy
  - CBT-E (Enhanced CBT) for bulimia/BED — address overvaluation of weight/shape, reduce restraint
  - DBT for emotional eating — distress tolerance, emotion regulation
- **Medication**: Fluoxetine (Prozac) FDA-approved for bulimia; SSRIs for BED/comorbid depression/anxiety; Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) FDA-approved for moderate-to-severe BED
- **Levels of care**: Inpatient (medical instability) → Residential (24hr eating support) → Partial Hospitalization (PHP, 6-10hr/day) → Intensive Outpatient (IOP, 3hr/day 3x/week) → Outpatient

## Recovery
- **Relapse is common**: 20-40% relapse rate — not failure, part of recovery process
- **Full recovery possible**: 50-60% recover completely, 30% improve significantly, 10% remain chronic
- **Key factors**: Early intervention (best prognosis), family support, addressing co-morbidities (depression, anxiety, trauma)
- **Weight restoration ≠ cure**: Psychological recovery (body acceptance, normalized eating) takes much longer
- **Body image work**: Mirror exposure (describe objectively without judgment), body functionality focus (what body does), clothing flexibility (wear what fits, not target size)

## Supporting a Loved One
- **Use "I" statements**: "I'm worried about your health" not "You're too thin / eating too much"
- **Avoid commenting on appearance**: "You look healthy" can mean "You look fat" to someone with ED
- **Don't police food**: Avoid monitoring / controlling food intake unless part of treatment plan
- **Encourage professional help**: Gently suggest ED-specialized therapist (not general therapist)
- **Be patient**: Recovery is non-linear — good days and bad days
- **Separate person from disorder**: "The eating disorder is telling you that... but I see..."

## Prevention
- **Media literacy**: Critically evaluate body ideals in media — "thin ideal" internalization is risk factor
- **Body neutrality**: Focus on body function over body appearance — "My body carries me through life"
- **No diet talk**: Avoid commenting on own or others' food/weight around children — kids absorb parent attitudes
- **Family meals**: Eating together, varied foods, no food shaming — protective factor for adolescents
- **Sport/art settings**: Weight-restrictive sports (gymnastics, wrestling, ballet) and appearance-focused arts have higher ED rates — monitor eating attitudes
