# Pharmacology & Medication Knowledge

## Drug Categories

### Pharmacokinetics (ADME)
- **Absorption**: oral (GI tract → liver → systemic — first-pass metabolism reduces bioavailability). Sublingual (bypasses liver — nitroglycerin). Intravenous (100% bioavailability, immediate). Transdermal (slow, steady — patches). Inhalation (rapid lung absorption — albuterol). Subcutaneous (slow, insulin). Intramuscular (moderate, vaccines)
- **Distribution**: protein binding (albumin — only free drug is active). Volume of distribution (Vd): how extensively drug leaves bloodstream. Large Vd = drug concentrated in tissues (SSRIs Vd ~20L/kg). Small Vd = stays in blood (heparin Vd ~0.06L/kg). Blood-brain barrier: lipid-soluble (alcohol, caffeine, antidepressants, opioids) pass; water-soluble (many antibiotics) don't
- **Metabolism**: liver (CYP450 enzyme system — CYP3A4 metabolizes 50%+ of drugs. CYP2D6 for many antidepressants/opioids). Genetic variants: slow vs rapid metabolizers. Cytochrome P450: inhibited by grapefruit juice (CYP3A4 — can cause dangerous levels of many drugs). Induced by alcohol (chronic), St. John's Wort, certain anticonvulsants. Examples of drug interactions: fluoxetine (Prozac) inhibits CYP2D6 → codeine not converted to morphine (less effective). Many antidepressant/antipsychotic interactions involve CYP enzyme inhibition/induction between SSRIs and TCAs (significant)
- **Elimination**: kidneys (glomerular filtration + tubular secretion). Half-life: time for drug concentration to fall 50%. Usually 4-5 half-lives to reach steady state. Creatinine clearance (CrCl) estimates kidney function — adjust drug doses for renal impairment. Renal clearance = (urine concentration × urine flow) / plasma concentration

### Drug Interactions
- **Category**: pharmacokinetic (affects ADME — grapefruit + statins: CYP3A4 inhibition → higher statin levels, risk of myopathy). Pharmacodynamic (additive/synergistic effects — alcohol + benzodiazepines → respiratory depression). Combined effects: NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) + anticoagulants (warfarin, apixaban) = increased bleeding risk. ACE inhibitors + potassium supplements = hyperkalemia (high K+ dangerous, cardiac arrest risk)
- **Polypharmacy**: 5+ medications → increased risk of interactions, falls, confusion, adverse events. Common in elderly. "START/STOPP" criteria: Screening Tool of Older Persons' Prescriptions — guide to potentially inappropriate meds. STOPP: avoid benzodiazepines in elderly (falls, memory issues). Beer's Criteria (AGS list of potentially inappropriate medications for older adults): avoid anticholinergics (amitriptyline, diphenhydramine, dicyclomine) due to confusion/falls. Long-term PPI >8 weeks without clear indication. NSAIDs in elderly with CKD
- **Medication reconciliation**: compare what patient IS taking vs what they SHOULD be taking. At every hospital admission + discharge. Common discrepancies: wrong dose, missing meds, duplicate therapy. Preventable harm: 1 in 5 hospital discharges have a medication discrepancy

### Common Medications (by System)

#### Cardiovascular
- **ACE inhibitors** (lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril): lower BP, heart failure, post-MI, nephroprotection in diabetes. Side effects: dry cough (20%, from bradykinin accumulation — try ARB instead if persistent), angioedema (<1%, life-threatening: face + throat swelling, ER. Black patients have higher risk of angioedema). Monitor K+ + Cr. Avoid in pregnancy (fetal renal damage)
- **ARBs** (losartan, valsartan, olmesartan): similar to ACE but NO cough. Preferred if ACE intolerant. Also monitor K+ + Cr. Side effects: dizziness, fatigue
- **Beta blockers** (metoprolol, atenolol, carvedilol): hypertension, angina, heart failure, post-MI, migraine, performance anxiety. Contraindicated: asthma (can trigger bronchospasm), bradycardia, heart block. Selective β1 (metoprolol) vs non-selective (propranolol — blocks β2 in lungs = asthma risk). Fatigue, cold hands, decreased exercise tolerance, nightmares
- **CCBs**: dihydropyridine (amlodipine, nifedipine: vasodilators = edema, headache). Non-dihydropyridine (verapamil, diltiazem: AV node blockers, constipation, bradycardia — DON'T combine with beta blockers due to heart block + asystole risk)
- **Statins** (atorvastatin, rosuvastatin, simvastatin): LDL reduction. Side: muscle pain (myalgia 10-20% — trial NNT shows higher incidence), rarely myositis (CK elevation >10× normal) + rhabdomyolysis (dark urine, kidney failure) 0.01%. Liver monitoring: ALT needed? Controversial — only for symptoms now. Drug interactions: simvastatin + amiodipine = increased statin toxicity risk (limit simvastatin to 20mg). Grapefruit: atorvastatin + grapefruit (moderate interaction — can increase myopathy risk)
- **Diuretics**: thiazides (HCTZ, chlorthalidone: BP, hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hyperglycemia, gout). Furosemide (loop diuretic: heart failure, fluid overload, electrolyte depletion). Spironolactone (K-sparing: heart failure, edema, hirsutism (women PCOS), gynecomastia in men)

#### Diabetes
- **Metformin**: first-line type 2. Reduces hepatic glucose output. GI side effects (nausea, diarrhea — 20%, start low + go slow. Use extended release). Lactic acidosis risk: rarely in kidney disease/ heart failure (stop if CrCl <30). Must hold before + after IV contrast (48 hrs)
- **GLP-1 agonists** ( Ozempic/semaglutide, Mounjaro/tirzepatide): for T2D + weight loss. Significant weight loss (5-15% body weight). Weekly injection. GI side effects (nausea, vomiting — titrate up slowly). Pancreatitis risk (rare). Good CV outcomes. Expensive ($800-1,200/month). GLP-1 also now approved for weight loss (Wegovy, Zepbound — higher doses, same active ingredients). GLP-1 shortages: due to weight loss demand
- **SGLT2 inhibitors** (dapagliflozin/Farxiga, empagliflozin/Jardiance): for T2D + heart failure + CKD. Reduce CV death, HF hospitalization, kidney progression. Side: UTIs, genital yeast infections. Rare: ketoacidosis (DKA) with normal blood glucose (euglycemic DKA). "SGLT2i: diabetes drugs with incredible cardio-renal benefits"
- **Insulin**: rapid-acting (lispro, aspart, glulisine — onset 5-15 min, peak 30-90 min, duration 3-5 hrs). Short acting (regular — onset 30 min, peak 2-3 hr, duration 6-8 hr). Intermediate (NPH — onset 1-2 hr, peak 4-8 hr, duration 12-16 hr). Long-acting (glargine/Lantus, detemir/Levemir — onset 2-4 hr, no pronounced peak, duration 20-24 hr). Ultra-long (degludec/Tresiba — >42 hr). Concentrations: U-100 standard (100 units/mL), U-200 (concentrated, 200 units/mL), U-300 (Toujeo — lasts >24 hours)

#### Pain & Inflammation
- **NSAIDs**: ibuprofen (Advil, 200-800mg q6-8h, OTC max 1200mg/day). Naproxen (Aleve, 220-550mg q12h, OTC max 660mg/day). Diclofenac (Voltaren topical gel, oral 50mg q8h). Celecoxib (Celebrex, COX-2 selective, less GI ulcers. Monitor: ST-elevation myocardial infarction? Actually COX-2 spares COX-1 (GI protection) but increases thrombotic risk (MI, stroke) likely same as all NSAIDs). Side effects: GI bleeding (ulcers + perforation), kidney damage (Na retention, AKI, interstitial nephritis), CV events (MI, stroke — especially COX-2 + high dose). Limit OTC use to <10 days for pain. Take with food. NEVER combine aspirin + NSAID + blood thinner without doctor (bleeding risk)
- **Acetaminophen** (Tylenol, paracetamol): analgesic + antipyretic. No anti-inflammatory. Safe for stomach (doesn't cause ulcers). Liver toxic: max 4,000 mg/day (US), 3,000 mg/day (EU), 2,000 mg/day recommended for >65+ or with liver disease. Antidote: N-acetylcysteine. Overdose >7g = liver failure within 24-72 hrs
- **Opioids**: morphine (gold standard, immediate + extended release). Oxycodone (Percocet: oxycodone + acetaminophen). Hydromorphone (Dilaudid, 5-7x potency of morphine). Fentanyl (50-100x morphine, patches: indicated for opioid-tolerant chronic pain, NOT acute pain. Patches: 12.5-100 mcg/hr, 72 hr wear. Do NOT patch with heat source (sauna, heating pad): fentanyl releases too fast). Side: constipation (mandatory stool softener + stimulant laxative — all chronic users need laxatives). Respiratory depression (cause of death in overdose). Naloxone (Narcan) reverses OD for all opioids: 2-4 mg nasal spray, 0.4 mg IV/IM, may need repeat after 2-3 min (fentanyl may require multiple doses). Addiction risk: high, especially with IV/rapid onset use. Opioid epidemic: 70,000 opioid deaths in US 2020 (mostly fentanyl — from illicit supply, not prescriptions). Prescribing: limit to 3-5 days for acute pain (dental, post-op, fractures)

#### Psychiatric
- **SSRIs** (see mental health file). Fluoxetine (Prozac): activating, long half-life (days = mild withdrawal). Sertraline (Zoloft): well tolerated, good for depression + anxiety + OCD + panic. Paroxetine (Paxil): sedating, worst withdrawal, weight gain, sexual side effects most prominent. Escitalopram (Lexapro): best tolerated, few drug interactions, very commonly prescribed
