# Neurodivergence: ADHD & Autism

## What is Neurodivergence
- **Neurodiversity paradigm**: brain differences are natural variation, not disorders to be cured — autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia, Tourette's, etc. are normal human variations
  - "Nothing about us without us" — neurodivergent-led advocacy. Social model of disability: people are disabled not by their neurotype but by society not accommodating it
- **Not a defect**: different wiring, not broken. Comes with strengths AND challenges. About 15-20% of population is neurodivergent
- **Comorbidity**: ADHD + autism co-occur in 30-80% of cases (depending on study). Many also have anxiety, depression, OCD, dyspraxia, dyslexia — this is the rule, not the exception

## ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder)

### Core Traits
- **Inattention**: difficulty sustaining focus, easily distracted, forgetful, loses things, doesn't seem to listen, difficulty organizing, avoids sustained mental effort — BUT can hyperfocus (intense focus on interesting tasks for hours, especially high-interest/low-demand. Cannot be directed — hyperfocus on work vs video games) on interests
  - Executive dysfunction: not "can't pay attention" — can't regulate attention. Brain has a dimmer switch, not on/off
- **Hyperactivity**: fidgeting, restlessness, can't stay seated, feels "driven by a motor" — may appear as internal restlessness in adults (not outward running around). Many adults outgrow obvious hyperactivity: manifests as always busy, trouble relaxing
- **Impulsivity**: blurting out, interrupting, making decisions without thinking, impatience
- **Time blindness**: "now" vs "not now" — 5 minutes feels like an eternity, 5 hours feels like nothing. Past and future feel distant — ADHD brain lives in "now or not now." Not lazy — literally perceives time differently

### Common Experiences (often missed by neurotypical people)
- **Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD)**: extreme emotional reaction to real or perceived rejection, criticism, or failure — feels physical pain. Can look like rage, crying, or immediate withdrawal. Not a formal diagnosis but widely recognized in ADHD community. Triggers: faces: being told you did something wrong, constructive criticism, feeling left out. Emotional intensity is WAY out of proportion with trigger — and you KNOW it but can't stop it
- **Task paralysis**: can't start a task even though you desperately want to — feels like being frozen. Common with boring/overwhelming tasks (cleaning, paperwork, bills). Nanotask technique: do the TINIEST step (open the document, pick up one sock)
- **Working memory problems**: walk into room and forget why. Can't hold multiple instructions. Losing train of thought mid-sentence. Need to write everything down immediately or it's gone
- **Emotional dysregulation**: emotions are intense, quick to anger/cry/excitement. Mood swings within hours. Emotional hangover: feeling drained for hours after an argument
- **Hyperfocus**: intense concentration (hours) on interesting/stimulating tasks — at the expense of everything else (forgetting to eat, sleep, pee). Can't be directed — only happens on high-interest tasks, not things you "should" do

### ADHD Management
- **Medication**: stimulants (methylphenidate/Ritalin/Concerta, amphetamine/Adderall/Vyvanse) — first-line treatment, 70-80% response rate, short-acting (4 hr) or extended-release (10-12 hr). Non-stimulants: atomoxetine/Strattera (SNRI, takes 4-6 weeks), guanfacine (blood pressure med that also helps impulse control and emotional regulation, alpha-2 agonist)
  - Stimulants work by increasing dopamine/norepinephrine in prefrontal cortex — help with focus, impulse control, motivation. Side effects: appetite suppression, insomnia, anxiety, tics. NOT addictive at therapeutic doses (but abuse potential exists — schedule II controlled substance)
- **Strategies**: body doubling (having someone present while you work — doesn't need to help, just presence. Focusmate.com has free body doubling sessions). Timers (Pomodoro 25/5 — short enough to accept). Lists (write everything down immediately — brain dump. Use app or notebook for "second brain". Don't trust your memory). Externalize everything (keys: always same hook. Wallet, phone, sunglasses = same pocket). Alarms and timers for everything (get ready, appointments, cooking). "Just start" doesn't work — use 5-minute rule (do for 5 min, permission to stop — often continues)
- **Executive dysfunction hacks**: break tasks into absurdly small steps (task: taxes → step 1: find last year's tax return. step 2: open tax software. step 3: find W-2). Remove friction (keep meds + water by bed, lay out clothes night before). Add friction to bad habits (put apps behind extra taps, keep junk food in hard-to-reach cupboard). Body doubling (Focusmate or friend). The "just do 5 minutes" rule

## Autism Spectrum

### Core Traits
- **Social communication differences**: difficulty with back-and-forth conversation, reading social cues (tone, body language, sarcasm — some autistic people interpret literally), eye contact (uncomfortable or forced), making friends, understanding unwritten social rules
  - Theory of mind: understanding others' thoughts/intentions — difficult for many, but many high-masking autistics develop cognitive empathy through learned rules (doesn't feel intuitive)
- **Restricted/repetitive behaviors (stimming)**: hand-flapping, rocking, spinning, humming, repeating words/phrases (echolalia), lining up objects. Purpose: self-regulation (calming, focusing, processing overload). NOT bad/needs stopping unless harmful
  - Stimming is adaptive — helps regulate sensory input, express emotion, maintain control. Pathologizing stimming is harmful
- **Sensory sensitivities**: hyper (oversensitive — overwhelmed by sounds/lights/textures/smells/tastes) or hypo (undersensitive — seeks intense input, high pain tolerance, doesn't notice hunger/injury). Many individuals are hypersensitive in some senses + hyposensitive in others
  - Common: sound sensitivity (vacuum, blender, sirens, leaf blowers, loud restaurants — can't filter background noise). Light sensitivity (fluorescent hum, flicker). Tactile (tags in clothes, certain fabrics, food textures). Taste (picky eating common — safe foods are foods with predictable texture/taste). Smell sensitivity (strong smells may trigger nausea)
- **Need for routine & predictability**: distress over unexpected changes, need to know what's happening, difficulty with transitions. Autistic inertia: difficulty starting tasks AND difficulty stopping tasks — like mental train with no brakes. Changing tasks is genuinely difficult

### Masking (Camouflaging)
- Copying neurotypical behaviors to fit in: forcing eye contact, rehearsing conversations, suppressing stims, imitating social behavior — comes at a huge cost (exhaustion, burnout, identity loss)
  - "I feel like I'm performing being human" — common autistic experience. Masking allows passing as neurotypical but leads to autistic burnout
- **Autistic burnout**: prolonged exhaustion, loss of skills, increased sensory sensitivity, reduced ability to mask, depression, suicidal ideation — from long-term masking + sensory overload. Recovery takes months to years of reduced demands + unmasking
  - Triggers: major life changes, trauma, prolonged masking, chronic overwhelm. Not the same as depression — caused by cumulative overload

### Autism Management (not "treatment")
- **Accommodations ≠ cure**: autism is lifelong. Support focuses on reducing overwhelm + building on strengths, NOT making someone act neurotypical
- **Sensory tools**: noise-canceling headphones (over-ear > in-ear), fidget toys (spinner, cube, tangle, chew necklace), sunglasses (even indoors for light sensitivity), weighted blankets (deep pressure = calming), stim toys, dimmable lighting at home
- **Predictability**: visual schedules, advance notice of changes, routine, preparation for transitions (countdown: "10 minutes until we leave"), social stories for new experiences
- **Social scripting**: practice common conversations, develop scripts for social situations. Can learn social rules intellectually (not intuitively) — like learning a foreign language
- **Special interests**: intense, focused interests — source of joy, expertise, and regulation. NOT restricted/repetitive pathology — can lead to career, skill, community. Use interests for motivation + connection
- **Self-advocacy**: asking for accommodations, setting boundaries, communicating needs. For kids: teach self-advocacy early. For adults: disclose when safe + needed (workplace accommodations, social situations)

## Late Diagnosis
- **ADHD**: many adults diagnosed later in life (especially women, people of color, high achievers, inattentive type). Life-long (diagnosed in childhood = hyperactive boys first, now recognizing inattentive and combined presentations, more women and men inattentive diagnosed as adults). Signs in childhood: daydreaming, messy room, lost things, procrastination, felt "lazy but smart." Untreated ADHD: higher rates of accidents, divorce, unemployment, substance use, anxiety, depression
- **Autism**: increasing adult diagnoses (levels 1/"Asperger's") especially women (better at masking, social camouflage, lower social expectations for women. Often misdiagnosed as anxiety, depression, BPD before autism). Many diagnosed after their child is diagnosed (parent recognizes self in child's traits)

## When to Seek Diagnosis
- **ADHD**: if symptoms cause significant impairment in 2+ settings (work, home, social, school). Diagnosis: clinical interview + rating scales + collateral info from family (no blood test or brain scan). Multimodal (medication + therapy + coaching) most effective
  - If you wonder "do I have ADHD?": take validated self-screen (ASRS v1.1) and discuss with professional. "If you think you have ADHD, you might" — many people recognize symptoms late (especially after finding information)
- **Autism**: if social communication + sensory issues significantly impact quality of life. Assessment: ADOS-2 (gold standard, observation), ADI-R (interview), clinical history, sensory profile
  - Self-diagnosis: valid (formal diagnosis expensive + inaccessible for many + may be unwanted by others. Autistic community widely accepts self-diagnosis as legitimate path, especially for women, LGBTQ+, people of color)
