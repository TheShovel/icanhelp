# Sleep Science & Disorders

## Sleep Stages (90 min cycles)
- **NREM 1**: light sleep, easy to wake, drifting in/out (5%)
- **NREM 2**: deeper, heart rate slows, body temperature drops (45-55%)
- **NREM 3**: deep sleep / slow wave — hardest to wake, restorative, growth hormone released, immune system repairs (15-25%)
- **REM**: rapid eye movement, vivid dreaming, brain active, memory consolidation (20-25%)
- You cycle through all stages 4-6 times per night — early cycles have more deep sleep, later cycles more REM
- Deep sleep is when physical recovery happens. REM is when emotional/memory processing happens

## How Much Sleep
- Adults (18-64): 7-9 hours
- Older adults (65+): 7-8 hours
- Teenagers (14-17): 8-10 hours
- Children (6-13): 9-11 hours
- Less than 6 hours consistently = increased risk of: heart disease, diabetes, obesity, depression, dementia, early death
- More than 9 hours consistently (adults) may also indicate health issues
- Sleep debt accumulates — one weekend of sleeping in doesn't fully recover a week of sleep deprivation
- Quality matters as much as quantity: uninterrupted sleep > fragmented sleep

## Sleep Hygiene
- **Consistent schedule**: same bedtime + wake time 7 days/week (even weekends) — most important factor
- **Morning light**: sunlight within 30 min of waking sets circadian clock — go outside or sit by bright window
- **Dark bedroom**: blackout curtains, cover LEDs (alarm clock, router, charger lights), wear eye mask
- **Cool room**: 65-68°F (18-20°C) — body temperature drops during sleep, cool room helps
- **Quiet**: earplugs, white noise machine/fan/ app — consistent background noise masks disruptive sounds
- **No screens**: 30-60 min before bed — blue light suppresses melatonin (use night mode, dim screens, or read a book instead)
- **No caffeine after 2 PM**: half-life of caffeine is 5-6 hours — even afternoon coffee affects sleep
- **Alcohol**: helps you fall asleep (sedative) but fragments second half of night (less REM, more waking)
- **Exercise**: daily exercise improves sleep — but try to finish 2-3 hours before bed (intense exercise too close to bed can disrupt)
- **Food**: heavy/spicy meals before bed = indigestion, heartburn — stop eating 2-3 hours before bed
- **Wind-down routine**: 30 min before bed, same relaxing activities (read, stretch, warm bath, meditation)

## Common Sleep Disorders
- **Insomnia**: difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or early waking — 3+ nights/week for 3+ months
  - CBT-I (cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia) is first-line treatment — more effective than medication long-term
  - Sleep restriction: limit time in bed to actual sleep time, gradually increase as efficiency improves
  - Stimulus control: only go to bed when sleepy, get out of bed if awake >20 min (don't lie there frustrated)
- **Sleep apnea**: breathing stops repeatedly during sleep (snoring, gasping, choking, daytime fatigue)
  - Obstructive (most common): throat muscles relax and block airway
  - Central: brain doesn't signal breathing. Complex: both
  - Diagnosis: sleep study (at home or in lab). Treatment: CPAP machine (gold standard), oral appliance, weight loss
  - Untreated: high blood pressure, heart disease, stroke, diabetes, early death
  - Signs: loud snoring, gasping/choking sounds, waking with dry mouth/headache, daytime sleepiness
- **Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)**: irresistible urge to move legs (worse at rest, evening, relieved by movement)
  - Can be primary (genetic) or secondary (iron deficiency, pregnancy, kidney disease)
  - Treatment: iron supplements (if deficient), dopamine agonists (Ropinirole), gabapentin, lifestyle (reduce caffeine, moderate exercise)
- **Narcolepsy**: excessive daytime sleepiness, sudden sleep attacks, cataplexy (sudden muscle weakness triggered by emotion)
  - Usually starts in teens/20s. Treatment: stimulants (modafinil), sodium oxybate, antidepressants for cataplexy
- **Circadian rhythm disorders**: delayed sleep phase (natural night owl, can't fall asleep until 2-4 AM), advanced (early bird), shift work disorder
  - Treatment: timed light exposure, melatonin timing, strict schedule

## Melatonin
- Natural hormone produced by pineal gland in response to darkness — signals "time to sleep"
- As a supplement: 0.3-5 mg, 30-60 min before bedtime
  - LOWER doses (0.3-1 mg) may be more effective than high doses — closer to natural levels
  - Higher doses (5-10 mg) can cause vivid dreams, grogginess next day, hormonal disruption
- Melatonin is NOT a sleep drug — it sets circadian timing, doesn't force sleep
- Best for: jet lag, shift work, delayed sleep phase. NOT the main solution for general insomnia
- Long-term safety data is limited — use short-term only unless directed by doctor
- Melatonin is a hormone — it can affect puberty timing, reproductive hormones. Consult pediatrician before giving to children

## Shift Work & Jet Lag
- **Shift work**: rotating shifts disrupts circadian rhythm, increases health risks (heart disease, cancer, accidents)
  - Strategies: fixed schedule > rotating, forward rotation > backward (days→evenings→nights), blackout curtains + white noise for daytime sleep, strategic caffeine (at start of shift, not last 4 hours), bright light at work, dim light commute home
- **Jet lag**: eastbound (harder) = advance sleep schedule, westbound = delay
  - Pre-trip: gradually shift schedule 30 min/day toward destination
  - During flight: set watch to destination time, sleep according to destination
  - On arrival: morning blue light exposure (reset clock eastbound), avoid long naps
  - Melatonin at destination bedtime (not before)

## Sleep & Technology
- Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production — use night mode/blue light filter after sunset
- Smartphones in bedroom: notification buzz fragments sleep, even if you don't fully wake
- Tracking: fitness trackers/smartwatches estimate sleep stages (not clinically accurate but useful for patterns)
- Sleep apps: white noise, guided sleep meditation, sleep stories (Calm, Headspace)
- Weighted blankets: 10-15% body weight, deep pressure stimulation → reduces cortisol, increases serotonin
- CPAP machines for sleep apnea — lifetime compliance needed (good masks = comfortable, modern machines = quiet)

## When to See a Sleep Specialist
- Loud snoring + gasping/choking (possible sleep apnea)
- Falling asleep during activities (driving, meetings, conversations)
- Taking >30 min to fall asleep regularly despite good sleep hygiene
- Waking up early and can't get back to sleep
- Leg discomfort at night that prevents sleep
- Needing caffeine to stay awake during the day most days
- Unrefreshing sleep despite adequate hours
