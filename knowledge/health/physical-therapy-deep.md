# Physical Therapy Deep Dive

Rehabilitation principles, common conditions, and therapeutic exercises.

## Assessment
- **Subjective**: History of present condition, mechanism of injury, pain characteristics (location, quality, intensity, timing, aggravating/ easing factors), past medical history, medications, imaging, functional limitations, goals
- **Objective**: Observation (posture, gait, swelling, asymmetry), Palpation (tenderness, warmth, muscle tone), Range of motion (active vs passive — goniometer measurement), Strength testing (manual muscle testing 0-5 scale), Special tests (provocative maneuvers for specific conditions), Neurological (sensation, reflexes, motor control), Functional tests (sit-to-stand, step-up, balance)
- **Outcome measures**: Numeric Pain Rating Scale (0-10), Oswestry Disability Index (back pain), DASH (shoulder/arm/hand), Lower Extremity Functional Scale, Berg Balance Scale, Timed Up and Go

## Pain Science
- **Acute pain**: Protective — signals actual or potential tissue damage. Usually resolves with healing (days-weeks). Rest initially then graded movement
- **Chronic pain**: Pain persists beyond normal healing (>3 months). Nervous system changes — central sensitization (nerves become hyper-responsive). Pain ≠ tissue damage. Treat with: exercise (graded exposure), pain neuroscience education, psychological support
- **Red flags**: Signs of serious pathology — unexplained weight loss, fever/night sweats, night pain, bowel/bladder changes, saddle anesthesia, bilateral leg numbness/weakness, history of cancer, IV drug use, structural deformity. Refer to physician
- **Yellow flags**: Psychosocial risk factors for chronicity — fear of movement (kinesiophobia), catastrophizing, poor coping, job dissatisfaction, depression, passive coping strategies. Address alongside physical treatment

## Common Conditions & Treatment
- **Low back pain**: Most common MSK condition. 80% of people experience. Specific vs non-specific (90% non-specific — no identifiable pathology). Treatment: mobilization, core stabilization, directional preference exercises (McKenzie), general strengthening, staying active. Red flags: cauda equina (emergency), fracture, cancer, infection
- **Rotator cuff pathology**: Tendinopathy (overuse, often in overhead athletes/workers), partial/full tears (age-related degenerative > acute traumatic). Treatment: rotator cuff strengthening (eccentric exercises), scapular stabilization, activity modification. Full tear in young/active → surgical repair
- **ACL reconstruction rehab**: Prehab (strengthening before surgery improves outcomes). Phases: 0-2wk (control pain/swelling, quad activation, extension), 2-6wk (full ROM, closed-chain strengthening), 6-12wk (open-chain exercises, balance), 12-24wk (return to running, plyometrics), 6-12mo (sport-specific training, return to sport test — hop tests, isokinetic testing). Crucial: regain full extension ASAP
- **Knee osteoarthritis**: Degenerative joint disease. Treatment: quad strengthening (vastus medialis specific), low-impact exercise (bike, swimming, elliptical), weight management (1lb weight loss = 4lb reduction in knee load), bracing/ taping, pain management. Joint replacement for end-stage
- **Plantar fasciitis**: Heel pain, sharp with first steps in morning, improves then worsens with prolonged standing. Treatment: calf stretching (gastrocnemius/soleus), plantar fascia-specific stretch (pull toes back), night splint, proper footwear (good arch support), Graston/needling, NSAIDs. Usually resolves in 6-12 months. Rarely surgery
- **Cervicogenic headache**: Headache originating from cervical spine (C1-C3). Treatment: upper cervical mobilization, suboccipital release, deep neck flexor strengthening, postural correction. Rule out: migraine, tension headache, cluster headache

## Therapeutic Exercise Principles
- **SAID principle**: Specific Adaptation to Imposed Demands — body adapts specifically to how it's trained. Running improves running, swimming improves swimming. Train the movement you need
- **Progressive overload**: Gradually increase load, volume, or difficulty to continue gains. 10% rule: increase no more than 10% weekly. Plateau → change variable
- **Graded exposure**: For chronic pain — start at tolerable level (does not flare pain), gradually increase. Pain ≠ damage. Pace: work to tolerance, stop BEFORE pain spikes
- **Open vs closed chain**: Open chain (hand/foot free — leg extension machine, bicep curl) — more isolated, shear forces on joints. Closed chain (hand/foot fixed — squat, push-up) — more functional, compressive forces stabilize joints
- **Eccentric vs concentric**: Eccentric (lengthening under tension — lowering weight) produces more force, stimulates tendon healing. Important for tendinopathy (eccentric heel drops for achilles tendinopathy)

## Manual Therapy Techniques
- **Joint mobilization**: Low-velocity passive movement within joint range. Grades I-II (pain relief), III-IV (stretch tight structures). Maitland, Kaltenborn, Mulligan
- **Soft tissue mobilization**: Massage, myofascial release, instrument-assisted (Graston, IASTM). Release tension, reduce adhesions, stimulate healing
- **Dry needling**: Thin needles into trigger points (taut bands in muscle). Not acupuncture — western medical approach. Releases knots, reduces pain. Overactive muscle: inhibit with dry needling. Evidence moderate
- **Neural mobilization**: Sliding/gliding nerve through surrounding tissue. For radicular pain, nerve tension signs (SLR, slump test). Median nerve (carpal tunnel), Sciatic nerve (sciatica)

## Modalities
- **Ice/cryotherapy**: Acute injury first 48-72hr — reduces swelling, pain relief, limits tissue damage. 20min on, 40min off. Not for chronic stiffness (slows healing if overused)
- **Heat/thermotherapy**: Chronic pain, muscle tightness, joint stiffness. Increases blood flow, relaxes muscle spasm. 20min. Contraindicated: acute injury, inflammation, impaired sensation
- **TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation)**: Electrical stimulation for pain relief. High frequency (90-130Hz, low intensity) = gate control theory. Low frequency (2-4Hz, high intensity) = opioid release
- **Ultrasound**: Deep heating (1-3 MHz). Controversial evidence for most conditions. No proven benefit over placebo for most MSK conditions — largely replaced in evidence-based practice
- **Iontophoresis**: Electrical current drives medication (usually corticosteroid or NSAID) through skin. For localized inflammation (plantar fasciitis, tennis elbow)

## Return to Sport Criteria
- **No pain during or after activity**
- **Full range of motion** compared to uninvolved side
- **Strength**: 90%+ of uninvolved side (isokinetic testing)
- **Functional tests**: Hop tests (single leg hop for distance, triple hop, cross-over hop) — 90%+ limb symmetry
- **Sport-specific movements**: Cutting, jumping, pivoting without pain/hesitation
- **Graduated return**: No restrictions → light practice → full practice → competition. Progress only if no pain/swelling after previous step
