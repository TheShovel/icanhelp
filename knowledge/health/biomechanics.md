# Biomechanics & Human Movement

## Skeletal System

### Joint Types
- **Ball and socket**: multi-axial (flexion/extension + abduction/adduction + rotation). Shoulder (glenohumeral — most mobile joint, trade-off stability for range of motion. Shallow socket = easy dislocation). Hip (acetabulofemoral — deeper socket, more stable, less range than shoulder). Hip dislocation in car crash: dashboard to knee in seated position
- **Hinge**: one plane of movement. Knee (tibiofemoral — flexion/extension, slight rotation when flexed). Elbow (humeroulnar). Ankle (talocrural — dorsiflexion/plantarflexion). Patellofemoral joint at knee: kneecap tracks in groove; when it doesn't, irritation = patellofemoral pain syndrome
- **Saddle**: bi-axial with slight rotation. Thumb (carpometacarpal — opposable thumbs!). Allows opposition (thumb to touch other fingers) — key human dexterity trait
- **Pivot**: rotation. Neck (atlantoaxial — "no" movement between C1+C2 — shakes head no: C1 rotates around C2 dens). Radioulnar (forearm pronation/supination — turning palm up/down. Radius rotates around ulna)
- **Gliding**: flat surfaces slide. Carpal bones (wrist). Tarsal bones (ankle). Vertebrae facet joints

### Spine
- **Vertebrae**: 7 cervical (C1-C7 — neck), 12 thoracic (T1-T12 — ribs attach), 5 lumbar (L1-L5 — lower back, largest), sacrum (5 fused), coccyx (tailbone, 3-5 fused)
  - Curves: cervical (lordosis = forward curve), thoracic (kyphosis = backward), lumbar (lordosis). Scoliosis = lateral curvature (S- or C-shaped). Kyphosis/lordosis are normal in correct proportions
- **Intervertebral discs**: cushion between vertebrae — nucleus pulposus (gel center) + annulus fibrosus (tough outer rings). Disc herniation = nucleus pushes through annulus → presses on nerve → radicular pain (sciatica). Highest risk: L4-L5, L5-S1 (most weight + movement). Age-related disc degeneration = loss of water content, narrowing
- **Posture**: ideal = ears over shoulders over hips over knees over ankles (plumb line). Forward head posture ("text neck") = increases effective head weight (10-12 lb head → 60 lb load at 60° forward tilt). Rounded shoulders = weak rhomboids + tight pecs

### Gait Cycle
- **Stance phase** (60% of gait): heel strike → foot flat → mid-stance → heel off → toe-off. Weight acceptance, single-leg support, propulsion
  - Heel strike: heel contacts ground, ankle dorsiflexion (foot lifts up). Foot flat: absorbs shock, mid-stance: leg supports body weight directly over foot. Heel off: plantarflexion starts (calf muscle/Achilles begins). Toe off: foot leaves ground, propulsion fully underway
- **Swing phase** (40%): toe-off → mid-swing → heel strike. Foot clears ground, leg swings forward
- **Muscles**: glutes (hip extension during stance), quads (knee stability at heel strike), hamstrings (decelerate leg at end of swing), calf (push-off), tibialis anterior (foot clearance during swing)
  - Running gait: flight phase (both feet off ground). Cadence (steps/min) ~180 average; elite runners near 180-200. Overstride = heel striking in front of body = braking force + injury

## Muscle Physiology

### Types of Contractions
- **Isometric**: muscle tension, no length change. Planks, wall sits, holding a weight steady. Builds strength at specific joint angle. Important for stability
- **Isotonic concentric**: muscle shortens during contraction (lifting phase of bicep curl). Force generated > external load. Moves joint
- **Isotonic eccentric**: muscle lengthens under tension (lowering phase of bicep curl). Force generated < external load. Stronger than concentric (can lower more than you can lift). Produces most muscle damage — delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) primary here
  - Eccentric exercise: highest force production, most muscle damage + growth stimulus. Good for rehab (tendinopathy). But too much leads to excessive soreness + injury risk
- **Isokinetic**: constant speed throughout range (requires special equipment — Biodex, Cybex, Humac Norm). Used for testing/rehab

### Muscle Fiber Types
- **Type I (slow-twitch/oxidative)**: endurance — marathon running, posture. Slow to fatigue. Red (myoglobin-rich, mitochondria-dense). Small motor units. Crucial for any sustained submaximal work (>20 sec)
- **Type IIa (fast-twitch oxidative-glycolytic)**: intermediate — 800m run, swimming. Moderate fatigue resistance. Pink in color. Responds well to both endurance + strength training
- **Type IIx (fast-twitch glycolytic)**: power/speed — sprinting, heavy lifting. Fast to fatigue. White (few mitochondria, much glycogen). Recruited only for high-intensity needs (heavy weight, sprint). Can convert to IIa with endurance training; IIa to IIx with heavy strength/power training

### Sliding Filament Theory
- Myosin (thick filament) + actin (thin filament) = cross-bridges → pull → sarcomere shortens. Calcium released from sarcoplasmic reticulum → binds troponin → moves tropomyosin → exposes binding site on actin → myosin attaches → power stroke → ATP detaches → reset
  - Rigor mortis: no ATP → cross-bridges stay locked → stiff muscles ~2-6 hours after death, release ~48 hours as proteolysis degrades muscle tissue

## Common Injuries

### Sprains (ligament) & Strains (muscle/tendon)
- **Grade I**: mild (stretch, microscopic tearing). Swelling, pain with movement, no instability. Recovery: 1-3 weeks
- **Grade II**: moderate (partial tear). More pain, swelling, some instability (positive laxity test), limited ROM. Recovery: 4-8 weeks
- **Grade III**: complete tear. Instability, significant swelling, unable to bear weight/use muscle. May require surgery. Recovery: 3-6 months. Often no pain after initial moment (ligament has no nerve endings)

### Common Overuse Injuries
- **Runner's knee (patellofemoral pain syndrome)**: pain behind/around kneecap (especially stairs, squatting, sitting with bent knees). Causes: weak VMO (inner quad), tight IT band/hamstrings, hip weakness. Treat: strengthen quads (straight leg raises, step downs), glutes, stretch hamstrings/calves
- **IT band syndrome**: lateral knee pain (outside). Tight iliotibial band rubs against lateral femoral condyle when flexing/extending knee. Common in runners (main cause: weak glute med/ham — IT band mobilizing). Treat: foam roll (not over knee!), hip/glute strengthening, side-lying leg lifts/clam shells. Avoid: rolling IT band over knee itself — can cause inflammation
- **Achilles tendinopathy**: pain in heel/Achilles. Degeneration (not inflammation). Mid-portion vs insertional. Eccentric heel drops (Alfredson protocol — gold standard rehab: 180 eccentric drops daily, heavy slow resistance 3x/week)
- **Shin splints (medial tibial stress syndrome)**: pain along shinbone. Early: before running — take time off. If running while it hurts: may progress to stress fracture. Causes: overtraining, weak hips, flat feet/poor arch support, improper shoes
  - Treat: rest, ice, strengthening (calf raises, toe curls, arch domes), gradual return to running. Rule out stress fracture: point tenderness vs diffuse pain

## Biomechanical Principles
- **Torque (moment)**: rotational force = force × perpendicular distance from axis. Longer lever = more torque required. Joints = fulcrums, bones = levers, muscles = effort
  - Example: holding weight out at arm's length = shoulder must generate much more force than weight due to long lever arm. Could be 5x+ body weight in rotator cuff force for small hand weight
- **Muscle moment arm**: distance from joint center to tendon insertion. Greater moment arm = more torque for given force (but muscle must shorten more). Why deltoid attaches ~shoulder — small moment arm requires large force but allows speed at hand
- **Ground reaction force (GRF)**: running: 2-3x body weight impact force; sprinting: 3-5x; hard landing: up to 7-10x. Heel strike running = rapid braking force, higher peak impact than forefoot strike (one reason minimal/barefoot advocates recommend midfoot landing). Knee loads: up to 5x BW during squat

## Proprioception & Balance
- **Proprioception**: sense of body position in space — muscle spindles (detect stretch rate), Golgi tendon organs (detect tension), joint mechanoreceptors. Without it, you can't coordinate movement with eyes closed. Also vital for fine motor control (threading a needle) and great for safe joint position sense
  - Test: stand on one leg, eyes closed — should maintain for 20-30 sec (age dependent — normal 30 sec for healthy adults under 50. Poor = fall risk). Rehabilitation after joint injury: re-train proprioception before return to sport (e.g., ankle sprain = diminished joint position sense = increased risk of re-injury)
- **Balance**: integration of visual + vestibular + somatosensory systems. Aging: declines starts ~50; contributes to fall risk. Training: tai chi, single-leg stance, standing on unstable surfaces, Bosu ball, perturbations (force platform or unexpected push)
