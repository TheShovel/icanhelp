# Asthma — Respiratory Health

## What Asthma Is

Chronic inflammatory airway disease: bronchoconstriction + mucus hypersecretion + airway wall remodeling. Reversible (contrast with COPD — fixed obstruction). Affects ~8% of US adults, ~7% children. Onset: childhood (often allergic) or adult-onset (often non-allergic, harder to control). Diagnosis: spirometry (FEV1/FVC ratio <0.70, 12%+ improvement after bronchodilator). Peak flow variability monitoring also used

## Common Triggers

- **Allergens**: dust mites (use allergen-proof covers, wash bedding hot weekly, reduce humidity <50%), pollen (check counts, keep windows closed, shower after outdoor), mold (fix leaks, dehumidifier, bleach kills surface mold but not porous — replace drywall if needed), pet dander (bathe pet weekly, HEPA filter, keep out of bedroom), cockroach droppings (common in urban environments — bait traps, professional pest control)
- **Irritants**: tobacco smoke (all forms — 1st, 2nd, 3rd hand), air pollution (PM2.5, ozone — check Air Quality Index, limit outdoor when >100), strong odors (perfume, cleaning products, paint fumes), cold dry air (covered breathing with scarf, pre-warm air), occupational (flour, wood dust, chemicals, latex)
- **Other**: exercise (EIB — exercise-induced bronchoconstriction: symptoms 5-10 min after exercise, resolves 30-60 min. Prevention: warmup 5-10 min, 2 puffs albuterol 15 min before), respiratory infections (viruses trigger 80%+ of pediatric exacerbations — annual flu vaccine, avoid sick contacts), emotional stress, GERD (stomach acid micro-aspiration contributes, especially at night), aspirin/NSAIDs in sensitive patients (AERD — aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease, often with nasal polyps + sinusitis)

## Medications

- **Rescue inhalers (short-acting bronchodilators, SABA)**: albuterol (ProAir, Ventolin, Proventil) — relax airway smooth muscle within 5 min, lasts 4-6 hrs. Levalbuterol (Xopenex) — similar, fewer cardiac side effects (controversial, more expensive). Use only as needed. Using >2x/week (or >1 canister/month) = poor control — escalate controller therapy. Side effects: tremor, tachycardia, jitteriness (transient)
- **Controller medications (daily use, anti-inflammatory)**: inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) — first-line for persistent asthma. Fluticasone (Flovent), budesonide (Pulmicort), mometasone (Asmanex), beclomethasone (Qvar). Not addictive, NOT same as anabolic steroids. Take daily even when well. Rinse mouth after each dose (prevents oral thrush). Low-dose ICS reduces exacerbations ~50%
  - Combination ICS/LABA: fluticasone/salmeterol (Advair), budesonide/formoterol (Symbicort), mometasone/formoterol (Dulera). LABA = long-acting bronchodilator. Never use LABA alone without ICS for asthma (increased mortality risk — SMART studies). Some combos now used as both controller and rescue (SMART therapy — Symbicort as needed + daily)
- **Leukotriene receptor antagonists**: montelukast (Singulair). Oral, good for allergic asthma + EIB + aspirin-sensitive. Less effective than ICS but better adherence (pill vs inhaler). Black box (2020): serious neuropsychiatric events (aggression, depression, suicidal thoughts) — monitor symptoms. Do not start for mild symptoms unless alternatives not tolerated
- **Biologics (add-on for severe persistent asthma)**: omalizumab (anti-IgE) — for allergic; mepolizumab/benralizumab (anti-IL5) — for eosinophilic; dupilumab (anti-IL4R) — for T2-high asthma. Given as injection every 2-8 weeks. Expensive ($30k+/year), but reduces exacerbations 50-70% in appropriate patients
- **Oral corticosteroids**: prednisone burst (40-60mg/day 5-7 days, taper not needed for short courses) for exacerbations. Systemic steroids NOT for daily use — side effects (osteoporosis, diabetes, weight gain, cataracts, adrenal suppression). Use >2 bursts/year = poor control, evaluate for biologic

## Inhaler Technique (Critical for Drug Delivery)

- **MDI (metered dose inhaler)**: shake 5-10 sec, exhale fully, seal lips around mouthpiece, actuate while starting slow deep breath (3-5 sec inhale), hold breath 10 sec (or as long as comfortable), exhale slowly. Wait 30-60 sec between puffs. ALWAYS use with spacer if possible — improves lung deposition 2-4x, reduces oropharyngeal deposition. For infants/toddlers: use mask + spacer. Clean spacer monthly in warm soapy water, drip dry (do NOT rinse inside). Wash mouthpiece weekly
- **DPI (dry powder inhaler — Advair Diskus, Pulmicort Flexhaler)**: rapid forceful inhalation needed (no spacer). Do NOT exhale into device (moisture clumps powder). Load device just before use (not in advance). Close after use to protect from humidity. Different devices have different loading steps — check specific instructions
- **SMI (soft mist inhaler — Spiriva Respimat)**: slow, steady breath (like MDI but slower). Uncommon for asthma (mainly COPD). No spacer needed
- **Nebulizer**: for acute severe asthma or inability to use MDI despite spacer. Machine converts liquid to mist. Takes 10-15 min. Not superior to MDI + spacer if proper technique used. Higher dose often given (2.5-5mg albuterol vs 2 puffs MDI ~180mcg)

## Peak Flow Monitoring

Use personal best (highest reading over 2 weeks when well). Zones: Green (80-100% personal best) — good control. Yellow (50-80%) — caution, may need extra medication. Red (<50%) — medical emergency. Measure standing, before meds, same time daily. Device cheap ($10-30). Best for moderate-severe asthma; mild asthma: symptom-based monitoring adequate

## Asthma Action Plan

Color-coded zones (green/yellow/red). Written with clinician. Specifies daily controller regimen, symptoms that require adjustments, when to use rescue, when to seek emergency care. Updated annually. School + workplace copies important

## Signs of an Attack vs Emergency

- **Mild-moderate attack**: mild wheezing, slight shortness of breath (can finish sentences), coughing at night/early morning, peak flow 50-80%. Action: use rescue inhaler (2-4 puffs, can repeat every 20 min 3x). If not improving after 2 treatments, go to emergency
- **Severe attack (go to ER)**: breathless at rest, cannot speak full sentences, chest tightness/tripod position (hands on knees), severe wheezing (or silent chest = no wheezing = severely limited airflow — worse sign), cyanosis (lips/fingertips blue), use of accessory muscles (neck/chest retractions), peak flow <50%, albuterol not helping. Call 911. DO NOT lie them flat (worsens airflow). Keep upright. While waiting: continue albuterol (4-6 puffs every 20 min via spacer)

## Prevention

- Get influenza and pneumococcal vaccines
- Identify and avoid triggers (allergen avoidance, smoking cessation)
- Maintain daily controller adherence (poor adherence = #1 cause of uncontrolled asthma)
- Annual spirometry to track lung function
- Home allergen control (dust mite covers, HEPA filter, humidity control)
- Exercise regularly (improves lung function + reduces exacerbations — use pre-treatment albuterol if needed)
