# Gut Health & Digestion

## The Gut Microbiome
- **What it is**: trillions of bacteria, viruses, fungi living in your digestive tract — weigh ~2-3 lbs total
  - ~500-1,000 species of bacteria, mostly in large intestine
  - You have more bacterial cells than human cells (~1.3:1 ratio)
  - Each person's microbiome is unique (like a fingerprint)
- **What it does**: digest fiber (humans can't digest fiber — BACTERIA do), produce vitamins (B12, K, biotin, folate), regulate immune system, protect against pathogens, influence mood (gut-brain axis), break down toxins and medications
- **Dysbiosis**: imbalance of gut bacteria — too many "bad" bacteria, not enough "good"
  - Linked to: IBS, IBD, allergies, autoimmune diseases, obesity, depression, anxiety, Parkinson's, autism (controversial links being researched)
  - Causes: antibiotics (broad-spectrum kill both good and bad), poor diet (low fiber, high sugar/processed), chronic stress, lack of sleep, excessive alcohol

## Gut-Brain Axis
- **Vagus nerve**: major communication highway between gut and brain — 80% of signals go from gut→brain (not brain→gut!)
  - Enteric nervous system: "second brain" — 500M+ neurons in gut, produces 90% of serotonin and 50% of dopamine
  - Gut bacteria produce neurotransmitters: GABA (calming), serotonin (mood), dopamine (reward), acetylcholine (memory)
  - This is why gut health affects mental health and vice versa — "butterflies in stomach" is real neurology
- Stress → altered gut motility and permeability → dysbiosis → inflammation → affects mood → more stress (feed-forward cycle)

## Supporting Gut Health
- **Dietary fiber**: 25-35g/day from diverse sources
  - Soluble fiber (oats, apples, beans, carrots, psyllium): feeds good bacteria → short-chain fatty acids (butyrate!) that heal gut lining
  - Insoluble fiber (whole grains, nuts, seeds, vegetables): bulks stool, keeps things moving
  - Diverse fiber = diverse microbiome (different bacteria eat different fibers)
- **Fermented foods**: yogurt (live cultures!), kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, kombucha, miso, tempeh — contain probiotics
  - Study (2021, Stanford): 10 servings/week of fermented foods increased microbiome diversity and reduced inflammatory markers
- **Polyphenols**: plant compounds that gut bacteria love — berries, dark chocolate (70%+), green tea, coffee, red wine (in moderation), olive oil, nuts
- **Prebiotics**: food for good bacteria — garlic, onions, leeks, asparagus, bananas (slightly green), oats, Jerusalem artichokes, chicory root
  - Prebiotics are just as important as probiotics (you need to feed the good bacteria)
- **Diverse whole foods**: studies show people who eat 30+ different plant species/week have healthier, more diverse microbiomes
  - Aim for variety: different colors, different types, different families

## What Hurts Gut Health
- **Antibiotics**: necessary for bacterial infections but also decimate gut bacteria — take probiotics during and after
  - Each course of antibiotics can affect microbiome for months to years
  - Only take antibiotics when prescribed and necessary (not for viral infections)
- **Processed foods**: emulsifiers, artificial sweeteners (saccharin, sucralose, aspartame), preservatives can damage microbiome
  - High sugar feeds "bad" bacteria and yeast
  - Highly processed diet can cause significant microbiome changes within days
- **Chronic stress**: stress hormones (cortisol) reduce microbiome diversity, increase intestinal permeability ("leaky gut")
- **Alcohol**: excessive drinking damages gut lining, alters microbiome composition
  - Moderate red wine (polyphenols) may actually be beneficial — other alcohols (beer, liquor) less so
- **NSAIDs** (ibuprofen, aspirin, naproxen): damage gut lining, increase permeability — take with food, limit frequency
- **Lack of sleep**: disrupts microbiome circadian rhythms — gut bacteria have their own daily cycles!

## Common Gut Issues

### IBS (Irritable Bowel Syndrome)
- Functional disorder (no structural damage found) — abdominal pain, bloating, diarrhea, constipation, or both
  - 10-15% of population, more common in women, often starts in teens/20s
  - Treatment: low FODMAP diet (elimination phase 2-6 weeks, then reintroduction), fiber (soluble > insoluble for IBS), peppermint oil, stress management, gut-directed hypnotherapy, medications (antispasmodics, low-dose antidepressants for pain)
  - FODMAPs = Fermentable Oligosaccharides, Disaccharides, Monosaccharides, And Polyols — short-chain carbs that ferment in gut → gas + bloating + pain

### SIBO (Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth)
- Bacteria from large intestine migrate into small intestine (where there shouldn't be much)
  - Symptoms: bloating (especially after meals, worse later in day), gas, diarrhea or constipation, nutrient malabsorption (B12, iron, fat-soluble vitamins)
  - Risk factors: low stomach acid (PPI use), slow motility, structural issues (adhesions from surgery), diabetes
  - Diagnosis: breath test (lactulose or glucose — measure hydrogen/methane)
  - Treatment: antibiotics (rifaximin — targets gut, not absorbed), elemental diet (2 weeks, liquid only), prokinetics to prevent recurrence

### GERD (Gastroesophageal Reflux)
- Stomach acid flows back into esophagus → heartburn, regurgitation, chest pain, chronic cough, hoarseness
  - Not always caused by TOO MUCH acid — often weak LES (lower esophageal sphincter) or Too SLOW stomach emptying
  - Triggers: large meals, lying down after eating, spicy/fatty/acidic foods, coffee, alcohol, smoking, tight clothing, obesity
  - Treatment: lifestyle (elevate head of bed 6", don't eat 3 hours before lying down, lose weight), antacids (TUMS), H2 blockers (Pepcid/Famotidine), PPIs (Omeprazole/Prilosec — short term only, long-term risks)

### IBD (Inflammatory Bowel Disease)
- Crohn's disease (anywhere in digestive tract, "skip lesions", deep inflammation) or Ulcerative Colitis (colon only, continuous, surface inflammation)
  - Autoimmune — immune system attacks digestive tract
  - Symptoms: bloody diarrhea, abdominal pain, weight loss, fatigue, fever
  - Not the same as IBS (IBS = functional disorder, no inflammation. IBD = visible inflammation, can cause permanent damage)
  - Treatment: anti-inflammatory meds (mesalamine), immunosuppressants, biologics (Humira, Remicade), surgery (removal of affected section)
  - Diet: avoid trigger foods (varies by person), some find relief with specific carbohydrate diet, IBD-AID diet, or exclusive enteral nutrition (liquid diet in children)
