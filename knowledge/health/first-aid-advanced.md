# First Aid: Advanced & Wilderness

## Wound Care (Advanced)

### Cleaning & Irrigation
- **Wound irrigation**: high-pressure irrigation (18 gauge catheter + 35-60 mL syringe, created ~8 PSI = optimal for bacteria removal). Saline best (can make: 1L water + 1 tsp salt, boil 15 min for sterile). Tap water OK if no saline. Pressure: high enough to remove debris but not high enough to drive bacteria deeper. Irrigation: hold syringe 2-4" above wound with moderate to strong stream, using steady pressure, 50-200 mL per inch of laceration. Avoid: hydrogen peroxide (cytotoxic — kills healthy tissue, impairs healing — only use for first cleaning if wound heavily contaminated). Povidone-iodine (Betadine): dilute 1:10 with saline, use as initial cleanser only, don't use repeatedly. Better: use copious saline/tap water irrigation
- **Debridement**: remove foreign material, devitalized tissue, clotted blood from wound, loose debris. Use sterile tweezers + saline + gauze. Blood clots larger than a pea? Don't remove — that would cause more bleeding. Only remove loose contaminated clots
- **Closing**: primary closure (stitches/steri-strips/surgical glue within 6-12 hours for clean wound, within 12-24 hours for clean facial wounds. Older = higher infection risk). Do NOT close if contaminated, animal/human bite (high infection risk — leave open, pack with gauze, allow to heal by secondary intention/delayed primary closure 3-5 days). Deep wounds: close in layers (deep absorbable sutures + skin sutures/steri-strips). Through-and-through sutures: bite each edge equally, depth to close dead space. Don't suture over joints (flexion/extension breaks closure)
  - Wound edges: if not gaping (<1 cm separation, clean): can approximmate edges with butterfly/steri-strip. If gaping >1 cm or deep (through dermis): sutures may be needed. For wound glue (Dermabond): can use for clean, straight edges with good approximation — but NOT over joints (flexion pops it open), on hair-bearing skin (hair glued together), in wet areas (axilla — sweat, perineum)
- **Infection signs**: increasing pain after initial improvement (worst post-injury day 5-7), redness spreading beyond wound margin (>1 cm from edges at day 3), swelling, warmth, fever (>100.4°F), purulent discharge (green/yellow foul drainage). Treatment: wound culture, start antibiotics (cephalexin, Bactrim for skin, keep wound open + packed, rebandage twice daily

### Burns (Advanced)
- **Rule of nines**: estimate burn % BSA. Adult: head + neck 9%, front torso 18%, back torso 18%, each arm 9%, each leg 18%, perineum 1%. For children: head larger % (18%), legs smaller (13.5% each). Palm of patient's hand (with fingers) = ~1% BSA
  - Use r/o palm for small areas: patient's hand with fingers = 0.5%? 1%? For scattered small burns, the "palm method" (palm of patient's hand including fingers = ~1% of body surface) is accurate for estimating scattered burns
- **Critical burns**: >10% TBSA in children, >15% in adults, >5% full-thickness. Third-degree on face, hands, feet, perineum, joint surfaces. Inhalation injury (facial burns, singed nasal hairs, hoarse voice, carbonaceous sputum, soot in mouth). Circumferential deep burns (need escharotomy to prevent compartment syndrome in limbs / constriction across chest)
  - Partial-thickness (second-degree): superficial — blanch, moist, blistered, very painful. Deep — mottled with white areas, less blanching, painful to pinprick
  - Full-thickness (third-degree): waxy white, leathery, charred (eschar), firm — no pain to pinprick (nerve endings destroyed). Fourth-degree: extends into muscle, tendon, bone

### Hypothermia & Cold Injuries
- **Mild** (95-90°F / 35-32°C): shivering, miserable, can't do fine motor tasks (stumbling, confusion). Passive rewarming: warm dry clothes, blankets, insulation from ground, warm sweet drinks (carbs, not caffeine, not alcohol). Shivering stops above 90°F
- **Moderate** (90-82°F / 32-28°C): no shivering (energy exhausted), altered mental status (confusion, apathy, paradoxical undressing — blood vessels in hands/feet dilate as they shut down, person feels hot, removes clothing — sign of impending death). Pulse may be difficult to detect. Active rewarming: hot packs on neck/axillae/groin/chest, warm IV fluids, warm humidified oxygen. Handle gently: rough movement can induce cardiac arrest (V-fib due to irritable cold heart). "They're not dead until they're warm and dead" — resuscitation may succeed even with prolonged cold-water submersion
- **Severe** (<82°F / 28°C): unconscious, no vital signs possible. Full CPR + active rewarming. Rewarming: if cardiac arrest: continuous CPR until core temp >85°F (30°C) before shocks/meds effective, because cold heart is resistant to defibrillation + drugs
- **Frostbite**: ice crystals in tissue — affects extremities (fingers, toes, nose, ears, cheeks). Superficial: white, waxy, no blisters (warm by body heat, don't rub — rub causes ice crystals to shred tissue). Deep: white, hard, insensitive, large blisters appear (red = good; dark purple = bad prognosis, dead tissue) within 24-48 hours
  - Rewarm: water 99-102°F (37-39°C), 15-30 minutes or until tissue flushes pink/red. Thawing is EXTREMELY PAINFUL (analgesics needed). Do NOT re-freeze after thawing (worse damage — ice crystals reformed shred everything). No fire/ heat lamp (burns numb tissue — not felt). After thaw: keep elevated, sterile dressings, AVOID debride tissue — mummification demarcates over weeks. Amputation: wait weeks-months, clear demarcation. Vasodilators (iloprost, nifedipine) in hospital may reduce amputation rate. Thrombolytic therapy (tPA) within 24 hours can salvage digits if given

### Snakebites
- **Pit vipers** (rattlesnake, copperhead, cottonmouth): hemotoxic venom. Pain, swelling, bleeding. NOT: cut + suck, tourniquet, ice (all harmful — cut increases bleeding, cryotherapy causes frostbite/tissue death, tourniquet concentrates venom). Do: calm victim (no running — increases venom spread), keep affected limb at or below heart level, remove jewelry (swelling), splint limb, transport to hospital
  - Copperhead (less severe, often no antivenom needed). Rattlesnake: can be severe; antivenom (CroFab or Anavip) if progressive swelling, bleeding, low platelets, systemic symptoms. Symptoms: progressive swelling >12 inches from bite in 24 hours, nausea/vomiting, metallic taste, perioral tingling, bleeding gums, hypotension, coagulopathy
  - Anti-venom: most effective within 4-6 hours of rattlesnake envenomation, cost: $2,000-20,000 per dose (average 4-8 vials needed)
- **Coral snake** (neurotoxic venom): rare bites (reclusive). Symptoms may be delayed 12 hours: slurred speech, double vision, difficulty swallowing, respiratory paralysis (drop jaw is classic). Antivenom: coral snake antivenom (now difficult to get, not produced in US anymore — limited supply, imported from Mexico for coral snake envenomation). Treatment: early intubation for respiratory support

### Spinal Injury Management
- **Suspect**: mechanism (fall >10 ft, MVC >35 mph, diving, major trauma), altered mental status, neurological deficit (numbness/twitching/tingling/weakness in limbs), distracting injury (any significant painful injury — person may not notice spine pain), midline spinal tenderness
- **Immobilization**: manual stabilization (hold head in neutral alignment, inline from ears, don't move). C-collar (rigid cervical collar, properly sized: chin to sternum distance, measure from base of neck to shoulder). Long spine board (for extrication only — 30-60 min max. Logroll: rocks: 4+ people, head stabilized, rotate as a unit, avoid twisting spine). Scoop stretcher: splits in half, slides under patient with minimal movement. Vacuum mattress: best for transport, molds to patient, distributes pressure + immobilizes well
  - Clearance: NEXUS criteria (if meets all 5: no midline tenderness, no focal neurological deficit, normal level of alertness, no intoxication, no distracting injury → can remove collar. If any present = x-ray/CT needed). Canadian C-spine Rule: age ≥65, dangerous mechanism, paresthesias in extremities — if none of these: can test range of motion (can rotate head 45° each direction — yes = low risk, no = imaging)

## Outdoor First Aid

### Heat Stroke
- **Emergency**: core temperature >104°F (40°C) + altered mental status (confusion, unconsciousness, seizure). HOT dry skin (if exertional heat stroke: may still be sweating — from working muscle-generated heat, not anhidrosis). RAPID COOLING essential (survival depends on minutes): cool water immersion (torso + limbs, stirring water, 35°F 45-50°F ideal). If no immersion: spray + fan (evaporative cooling), ice packs on neck/axillae/groin, cold wet sheets. Goal: lower core temp to 102°F (39°C) in first 30 minutes. Stop cooling at 102°F to avoid overshoot hypothermia
  - DO NOT: give antipyretics (Tylenol/ibuprofen — don't work for exertional hyperthermia, liver toxic with heat stroke), force fluids (if unconscious, aspiration risk. IV fluids if available)
- **Heat exhaustion**: heavy sweating, cool/clammy skin, body temp <104°F, headache, nausea, dizziness, weakness, thirst. Treatment: stop activity, move to shade/cool place, drink cool water/sports drink (dilute 1:1 with water). Rest 24 hours before returning to activity — risk of progression to heat stroke

### Altitude Sickness
- **Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS)**: headache + nausea, fatigue, dizziness, sleep difficulty. Usually >8,000 ft. Treatment: stop ascent, rest, acetazolamide (Diamox 125-250mg BID), descend if severe. Prevent: gradual ascent (<1,000 ft/day above 10,000 ft), acetazolamide prophylaxis, climb high/sleep low
- **HACE**: high-altitude cerebral edema — confusion, ataxia (can't walk heel-to-toe), altered mental status, vomiting. EMERGENCY: descend immediately! Dexamethasone 8mg then 4mg q6h. Supplemental O₂ if available. Gamow bag (portable hyperbaric chamber — simulates descent 4,000-5,000 ft). If can't descend: Gamow bag until descent possible
- **HAPE**: high-altitude pulmonary edema — dyspnea at rest, cough (may produce frothy pink sputum), crackles in lungs, cyanosis. EMERGENCY: descend immediately. Nifedipine 30mg ER q12h or sildenafil (Revatio) 50mg q8h. O₂. Also: Gamow bag, positive-pressure breathing. Prevent: slow ascent, nifedipine prophylaxis for susceptible

### Drowning & Water Safety
- **Drowning**: respiratory impairment from submersion/immersion in liquid. Not just death (fatal vs non-fatal drowning). Silent (no splashing/calling out — instinctive drowning response: mouth goes under, then above for fraction of second, not enough to call out. Arms pushing down at sides, not waving). In water: head low in water (mouth at water level, tilted back). Glassy/empty eyes, hyperventilating, trying to swim in direction but not moving (vertical body). Untrained rescuer: throw, don't go (rescue tube, buoy, long pole). If swimming rescue: approach from behind (drowning person may push you under — panic). Tow with rescue equipment or by pulling on shirt collar
  - Rescue breathing: begin ASAP (5 initial breaths, then 30:2 CPR). Oxygen first priority (drowning = hypoxic arrest). Compressions still important — start compressions after giving breaths. Don't waste time clearing airway (breaths will push water into lungs — water absorbed into circulation rapidly). Victims submerged <30 min in cold water (<50°F): still potentially resuscitable with good neurologic outcome, especially children. Consider near-drowning in: elderly + hypothermia after prolonged submersion (rare but possible in water <70°F)

## Aircraft, Marine & Emergency Medicine
- **Wilderness wound evacuation criteria**: infection not responding to oral antibiotics, wound gaping >1cm, bleeding not controlled, nerve/vascular injury, deep structure exposed (tendon, bone, joint), crush injury with possibility of compartment syndrome, significant contamination (fecal, soil with organic debris + unknown previous tetanus vaccination status). On extended expedition: may need to evacuate casualty if cannot provide adequate wound care (no running water, sterile supplies, wound will have higher infection risk)

## Wilderness Pharm
- **Emergency antibiotics**: doxycycline (broad, safe, sun-sensitivity). Cephalexin (skin, wounds). Ciprofloxacin (GI/GU, good for traveler's diarrhea — but Achilles tendon rupture risk). Amoxicillin (dental, respiratory). Azithromycin (respiratory, skin). Carry in expedition medical kit
- **Pain**: ibuprofen (anti-inflammatory, for sprains, arthritis, mountain sickness). Acetaminophen (fever, pain — safe for altitude but no anti-inflammatory effect). Tramadol (moderate pain, less respiratory depression than morphine, OK at altitude? possibly better). Opioids for severe pain (morphine, oxycodone — use cautiously at altitude, respiratory depression + hypoxia dangerous)
- **Altitude**: acetazolamide/Diamox (prevent + treat AMS). Dexamethasone (HACE). Nifedipine (HAPE). Sildenafil (HAPE). All can be life-saving — need to be in backcountry medical kit if traveling above 10,000 ft
