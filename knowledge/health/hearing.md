# Hearing Health — Audiology

## How Hearing Works

Sound waves → outer ear (pinna collects, ear canal directs) → eardrum vibrates → middle ear (3 ossicles: malleus, incus, stapes amplify vibration 22x) → inner ear (cochlea — fluid-filled, hair cells convert vibration to electrical signals) → auditory nerve → brainstem → auditory cortex. Damage to outer/middle ear = conductive loss (often reversible). Damage to inner ear/nerve = sensorineural loss (often permanent)

## Types of Hearing Loss

- **Conductive**: sound not reaching inner ear. Causes: earwax impaction (most common — cerumen impaction, ~12% incidence — use mineral oil/glycerin drops or professional microsuction/irrigation. DO NOT use cotton swabs — pushes wax deeper), middle ear fluid (otitis media with effusion — common in children after URI, may need tympanostomy tubes), eardrum perforation (trauma, infection, pressure change — small perforations heal spontaneously in weeks), otosclerosis (stapes bone abnormal growth — surgical stapedectomy effective), cholesteatoma (skin cyst in middle ear — requires surgical removal). Often treatable or surgically correctable
- **Sensorineural (SNHL)**: inner ear hair cells or auditory nerve damage. Causes: aging (presbycusis — high-frequency loss, gradual onset, begins ~age 50, accelerated by noise + genetics. Speech understanding worse in background noise even when loud enough), noise exposure (NIHL — see below), ototoxic medications (aminoglycoside antibiotics — gentamicin, tobramycin — permanent; loop diuretics — furosemide — often reversible; cisplatin chemotherapy — permanent; high-dose aspirin — reversible tinnitus/hearing), Meniere's disease (episodic vertigo + fluctuating hearing loss + tinnitus + aural fullness — salt restriction + diuretics + intratympanic steroids), acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma — benign tumor of CN VIII: unilateral hearing loss, tinnitus, imbalance — MRI diagnosis, treatment: observation/radiation/surgery), sudden sensorineural hearing loss (SSNHL — >30dB over 3 contiguous frequencies within 72 hrs. Medical emergency! Treat with high-dose steroids within 2 weeks — better outcomes the sooner treated. 50% recover normal hearing with treatment)
- **Mixed**: both conductive + sensorineural components

## Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)

- **Mechanism**: loud noise kills cochlear hair cells (permanent, no regeneration in mammals). Damage cumulative + irreversible
- **Risk**: sounds >85 dB prolonged. <85 dB safe for 8 hrs (OSHA PEL). Every 3 dB increase halves safe exposure time. 88 dB = 4 hrs. 91 dB = 2 hrs. 100 dB (motorcycle, headphones max volume) = 15 min. 110 dB (concert, power tools) = <2 min. 120+ dB (sirens, gunfire) = immediate damage (even single exposure). 140 dB = pain threshold + immediate injury
- **Signs**: tinnitus after noise exposure, muffled hearing, difficulty understanding speech in noise, needing TV volume higher than others, family reports you speak loudly
- **Prevention**: 80 dB threshold for protection (some guidelines: 85). Move away from source. Take 5 min quiet breaks per hour of noise. Use NIOSH SLM app to monitor levels. Headphones: keep <60% max volume, use noise-canceling (reduce need to raise volume). Follow 60/60 rule: 60% volume for 60 min max

## Hearing Protection

- **Earplugs (insert)**: foam (slow-roll thin then expand — best cheap option, NRR 28-33 when inserted correctly. Incorrect insertion = NRR drops to 5-10). Pre-molded silicone (reusable, NRR 20-26, easier insertion). Custom-molded (audiologist-made, NRR 25-30, comfortable for long wear). Musician (flat attenuation — reduces volume without distorting frequency balance). Insert correct: roll thin, reach behind head with opposite hand, pull ear up/back, insert, hold until expands. Check by cupping hands over ears — sound should drop further, not same
- **Earmuffs (circumaural)**: NRR 20-30. Better for intermittent exposure (easy on/off). Must seal all around ears — glasses/hats/hair break seal. Less effective than properly inserted plugs in some tests. Best for consistent fit
- **NRR (Noise Reduction Rating)**: decibel reduction in ideal lab conditions. Real-world reduction ~50-60% of labeled NRR. Formula: (NRR - 7) / 2 = actual reduction. Example: NRR 30 → real-world ~11.5 dB reduction. Doubling up (plugs + muffs) adds 3-6 dB more
- **When required**: concerts, clubs, sporting events, shooting range (double up mandatory), motorcycles, power tools, lawn equipment, factory/construction, any speech-in-noise difficulty after exposure

## Tinnitus

Perception of sound without external source — not a disease, a symptom. Types: subjective (only you hear — 99% of cases. Caused by hearing loss, ear injury, medications, TMJ, vascular issues), objective (clinician can hear — rare, usually vascular/mechanical: pulsatile tinnitus — blood vessel flow — often treatable — evaluate for aneurysm, AV malformation, glomus tumor, high-riding jugular bulb). Prevalence: ~15% of adults, ~2% severe. Most common cause = hearing loss (auditory deprivation leads to neural hyperactivity). Management: sound therapy (masking — white noise, nature sounds, hearing aids — reduce contrast between tinnitus and background. Best for sleep), cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT — reduces distress, not loudness — best evidence-based treatment, tinnitus retraining therapy = CBT + sound therapy), stress reduction (worse with anxiety, poor sleep, depression — addressing these helps), avoid triggers (caffeine, alcohol, NSAIDs, loud noise), medications (no FDA-approved for tinnitus. Off-label: amitriptyline/nortriptyline for sleep, clonazepam for severe anxiety — risk of dependence, limited evidence). Supplements: ginkgo biloba, zinc, B12 — mixed evidence, not recommended. Warning: unilateral tinnitus + hearing loss + dizziness = MRI needed to rule out acoustic neuroma

## Hearing Aids vs Cochlear Implants

- **Hearing aids**: amplify sound for sensorineural or mixed loss. Microphone → amplifier → receiver → ear canal. Prescription required (audiologist programming). Types: BTE (behind-the-ear, most power, for severe loss), RIC/RITE (receiver-in-canal, small, for mild-moderate), ITE/ITC (in-the-ear/canal, cosmetic but less power + features). Features: directional microphones (reduce background noise), feedback cancellation, Bluetooth, rechargeable. OTC hearing aids (FDA approved 2022 for mild-moderate loss — no prescription, lower cost, self-programmed via app. Good for mild loss but cannot replace professional fitting for complex losses). Do NOT buy from infomercials/online cheap amplification devices — those are sound amplifiers (PSAPs), not hearing aids, risk further damaging hearing. Acceptance barrier: average person waits 7-10 years after noticing loss before seeking help
- **Cochlear implants**: bypass damaged hair cells — directly stimulate auditory nerve. Surgery: electrode array inserted into cochlea, external processor worn on ear/head. Candidacy: severe-profound SNHL, inadequate benefit from hearing aids (<60% sentence recognition in best-aided condition), no medical contraindications. Bilateral implants better for sound localization + speech in noise. Age at implantation critical for children (best outcomes <18 months, potentially <12 months — spoken language develops near-normal). Adults: good outcomes if duration of deafness short. Outcomes: most understand speech without lipreading. Music perception varies. MRI compatible (but may need magnet removal/repositioning depending on model). Cost ~$50-100k (covered by most insurance/Medicare). Not reversible (implant destroys residual hearing in implanted ear)

## When to See an Audiologist

- Difficulty hearing conversations, especially in background noise
- Family/friends say TV too loud or you don't hear them
- Tinnitus in one or both ears lasting >1 week
- Ear pain, drainage, fullness, pressure
- Sudden hearing loss (emergency — same day or next day, see ENT)
- Plugged feeling not cleared by gentle cleaning or drops
- Dizziness/vertigo (balance evaluation — audiologist can do VNG or videonystagmography; some refer to ENT/neurology)
- Occupational noise exposure (baseline + annual monitoring)
- Children: failed newborn hearing screen (mandatory follow-up by 3 months), fails school screening, speech delay, frequent ear infections. Normal hearing = critical for language development before age 3

General screening: adults 50+ should have baseline audiogram. Annual if hearing loss, occupational noise, tinnitus, ototoxic medication, or diabetes (higher hearing loss prevalence)
