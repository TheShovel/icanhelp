# Pet Health & Nutrition

## Dog Nutrition

### Commercial Food
- **AAFCO statement**: required on all dog foods. "Complete and balanced" for life stage (puppy, adult, senior). "All life stages" may have too much calcium for large breed puppies (hip dysplasia risk — large breed puppy food has lower calcium + lower density for slower, proper growth)
  - Ingredients listed by weight: first ingredient is often water (for fresh meats). Corn/wheat/rice are not bad per se — cooked grains are digestible, provide energy. More important: nutrient profile (protein, fat, fiber, moisture) + brand reputation + your dog thrives on it
- **Food types**: dry/kibble (convenient, crunchy = dental benefits? Not really — mechanical abrasion minimal. Chewing a biscuit vs a dry kibble, the kibble is gone in 2 seconds, doesn't significantly clean teeth). Wet/canned (higher moisture, lower calorie density, good for hydration). Raw (Bones + organ meat — BARF diet — controversial: bacterial risk (Salmonella, E. coli) to dog + household. No proven benefit over high-quality cooked diet. If feeding raw: high hygiene precautions). Dehydrated/freeze-dried (close to raw in nutrition, requires rehydration, convenient for travel)
  - Grains in dog food: not inherently bad — dogs digest carbohydrates, produce amylase. Some dogs are allergic to specific grains (corn, wheat), but this is less common than chicken/beef allergies. Grain-free linked to DCM in some dogs (tapioca, lentils, peas, potatoes as carbs — FDA alert 2018). Most affected: golden retrievers, especially when fed grain-free + legume-heavy food. If you need a grain-free for allergy reasons, rotate proteins and brands. Bully breeds/Boston Terriers often have grain allergies; work with vet
- **Feeding amounts**: varies by brand (read bag, adjust for activity level, body condition score). General: ~2-3% of ideal body weight per day (for average activity). More for active dogs, less for seniors/couch potatoes. Body condition score (5/9 ideal: ribs felt without excess fat, waist visible from above, abdominal tuck from side). At ideal weight: feed to maintain
- **Homemade food**: MUST be balanced (calcium-phosphorus ratio, taurine, essential fatty acids, vitamins/minerals). Unbalanced = serious health issues (nutritional secondary hyperparathyroidism from calcium deficiency — fragile bones, fractures). Use recipes from veterinary nutritionist (BalanceIt.com, Tufts nutrition). Not trial and error

### Common Issues
- **Food allergies**: most common allergens = chicken, beef, dairy, egg, wheat, soy (not grains!). Symptoms: itchy skin, ear infections, paw licking (not primarily digestive). Diagnosis: 8-12 week food trial with novel protein (kangaroo, rabbit) or hydrolyzed protein. Blood/saliva allergy tests inaccurate for food
- **Weight management**: 60% of US dogs overweight. Obesity = shorter lifespan (≤2.5 years less), arthritis, diabetes, breathing issues, cancer, exercise intolerance. Weight loss: reduce calories 20-30%, measure food (don't guess), reduce treats (or use green beans, baby carrots). Exercise increase gradually
- **Dental health**: 80% of dogs over 3 have dental disease. Daily brushing ideal (poultry-flavored toothpaste — NOT human toothpaste — xylitol toxic). Dental chews: Veterinary Oral Health Council (VOHC) approved = proven effectiveness. Look for seal — not all chews are equal

## Cat Nutrition
- **Obligate carnivores**: require specific nutrients from animal tissue — taurine (essential amino acid, only in meat. Deficiency = dilated cardiomyopathy + blindness — blind cats from taurine deficiency). Arachidonic acid (fatty acid only in animal fat). Vitamin A (cats can't convert beta-carotene). Vitamin B12? Possibly some plant foods — but must be animal-based
  - High protein, moderate fat, low carbohydrate (cats have no dietary need for carbs; can digest but weren't evolved for high carb intake). Ideal: wet food (canned) > dry (kibble) — higher moisture, lower carbs, more species-appropriate. Dry food = convenient, cheaper, but chronic dehydration + UT issues risk
- **Wet vs dry**: wet food closer to natural diet (moisture ~70% vs dry ~10%). Cats have low thirst drive (evolved from desert ancestors) — need moisture in food. Dry food + inadequate water = concentrated urine = crystals/ stones, UT inflammation, FLUTD. Feed at least some wet food daily, ideally all wet. Many cats with FLUTD (Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease): stress + high dry diet predispose to crystals, hematuria, urethral plugs (especially in male cats)
- **Feeding**: cats prefer multiple small meals (simulate natural hunting). Puzzle feeders slow fast eaters + provide mental enrichment. Food puzzles: commercial, egg carton, paper towel roll, muffin tin with tennis balls — hide small portion of food in each
  - Free feeding → obesity risk (especially with dry food, which is calorie-dense). Better: timed meals 2-3x/day
- **Water**: clean, fresh, AWAY from food (cats evolved to avoid water near kill — contamination). Drinking fountains (moving water = more appealing). Multiple bowls (different rooms, different levels). Don't use plastic bowls (plastic can cause chin acne + bacterial growth). Ceramic/glass/stainless steel, wide enough so cat's whiskers don't touch sides. Cats often avoid: tap water in some areas with heavy chlorine

## Common Pet Medications

### Parasite Prevention
- **Flea/tick**: Frontline (fipronil, topical — kills fleas + ticks, lasts 1 month). Nexgard (oral, beef-flavored chew — starts killing fleas in 4 hours, ticks in 48 hours). Bravecto (oral or topical, lasts 3 months). Revolution (selamectin, topical — fleas, heartworm, ear mites, some ticks). Advantix (imidacloprid + permethrin — dogs only! Toxic to cats). Seresto collar (8-month continuous flea+ tick collar, effective but some safety reports of adverse events — collar no less safe than other topicals. Serious issues reported but still rare over millions sold)
  - Flea life cycle: adult (on pet), eggs (fall off in environment), larvae (in carpets + bedding), pupae (in cocoons, can survive months). Treat pet for 3+ months straight to break cycle. Treat ALL pets in household. Vacuum frequently + wash bedding in hot water
- **Heartworm**: monthly preventive (oral: Heartgard/ivermectin, Interceptor/milbemycin, Sentinel). Topical: Revolution. Injection: ProHeart (6 or 12 month, given by vet). Heartworm disease: transmitted by mosquitoes. Worms grow in heart + lungs. Prevention easier + safer than treatment (treatment painful, expensive, risky — melarsomine injections into back muscle, exercise restriction for 2 months). Year-round prevention in most US regions (even in winter — mosquitoes inside)
- **Deworming**: roundworms, hookworms, whipworms, tapeworms. Fenbendazole (Panacur — broad spectrum, 3-day course, safe for puppies, pregnant dogs). Pyrantel pamoate (hookworms + roundworms). Praziquantel (tapeworms — from fleas, doesn't kill fleas themselves)

### Common Medications
- **NSAIDs**: Carprofen (Rimadyl, Novox) — most common canine NSAID. Meloxicam (Metacam). Grapiprant (Galliprant — newer, fewer GI side effects, labeled for OA pain). Gabapentin: pain (neuropathic), anxiety, seizures. Trapentadol (?) Actually tapentadol (Nucynta) is human. Vet: Tramadol was commonly used but effectiveness in dogs is poor (metabolized different from humans — dogs don't produce the active metabolite M1 efficiently). Better to use a full μ-opioid like buprenorphine for severe pain
  - NEVER give dogs: ibuprofen (Advil — can cause GI ulcers + kidney failure at one regular tablet), acetaminophen (Tylenol — toxic to dogs at moderate doses, causes liver failure + methemoglobinemia + red blood cell damage), naproxen (Aleve), aspirin (not routinely recommended, can cause GI bleeding)
- **Pain management**: post-surgery: NSAID + gabapentin (± amantadine for chronic pain). For osteoarthritis: weight loss + exercise + joint supplements (glucosamine/chondroitin: evidence mixed, may help some dogs. Adequan (polysulfated glycosaminoglycan injections): better evidence for OA). NSAID (Galliprant or Carprofen as needed). Solensia (frunevetmab) — monoclonal antibody for feline OA pain, monthly injection
  - CBD: growing interest, limited evidence (interaction with endocannabinoid system — some studies show reduced pain, increased activity in dogs with OA). Make sure product has third-party testing for THC content (low THC safe for dogs). Not FDA approved

## Vet Visits
- **Puppy/kitten schedule**: 8, 12, 16 weeks (vaccines + deworming + exam). Rabies: 12-16 weeks (legally required, 1 or 3 year depending on state). DHPP (distemper, hepatitis, parainfluenza, parvovirus). FVRCP (feline viral rhinotracheitis, calicivirus, panleukopenia). FeLV (feline leukemia — recommended for kittens/outdoor cats)
- **Adult annual**: exam + boosters (DHPP/FVRCP + rabies). Heartworm test (annual), fecal (intestinal parasites), blood panel (seniors 7+), dental check. "Senior" for dogs: small breeds = 10-12 for senior; giant breeds = 5-7
- **Signs something's wrong**: not eating >24h, vomiting/diarrhea >24h, lethargy, limping >1 day, labored breathing, coughing, excessive drinking/urinating, sudden behavior change, lumps that grow/change, bad breath (dental disease), inappropriate elimination
