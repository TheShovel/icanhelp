# Nutrition Science — Advanced

## Macronutrient Ratios by Goal

- **General health**: Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Ranges (AMDR) — carbs 45-65%, protein 10-35%, fat 20-35% of calories. Most active adults thrive at ~50% carb, 20% protein, 30% fat
- **Muscle gain**: protein 1.6-2.2g/kg body weight. Carbs 4-7g/kg (supports training intensity + glycogen replenishment). Fats 0.8-1.5g/kg (hormone function + EFAs). Surplus 300-500 kcal/day above maintenance
- **Fat loss**: protein 1.8-2.7g/kg (lean mass preservation — higher end for leaner individuals + deficit deeper). Carbs 2-4g/kg (adequate to maintain training output). Fats 0.6-1.0g/kg. Deficit 300-500 kcal/day. Aggressive cut: 500-750 deficit, higher protein (2.3-3.1g/kg FFM)
- **Endurance performance**: carbs 6-10g/kg (up to 12g/kg for extreme volume — e.g., Tour de France). Protein 1.2-1.6g/kg. Fat 20-30% of calories. Periodized carb intake: high periodization on training days, lower on easy/rest days (train low, compete high — increases mitochondrial biogenesis)
- **Ketogenic (therapeutic)**: <50g carbs/day (typically 20-30g net). Protein moderate 1.2-1.8g/kg (too high = gluconeogenesis reduces ketosis). Fats 70-80% of calories. Used for epilepsy, GLUT1 deficiency, some neurodegenerative conditions. Evidence for weight loss short-term only (not superior to other diets at 12 months in meta-analyses). Contraindicated: type 1 diabetes without careful medical monitoring, pancreatic lipase deficiency, some metabolic disorders

## Macronutrient Quality

- **Carb quality**: prioritize intact whole grains (steel-cut oats, barley, quinoa, farro, brown rice — processing preserves grain structure → slower digestion), legumes, vegetables, fruits. Minimize added sugars (<10% of calories, ideally <5%). Fiber: 25-35g/day (average US intake ~15g — major gap). Soluble (oats, beans, apples, carrots, psyllium) → lowers LDL + blunts glucose response. Insoluble (wheat bran, nuts, celery) → stool bulk + regularity. Fermentable fiber (inulin, resistant starch, FODMAPs) → gut bacteria SCFAs. Resistant starch: in cooked-then-cooled potatoes, pasta, rice, green bananas, legumes. Feed gut microbiome + improve insulin sensitivity
- **Fat quality**: prioritize unsaturated over saturated. Replace 5% of calories from sat fat with PUFA → reduces CVD ~20%. Use olive oil (EVOO high in polyphenols), avocado, nuts (especially walnuts for ALA), fatty fish 2x/week (salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring — 1g EPA+DHA per 3.5 oz). Minimize industrial seed oils (soybean, corn, cottonseed, sunflower — high omega-6 → not inherently harmful, but high heat processing creates oxidation products, in context of low omega-3 intake shifts ratio unfavorably). No reason to avoid moderate amounts of these oils if total diet balanced
- **Protein quality**: PDCAAS (Protein Digestibility Corrected Amino Acid Score) and DIAAS (Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid Score — newer, more accurate, accounts for ileal digestibility). Complete proteins score ≥1.0 (whey, casein, egg, milk, soy, pea isolate). Leucine content critical for MPS (muscle protein synthesis) threshold — ~2-3g leucine per meal necessary for maximal MPS stimulation. Whey highest leucine (~11% by weight), soy ~8%, pea ~8%, rice ~7%. Plant proteins often lower in methionine + lysine, but variety across day meets needs. Protein blending (pea + rice = complete profile, DIAAS comparable to whey)

## Micronutrient Deficiencies — High Risk Populations

- **Iron**: premenopausal women (heavy menstruation — 2-3x higher requirement: 18mg vs 8mg RDA), vegans/vegetarians (non-heme iron lower bioavailability — 5-12% vs 25% for heme. Requirements higher 1.8x). Athletes (foot strike hemolysis + sweat losses + increased erythropoiesis — especially endurance runners). Blood donors. Deficiency: ferritin <30 ng/mL (even without anemia — impaired endurance, brain fog, restless legs). Before full anemia (low hemoglobin), iron deficiency without anemia (IDWA) more common. Treatment: ferrous sulfate 325mg (65mg elemental) every other day (hepcidin theory — daily dosing less efficient due to hepcidin upregulation. Alternate day better absorption or every day with more time). Take on empty stomach, with vitamin C. Avoid tea/coffee within 1 hr (tannins block absorption). Side effects: constipation, dark stools
- **Vitamin B12**: vegans (0% from diet — MUST supplement), elderly (atrophic gastritis reduces intrinsic factor → impaired absorption in 10-30% of 65+), metformin users (20-30% absorbed less), PPI users (reduced stomach acid impairs B12 release from food), Crohn's/IBD (ileal malabsorption). Deficiency: methylmalonic acid (MMA) and homocysteine elevated — more sensitive markers than serum B12 (normal range often >200 pg/mL but symptoms may appear 200-400). Neurologic symptoms before anemia: peripheral neuropathy, balance problems, memory decline, mood changes. Treatment: 1000mcg cyanocobalamin daily (oral works for deficiency even in pernicious anemia with high dose — enough absorbed via passive diffusion). For severe neurological symptoms: IM B12 1000mcg weekly × 4 weeks, then monthly
- **Vitamin D**: <20 ng/mL = deficient, 20-30 = insufficient, 30-50 = optimal (controversial — some argue 30-80). Risk: northern latitude, darker skin (more melanin reduces UVB conversion), obesity (sequestered in adipose), elderly (reduced skin synthesis), indoor lifestyle, sunscreen (reduces synthesis 99% — but still wear it; dietary/supplement compensation possible). Functions: calcium absorption, immune modulation, bone health, muscle function. Extra-skeletal: equivocal evidence for CVD, cancer, autoimmune prevention. Supplement: 1000-2000 IU/day for most. For deficiency correction: 6000 IU/day for 8 weeks then 2000/day maintenance. 50,000 IU once weekly for 8 weeks (prescription, for severe deficiency)
- **Magnesium**: 50-60% of US below RDA. RDA: 310-420 mg/day (higher for men). Food sources: pumpkin seeds (168mg/oz), almonds (80mg/oz), spinach (157mg/cup cooked), black beans (120mg/cup), dark chocolate (64mg/oz). Depletion risk: alcohol (increases excretion), GI diseases (IBD, celiac, diarrhea), diabetes, high-dose zinc supplements, PPIs (long-term). Deficiency linked to: muscle cramps (especially nocturnal leg cramps, fasciculations), anxiety, insomnia, migraines (low Mg linked to more frequent attacks), constipation, hypertension, arrhythmias. Supplement forms: magnesium glycinate (best absorbed + gentlest on GI, good for sleep/anxiety), citrate (cheap, good absorption but can cause loose stools — good if constipation also present), malate (energy, mitochondria support), threonate (claimed to cross BBB but evidence thin), oxide (cheapest, poorly absorbed ~4%, mostly laxative. Avoid). Dose: 200-400mg elemental. Take with food. Too much (especially citrate/oxide) → diarrhea
- **Iodine**: RDA 150mcg, pregnancy 220mcg, lactation 290mcg. Sources: iodized salt (77mcg per 1/4 tsp — half a tsp covers RDA), seafood, dairy (iodine in feed + disinfectants). Seaweed: variable — kelp extremely high (may exceed 1000mcg/g — risk of hyperthyroidism). Deficiency: goiter, hypothyroidism. In pregnancy: severe deficiency → cretinism (permanent intellectual disability) + increased miscarriage risk. US: largely sufficient due to iodized salt + dairy, but decreasing trend (less salt at home, sea salt/kosher salt typically not iodized). Note: sea salt, pink Himalayan salt contain minimal iodine. Use iodized salt for primary cooking salt
- **Calcium**: RDA 1000mg (men 50-70) to 1200mg (women 50+ / men 70+). Food first: dairy (milk 300mg/cup, yogurt 300mg/cup, cheese 200-300mg/oz), fortified plant milks (300mg/cup — shake carton, calcium settles), calcium-set tofu (200-300mg/half cup), sardines with bones (325mg/3oz), kale (100mg/cup cooked — but oxalates reduce absorption ~50% vs dairy). Leafy greens: kale best (low oxalate), spinach high oxalate (Ca not bioavailable). Supplement if deficient: calcium carbonate (cheapest, 40% elemental, take with food — requires stomach acid). Calcium citrate (21% elemental, better absorption, no food needed, good for older adults on PPIs). Dose: 500mg max per single dose (absorption saturates). No evidence supplements prevent fractures in general population (RCTs: slight reduction in bone loss but not fracture risk). Excess >2000mg/day may increase kidney stones + CVD risk (controversial). Get from food when possible

## Glycemic Index vs Glycemic Load

- **GI**: ranking (0-100) of how fast 50g carbs from a food raises blood glucose vs pure glucose. Low <55, medium 55-69, high >70. GI depends on: food structure (whole vs processed), fiber content, fat/protein presence, ripeness, cooking method (al dente pasta lower GI than mushy). Limitation: GI based on 50g carb serving — unrealistic for most foods (e.g., watermelon GI 72 but slice has ~11g carbs → low glycemic effect). GI does not consider serving size
- **GL**: GI × carbs per serving ÷ 100. Represents actual glycemic effect of a typical serving. Low <10, medium 11-19, high >20. Watermelon: GI 72, carbs 11g → GL 7.9 (low). White rice: GI 73, 1 cup 45g carbs → GL 32.9 (high). GL more clinically relevant than GI for blood sugar management
- **Practical**: focus on low glycemic load meals, not just individual foods. Pair high GI foods with protein/fat/fiber to reduce overall GL. Vinegar (acetic acid) + lemon juice added to meals reduces glycemic response ~20-30%. Cooking + cooling starches (potato/pasta salad, sushi rice) increases resistant starch → lowers GL. Insulinemic index different from GI — dairy has low GI but high insulin secretion (from whey). Don't assume low GI = healthy (ice cream low GI but high sat fat + sugar)

## Protein Quality and Timing

- **PDCAAS**: scores 0-1.0 based on amino acid profile × digestibility. Limitation: truncated at 1.0 (value of excess amino acids not captured), based on fecal digestibility (missing ileal absorption of individual AAs). Egg white, milk, whey, casein, soy, pea isolate = 1.0. Beef = 0.92. Chickpeas = 0.78. Wheat gluten = 0.25. Brown rice = 0.75
- **DIAAS**: newer (FAO 2011). Based on ileal digestibility of individual amino acids. Not truncated. Whey = 1.09. Soy = 0.90-1.00. Pea = 0.82-0.89. Rice = 0.75. Maize = 0.36. More accurate for rating protein quality in mixed meals. Plant proteins typically score lower due to antinutrients (trypsin inhibitors in legumes, phytates in grains) that reduce digestibility
- **Protein timing**: total daily protein more important than timing. For athletes: evenly distribute meals with 0.4g/kg per meal (× 4 meals = 1.6g/kg) maximizes MPS vs skewed distribution. Protein before sleep: 30-40g casein (slow-digesting) increases overnight MPS + recovery — evidence for muscle adaptation, especially during calorie deficit. Post-exercise: protein window is ~4-6 hours (not narrow 1-hour). Leucine threshold: ~2-3g per meal to maximize MPS (translate to 25-35g high quality protein per meal for 70kg person)
- **Plant-based protein considerations**: methionine (low in legumes) + lysine (low in grains) — complementary across day, not same meal. Leucine content lower per gram of protein. To compensate: eat ~15-20% more total protein (1.6-2.0g/kg vs 1.2-1.6g/kg for omnivores). Best sources: soy (tofu, tempeh, edamame), pea protein, seitan (wheat gluten — high leucine, but avoid if celiac), pumpkin seeds, hemp seeds, quinoa. Protein digestibility improved by cooking + soaking + sprouting + fermentation

## Periodized Nutrition for Athletes

- **Carbohydrate periodization**: "Fuel for the work required." High availability on heavy training/competition days (10-12g/kg). Moderate on moderate days (5-7g/kg). Low on rest/easy days (3-5g/kg). Train low: occasional training in low glycogen state to upregulate fat oxidation + mitochondrial biogenesis + AMPK signaling. Use selectively (1-2x/week, before key sessions only after adaptation, 1-2 week blocks). Risks: reduced training quality, impaired immune function, increased catabolism
- **Protein periodization**: higher during deficit/cutting phases (2.3-3.1g/kg FFM vs 1.6-2.2). Pre-sleep protein (casein) added during intense training blocks. Leucine supplementation (if plant-based) during growth/hypertrophy phases
- **Fat adaptation**: keto for endurance athletes → impaired performance at high intensities (glycolytic/VO2max work). Fat adapted athletes use more fat at same relative intensity, but high-intensity performance consistently worse in RCTs. Not recommended for elite athletes in explosive/power sports
- **Recovery nutrition**: post-exercise window: 0.8-1.2g/kg carbs + 0.3-0.5g/kg protein within 2 hours if next session <8 hrs. Protein alone insufficient for glycogen replenishment. Full recovery eating pattern more important than single post-workout meal for >24hr recovery

## Nutrigenomics Basics

- **Gene-diet interactions**: how genetic variants affect nutrient metabolism, and how nutrients affect gene expression (epigenetics + transcription factors)
- **MTHFR C677T**: 30-60% population variant (varies by ethnicity). Reduced MTHFR enzyme activity → impaired folate metabolism → higher homocysteine. Clinical impact: increased risk in some studies for neural tube defects (offspring + maternal variant), cardiovascular (homocysteine marker), depression (methylfolate supplementation may help treatment-resistant depression — evidence in those with MTHFR variant + depression, proven improvement with adjunct L-methylfolate 15mg). L-methylfolate vs folic acid: MTHFR variants may not efficiently convert folic acid to active form — supplement with methylfolate instead. Impact on homocysteine is modest, most benefit seen in those with high homocysteine role. Implications for vegans: if MTHFR variant, methylfolate supplement important (especially in pregnancy). Do not self-supplement based on 23andMe without consulting physician
- **FTO (fat mass and obesity associated)**: carried by ~44% Europeans. Associated with higher BMI, increased appetite, preference for energy-dense foods, reduced satiety. The effect: small — average 2-3 kg difference across lifetime between risk + non-risk carriers. Mechanism: FTO risk allele affects ghrelin regulation (higher circulating ghrelin → increased appetite + food intake). Modifiable by: high protein diet (20-25% protein vs 15% attenuates weight regain in FTO carriers — DiOGenes study). Physical activity (PA reduces FTO effect on obesity by ~40% — carriers can overcome genetic risk with exercise). NOT deterministic — environmental factors modify expression significantly
- **APOE**: APOE ε4 = strongest genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's (2-4x increased risk for one copy, 10-12x for two copies). ~15% carry one copy. Carriers may have differential response to dietary fat: some evidence they respond better to low saturated fat + MUFA-rich diet (Mediterranean). Fish oil (DHA) supplementation: mixed evidence in APOE4 carriers — some studies suggest lower benefit than non-carriers (possible differential DHA metabolism). Important: having APOE4 does not guarantee Alzheimer's — significant interaction with lifestyle (diet, exercise, cognitive reserve, sleep) modulates risk. Not tested clinically for most individuals (testing available but ethical considerations: insurance implications in some countries, psychological burden)
- **Lactase persistence (LCT)**: ~65% of world population lactose intolerant (adult). Northern European descent: >90% lactase persistent. East Asian, West African, Native American: <30% persistent. Managed: lactose-free dairy, lactase enzyme supplements, yogurt/hard cheeses (low lactose). Many can tolerate up to 12g lactose (1 cup milk) with meal, or gradually increase tolerance via gut adaptation
- **Caffeine metabolism (CYP1A2)**: "Slow" vs "fast" metabolizers. Slow (rs762551 A/A — ~50% of population) — accumulating higher caffeine levels. Associated with increased risk of: myocardial infarction in slow metabolizers consuming 2-3+ cups coffee/day vs fast (CYP1A2*1F study, case-control, 2014). Reduced exercise performance by caffeine in some trials. Fast metabolizers: more likely to get ergogenic benefits from caffeine. Practical: slow metabolizers are more likely to feel side effects (jittery, poor sleep, palpitations) — limit intake to 1 cup/day or earlier in the day. Note: quality of evidence for CYP1A2-based advice is moderate (contradictory findings in different populations)
- **Vitamin D receptor (VDR)**: variants affect vitamin D transport + utilization. Multiple VDR polymorphisms (FokI, BsmI, TaqI, ApaI) linked to bone density, immune function, chronic disease risk. Carriers of certain VDR variants may require higher vitamin D intake to maintain optimal serum levels (some studies: threshold 50 ng/mL vs 30 ng/mL for gene expression)
- **Epigenetics**: bioactive food compounds affect DNA methylation + histone modification. Folate, B12, choline, methionine = methyl donors (methylation cycle substrates). Sulforaphane (broccoli sprouts) → upregulates Nrf2 → antioxidant enzyme expression. Curcumin (poor bioavailability) → affects NF-κB, COX2, inflammatory pathways. Resveratrol (red grapes, low bioavailability) → SIRT1 activation. Polyphenols broadly affect gene expression related to inflammation, oxidative stress, metabolic health. Epigenetic changes reversible and diet-responsive — basis for "food as medicine" at molecular level
