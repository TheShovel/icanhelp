# Medication Safety

## Basic Rules
Take medications exactly as prescribed or directed on the label. Do not stop antibiotics, blood pressure medicine, psychiatric medicine, steroids, seizure medicine, or blood thinners without medical guidance unless emergency instructions say otherwise.

## Keep an Updated List
Maintain a list of prescriptions, over-the-counter medicines, supplements, doses, timing, allergies, and the prescribing clinician. Bring it to appointments and emergency visits. Include eye drops, inhalers, patches, creams, and injections.

## Avoid Interactions
Ask a pharmacist before combining new medicines, supplements, alcohol, or recreational substances. Common interaction risks include blood thinners, sedatives, antidepressants, seizure medicines, heart rhythm drugs, and grapefruit with certain medications.

## Dosing Safety
Use the measuring device that comes with liquid medicine. Kitchen spoons are inaccurate. Check whether medicine should be taken with food, on an empty stomach, at bedtime, or away from minerals like calcium, iron, or magnesium.

## Children and Pets
Store all medicine locked or out of reach. Child-resistant caps slow children down but are not childproof. Never call medicine candy. Many human medications are dangerous to pets; call a veterinarian or poison control if exposure occurs.

## Missed Dose
Read the label or ask a pharmacist. Many medicines should be taken when remembered unless it is almost time for the next dose. Do not double up unless instructions specifically say to.

## Disposal
Use pharmacy take-back programs when available. Do not keep old opioids, sedatives, or antibiotics “just in case.” Some high-risk medicines have special disposal instructions; follow local guidance.

## Red Flags
Seek urgent help for trouble breathing, swelling of lips/tongue/throat, severe rash, fainting, chest pain, confusion, severe bleeding, overdose, or suicidal thoughts. For poison exposure, contact poison control or emergency services.

## Medication Reconciliation
After hospital visits or specialist appointments, compare old and new medication lists. Clarify duplicates, stopped medicines, dose changes, and brand/generic names before taking everything together.
