# Eye Health — Vision and Eye Care

## Common Refractive Errors

- **Myopia (nearsightedness)**: eyeball too long or cornea too curved — distant objects blurry. Onset typically childhood, progresses until ~age 20. Increasing globally (myopia epidemic — 50% of world by 2050). High myopia (>-6.00D) increases risk of retinal detachment, glaucoma, myopic maculopathy. Management: glasses, contacts, orthokeratology (CRT — overnight rigid lenses reshape cornea temporarily), atropine 0.01% drops slow progression in children. Under-correcting myopia does NOT slow progression (contrary to old belief)
- **Hyperopia (farsightedness)**: eyeball short or cornea flat — near objects blurry (distance may be clear). Often congenital. Children can compensate via accommodation (ciliary muscle contraction). Presbyopia symptoms appear earlier in hyperopes (limited accommodation reserve). Management: glasses (convex lenses), contact lenses, LASIK/PRK
- **Astigmatism**: irregular corneal curvature (football vs basketball shape) — distorted vision at all distances. Causes: genetics, eye rubbing (especially in keratoconus), post-surgery. Management: toric contact lenses or glasses (cylindrical correction). Not correctable with standard spherical contacts. LASIK/PRK effective
- **Presbyopia**: age-related loss of accommodation (lens stiffens, ciliary muscle weakens). Usually noticeable after age 40. Near vision blurs — need to hold reading material farther away. Management: reading glasses, bifocals/progressives, multifocal contacts, monovision (one eye corrected for distance, other for near). LASIK monovision option

## Eye Exam Frequency

- **Children**: first comprehensive exam at age 3 (earlier if concerns), then before kindergarten, annually through school. Amblyopia (lazy eye) must be caught before age 7-9 for effective treatment. School vision screenings miss ~50% of problems
- **Adults age 18-60**: every 1-2 years if no problems. Annually if wear contacts, have diabetes, family history of glaucoma/macular degeneration, or work visually demanding job
- **Adults 61+**: annual exams (cataract, glaucoma, macular degeneration risk increases). Dilated exam every 1-2 years
- **Emergency**: sudden vision loss, flashes/floaters (possible retinal detachment — see curtain-like shadow), eye pain, double vision, foreign body (especially metal/glass), chemical splash (immediate 15+ min irrigation — call poison control). Floaters: new + flashes = retinal detachment until proven otherwise

## Digital Eye Strain (Computer Vision Syndrome)

Symptoms: eye strain, dry eyes, headache, blurred vision, neck/shoulder pain. Screen brightness should match ambient light. Work distance 20-30 inches (arm's length), screen slightly below eye level (15-20°). Blink more — normal blink rate 15-20/min, drops to 5-7/min on screens. Use lubricating drops (preservative-free if >4x/day). 20-20-20 rule: every 20 min, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds. Blue light blocking glasses: no high-quality evidence reduces eye strain — blue light does not damage retina at screen levels (only at very high intensity from UV sources/arc welding). Eye strain = focusing + blink issues, not blue light

## Cataracts

Lens opacification — world's #1 cause of reversible blindness. Risk: age (most common — 50% of people 75+), UV exposure, smoking, diabetes, corticosteroids (especially systemic or topical long-term), trauma. Symptoms: cloudy/blurry vision, glare/halos around lights, faded colors, frequent prescription changes. Treatment: surgery (phacoemulsification — breaks up lens with ultrasound, replaces with IOL — intraocular lens). Most effective medical procedure in history (~98% success rate). Common IOL options: monofocal (good distance, need reading glasses), multifocal (distance + near, but glare/halos), toric (corrects astigmatism). Do NOT wait for cataract to be "ripe" — outdated advice. Surgery when vision affects daily life

## Glaucoma

Optic nerve damage, often from elevated intraocular pressure (IOP). "Silent thief of sight" — no early symptoms, peripheral vision lost first (central vision last). Leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide. Types: open-angle (~90% — gradual, slow progression, drainage angle open but trabecular meshwork clogged), angle-closure (sudden — IOP spikes, eye pain, red eye, cloudy vision, halos, nausea — medical emergency. Can precipitate in dark rooms/with anticholinergics. Treatment: laser iridotomy). Risk factors: age 40+, Black/African descent (higher risk, earlier onset, more severe), family history, high myopia, diabetes, corticosteroid use. Screening: IOP (normal 10-21 mmHg — BUT many with normal IOP develop glaucoma: normal-tension glaucoma. Glaucoma can occur without high IOP). Diagnosis: dilated exam + OCT (optical coherence tomography of optic nerve/retinal nerve fiber layer) + visual field testing. Treatment: prostaglandin analogs (latanoprost once daily — IOP reduction 25-33%, best first-line), beta-blockers (timolol), alpha-agonists (brimonidine), carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (dorzolamide — topical, or acetazolamide — oral for acute), laser trabeculoplasty (SLT — outpatient, good alternative to drops), surgery (trabeculectomy or drainage implants for refractory cases). Treatment = lifelong. No cure

## Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

Macula (central retina) damage — central vision loss, peripheral vision intact. Dry AMD (90% — drusen deposits, gradual thinning, slow progression). Wet AMD (10% — abnormal blood vessels grow under retina, leak fluid/blood, rapid central vision loss — emergency). Risk: age 50+, smoking (#1 modifiable risk), family history, light-colored irises, obesity, hypertension. Amsler grid home monitoring: check daily, cover each eye. Distortion or missing area = wet AMD until proven otherwise. Treatment: dry — no cure, AREDS2 vitamins (vitamin C 500mg + E 400IU + zinc 80mg + copper 2mg + lutein 10mg + zeaxanthin 2mg — reduces dry AMD progression by ~25% in intermediate-stage. NOT beneficial for early AMD or prevention), smoking cessation, UV protection. Wet — anti-VEGF injections (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab) into vitreous every 4-8 weeks, lifelong. Wet AMD becomes scarred and permanent if untreated

## Contact Lens Hygiene

- Clean hands before handling. Use fresh solution each time — never top off old solution. Rub lenses 5 seconds each side even with "no-rub" solutions. Replace case monthly. Do NOT rinse lenses with tap water (Acanthamoeba keratitis risk — hard to treat, can cause blindness)
- Daily disposables = safest (no cleaning, less infection risk, better oxygenation)
- Do NOT sleep in lenses unless specifically approved for extended wear (risk of corneal ulcer increases 5-10x). Swimming, hot tubs, showers with lenses = increased infection risk. If lens drops on floor/ground: clean with solution, then disinfect. If lens feels scratchy or eye red/painful: remove immediately. If discomfort persists >1 hr, see eye doctor. Signs of infection: red eye, pain, photophobia, discharge, blurred vision not improved by blinking, lens uncomfortable. Remove immediately → do not wear until seen by professional
