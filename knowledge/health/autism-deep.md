# Autism Spectrum Deep Dive

Autism across the lifespan — diagnosis, support, and strengths-based approaches.

## Diagnosis & Spectrum
- **DSM-5 criteria**: Persistent deficits in social communication/interaction (3 domains) + restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities (2 of 4 types). Symptoms must be present in early development, cause clinically significant impairment, not better explained by ID or global delay
- **Social communication deficits**: Social-emotional reciprocity (back-and-forth conversation, sharing interests, initiating/social responses), nonverbal communication (eye contact, body language, facial expressions, gestures), developing/maintaining relationships (making friends, imagination, adjusting behavior to context)
- **Restricted/repetitive behaviors**: Stereotyped/repetitive movements/speech (hand flapping, rocking, echolalia), insistence on sameness/routines (distress at small changes), highly restricted fixated interests (intense focus on specific topics, attachment to unusual objects), sensory hyper/hyporeactivity (under/over-responsive to sounds, textures, lights, pain, temperature)
- **Levels of support**: Level 1 (requiring support — noticeable difficulties but can function independently), Level 2 (requiring substantial support — marked deficits, limited initiation), Level 3 (requiring very substantial support — severe deficits in communication, extreme distress at change). Not related to intelligence

## Sensory Processing
- **Hypersensitivity (over-responsive)**: Sounds (vacuum, blender, fireworks, crowds, high-pitched noises), lights (fluorescent flicker, bright sunlight, strobes), touch (tags, seams, certain fabrics, unexpected touch), taste/texture (mushy foods, strong flavors, mixed textures), smell (perfume, cleaning products, cooking smells), proprioception (dislike of tight clothing, crowds)
- **Hyposensitivity (under-responsive)**: High pain tolerance, difficulty feeling temperature, seeking strong sensory input (crashing, spinning, deep pressure), not noticing when hungry/full, unaware of messy face/hands
- **Sensory accommodations**: Noise-canceling headphones, tinted glasses (FL-41 for fluorescent light sensitivity), weighted blankets/lap pads, chewelry (chewable jewelry for oral sensory needs), compression clothing, fidget toys, dimmable lights, fragrance-free environments
- **Proprioceptive input**: Deep pressure activities (heavy work: pushing, pulling, carrying, climbing). Calms the nervous system. Trampolines, compression, yoga poses, climbing

## Communication Differences
- **Literal interpretation**: Difficulty with sarcasm, idioms ("break a leg"), implied meaning. Say what you mean directly and clearly. "Please close the window" not "Is it cold in here?"
- **Delayed processing**: May take longer to understand and respond to verbal information. Wait 5-10 seconds before repeating or rephrasing. Providing written information in advance helps
- **Nonverbal communication**: May not use typical gestures, eye contact may be uncomfortable/painful (can listen better without looking). Do not assume lack of eye contact = disinterest or dishonesty. Alternative: look at person's mouth or ear, or face generally
- **Echolalia**: Repeating words/phrases — can be immediate (parroting) or delayed (quoting movies, previous conversations). May serve different functions: self-regulation, processing, communication, stimming. Don't stop echolalia — figure out what it communicates
- **Scripting**: Rehearsing conversations, preparing responses in advance. Common social strategy. Let autistic person use scripts — provides predictability

## Strengths-Based Perspective
- **Intense focus (hyperfocus)**: Can concentrate deeply on interests for hours. Productive when interest aligns with work/study. "Special interests" bring joy, expertise, career paths
- **Pattern recognition**: Often excellent at detecting patterns, inconsistencies, details others miss. Strengths in data analysis, quality assurance, debugging, systems thinking
- **Honesty & directness**: Say what they mean, no hidden agenda, don't play social games. Reliable communicators — can be trusted to give honest feedback
- **Loyalty & reliability**: When trust is established, deeply loyal. Follow rules, keep commitments. Good friends, employees, partners — not interested in superficial relationships
- **Memory**: Exceptional memory for facts, details, events of interest. Calendar/memorization skills. Encyclopedia knowledge about special interest areas
- **Authenticity**: Less likely to conform to social pressure. See the world from unique perspective. Creativity, invention, outside-the-box thinking. Many scientific/artistic breakthroughs by autistic minds

## Meltdowns vs Shutdowns
- **Meltdown**: Involuntary response to sensory/ emotional overwhelm. Looks like "temper tantrum" but is NOT — person has lost control, may scream, cry, self-harm, or become aggressive. Not behavioral choice — neurological. Need to remove triggers and let run course
- **Shutdown**: Quiet version of overwhelm — person goes non-verbal, still, unable to respond. Looks like ignoring but physically can't process or respond. Give space, quiet, time to recover
- **Recovery period**: After meltdown/shutdown, may need 30 min to hours of quiet/alone time. Sensory sensitivity increased. Exhaustion afterwards
- **Prevention**: Recognize early warning signs (irritability, increased stimming, withdrawal) → reduce sensory input, take break, use calming strategies

## Accommodations & Support
- **Stimming**: Self-stimulatory behavior (hand flapping, rocking, pacing, humming, spinning). Serves as self-regulation (calming, focus, emotional regulation). Never force someone to stop stimming — provide appropriate alternatives if harmful
- **Predictability**: Schedules, routines, advance notice of changes. Visual schedules (pictures for kids, written for adults). Transition warnings ("10 minutes until we leave")
- **Choice & control**: Offer limited choices (not open-ended). "Red or blue shirt?" not "What do you want to wear?" Too many choices = analysis paralysis
- **Low arousal approach**: Calm voice, slow movement, non-threatening body language. Don't loom over person — sit or crouch at eye level. Avoid confrontation, anger, sudden movements

## Co-occurring Conditions
- **Anxiety (40-50%)**: Social anxiety, generalized anxiety, OCD — very common. Sensory issues + social confusion + unpredictability = constant state of vigilance
- **ADHD (30-50% overlap)**: Both have executive function challenges, but different root causes (ADHD: inattention, impulsivity; ASD: rigidity, sensory, social). "AuDHD" is common
- **Depression (20-30%)**: Often from lifetime of masking, burnout, social rejection, feeling different. Behavioral activation, adapted CBT helpful
- **Sleep disorders (50-80%)**: Difficulty falling asleep (racing thoughts, sensory issues), staying asleep, irregular sleep-wake cycles. Melatonin, strict routine, weighted blankets, blackout curtains
- **GI issues (30-70%)**: Constipation, diarrhea, abdominal pain, IBS. May relate to sensory food preferences, limited diet, stress, altered microbiome

## Autism in Women & Girls
- **Diagnosed later** (average age 7-12 vs 3-5 for boys) — many not diagnosed until adulthood. Better camouflaging skills (masking) hide struggles
- **Masking**: Imitating neurotypical social behavior — learned scripted responses, forced eye contact, suppressing stims. Exhausting — leads to autistic burnout (prolonged loss of function, increased support needs)
- **Different presentation**: Special interests may appear more "socially acceptable" (animals, celebrities, psychology, fiction) but still intense. More social motivation but difficulty executing. Higher rate of eating disorders, self-harm
- **Misdiagnosis**: Often diagnosed with BPD, eating disorders, social anxiety, OCD before autism identified

## Support Approaches
- **ABA therapy**: Controversial — some find it helpful for skill building, others find it traumatic (forced compliance, masking, suppressing autistic behaviors). Autism self-advocacy movement opposes ABA. Focus should be on building skills, not eliminating autistic traits
- **Speech therapy**: For functional communication, not eliminating differences. AAC devices for non-speaking. Social communication (explicit instruction, not forced)
- **Occupational therapy**: Sensory integration, fine motor skills, daily living skills. Often very helpful. Sensory diet: scheduled sensory activities throughout day
- **Social skills training**: Explicit teaching — not "just play with kids". Social stories, video modeling, peer buddies, social groups. Focus on safety and choices, not "act normal"
