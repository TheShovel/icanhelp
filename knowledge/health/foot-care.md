# Foot Care — Podiatry

## Common Foot Issues

- **Plantar fasciitis**: heel pain on first steps in morning (after non-weight-bearing rest) — sharp/stabbing, eases after a few minutes, worsens with prolonged standing. Cause: repetitive strain on plantar fascia (ligament from heel to toes). Risk factors: tight calves (Gastroc-soleus tightness = primary contributor), high arches or flat feet, prolonged standing, running (especially with poor footwear/rapid mileage increase), obesity, sudden increase in activity. Treatment: calf + plantar fascia stretching (towel calf stretch, wall calf stretch, roll foot on frozen water bottle or tennis ball/golf ball — 10 min 2x/day), supportive shoes (no flat flip-flops — need arch support and heel cushioning), OTC heel cups/Spenco orthotics, night splints (keeps fascia stretched during sleep — effective but compliance low), NSAIDs (naproxen 220-440mg 2x/day for 1-2 weeks for acute flares). Shockwave therapy (for chronic >6 months — ~70% success). Steroid injection (short-term relief, risk of fascia rupture — use sparingly). Surgery: <5% need it — partial fasciotomy (last resort, success ~70-80%, risk of nerve injury, arch instability). Recovery: 70% better in 6-12 months with conservative care. Sharp pain at heel = plantar fasciitis until proven otherwise. Numbness/top of foot pain = not PF (consider tarsal tunnel syndrome or peroneal nerve entrapment). Do NOT walk barefoot on hard surfaces
- **Bunions (hallux valgus)**: big toe deviates toward second toe — metatarsal head becomes prominent, joint may become painful. Cause: genetics (foot type) + tight/narrow shoes (high heels, pointed toes). Wearing wide shoes prevents worsening but does not reverse. Bunionettes (tailor's bunion) = same on pinky toe side. Treatment: wide toe box shoes (avoid pointed, avoid heels >2 inches), toe spacers (silicon, between 1st-2nd toe), bunion splints at night (may slow progression). Surgery (bunionectomy): for persistent pain (not cosmetic) — osteotomy (cut + realign bone), 6 weeks to 6 months recovery, may require non-weight bearing. Recurrence possible. Non-surgical: pain control + shoe modification. Bunions are bone deformities — no exercise or device "cures" them
- **Ingrown toenails (onychocryptosis)**: nail edge grows into skin — pain, redness, swelling, possible infection (paronychia). Most common: big toe. Causes: improper nail trimming (rounding corners — cut straight across), tight shoes, trauma (stubbing, sports), nail shape (involute/pincer nail). Treatment early: soak in warm water + Epsom salts 15-20 min 2-3x/day, lift nail edge with sterile cotton or dental floss under corner, antibiotic ointment if infection. For infection with pus: oral antibiotics (cephalexin, clindamycin — covers Staph + Strep). Procedures in clinic: partial nail avulsion (remove side of nail, apply phenol to nail matrix -> prevents regrowth — 95% success, low recurrence). Permanent removal: complete nail avulsion + phenolization (rarely needed). Do NOT cut off the ingrown part yourself — worsens. Do NOT dig into corner with sharp objects. Prevention: cut nails straight across, not too short, edges just past the tip of toe
- **Athlete's foot (tinea pedis)**: fungal infection (Trichophyton rubrum most common) — itching, burning, cracked peeling skin between toes (especially 4th-5th). Spread from contaminated floors (gym, locker room, pool). Types: interdigital (most common — between toes), moccasin (entire sole — thick, dry, peeling), vesicular (blisters). Treatment: OTC terbinafine 1% cream (Lamisil) — best cure rate ~70% in 1 week (superior to clotrimazole). Continue 1 week after symptoms clear. For moccasin type or resistant cases: oral terbinafine (Lamisil 250mg/day 2 weeks) or itraconazole (pulse dosing). Recurrence common (>50%). Prevention: dry between toes after shower (with separate towel, not the body towel), antifungal powder, change socks daily (cotton or moisture-wicking), shower shoes in public areas. NOT eczema — steroids (hydrocortisone) can worsen tinea. KOH prep scraping confirms
- **Flat feet (pes planus)**: arch collapses when standing. Flexible flat feet (arch appears when not weight-bearing — most common, usually asymptomatic, normal variant — 20-30% of population, most need no treatment). Rigid flat feet (no arch even non-weight-bearing — more likely symptomatic + underlying condition: tarsal coalition, posterior tibial tendon dysfunction). Treatment: only if painful. Supportive shoes (motion control/stability shoes), arch supports/orthotics (OTC or custom — custom not shown superior to quality OTC for most cases), physical therapy (strengthen intrinsic foot muscles + posterior tibialis). Posterior tibial tendon dysfunction (PTTD): adult acquired flatfoot — tendon degenerates, arch collapses, "too many toes" sign when viewed from behind. Progressive: Stage I (tendonitis, no collapse) → Stage IV (arthritis, rigid deformity). Treatment by stage: rest/orthotics/bracing → surgical reconstruction. Do NOT assume flat feet need orthotics — most are asymptomatic
- **Hammertoes**: toe bent at PIP joint (looks like hammer). From muscle imbalance + tight shoes. Flexible (can straighten manually) vs rigid (fixed contracture). Treatment: roomy toe box shoes, toe exercises (pick up marbles with toes), crest pads/straightening splints. Surgery if painful: tendon release for flexible, joint resection for rigid
- **Plantar warts (verruca)**: HPV in skin — thickened skin with black dots (thrombosed capillaries). Different from callus (no dots, skin lines preserved). May be painful with side-to-side pressure. Can resolve spontaneously (20% in 2 months, 65% in 2 years). Treatment: OTC salicylic acid (40% patch — best evidence, 70% success). Cryotherapy (liquid nitrogen — clinic, every 2-3 weeks, ~70% success, painful). Cantharidin (blistering agent). Do NOT cut at home. Avoid treatment if painless/not spreading — observation is fine. Immunocompromised = more resistant
- **Corns and calluses**: callus = diffuse thickened skin from friction/pressure. Corn = small, well-defined, deep core (hard corn on toe, soft corn between 4th-5th toes). Treatment: pumice stone after soaking (gentle, don't bleed), moisturizer (urea 10-40% cream for thick calluses), donut pads (reduce pressure). NEVER cut corns with razor blade — infection risk. If neuropathic (diabetic), see podiatrist only

## Proper Footwear Selection

- **General fit rules**: measure feet at end of day (feet swell). Try both shoes (most people have one foot slightly larger). Fit to larger foot. Width should allow toe wiggle (don't size up just for width — find a wide size). Heel should not slip. 1/2 to 1 thumb width from longest toe to shoe end. Break-in: should be comfortable from day 1 (shoes that hurt "need breaking in" = wrong shoes — exception: leather dress shoes)
- **Running shoes**: replace every 300-500 miles (cushioning breaks down before sole wears out). Types by pronation: neutral (normal arch), stability (mild-moderate overpronation, low arch — e.g., Brooks Adrenaline, ASICS Kayano), motion control (severe overpronation — heavy, rigid — e.g., Brooks Beast, Saucony Echelon). Get fitted at running store (gait analysis). Rotate 2 pairs if running >25 miles/week
- **Work boots**: steel toe if required. Ensure toe wiggle room (steel toe cramped = ingrown nails + bunions). Insoles (add arch support if needed). Replace when tread worn or sole beginning to separate
- **What to avoid**: flat flip-flops/slides for prolonged walking (no arch support, increased plantar fasciitis risk), high heels (>2 inch = increased forefoot pressure + shortened Achilles + bunions/hammertoes), shoes that bend in middle (should bend at ball of foot, not midfoot — indicates weak construction), shoes with no heel counter (back of shoe stiffener)

## Foot Stretches and Strengthening

- **Calf stretches**: wall stretch (lean forward, back leg straight, heel down — hold 30 sec, 3x each leg). Downward dog (yoga — full calf + hamstring stretch). Towel calf stretch (towel under ball of foot, pull toward body, knee straight + bent — each 30 sec)
- **Plantar fascia stretch**: seated, cross foot over knee, pull toes toward shin until feel stretch in arch. Hold 30 sec, 3x each morning before stepping out of bed (critical — first step pain)
- **Towel scrunches**: place towel on floor, curl toes to pull it toward you — strengthens intrinsic foot muscles
- **Ankle alphabet**: trace alphabet with big toe — improves ankle ROM + proprioception
- **Heel raises**: stand on edge of step, lower heels below step, hold 30 sec. Moderate progression. Do not do if acute plantar fasciitis
- **Gastrocnemius vs soleus**: gastrocnemius (knee straight) cross knee + plantar flexion. Soleus (knee bent) — works on both for full stretch

## Diabetic Foot Care (Critical)

Diabetes causes peripheral neuropathy (loss of sensation) + peripheral vascular disease (poor circulation) + impaired healing + increased infection risk. Foot ulcers precede 85% of diabetes-related amputations. Examine feet daily (use mirror to see sole — look for cuts, blisters, redness, swelling, nail changes, callus with blood). Do NOT soak feet (dries skin, increases risk fissures). Wash with lukewarm water (test with elbow, not foot), dry gently especially between toes. Moisturize (apply lotion to feet but NOT between toes — excess moisture there promotes fungal infection). Trim nails straight across, file edges. Never walk barefoot (even indoors). Wear well-fitted diabetic shoes (extra depth, wide toe box, seamless interior, removable insoles). Medicare covers 1 pair depth shoes + 3 pairs inserts per year for diabetes with neuropathy/callus/deformity/ulcer history. Check shoes inside before wearing (foreign objects). Professional foot exam: monofilament test for neuropathy (10g monofilament — loss of sensation at ≥2/10 sites = neuropathy), pulse check (dorsalis pedis + posterior tibial — absent = PAD), foot structure/inspection. Refer to podiatrist annually (or every visit if high risk: hx ulcer/amputation, Charcot foot, ESRD, PAD, neuropathy). **Red flags**: non-healing ulcer >2 weeks, black tissue (gangrene), red/hot/swollen foot without injury (Charcot foot until proven otherwise — neuroarthropathy: bone/joint collapse from neuropathy, can mimic infection, requires offloading + total contact cast ± surgery). Any diabetic foot wound = medical emergency. Do not treat at home

## When to See a Podiatrist

- Heel pain >6 weeks despite home treatment (plantar fasciitis not responding)
- Tried orthotics, better shoes, stretches — still pain
- Ingrown toenail with infection (pus, redness spreading, red streaks)
- Diabetic foot check (annually mandatory for high risk)
- Nail fungus (onychomycosis) — prescription oral terbinafine vs laser vs topical (topicals need frequent application, poor penetration — oral terbinafine 12 weeks best evidence. Check LFTs before. Nail takes 6-12 months to grow out)
- Hammertoes or bunions with significant pain limiting shoe wear
- Suspicious mole/wart/cyst on foot (skin cancer on feet — acral melanoma can mimic wart — DO NOT assume it's benign)
- Ankle sprain not healing >4 weeks (possible peroneal tendon tear or ATFL rupture — needs MRI and possibly surgical repair)
- Custom orthotics: for specific foot biomechanical problems, not for everyone. Only if OTC inserts + stretching insufficient
