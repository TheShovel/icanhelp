# Osteology & Human Skeletal Anatomy

## Axial Skeleton (80 bones)

### Skull (22 bones)
- **Cranial (8)**: frontal (forehead, brow ridge, sinuses). Parietal (2 — sides/roof). Temporal (2 — ear bones — mastoid process, styloid process, temporomandibular joint). Occipital (back/base — foramen magnum where spinal cord exits, occipital condyles articulate with atlas C1). Sphenoid (behind eyes — looks like bat, contains sella turcica housing pituitary gland). Ethmoid (between eyes, cribriform plate for olfactory nerves — nerve fibers pass through tiny holes for smell)
  - Sutures: coronal (front-parietal), sagittal (between parietals), lambdoid (parietal-occipital), squamosal (temporal-parietal). Fontanelles: soft spots in infant skull (allow passage through birth canal + brain growth). Metopic suture: frontal suture fuses age 2-6; if persists, can cause trigonocephaly
  - Sinuses: frontal, maxillary, ethmoid, sphenoid — air-filled, lighten skull, resonate voice, produce mucus. Sinusitis = inflammation. Maxillary sinuses largest, infection can feel like toothache
- **Facial (14)**: maxilla (upper jaw holds upper teeth, palatine process = hard palate, maxillary sinuses). Zygomatic (cheekbones). Mandible (lower jaw — ONLY movable skull bone. Temporomandibular joint = TMJ — jaw clenching/grinding, pain, clicking). Nasal (bridge of nose). Lacrimal (medial eye — tear duct). Palatine (posterior hard palate). Inferior nasal conchae (turbinates — warm, humidify air). Vomer (nasal septum)
- **Hyoid**: U-shaped, only bone not articulating with any other — suspended by muscles + ligaments from styloid processes. Attachment for tongue muscles. Unique: broken during strangulation = hyoid fracture (seen on autopsy), "hanging fracture"

### Vertebral Column (26 bones)
- **Cervical (7)**: C1 atlas (holds skull, "yes" movement — nodding), C2 axis (dens/odontoid process acts as pivot — "no" movement). C3-C7 typical vertebrae with uncinate processes (uncarthrosis can narrow IVF and cause cervical radiculopathy). C7 = vertebra prominens (most prominent spinous process, palpable at base of neck)
  - Vertebral arteries: pass through transverse foramina of C1-C6. Injury here can cause vertebral artery dissection (stroke from neck manipulation/trauma). "Bow hunter's stroke": neck rotation compresses vertebral artery
- **Thoracic (12)**: articulate with ribs. Heart-shaped body. Long inferior-pointing spinous processes. T1-T4 correspond to level of sternal angle (Angle of Louis — landmark for counting ribs)
- **Lumbar (5)**: largest, kidney-shaped body, weight bearing. L4-L5-S1 most common site of disc herniation (sciatica). Lumbar vertebrae: mammillary and accessory processes for muscle attachments
- **Sacrum (5 fused)**: triangular. Sacral foramina (4 pairs for sacral nerves). Sacral canal (continuation of vertebral canal). Sacral hiatus (access for caudal epidural block). Auricular surface (articulates with ilium via sacroiliac joint — can be source of pain with SI joint dysfunction)
- **Coccyx (4 fused, sometimes 3-5)**: tailbone. Sits between gluteal muscles. Fractures from fall backward onto hard surface → pain with sitting, painful bowel movements, sitting on donut cushion for weeks

### Thorax (25 bones)
- **Sternum (3 parts)**: manubrium (top — articulates with clavicle + first 2 ribs. Sternal angle = Angle of Louis — landmark for rib 2, where trachea bifurcates into bronchi). Body (gladiolus — longest segment, sides 3-6 ribs). Xiphoid process (tip — can be broken during CPR compressions)
- **Ribs (12 pairs)**: True (1-7, attach directly to sternum via costal cartilage). False (8-10, costal cartilage attaches to 7th rib's cartilage). Floating (11-12, no anterior attachment — end in muscle). Rib fracture: most common at posterior angle, usually from blunt trauma. Multiple consecutive rib fractures = flail chest (segment moves opposite direction during breathing = paradoxical movement). Girl: rib count = same as men (myth busted)

## Appendicular Skeleton (126 bones)

### Upper Limb (64 bones)
- **Pectoral girdle**: clavicle (collarbone — most commonly fractured bone, S-shaped, brachial plexus passes beneath). Scapula (shoulder blade — spine, acromion, coracoid process, glenoid fossa for humerus articulation, supraspinatus/infraspinatus fossae. "Winged scapula" = long thoracic nerve injury)
  - Shoulder = glenohumeral joint (ball + shallow socket = most mobile, least stable — accounts for 50% of dislocations). AC joint (acromioclavicular): separated shoulder (A/C joint injury = separation, grades I-VI)
- **Arm**: humerus (head, greater + lesser tuberosities, anatomical vs surgical neck, deltoid tuberosity, medial + lateral epicondyles, olecranon fossa). Radial nerve wraps around humerus spiral groove — fracture here = wrist drop (Saturday night palsy)
- **Forearm**: radius (lateral, thumb side — radial head, radial tuberosity, styloid process). Ulna (medial — olecranon elbow, coronoid process, styloid process). Two bones articulate for pronation/ supination. Radial head fracture = common after fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH). Monteggia fracture: proximal ulnar fracture + radial head dislocation. Galeazzi fracture: radial shaft fracture + distal radioulnar joint dislocation
- **Hand**: carpals (8 — scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum, pisiform, trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, hamate). Scaphoid most commonly fractured carpal bone (fall on outstretched hand). Lunate most commonly dislocated. Navicular: continue = scaphoid. Metacarpals (5 — neck fractures = boxer's fracture, 5th MC neck most common). Phalanges (14 — fingers: proximal, middle, distal. Thumb: proximal, distal only)

### Lower Limb (62 bones)
- **Pelvic girdle**: 2 hip bones (os coxae — ilium, ischium, pubis). Acetabulum (hip socket). Obturator foramen (largest foramen in body). Pubic symphysis (cartilaginous joint). Pelvis inlet + outlet shapes: gynecoid (female, round, favorable for childbirth), android (male, heart-shaped). Sacroiliac joint: common source of lower back pain
  - Sex differences: female pelvis wider, subpubic angle >90°, sciatic notch wider, pelvic inlet more round. Male: narrower, subpubic angle <90°. Determined from fossil or skeleton for forensic anthropology + ancient burials
- **Thigh**: femur (longest + strongest bone. Head, neck, greater + lesser trochanter, linea aspera (posterior ridge, attachment for adductors). Neck fractures = common in elderly (osteoporosis — "fragility fracture of the hip - often from a fall")
- **Leg**: tibia (medial, weight-bearing — tibial tuberosity, medial malleolus ankle. Tibial stress fracture = shin splints progression). Fibula (lateral, non-weight-bearing, lateral malleolus, proximal fibula head = attachment for biceps femoris tendon, common peroneal nerve wraps around neck → peroneal nerve injury)
- **Foot**: tarsals (7 — talus (articulates with tibia/fibula at ankle mortise), calcaneus (heel bone = "heel bone" — calcaneal fracture from fall from height: axial load fractures + may have intra-articular extension). Navicular, cuboid, 3 cuneiforms (medial, intermediate, lateral). Metatarsals (5). Phalanges (14). Sesamoids (2 under big toe in tendon of flexor hallucis brevis). Arch: medial longitudinal = spring ligament, plantar fascia — falling arches = flat feet (pes planus). Plantar fasciitis: inflammation of plantar fascia insertion at calcaneus. Calcaneal fracture: often from landing from a height (jump/dive). Most common foot fracture = metatarsal stress fracture (march fracture) in runners + hikers

## Joints
- **Synovial** (most movable): ball and socket (shoulder/hip). Hinge (knee/elbow). Pivot (neck — atlantoaxial). Condylar/ellipsoid (wrist — radiocarpal). Saddle (thumb CMC joint — gives opposable thumbs). Gliding (intercarpal, acromioclavicular, sternoclavicular, facet joints). Six types determined by shape + movement
  - Ligaments: connect bone to bone. ACL, PCL, MCL, LCL in knee. Stabilize joints. Sprain = stretch/tear. Synovial fluid nourishes cartilage + reduces friction. Bursa: fluid-filled sac reduces friction between bone + tendon/skin. Bursitis = inflammation
- **Cartilaginous**: pubic symphysis, intervertebral discs. Some movement (amphiarthrosis). Strong, slightly flexible
- **Fibrous**: skull sutures, distal tibiofibular joint. No movement (synarthrosis). Bound by dense connective tissue

## Bone Cells & Remodeling
- **Osteoblasts**: build bone. Secrete osteoid (unmineralized bone matrix), become osteocytes when surrounded. Activated by mechanical load + PTH (parathyroid hormone). BONE FORMATION via osteoblast activity
- **Osteoclasts**: resorb (break down) bone. Large, multinucleated. Derived from immune stem cells (macrophage lineage). Activated by RANKL (released by osteoblasts). Osteoporosis: osteoclast activity > osteoblast → net bone loss. RANKL inhibitors (denosumab/Prolia) = osteoporosis treatment
- **Osteocytes**: mature bone cells (former osteoblasts), maintain bone matrix, sense mechanical load + communicate via dendrites through canaliculi. Most numerous bone cell. Mechanosensing: signal via calcium signaling → regulate osteoblast/osteoclast activity. Play crucial role in calcium homeostasis (respond to PTH: release calcium from bone surface into blood)
- **Wolff's law**: bone adapts to load — more stress = more density (weightlifters' bones denser than non-athletes). Disuse = atrophy (astronauts lose 1-2% bone mass per month in microgravity). This is orthopedics/bone adaptation in response to mechanical loading. Sclerostin: protein produced by osteocytes in response to disuse — inhibits bone formation. Sclerostin inhibitors (romosozumab/Evenity) = new osteoporosis drug that BOTH builds bone + reduces resorption
- **Bone types**: cortical/compact (80% of skeletal mass, hard outer layer, slow turnover). Cancellous/trabecular/spongy (20% — honeycomb inside vertebra, hip, ends of long bones. High surface area = faster turnover. First affected by osteoporosis). Good blood supply: cancellous. Fractures across bone types heal differently
- **Fracture healing stages**: hematoma (Days 1-5 — blood clot forms). Soft callus (Week 1-2 — fibrocartilage forms). Hard callus (Week 2-6 — woven bone → lamellar bone). Remodeling (Month 1-2+ — months to years, restoring original shape + strength via osteoclast+osteoblast action parallel to Wolff's law
  - Delayed union: >6 months to heal. Non-union: fails to heal — gap filled with fibrous tissue. Cause: poor blood supply, infection, motion at fracture, smoking (delays union significantly — nicotine constricts microvessels, reduces osteoblast activity), vitamin D deficiency. Treatment: bone graft, bone stimulator (ultrasound/electrical), revision surgery with plate + bone morphogenetic protein (BMP)
