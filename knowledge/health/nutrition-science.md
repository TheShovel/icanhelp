# Nutrition Science & Diet Deep Dive

## Macronutrients Deep Dive

### Carbohydrates
- **Types**: simple sugars (glucose, fructose, sucrose — found in fruit, honey, table sugar. "Simple" = rapidly digested/absorbed. Fructose: from fruit — NOT same as high-fructose corn syrup: natural vs added). Complex/starch (long chains of glucose — bread, pasta, rice, potatoes, legumes. "Complex" = slower digestion, more gradual blood sugar rise). Fiber (indigestible plant carbohydrates — soluble: oats, beans, apples, carrots, psyllium — dissolves in water, forms gel, lowers cholesterol. Insoluble: wheat bran, vegetables, nuts — bulks stool, speeds transit)
  - Glycemic index (GI): how fast a food raises blood sugar (pure glucose = 100). High (>70) vs low (<55). But GI ≠ health — ice cream (low GI from fat) not healthier than watermelon (high GI). Glycemic load (GL) = GI × carbs per serving. More relevant. Apple: GI 40, GL 6. Apple juice: GI 44, GL 12 (more concentrated carb, less fiber)
  - Whole grains: contain all parts of grain (bran + germ + endosperm). Refined grains = only endosperm (stripped of fiber + vitamins + minerals + phytochemicals). Brown rice vs white rice: brown = whole grain with more fiber but very little difference from white rice except bran layer
  - How many carbs? Acceptable range: 45-65% of calories (IOM). 150g+ per day is general target for brain function. Very low carb (<50g) = ketosis (as low as 20g net)

### Protein
- **Amino acids**: 20 total. 9 essential (must get from food). Complete protein = all 9 in adequate amounts (animal proteins: meat, dairy, eggs, soy, quinoa, buckwheat, amaranth, hemp, chia seeds). Incomplete proteins: lack some essential amino acids
  - Complementary protein myth: rice + beans = complete protein. Thought you needed to combine at same meal — proven wrong: body maintains amino acid pool over the day (24-hour window). No need to combine at one meal
- **Quality measures**: PDCAAS (Protein Digestibility Corrected Amino Acid Score — 1.0 = highest: egg white, milk, whey, soy, casein; 0.91 beef; 0.5 wheat gluten). DIAAS (newer, more accurate: takes into account digestibility across the GI tract, a better method for leucine and other essential amino acid availability). Animal proteins generally higher quality than plant proteins on an amino acid scale, but it's easy to meet needs on plant-based diet
- **How much?**: 0.8g/kg (general recommendation, minimum for deficiency prevention). 1.6-2.2g/kg (muscle gain/athletes). 2g/kg max (higher = no extra benefit, turned to fuel). 20-40g per meal spreads protein across day
  - Can excess protein harm kidneys? No (in healthy people. N Engl J Med study + others): higher intakes may be fine for healthy kidneys. For existing kidney disease: moderation. Extra protein = extra calcium loss in urine, but calcium intake offsets, studies don't show net bone loss

### Fats
- **Saturated fat**: solid at room temp — animal fats (butter, lard, red meat), coconut oil, palm oil. Historically linked to heart disease but nuance: replacing sat fat with polyunsaturated = heart benefit. Replacing with carbs (sugar/refined) = no benefit. Current consensus: moderate sat fat (5-10% of calories, not < 5%). Coconut oil raised LDL as much as butter? Somewhat
- **Unsaturated fat**: liquid at room temp. Monounsaturated (MUFA): olive oil, avocado, nuts, canola. Polyunsaturated (PUFA): omega-3 (fish: EPA + DHA = long-chain. ALA in flaxseed + walnuts + chia — humans convert poorly: only 5-10% ALA to EPA, less to DHA. Best source = fatty fish). Omega-6 (soybean, corn, sunflower, safflower oils). Best: replace sat fat with MUFA + PUFA — overall lower CVD + mortality
  - Omega-6 controversy: we eat too much omega-6 → inflammatory? Review: Omega-6 not inherently inflammatory; both omega-6 (linoleic acid) and omega-3s converted to signaling molecules; replacing omega-6 with omega-3 slightly moderates some inflammatory processes, but reducing omega-6 alone not shown to reduce inflammation in humans. Better: balance
- **Trans fat**: artificially hydrogenated oils (partially hydrogenated vegetable oil = PHVO). BANNED in US (2018, FDA). Raised LDL, lowered HDL, increased CVD risk (no safe level). Naturally occurring trans fat (dairy, beef — vaccenic acid) = not harmful at typical intake levels
- **Cholesterol**: dietary cholesterol has MINIMAL effect on blood cholesterol for most people — eggs, shrimp, organ meats no longer considered dangerous. Genetics, sat fat, trans fat have bigger impact. 1-2 eggs/day fine (up to 3 safe)

## Vitamins & Minerals Deep Dive

### Common Deficiencies
- **Vitamin D**: 40%+ of US population deficient. Sources: sunlight (10-30 min midday, variable by latitude + skin tone + season), fortified dairy, fatty fish. Deficiency: bone pain, muscle weakness, depression, impaired immune. Supplement: 600-800 IU/day, up to 2000 IU for darker skin + limited sun. Recommended daily intake: 600 IU for ages 1-70. Test blood level at 30-50 ng/mL. Lower than 30 = deficiency. Many need more to reach optimal range
- **Iron**: most common nutrient deficiency worldwide. Heme (animal-based, well absorbed — red meat, poultry) vs non-heme (plant-based, less absorbed — spinach, beans, fortified grains). Pair non-heme with vitamin C for absorption (add lemon juice to fortified cereals). Deficiency = anemia: fatigue, pale, shortness of breath, brittle nails, pica (craving ice/dirt)
  - Too much iron: men and postmenopausal women can have iron overload (hemochromatosis — genetic, can damage liver + heart). Avoid high dose supplemental iron without testing. Men and non-menstruating women: iron supplements usually not needed; red meat 1-2x/week enough
- **Vitamin B12**: only animal sources (meat, fish, dairy, eggs). Vegans MUST supplement (deficiency takes years to develop, can cause permanent nerve damage + megaloblastic anemia). Plant foods don't contain B12 (notable exception: nutritional yeast if B12-fortified). Recommended supplement for vegans: 25-100 mcg daily or 2000 mcg once/week
- **Iodine**: essential for thyroid function. Sources: iodized salt, seafood, seaweed (some very high in iodine — dose too high may cause thyroid dysfunction). Deficiency: goiter, hypothyroidism, mental impairment. Pregnant women: severe deficiency → fetal brain damage. 150 mcg/day
- **Calcium**: dairy (milk 300mg/cup, yogurt, cheese), fortified plant milks, leafy greens (kale but not all calcium absorbed), sardines w/bones. RDA: 1000mg/day for most adults, 1200 for women 50+/men 70+. Tofu set with calcium: 200-300mg per half cup
- **Magnesium**: dark leafy greens, nuts, seeds, whole grains, dark chocolate, beans. Adrenal + glucose metabolism, muscle + nerve function, sleep. Deficiency: muscle cramps, fatigue, anxiety, poor sleep, heart arrhythmias — common (50% of US below RDA). Supplement: 200-400mg glycinate (most absorbable/gentle)

## Popular Diets — Evidence Review
- **Mediterranean**: HIGHEST evidence for health — reduced CVD, type 2 diabetes, cognitive decline, cancer, mortality. Key components: olive oil, vegetables, fruits, fish, whole grains, legumes, nuts, moderate wine, low red meat + dairy. Multiple large RCTs (PREDIMED, Lyon Diet Heart Study)
  - The PREDIMED trial (Spain, 7,500 participants): Mediterranean + nuts or olive oil = 30% reduction in major cardiovascular events vs low-fat diet
- **DASH**: Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension — low sodium, high potassium/calcium/magnesium. Fruits, vegetables, low-fat dairy, whole grains, lean protein, low red meat + sweets. Proven in RCTs (DASH, OmniHeart) to reduce BP + lipids as well as some medications (systolic -8 to -14 mmHg)
- **Plant-based/vegan**: can be very healthy (if well-planned) — lower BMI, CVD, T2D, some cancers. Risk: B12 deficiency, iron deficiency (for heavy menstruating women), low calcium, low omega-3 (DHA). Well-planned: legumes, whole grains, nuts, seeds, plenty of vegetables. Supplement: B12 (mandatory), DHA (recommended), iron (if at risk)
- **Low-carb/Keto**: effective for weight loss short-term (less hunger, lower insulin). Challenges: sustainability, micronutrient gaps (no whole grains, fruit restriction, low fiber), high sat fat if done with poor food sources (bacon/sausages vs avocado/nuts/fish). Long-term safety: concerns about LDL increase in some individuals (but overall meta-analyses show low-carb vs low-fat similar for weight loss + heart disease effects). Very low carb can raise LDL in "lean mass hyper-responders" (thin people on keto with sky-high LDL — unknown long-term risk)
- **Intermittent fasting**: can be effective for weight loss (calorie restriction). Common: 16:8 (16 hr fast, 8 hr eating window), 5:2 (normal 5 days, 500 cal 2 days), OMAD (one meal a day). Mechanism: reduces eating window = fewer calories. Cellular benefits (autophagy = cellular cleanup). Not superior to continuous calorie restriction for weight loss (same calorie deficit = same outcome). Some find it easier to adhere, impossible for others. Potential issues: binge on eating days, missed morning energy, impaired satiety cues
- **Gluten-free**: essential for celiac disease (1%) and possibly non-celiac gluten sensitivity (~0.5-6%). Not healthier for general population — gluten-free products lower fiber, higher sugar + fat to compensate for taste

## Supplement Evidence
- **WORTH taking**: vitamin D (if deficient, most are). B12 (vegans, elderly, on metformin/PPI). Omega-3 (if not eating fatty fish 2x/week, pregnant women). Magnesium (if deficient; can help with cramps, constipation, sleep). Creatine (athletes + older adults — best researched sports supplement, improves muscle + cognition). Protein powder (convenient for meeting protein targets)
- **EVIDENCE LIMITED**: vitamin C (no cold prevention, may reduce duration slightly — not heart benefits shown. Most get enough from diet). Multivitamins (no mortality benefit in large trials — may cover gaps in poor diet, but no clear improvement in heart disease/cancer/mortality). B-complex (for energy — only if deficient. No extra energy if sufficient). Collagen (hydrolyzed collagen taken orally — very mixed evidence, but small studies for joint + skin. No proven benefit as whole-body protein source). Probiotics (mixed evidence, strain-specific benefits, not general health)
- **AVOID**: weight loss supplements (most unregulated, ineffective, can be dangerous — ephedra banned for safety). Testosterone boosters (don't work — to increase test you need prescription. Fenugreek + D-Aspartic Acid may raise very slightly but not meaningfully). Detox teas (liver + kidneys detox naturally — no food/supplement detox needed). High-dose fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K — can accumulate to toxic levels, especially A and D)
