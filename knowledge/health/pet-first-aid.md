# First Aid for Pets

## Dog First Aid Kit
- Gauze pads + rolled gauze (bandaging)
- Medical tape (self-adhesive/vet wrap is better — won't stick to fur)
- Hydrogen peroxide (3% — for inducing vomiting ONLY on vet instruction)
- Styptic powder or cornstarch (stops nail bleeding if cut too short)
- Digital thermometer (normal dog temp: 101-102.5°F — higher than humans!)
- Tweezers (ticks, splinters, foxtails)
- Muzzle (even friendly dogs bite when in pain — use pantyhose/rope/towel muzzle in emergency)
- Benadryl/diphenhydramine (for mild allergic reactions — 1mg per lb body weight, check with vet first)
- Saline eye wash (flush debris from eyes)
- Vet wrap (Co-flex, stretchy bandage that sticks to itself — doesn't stick to fur)
- Emergency blanket, towel, scissors

## When to Go to Emergency Vet
- **Trauma**: hit by car, fall from height, attacked by another animal, obvious fracture, bleeding that won't stop
  - Move pet carefully on rigid board (splint spine if spinal injury suspected). Dogs: muzzle before moving
- **Difficulty breathing**: open-mouth breathing (abnormal in cats), blue/pale gums, exaggerated chest/abdominal movement
- **Not urinating**: especially male cats (urethral blockage = emergency, fatal within 24-48 hours)
  - Signs: straining in litter box, crying, licking genitals, vomiting
- **Seizure**: lasting >5 minutes, or cluster (multiple within 24 hours). If first seizure: emergency
  - Clear area of hazards, don't put hands near mouth (won't swallow tongue). Time it. Cool down if overheated after
- **Bloated/retching** (large breed dogs): gastric dilatation-volvulus (GDV/stomach twists) — deadly within hours
  - Signs: unproductive retching/dry heaving, distended belly, restless, drooling, rapid breathing — GO NOW
- **Ingested poison**: call vet/Pet Poison Helpline ($85 consultation fee, 24/7: 855-764-7661) or ASPCA Poison Control ($95, 888-426-4435)
- **Hit by car (even if seems fine)**: internal injuries can kill hours later — get checked
- **Heat stroke**: excessive panting, drooling, staggering, collapse, red/purple gums — move to cool area, wet with cool (not cold!) water, offer water, go to vet
  - Cooling too fast (ice water) can cause shock — use cool water, fan, wet towels on neck/paws/groin
- **Bite wounds**: deep puncture (looks small on surface, can be deep abscess) — needs vet (antibiotics, cleaning, possibly drain)

## Common Pet Emergencies

### Bleeding
- **Paw pad**: wrap with gauze + vet wrap + sock (plastic bag over if going outside). Elevate
- **Ear**: bleeds A LOT — gauze pad, wrap head (not too tight, don't cover other ear/eye), vet
- **Nail quick cut**: styptic powder (Kwik-Stop), cornstarch, flour, or press with bar soap. If bleeding >5-10 min: vet
- **Pressure points**: for severe limb bleeding — press artery against bone (inner thigh for hind leg, inside upper foreleg)

### Choking
- **Dog**: check mouth (carefully! — risk of bite). If object visible, try to remove with pliers/tweezers. If not visible: modified Heimlich — small dogs: hold upside down, firm chest thrust. Large dogs: standing, fists under ribcage, thrust forward and up
  - Get to vet immediately if can't dislodge (object may be in trachea, not visible)
- **Cat**: check mouth, sweep with finger (carefully). Hold upside down, firm chest thrust. Heimlich: hold cat with back against you, place fist below ribcage, thrust upward

### Poisoning
- **Common toxins**: chocolate (theobromine — dark/baking chocolate worst), grapes/raisins, xylitol (gum/candy/peanut butter — can kill small amounts!), onions/garlic, ibuprofen/acetaminophen (Tylenol ADVIL/ibuprofen both toxic!), macadamia nuts, alcohol, marijuana/THC (edibles!), antifreeze (ethylene glycol — sweet taste, tiny amount fatal), lilies (CATS — kidney failure, even pollen), sago palm (all parts toxic, dogs), rat poison (anticoagulant)
  - CALL POISON CONTROL FIRST. Do NOT induce vomiting unless instructed (caustic substances cause more damage coming up, some substances don't respond to vomiting)
  - For dogs: hydrogen peroxide 3% (1 tsp per 5 lbs, 4 tsp max) if instructed by vet/professional
  - Never induce vomiting if: unconscious, having seizure, difficulty breathing, ingested sharp object, ingested petroleum product, ingested caustic substance

### Seizures
- Duration <2 min: keep calm, dim lights, remove hazards, time it
- >5 min or multiple in row: emergency
- After seizure: disoriented, may be aggressive — give space, dim lights, quiet. Call vet
- Common causes: epilepsy, toxins, head trauma, low blood sugar, heat stroke, liver/kidney disease, brain tumor

### Heat Stroke
- Signs: excessive panting, drooling, red/purple gums, vomiting, staggering, collapse, seizures
  - Dogs don't sweat (except paw pads) — panting is their only cooling mechanism. Brachycephalic breeds (pugs, bulldogs, Boston terriers) are HIGH risk — can't pant effectively
- First aid: move to shade/AC, cool water on paws/belly/neck/groin (not full body immersion), fan, offer small water if conscious. Stop cooling once temp <103°F to avoid overshoot
  - Do NOT use ice water (constricts blood vessels → traps heat inside)
- Prevention: NEVER leave in parked car (even 70°F day = 110°F inside in 30 min), limit exercise in heat, provide shade + water

### Fractures
- Signs: unable to bear weight, swelling, deformity, pain — dog/cat may bite
- Transport: gently lift onto rigid surface (board, blanket, car floor) — support above and below fracture. Don't try to realign bone
- Muzzle before moving (even best-behaved pets bite when in pain — cats: wrap in towel, leave head exposed for monitoring)

## Routine Health Monitoring
- **Temperature**: dog 101-102.5°F, cat 100.5-102.5°F. Rectal thermometer (lubricated, inserted 1")
  - Hypothermia: <99°F — warm gradually (blankets, warm water bottles wrapped in towel — not direct heat)
  - Fever: >103°F (dog), >103.5°F (cat) — vet visit
- **Heart rate**: small dog 90-140, large dog 60-90, cat 140-220 (at rest). Check on left side behind elbow
- **Gum color**: pink = healthy. Pale/white = shock/internal bleeding. Blue = oxygen deficiency. Brick red = heat stroke/toxic. Yellow-brown = liver issues
  - Capillary refill time: press gum, count seconds for color to return (<2 sec normal, >3 sec = poor circulation)

## Pet CPR
1. **Check responsiveness**: call name, tap gently, check breathing
2. **If not breathing**: clear airway (pull tongue forward, sweep mouth), give rescue breaths
   - Dog: close mouth, breathe into nose (seal with hand). Small: 1 sec breath. Medium/large: 2 sec. Chest rises = effective
   - Cat: breathe into mouth and nose simultaneously
3. **Compressions**: lateral recumbency (on side — cats/dogs barrel-chested), lay on right side, compress widest part of chest (NOT over heart for deep-chested dogs)
   - CPR for dogs/cats improves outcomes but rates of full recovery still low — best for drowning, anesthetic complications, electrical shock. Called "CPCR" now (cardiopulmonary cerebral resuscitation)
4. **Rate**: dogs >30 lbs: 60 compressions/min. Dogs <30 lbs + cats: 100-120/min
5. **Ratio**: 30 compressions : 2 breaths. Continue until vet takes over or pet recovers (check pulse after 2 min)

## Travel Safety
- **Car**: harness or crate (not free-roaming — danger in crash/sudden stop). Never in truck bed (projectile)
  - Airbags: dogs in passenger seat can be killed by airbag deployment — back seat (crated or harnessed) safest
- **Air travel**: cargo hold is dangerous for brachycephalic breeds (pugs, bulldogs — restricted breathing, can't regulate temp) — fly with them in cabin if possible (under seat, carrier)
  - Health certificate from vet (required within 10-30 days of travel, varies by destination)
- **Pet ID**: microchip + collar with tags (phone number). Update info if move. Not microchipped = most shelters can't reunite you
  - 1 in 3 pets get lost in lifetime — microchip = most effective way to get them back
- **Heat**: never leave in car (even 5 min, even with cracked windows). Provide shade, water, limit exercise in heat. Asphalt burns paw pads — test with back of hand (if too hot to hold 5 sec = too hot for paws)
