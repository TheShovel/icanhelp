# Physical Therapy & Rehabilitation

## Common Conditions & Exercises

### Low Back Pain
- **Most common cause**: mechanical (muscle strain, disc issues, facet joint). 80%+ of adults experience at some point. Often resolves in 4-6 weeks without specific treatment (but exercise helps)
  - Red flags (urgent): cauda equina syndrome (loss of bladder/bowel control, saddle anesthesia — numbness in saddle area, both legs weak — surgical emergency). Cancer/metastasis (night pain, weight loss, history of cancer). Infection (fever, IV drug use). Fracture (trauma, osteoporosis, chronic steroid use)
- **Exercises**: pelvic tilts (supine, flatten back to floor, hold 5 sec). Cat-cow (on hands and knees, slow, gentle). Child's pose (knees wide, reach forward). Glute bridge (supine, knees bent, lift hips, squeeze glutes). Prone press-ups (lying on stomach, press up on hands, keep hips on floor — extension improves discogenic pain). Supine knee to chest (one knee at a time, hold 30 sec, feel stretch in lower back/glute)
  - Avoid: toe touches (increases disc pressure, especially for discogenic back pain), sit-ups (loaded flexion on spine — substitute: dead bugs for core). For acute injury: ice 20 min on/2 hr off for 48-72 hours, early motion within pain tolerance (immobilize for > 48 hrs not recommended). Walking: start day 1, as tolerated (gentle motion speeds healing)
- **Core strengthening**: dead bug (supine, legs in tabletop, alternate arm + leg extension while maintaining neutral spine — no back movement). Bird dog (on all fours, extend opposite arm + leg, hold 5 sec, slow, controlled — 0-14 deg deviation. Progress: increase hold time, not speed)

### Neck Pain
- **Common causes**: poor desk posture (forward head), text neck, prolonged computer/laptop use, stress/tension (trapezius tightness). Occasionally disc herniation
  - Red flags: bilateral arm tingling, hand clumsiness (myelopathy = spinal cord compression), headache + vomiting (intracranial something?), fever + stiff neck (Meningitis — Kernig/Brudzinski signs?)
- **Exercises**: chin tucks (sitting or standing, pull head back, make double chin, hold 10 sec. Do frequently throughout day — 10-15 reps every 2 hours. Key exercise for forward head posture. Optional: use fingers to guide head back). Upper trap stretch (sit, one arm behind back, tilt ear to opposite shoulder, hold 30 sec). Levator scapulae stretch (same, but add slight rotation + tilt). Scapular retractions (squeeze shoulder blades together, hold 5 sec). Posture reset (roll shoulders back and down, chin tuck, lift chest. Do 20x/day, 10 sec each)
  - Strengthening: prone Ys + Ts + Ws (lying on stomach, lift arms in Y/T/W positions, squeeze mid-back)

### Knee Pain
- **Patellofemoral Pain** (runner's knee, anterior knee pain — pain behind kneecap with stairs, squatting, prolonged sitting
  - Quad strengthening: straight leg raise (lay flat, tighten quad, lift leg 12-18", hold 10 sec. 15-20 reps, 3 sets. Keep knee straight). Step downs (onto low block, control descent slowly, tap heel, push up. Bad knee up? Lead with good leg descending; step down with bad leg first if needed). Wall sits (back against wall, slide down to 45°, hold 20-45 sec. Avoid 90° if painful)
  - Patellar mobilization: while sitting, push kneecap side to side (up, down, inward, outward) to prevent adhesions. Hold each direction for 10 sec
- **IT band syndrome**: lateral knee pain — hip abductor strengthening essential (side-lying leg lifts, clamshells, standing hip abduction with band). Foam roll IT band (not over knee itself — over the lateral quad/greater trochanter/glute is more helpful). Also: glute medius activation. Stretch: standing IT band stretch (cross good leg in front of affected leg, lean to opposite side)

### Shoulder Pain
- **Rotator cuff**: supraspinatus (most commonly torn — painful arc 60-120° abduction). Impingement: pain with reaching overhead. Subacromial pain syndrome = umbrella term often from impingement, muscle weakness, or poor mechanics
  - Pendulum exercises (lean over, let arm hang, small circles clockwise/counter — gentle, early rehab, no resistance). Theraband ER (elbow at side, 90° bend, external rotation with band). Scapular setting (squeeze blades down+back). Prone horizontal abduction (lying stomach, thumb up, raise arm to side). Wall walks (fingers up wall, walk arm up sideways) for restoring overhead ROM
  - Avoid: overhead press, pull-down behind head, heavy internal rotation exercise. Never stretch across body in painful arc: sidelying sleeper stretch for posterior capsule tightness if present
- **Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis)**: gradual loss of motion (capsule tightens). 3 phases: freezing (painful, progressive loss 2-9 months), frozen (stiff 4-12 months), thawing (gradual return 5-24 months). PT: gentle passive stretching (not aggressive — can worsen). Do not force through: capsular stretching with low load, prolonged holds (5 min+ in each direction). Can take 1-2 years for improvement. Treat: NSAIDs, PT, sometimes cortisone injection (for pain, not cure), hydrodilatation (inject distend capsule), surgical release (capsular release) for refractory cases

### Ankle Sprain
- **Grade I (stretch)**: RICE (rest, ice, compression, elevation). Crutches if painful to bear weight generally not needed. 1-3 weeks recovery. WBAT: weight bearing as tolerated with crutches if needed
- **Grade II (partial tear)**: RICE + crutches initially (2-5 days). Splint/ brace (lace-up ankle brace for proprioception). PT: range of motion (alphabet with foot, towel stretch), balance (single-leg stance), strengthening (band ankle eversion — resisted side push of foot — to strengthen peroneals, key for lateral ankle stability). Recovery 4-8 weeks
  - Proprioception training: single leg stand (eyes open → eyes closed → unstable surface). Lateral hop to single leg (2-3 weeks post-injury)
- **Grade III (complete tear)**: immobilize 5-7 days, brace, PT, no early full weight. Some require surgical repair (young athletes, high demand, recurrent instability). Recovery 3-6 months

### Post-Surgery Recovery Principles
- **Inflammation phase** (0-3 days): RICE, gentle passive range of motion (PROM — moving joint without using muscle — via therapist or continuous passive motion machine). No active range of motion for muscle/tendon repair
- **Repair phase** (3 days - 6 weeks): protected motion (PROM, then active assisted). Avoid loading repaired structure. Gradual increase range of motion. Scar management: massage with lotion to desensitize, soften, and reduce adhesions. Keep incision moisturized but dry
- **Remodeling phase** (6 weeks - 12 months): strengthening, return to functional activities, sport-specific training, balance + proprioception. Maturation of scar tissue, recovery of full strength (may take 6-12 months depending on surgery type — ACL graft maturation 6-12 months)
- **General**: ice after exercise (if inflamed). Heat before (to loosen muscles). Listen to pain: differentiate "good pain" (stretch, muscle soreness) vs "bad pain" (sharp, joint pain, pinching). Don't push through bad pain

## Therapeutic Modalities
- **ICE**: acute injury, after activity, inflammation, pain. 15-20 min, wrap ice pack in thin towel. Never apply directly to skin (ice burn). Can repeat every 2 hours
- **HEAT**: before activity, chronic stiffness, muscle spasms (>48 hr post-injury). 15-20 min, moist heat (hydrocollator) > dry. Don't use if acute swelling or inflammation
- **TENS** (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation): pain modulation (gate control theory). Electrodes near pain site, mild electrical current. Short-term pain relief, some evidence for chronic pain, not for acute tissue injury. Contraindications: pacemaker, pregnancy (not over abdomen), epilepsy, over carotid sinus/ eyes/ heart
- **Ultrasound**: deep heating (tendons, ligaments). Mixed evidence — not shown to significantly speed recovery as stand-alone (systematic reviews show limited to no benefit over placebo for most conditions). Used by many PTs in conjunction with exercise. Phonophoresis: ultrasound to deliver topical medication (anti-inflammatory gel) through skin
- **Kinesiotape**: elastic tape applied to skin. Supports muscles + joints without restricting motion (unlike athletic tape). Claims: improved circulation, reduced pain, enhanced performance (thin evidence). Might help with proprioception, some pain reduction (but not curative). Mostly used by trend > science. Athletic tape (non-stretch) = for joint immobilization, stability

## Assistive Devices
- **Cane**: hold in OPPOSITE hand of painful/injured leg (normal gait: right leg + left arm swing together naturally. Cane in opposite hand mimics natural gait pattern). Correct height: wrist crease level when standing with cane at side. Walk: cane + bad leg = advance together, then good leg advances. 3-point gait: cane + bad leg together, then good leg. For bilateral weakness: walker or crutches
- **Crutches**: axillary (underarm — cheap, common, risk of nerve palsy if rest on armpits. Weight on hands, NOT armpits. Hand grip at wrist crease height). Forearm (Canada crutches — for long-term use, more natural gait). Platform (for hand/wrist injury — forearm rests on platform). Going up stairs: good leg first, then crutches + bad leg together. Down stairs: crutches + bad leg first, then good leg. Remember: up with the good, down with the bad
- **Knee brace**: prophylactic (prevent injury — for sports). Functional (ACL deficiency/recovery). Patellofemoral (strap below kneecap, offloads patellar tendon — for Osgood-Schlatter, runner's knee). Unloader (for osteoarthritis — takes load off arthritic knee compartment, reduces pain significantly)
