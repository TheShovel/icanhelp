# Sleep Hygiene Practical Guide

## Core Habits
- Keep wake time consistent, even after a bad night.
- Get outdoor light soon after waking when possible.
- Avoid caffeine late in the day; many people need an 8–10 hour cutoff before bed.
- Keep the room cool, dark, and quiet.
- Use the bed mainly for sleep and intimacy, not work or scrolling.

## Wind-Down Routine
A simple 20–45 minute routine helps signal sleep. Dim lights, stop stressful tasks, prepare tomorrow’s essentials, read something calm, stretch lightly, or take a warm shower. Repeat the same order most nights.

## Screens and Light
Bright light at night delays sleep signals. Reduce screen brightness, use night mode, and avoid intense content close to bed. If you wake during the night, keep lights dim and avoid checking notifications.

## If You Cannot Sleep
If you are awake for roughly 20–30 minutes and frustrated, get out of bed and do a quiet low-light activity. Return when sleepy. This prevents training the brain to associate bed with wakeful stress.

## Naps
Keep naps short, usually 10–30 minutes, and avoid late afternoon naps if they make bedtime harder. If insomnia is active, skipping naps temporarily can rebuild sleep pressure.

## Exercise
Regular exercise improves sleep quality. Morning or afternoon exercise works for most people. Intense late-night workouts can delay sleep for some, while gentle stretching may help.

## Alcohol and Food
Alcohol can make you sleepy but fragments sleep later. Heavy meals near bedtime can worsen reflux and discomfort. If hungry, choose a small snack rather than a large meal.

## When to Seek Medical Help
Seek medical advice for loud snoring with pauses in breathing, morning headaches, severe daytime sleepiness, restless legs, chronic insomnia lasting weeks, sudden sleep changes, or sleep problems linked to depression, anxiety, pain, medication, or substance use.

## Tracking Without Obsessing
Track bedtime, wake time, caffeine, naps, alcohol, exercise, and sleep quality for 1–2 weeks. Use patterns to adjust habits. Avoid staring at sleep scores every morning if it increases anxiety.
