# Estate Planning

Wills, trusts, powers of attorney, and end-of-life planning.

## Key Documents
- **Will (Last Will and Testament)**: Directs distribution of assets after death. Names executor (person who administers estate). Names guardians for minor children. Must be signed by testator + 2 witnesses (not beneficiaries). If no will (intestate), state law determines distribution — spouse gets 50-100%, children split remainder, no provision for unmarried partners
- **Revocable living trust**: Holds assets during life, avoids probate (private, faster, cheaper than court). Grantor controls assets as trustee during life. Successor trustee takes over upon death/incapacity. Pour-over will transfers any remaining assets into trust
- **Durable power of attorney (financial)**: Authorizes agent to manage finances if you become incapacitated. Can be "springing" (takes effect on incapacity) or immediate. Without POA, family must go to court for conservatorship
- **Advance healthcare directive**: Living will (states end-of-life treatment preferences) + healthcare power of attorney (appoints agent to make medical decisions). In some states combined. Specifies life support, DNR (do not resuscitate), feeding tubes, pain management
- **HIPAA authorization**: Allows designated people to access medical records. Needed separately from healthcare POA in some states
- **Beneficiary designations**: On retirement accounts (401k, IRA), life insurance, payable-on-death (POD) accounts. Override will. Keep updated after major life changes (marriage, divorce, birth)

## Probate
- **Court process** for validating will, distributing assets, paying debts. Takes 6-18 months average. Costs 3-7% of estate value in legal fees + court costs. Public record — anyone can see your assets and who inherits
- **Avoiding probate**: Revocable living trust, joint ownership with right of survivorship, POD/TOD (transfer-on-death) accounts, beneficiary designations. Small estates (varies by state, typically <$50k-$200k) may have simplified probate process
- **Executor responsibilities**: File will with court, notify beneficiaries and creditors, inventory and appraise assets, pay debts/taxes, distribute remaining assets, file final income tax return

## Trusts
- **Revocable living trust**: Can change or revoke anytime. No income tax benefit. Assets still in your estate for estate tax. Avoids probate. Most common
- **Irrevocable trust**: Cannot change or revoke (generally). Assets removed from estate for tax/creditor protection. Income tax paid by trust (higher brackets). Used for: Medicaid planning, life insurance trusts (ILIT), charitable trusts, asset protection
- **Special needs trust**: Provides for disabled beneficiary without disqualifying from government benefits (SSI, Medicaid). Trust pays for extras (education, travel, recreation) that benefits don't cover
- **Testamentary trust**: Created within will — takes effect after death. Used for minor children's inheritance (held until age specified, usually 25-30)
- **QTIP trust**: For married couples — provides income to surviving spouse, remainder goes to grantor's chosen beneficiaries (children from prior marriage). Defer estate tax until spouse dies

## Estate Tax
- **Federal estate tax**: 2024 exemption $13.61 million per person ($27.22 million married couple). Rates 18-40% over exemption. Portability: unused exemption passes to surviving spouse
- **State estate/inheritance tax**: 12 states + DC have estate tax (exemption $1M-$12.92M). 6 states have inheritance tax (tax paid by beneficiaries, based on relationship to deceased). Some states both
- **Gift tax**: Lifetime gifts reduce estate tax exemption. Annual exclusion: $18,000 per recipient per year (2024) — tax free. Unlimited gifts to spouse (US citizen), tuition/medical paid directly to provider, charitable gifts
- **Portability**: File IRS Form 706 even if estate below exemption — without filing, unused exemption lost

## Intestacy (Dying Without a Will)
- Distribution varies by state. Typical: spouse gets 50-100% depending on children. If no spouse and no children, parents inherit. If no parents, siblings. If no siblings, grandparents, then aunts/uncles, then state (escheat)
- Unmarried partner gets nothing — one of biggest reasons to have a will
- Court appoints administrator (usually spouse or next of kin) and guardian for children — may not be who you'd choose

## Life Insurance
- **Term life**: Pure death benefit, no cash value. 10, 20, or 30 year level term. Cheapest. Sufficient for most people (income replacement, mortgage payoff, college funding)
- **Whole life**: Permanent coverage + cash value component. Much more expensive. Rarely optimal — invest difference in term + index funds
- **How much**: 10-12x annual income. More if young children, mortgage, or special needs dependents. Calculator: future expenses (college, mortgage) + income replacement - current assets

## Final Arrangements
- **Funeral costs**: Average $8-10k (traditional burial). Direct cremation $1-2k. Pre-plan and pre-pay to lock in prices and relieve family burden
- **Body disposition**: Burial (casket, cemetery plot, vault, marker — $7k avg), cremation (urn, scattering/columbarium — $2k), green burial (natural decomposition, no embalming — conservation burial grounds), body donation to science (free)
- **Digital estate**: Make list of online accounts, passwords, digital assets (photos, crypto, domains, social media). Facebook legacy contact. Digital executor provisions in will
