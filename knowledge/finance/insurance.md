# Insurance: Types & How to Choose

## Core Principles
- Insure against **catastrophic losses** you can't absorb (house fire, lawsuit, major medical)
- Don't over-insure trivial things (extended warranties on small electronics)
- Higher deductibles = lower premiums, but keep deductible emergency fund
- Shop policies every 2–3 years; loyalty is rarely rewarded
- Check insurer financial strength (AM Best A+, S&P AA- minimum)
- Bundle auto + home for multi-policy discount (typically 10–25%)

## Health Insurance

### US System
- **Employer-sponsored**: most common, employer pays part of premium (average ~80% of single, ~70% of family)
- **Marketplace/ACA**: HealthCare.gov or state exchange, income-based subsidies (premium tax credits)
- **Medicaid**: income-based, free/low-cost, varies by state (expanded in 40 states under ACA)
- **Medicare**: 65+ or disabled, Parts A (hospital, free if you paid Medicare taxes 10+ years), B (medical, ~$175/mo 2024), D (prescriptions). Medicare Advantage (Part C) = private plan alternative, often includes dental/vision

### Key Terms
- **Premium**: monthly payment (you pay this regardless of whether you use care)
- **Deductible**: amount you pay before insurance starts paying (e.g., $1,500/year)
- **Copay**: fixed $ per visit ($30 for primary care, $60 for specialist)
- **Coinsurance**: % you pay after deductible (20%)
- **Out-of-pocket maximum**: most you'll pay in a year (~$8,000-9,000 individual). After this, insurance pays 100%
- **In-network vs out-of-network**: in-network = negotiated rates, less expensive. Out-of-network = much more expensive

### Plan Types
- **HDHP** (High Deductible Health Plan): lower premium, higher deductible ($1,600+/individual). Eligible for HSA (Health Savings Account). Best for young/healthy people who want to save for future
- **PPO**: more expensive, more flexible (see any doctor, no referral needed). Network discounts apply, out-of-network partial coverage (usually 70% after deductible)
- **HMO**: cheaper, need PCP (primary care physician) referral for specialist, limited to network. Best for cost-conscious patients

## Auto Insurance
- **Liability**: required by law — covers damage you cause to others. Minimum limits vary by state (commonly 25/50/25 = $25k per person, $50k per accident bodily injury, $25k property damage. Minimum coverage = too low, get 100/300/100)
- **Collision**: covers damage to YOUR car from accident (regardless of fault). Required if financing/leasing. Skip if car value < $5,000 (premium > potential payout)
- **Comprehensive**: covers non-collision damage (theft, vandalism, hail, deer, tree, fire). Also required for financed cars
- **Uninsured/Underinsured motorist**: covers you when hit by someone without enough insurance (hit-and-run too). 12%+ of drivers are uninsured
- **Rental reimbursement**: pays for rental car while yours is being repaired. Usually $30-50/day
- **Roadside assistance**: towing, flat tire, lockout, dead battery. Often cheaper than AAA ($5-15/year vs AAA $65-120)

## Homeowners/Renters Insurance
- **HO-3 (homeowners)**: covers dwelling, other structures, personal property, liability, loss of use. Named perils for property (excludes flood, earthquake, war, nuclear hazard — need separate flood insurance)
  - Replacement cost vs actual cash value: replacement = new for old (better but more expensive). Actual cash value = depreciated value (cheaper)
  - Water damage: covered if sudden (burst pipe). Not covered: flood (separate policy needed), gradual leak (maintenance issue). Sewer backup: separate rider
  - Liability: covers if someone sues you for injury on your property. Usually $100k-300k base; umbrella policy for $1M+ (purchase 1M umbrella policy costing ~$150-300/year)
- **Renters insurance**: covers personal property + liability + loss of use. Landlord's insurance covers the building, not your stuff. Very cheap (~$15/month for $30k coverage + $100k liability). Get it
- **Flood insurance**: separate NFIP policy (government, up to $250k building, $100k contents. Not enough for full replacement. Private flood insurance: faster, more coverage options). Required if in flood zone + federally backed mortgage. Not in flood zone? Still ~25% of flood claims come from low/moderate-risk areas

## Life Insurance
- **Term life**: pure insurance, fixed term (10/20/30 years). Lowest cost. Best for most people. Buy 30-year term to cover until kids are grown + mortgage paid off. Rule of thumb: 10-12x annual income
  - Cost: $30-50/month for $1M policy (30-year-old healthy non-smoker). Massive peace of mind for minimal cost
- **Whole life**: insurance + investment component. Very expensive, poor returns, high commissions. Almost always worse than "buy term and invest the difference" (Boglehead philosophy). Only makes sense for: estate planning (taxable estate >$13M), small business buy-sell funding, high-net-worth with already-maxed-out all other tax-advantaged accounts
- **Universal/variable life**: even more complex, higher fees, worse performance. Avoid unless very specific need and you understand the product

## Disability Insurance
- **More likely to become disabled than die** during working years (25% of workers will be disabled for 90+ days before retirement). Social Security Disability = very hard to qualify, only covers total disability, not partial. You need own-occupation coverage (pays if you can't do YOUR job, not any job)
  - Employer-provided: group coverage, often limited (60% of salary, may be taxable if employer pays premium). Typically short-term (3-6 months) then long-term (to age 65). Varies. If employer pays premium: benefits taxable. If you pay after-tax: benefits tax-free
  - Individual policy: own-occupation definition (if can't do your specific job, you qualify — even if you can do other work). More expensive but much better coverage. 60% of income, $2,000-4,000/year for $5k/month benefit (30yo professional). Get own-occupation, inflation protection, residual/partial disability rider. Must have it for any serious financial security
  - Waiting period: 90 days (longer = cheaper premium). Policy period: "to age 65" standard (then usually Medicare/SS becomes primary)

## Umbrella Insurance
- **$1M+ liability coverage** that sits on top of auto + homeowners (kicks in when underlying policies exhausted)
- Relatively cheap ($150-400/year for $1M). Essential if you have assets to protect (house, investments, future income). Covers: libel/slander, false arrest, rental unit liability, worldwide coverage

## When to Switch Providers
- Life changes (marriage, kids, new house, new car)
- Annual premium increases >10% without claims
- Poor claims service (find out from reviews before buying, not after hailstorm)
- Shop every 2-3 years, get 3+ quotes. Use independent agent (accesses multiple carriers) vs captive (one company, e.g., State Farm, Allstate, Farmers). Captive = less choice. Online comparison (Policygenius, Compare.com, The Zebra) can help
- Credit score impacts insurance rates — maintain good credit (except California, Michigan, Washington, Massachusetts which restrict use). In most states, poor credit = 2x+ premium for auto/home
