# Investing: Stocks, Bonds & Portfolio

## Stock Analysis

### Fundamental Analysis
- **Earnings**: revenue (top line). Net income (profit). EPS (earnings per share: net income / shares outstanding). EPS growth = stock price growth driver over long term. P/E ratio (price / earnings): PE = price / EPS. High P/E = expectations of high growth (overvalued?). Low P/E = undervalued? Not that simple — compare to industry + historical. Earnings yield = E/P = 1/PE. More meaningful for comparing to bonds: if earnings yield > bond yield, stocks may be attractive
  - PEG ratio = P/E / growth rate. <1 = possibly undervalued. Requires accurate growth estimation (forward guidance)
- **Valuation**: market cap (price × shares outstanding). Enterprise value (market cap + debt - cash). EV/EBITDA: valuation metric (enterprise value / earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization). P/B (price / book value per share — for banks/ financial). P/S (price / sales — for high-growth unprofitable companies). DCF (discounted cash flow) — project future cash flows, discount to present value. Most accurate but most assumptions
- **Profitability**: gross margin (revenue - COGS / revenue). Operating margin (operating income / revenue). Net margin (net income / revenue). ROE (return on equity: net income / shareholder equity). ROIC (return on invested capital — better metric, measures efficiency of using all capital)
- **Financial health**: debt-to-equity, current ratio (current assets / current liabilities), interest coverage ratio (operating income / interest expense). Dividend yield (annual dividend / stock price). Payout ratio (% of earnings paid as dividends)

### Technical Analysis
- **Trend**: higher highs + higher lows = uptrend. Moving averages (50-day, 200-day). Golden cross (50 crosses above 200) = bullish. Death cross (50 crosses below 200) = bearish. Support: price floor. Resistance: price ceiling. Breakout = breaks resistance
- **Volume**: high volume + price move = strong signal. Low volume = weak signal. Volume precedes price
- **Indicators**: RSI (relative strength index 0-100: >70 overbought, <30 oversold). MACD (moving average convergence divergence: signal line crossover). Bollinger Bands (standard deviation envelope around moving average; price hitting bands = overextended). Stochastic oscillator. RIS comparison: all lagging
- **Limitations**: technical analysis is not predictive — seeing patterns in noise. Markets are largely random (Efficient Market Hypothesis — weak form). But trends can persist for behavioral reasons (herding, anchoring, momentum)

### Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)
- **Weak**: past prices don't predict future (technical analysis useless). Semi-strong: all public info already priced in (fundamental analysis useless — stock price reflects all known data instantly). Strong: even insider info already priced in (impossible). Reality: markets are mostly efficient, but behavioral biases + information asymmetry create opportunities (momentum, value, quality factors exist across long periods)
  - Active vs passive: overwhelming evidence (SPIVA scorecard) that >90% of active fund managers underperform their benchmark over 10+ years. This is why index funds dominate for most investors

## Behavioral Finance
- **Loss aversion**: losing $100 hurts 2x more than gaining $100 feels good. Leads to holding losers too long (hoping to break even) + selling winners too soon (locking in small gains, missing further upside). Prospect Theory (Kahneman+Tversky)
- **Confirmation bias**: seek info that confirms existing beliefs. Ignore contradictory evidence. For investors: only read bullish analysis for stock you own
- **Recency bias**: recent events overweighed. "This time is different" — it rarely is. Investors buy what's performed well lately, sell what's done poorly (chasing performance → buy high, sell low)
- **Overconfidence**: men trade 45% more than women — resulting in lower returns (Barber+Odean). Overconfident investors trade too much, overtrading costs (commissions, bid-ask spread) eat returns
- **Herding**: following the crowd. Buying during manias (crypto 2021, tulips, tech bubble 2000). Selling during panics (March 2020). "Be fearful when others are greedy, greedy when others are fearful" — Warren Buffett
- **Anchoring**: fixate on a reference point (stock's 52-week high, purchase price). "I'll sell when it gets back to $100" — may never happen. Should evaluate based on future prospects, not past price

## Portfolio Management

### Asset Allocation by Age & Goal
- **Rule of 110**: (110 - age) = % in stocks, rest in bonds. For retirement. 25yo: 85% stocks/15% bonds. 55yo: 55/45. Aggressive alternative: 120-age. Conservative: 100-age
- **Three-fund portfolio**: total US stock (VTI, 45-60%). Total international stock (VXUS, 20-30%). Total US bond (BND, 10-30%). Accounts for >70% of your invested assets. Simpler than 15 funds, outperforms most. Rebalance once/year
- **Target-date fund**: automatically adjusts allocation (more stocks = young, more bonds = old). Set it and forget it. Vanguard Target Retirement 2050 (VFIFX). Expense ratio ~0.08%. Downside: slightly more conservative than optimal for some. Also hold bonds early (some prefer 100% stocks, no bonds until 40)
- **Tax-efficient placement**: put bonds (taxable interest) in tax-advantaged accounts (IRA/401k). Put stocks (qualified dividends, capital gains — lower tax rate, potential tax loss harvesting) in taxable account. REITs in IRA (non-qualified dividends, tax-inefficient — REIT dividends treated as ordinary income). International: may want in taxable to claim foreign tax credit

### Rebalancing
- When allocation drifts >5% from target : sell overperformers, buy underperformers. Frequency: annually, or when threshold breached. Skip rebalancing if it triggers taxes? New contributions/ dividends: deploy to underweight asset first (avoid selling). If drift continues, sell at year-end to reset. Backtesting: rebalancing improves returns slightly vs never rebalancing, but mainly reduces risk

### Currency & International Diversification
- **Why international**: US exceptionalism may not continue. Currency diversification. Different economic cycles. Non-US stocks are CHEAPER than US (P/E ~13x vs ~22x US for 2024). International includes companies like Toyota, Nestlé, Samsung, ASML, Novo Nordisk. In any 10-year period, either US or International tends to lead — you don't know which will outperform next
  - VXUS (total international) or IXUS. Developed only: VEA. Emerging: VWO. Historically: 60/40 US/international optimum for maximum Sharpe? Actually (varying figures). Many US investors under-allocated to international (home country bias) because US had huge outperformance 2010-2024. But that's recency bias

### Factor Investing
- Factors that historically outperform market: value (cheap stocks, P/B, P/E). Momentum (recent winners continue). Quality (high profitability, stable earnings, low debt, consistent margins). Size (small cap). Low volatility. These factors have worked across countries + decades, but periods of underperformance can last a decade. Factor ETFs: AVUV (small cap value), QMOM (momentum), QUAL (quality)
