# Investing Deep Dive

## Asset Classes
- **Stocks** (equities): own part of a company — highest long-term return (~10% annually historically) but highest volatility
  - Individual stocks: highest risk/reward — company-specific risk (diversify across 15+ stocks minimum)
  - Index funds/ETFs: S&P 500 (VOO, IVV), total market (VTI), international (VXUS) — instant diversification, low fees
- **Bonds** (fixed income): lend money to government/corporation for interest — lower return (~2-5%), lower risk
  - US Treasury bonds: safest (backed by US government). Corporate bonds: higher yield, default risk
  - Bond duration: longer = higher interest rate sensitivity (price drops when rates rise)
  - Bond funds: (BND, AGG) vs individual bonds (ladder for predictable income)
- **Real estate**: rental property, REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts — trade like stocks)
  - Direct: control, leverage (mortgage), tax benefits (depreciation), hands-on management
  - REITs: passive, liquid, diversified, dividend-paying (must distribute 90% of income)
- **Cash equivalents**: money market funds, high-yield savings, CDs — 3-5% currently, safe, liquid
- **Commodities**: gold, silver, oil, agricultural goods — inflation hedge, no cash flow, speculative
- **Crypto**: Bitcoin, Ethereum — extremely volatile, speculative, not producing anything — "greater fool theory" risk

## Index Funds & Passive Investing
- Most individual stock pickers fail to beat the market consistently (over 90% of professional fund managers underperform S&P 500 over 10+ years)
- **Low-cost index funds** (Vanguard, Fidelity, Schwab): expense ratios 0.03-0.10% vs actively managed funds 0.5-1.5%
  - A 1% fee difference over 30 years reduces final portfolio by ~30%
- **Jack Bogle** (Vanguard founder): pioneer of index investing — "Don't look for the needle in the haystack, just buy the haystack"
- Warren Buffett: 90% S&P 500, 10% bonds in his will for his wife

## Portfolio Allocation (Simplified)
- **Age in bonds**: 30 years old = 30% bonds, 70% stocks. 60 years old = 60% bonds, 40% stocks
  - Too conservative for modern times (longer lifespans, higher returns needed)
- **120 minus age**: 30 years old = 90% stocks, 10% bonds. More aggressive, common recommendation
- **Three-fund portfolio**:
  - Total US stock market (VTI) — % varies
  - Total international stock (VXUS) — 20-40% of stock allocation
  - Total US bond market (BND) — age-based %
- **Target-date fund**: automatically adjusts allocation as you approach retirement (set it and forget it)
  - Example: Vanguard Target 2050 (VFFF): 90% stocks → gradually shifts to 50% stocks by 2050

## Tax-Advantaged Accounts
- **401(k)**: pre-tax contributions (reduces taxable income now), tax-deferred growth, taxed on withdrawal
  - Employer match is free money — contribute at least to match (that's 50-100% return immediately)
  - 2024 limit: $23,000 ($30,500 if 50+)
- **Roth IRA**: after-tax contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals in retirement
  - 2024 limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+). Income limits apply (phase out $146-161k single)
  - Backdoor Roth: contribute to traditional IRA → convert to Roth (for high earners, no income limit)
- **Traditional IRA**: pre-tax, taxed on withdrawal. Income limits for deductibility if covered by workplace plan
- **HSA** (Health Savings Account): triple tax-advantaged — pre-tax in, grows tax-free, tax-free for medical expenses
  - After 65, HSA acts like traditional IRA (taxed on non-medical withdrawals)
  - Best retirement account nobody uses enough — max out before other accounts
- **Taxable brokerage**: after-tax money, tax on dividends/capital gains yearly — for money you need before retirement

## Dollar-Cost Averaging vs Lump Sum
- **Lump sum**: invest all money at once — historically outperforms DCA ~2/3 of the time (time in market > timing the market)
- **DCA**: invest fixed amount on schedule — reduces regret if market drops right after, mentally easier
- **Pick DCA if**: you're anxious about market timing, lump sum makes you nervous, or it's a very large amount
- Historically, lump sum wins 66% of the time, but DCA prevents panic-selling

## Market Crashes & Bear Markets
- Historically: bear markets happen ~every 3-6 years, average decline ~33%, average duration ~10 months
  - Recovery to new highs averages 2+ years
  - Since 1926: S&P 500 has had 20+ bear markets — it has recovered every single time
- **Best advice**: DON'T SELL during downturns. The market recovers, and you lock in losses by selling
  - Actually: BUY during downturns (stocks on sale) if you can
- A $10,000 investment in S&P 500 in 1980 (with dividends reinvested): worth ~$1,000,000+ in 2024
- Missing the 10 best days in the market over 30 years cuts returns in half — those days often cluster around crashes (you're out if you're out)

## Common Investing Mistakes
- **Timing the market**: trying to predict highs and lows — even professionals can't do it consistently
- **Panic selling**: selling during crashes locks in losses — the biggest destroyer of returns is investor behavior (not the market)
- **Chasing performance**: buying what recently did well (it's about to do poorly — mean reversion)
- **No diversification**: all in one stock/crypto/sector — one company failure = life savings gone
- **High fees**: expense ratios, trading commissions, fund loads — all compound against you
- **Day trading**: research shows 97% of day traders lose money over the long run — it's gambling, not investing
- **Too conservative**: keeping all money in cash/savings loses purchasing power to inflation (~3%/year)
- **Too complex**: 15+ funds, constant rebalancing, exotic strategies — a simple 3-fund portfolio outperforms most complex strategies

## Risk Management
- **Asset allocation** determines 90-100% of portfolio volatility — stock/bond split is the most important decision
- **Rebalancing**: sell winners, buy losers to return to target allocation — forces you to buy low, sell high
  - Rebalance annually or when allocation drifts >5% from target
- **Diversification**: across asset classes, geographies, sectors, company sizes
  - 15-30 stocks eliminate most company-specific risk
  - International: 20-40% of stocks (US has outperformed recently, not always)
- **Emergency fund first**: 3-6 months expenses in savings BEFORE investing — don't invest money you may need in 5 years

## Retirement Withdrawal
- **4% rule** (Trinity Study): withdraw 4% of portfolio in first year of retirement (adjusted for inflation yearly) = portfolio lasts 30 years with high probability
  - For 60 year old retiring with $1M: withdraw $40,000/year
  - Current research suggests 3-3.5% is safer (higher valuations, longer retirements, lower expected returns)
- Sequence of returns risk: bad returns in first 5 years of retirement can deplete portfolio even if average returns are fine
  - Solution: have 2-3 years of cash/bonds to spend during downturns (don't sell stocks when they're down)
- Social Security: take at full retirement age (67) or delay to 70 (8%/year increase). Taking early (62) = permanently reduced
- Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs): mandatory withdrawals from 401k/traditional IRA starting at 73 (penalty if not taken)
