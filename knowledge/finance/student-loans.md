# Student Loans

## Federal vs Private Loans

- **Federal loans:** Issued by U.S. Dept of Education (Direct Loan Program). Fixed interest rates set by Congress annually. No credit check (except PLUS). Include income-driven repayment, forgiveness, deferment, forbearance. Discharged on death/ disability. In default: wage garnishment up to 15%, tax refund offset, Social Security offset.
- **Private loans:** Issued by banks/credit unions (Sallie Mae, SoFi, Discover, Navient/others). Variable or fixed rates based on credit (co-signer often needed). No federal protections — no income-driven plans, limited forbearance/deferment. Can be discharged in bankruptcy only with undue hardship (rare). Cosigner stays liable if borrower defaults.
- **Interest rate comparison (2024–25):** Federal undergrad Direct: ~5.5%. Grad/PLUS: ~7.05%–8.08%. Private: 4%–15%+ depending on credit. Variable rates can increase over loan term.
- **Borrowing limits:** Federal: $5,500–$12,500/yr undergrad (dependent), $20,500/yr grad (unsubsidized only). Aggregate cap: $31,000 (dependent undergrad) to $138,500 (grad + undergrad). Private: up to cost of attendance minus other aid.

## Subsidized vs Unsubsidized

- **Subsidized (Direct Subsidized Loan):** Available only to undergrads with financial need. **Government pays interest while you're in school at least half-time, during grace period (first 6 months after leaving), and during deferment.** Interest subsidy saves thousands over loan term.
- **Unsubsidized (Direct Unsubsidized Loan):** Available to undergrads and grads regardless of need. **Interest accrues from day of disbursement** — capitalizes (added to principal) if unpaid when repayment begins. Grad PLUS and Parent PLUS are always unsubsidized.
- **Grace period:** 6 months after graduating, leaving school, or dropping below half-time. Subsidized: interest-free. Unsubsidized: interest accrues, capitalize at end of grace. Private: 6 months or none (check terms).
- **Interest capitalization triggers:** Entering repayment after grace, entering forbearance, leaving income-driven plan, loan consolidation. Capitalization increases total loan cost.

## Repayment Plans (Federal)

- **Standard Repayment Plan:** Fixed payments over 10 years (or 10–30 years for consolidated). Highest monthly payment, least total interest. Default plan if no other selected.
- **Graduated Repayment Plan:** Payments start low, increase every 2 years. 10-year term. Pay more interest overall (payments cover interest-only early on). Good for low starting salary.
- **Extended Repayment Plan:** Fixed or graduated over 25 years. Only available if >$30,000 in direct loans. Lower monthly, much more total interest.
- **Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) Plans:**

| Plan | Payment | Forgiveness Term | Who Qualifies |
|------|---------|-----------------|---------------|
| SAVE (Saving on a Valuable Education) | 5–10% discretionary income (new plan replacing REPAYE). No interest growth if payment covers interest. | 20 yr (undergrad only), 25 yr (any grad loans) | All Direct Loan borrowers (undergrad and grad) |
| PAYE (Pay As You Earn) | 10% discretionary income, capped at Standard 10-yr amount | 20 yr | New borrowers as of 2007, must demonstrate partial financial hardship |
| IBR (Income-Based Repayment) | 10–15% discretionary income, capped at Standard amount | 20 yr (new borrowers after 2014), 25 yr (older) | Partial financial hardship required |
| ICR (Income-Contingent Repayment) | 20% discretionary income or 12-yr fixed, whichever lower | 25 yr | Parent PLUS loans (after consolidation) |
- **Discretionary income:** For SAVE/PAYE: AGI minus 225% federal poverty guideline (225% FPL). For IBR: AGI minus 150% FPL. Payment recertification required annually. Recertify on time — late recert resets plan, interest capitalizes.
- **SAVE plan benefits:** No unpaid interest growth (if payment covers monthly interest, remainder subsidized). Lower-than-calculated payments for undergrad borrowers (5% discretionary income). Spousal income excluded if filing separately.
- **IDR forgiveness:** After 20–25 years of qualifying payments, remaining balance forgiven. DISCLAIMER: Under 2025 law, IDR forgiveness may be taxable (as of now, tax-free through 2025 under American Rescue Plan — state tax may apply in some states).

## Forgiveness Programs

- **Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF):** Requires working full-time for a qualifying employer (government, 501(c)(3) non-profit, Americorps/Peace Corps). 120 qualifying monthly payments under IDR plan while working full-time. Remaining balance forgiven tax-free. Must be Direct Loans (not FFEL/ Perkins — consolidate to Direct). Employment certification form submitted annually — recommended. **Most rejections due to wrong payment plan** (not IDR) or wrong loan type (need Direct Consolidation).
- **PSLF eligible employers:** Federal/state/local/tribal government. 501(c)(3) non-profit. AmeriCorps, Peace Corps. NOT partisan political organizations, labor unions, for-profit hospitals, even if they serve public good. Full-time = 30+ hr/week or employer's definition of full-time.
- **Teacher Loan Forgiveness:** Up to $17,500 for highly qualified teachers who teach 5 consecutive years in low-income school. Subject area depends on loan type. Can't use same years for PSLF.
- **IDR forgiveness vs PSLF:** IDR forgiveness = 20–25 yrs, taxable (after 2025). PSLF = 10 yrs, always tax-free. PSLF counts any full-time qualifying employment, not just public service — but employer type matters.
- **Closed school discharge / disability discharge:** If school closes while enrolled (or within 180 days of leaving), loans discharged. Total and permanent disability (TPD) discharge requires documentation from VA, SSA, or physician. Subject to 3-year post-discharge monitoring period (earnings limits).

## Consolidation vs Refinancing

- **Federal Direct Consolidation Loan:** Combine multiple federal loans into one. New weighted average interest (rounded up to nearest 1/8%). Lose credit for IDR payments if consolidating (temporary issue — can regain under IDR adjustment). Only federal loans. Benefits: single payment, access to IDR plans, can make Parent PLUS eligible for ICR/PSLF.
- **Private refinancing:** Replace federal loans (and/or private) with single private loan. Interest rate based on credit (possibly lower). **Loses all federal protections:** IDR, deferment, forbearance, forgiveness (PSLF, death/discharge). Only refinance federal loans if certain you won't need federal benefits. Refinance variable-rate private loans to fixed if rates are lower.
- **Refinancing strategy:** If stable high income, refinance at low fixed rate. If uncertain income or pursuing PSLF/IDR, keep federal. Never refinance federal loans to private and expect to go back.
- **Consolidation bonus:** Married couples can NOT consolidate jointly (ended 2006). Consolidation can combine Parent PLUS with Direct (makes Parent PLUS eligible for ICR/PSLF). Act now: IDR account adjustment counts pre-consolidation payments toward forgiveness if consolidated before deadline (usually 2024–25 window).

## Default Consequences

- **Definition:** No payment for 270+ days (Direct Loans) or 360 days (Perkins). Status reported to credit bureaus (severe derogatory). Federal loan default triggers collection fees up to 25% of balance, plus wage garnishment (15% of disposable income), tax refund offset, Social Security offset (up to 15% of benefits), and loss of eligibility for further aid/IDR.
- **Rehabilitation (one-time):** Make 9 on-time agreed monthly payments within 20 days of due date over 10 consecutive months. Amount based on income (usually $5–$50/month). After rehab: loans removed from default, collection fees waived, credit reporting updated to "paid as agreed." Only once per loan.
- **Consolidation out of default:** If rehab not yet defaulted (i.e., early default), can consolidate into Direct Loan (must make 3 consecutive agreed monthly payments first or sign up for IDR). If seriously defaulted, must rehab first.
- **Lump sum rehab payment:** Can pay all 9 months at once (request early rehab buyout). Must call servicer.
- **Statute of limitations:** Federal student loans have no statute of limitations — government can pursue indefinitely (wage garnishment, offset, etc.). Private loans have state SOL for lawsuits (3–10 years typically). But default still hurts credit.

## Deferment vs Forbearance

- **Deferment (subsidized benefit):** Postpone payments. Subsidized loans: interest not accrued during deferment. Eligibility: in-school at least half-time, unemployment (up to 3 years), economic hardship (Peace Corps, active duty), cancer treatment. Must apply with documentation.
- **Forbearance (general and mandatory):** Postpone or reduce payments. Interest accrues on ALL loan types (capitalizes at end). General forbearance up to 12 months, renewable up to 3 years (borrower request). Mandatory forbearance must be granted if: medical/dental residency, National Guard duty, teaching service that would qualify for Teacher Loan Forgiveness, payments >20% discretionary income.
- **Which is better:** Deferment is strongly preferred (no interest accrual on subsidized). Forbearance should be last resort — interest capitalization increases principal and total cost.
- **Current forbearance abuse:** During COVID payment pause (ended 2023), many borrowers had admin forbearance — zero interest, no payment credit toward PSLF or IDR forgiveness? Yes — CARES Act payments counted as qualifying payments for PSLF/IDR ($0 payments counted).
