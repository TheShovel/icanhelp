# Credit Score Practical Guide

## What Affects Scores
Major factors usually include payment history, credit utilization, length of credit history, account mix, and recent hard inquiries. Payment history and utilization often matter the most.

## Payment History
Pay at least the minimum by the due date. Autopay minimums can prevent accidental late payments. A payment usually must be 30+ days late before it is reported as late, but fees and penalties can happen earlier.

## Utilization
Utilization is balance divided by credit limit. Lower is generally better. Paying card balances before the statement closes can reduce reported utilization. High utilization on one card can hurt even if total utilization is moderate.

## Building Credit
- Open one simple no-fee card or secured card if needed.
- Use it for a small recurring purchase.
- Pay in full every month.
- Keep old no-fee accounts open if they are not causing problems.
- Avoid applying for many accounts in a short time.

## Checking Reports
Review credit reports for accounts you do not recognize, wrong balances, incorrect late payments, mixed identity files, or old negative items that should have aged off. Dispute inaccurate information with the credit bureau and the data furnisher.

## Freezes and Fraud Alerts
A credit freeze restricts new credit checks and helps prevent identity thieves from opening accounts. Freeze reports at the major bureaus if you are not actively applying. Temporarily lift freezes when applying for credit.

## Hard vs Soft Inquiries
Hard inquiries can affect scores and happen when applying for credit. Soft inquiries do not affect scores and happen for preapproval checks, self-checks, and some background reviews.

## Common Myths
- Carrying interest is not needed to build credit.
- Closing a card can reduce available credit and affect history, but sometimes it is worth it to avoid fees or temptation.
- Income is not directly part of most credit scores, though lenders consider income separately.
- Debit card use usually does not build credit.

## If Your Score Drops
Check recent utilization, missed payments, new accounts, closed accounts, hard inquiries, and report errors. A temporary drop after applying for credit or increasing balances may recover as accounts age and balances fall.
