# Credit Cards

## Card Types

- **Rewards cards (general):** Earn points (Chase Ultimate Rewards, Amex Membership Rewards, Citi ThankYou). Points redeem for travel, cash back, gift cards, or transfer to airline/hotel partners. Value varies widely (0.5–2.0+ cents per point).
- **Travel cards:** Airline co-branded (Delta SkyMiles, United Explorer, Southwest Rapid Rewards, American AAdvantage). Hotel co-branded (Marriott Bonvoy, Hilton Honors, World of Hyatt). General travel (Chase Sapphire Preferred/Reserve, Amex Gold/Platinum, Capital One Venture). Benefits: lounge access, travel credits, Global Entry/TSA PreCheck credits (every 4 years), no foreign transaction fees, primary rental car insurance.
- **Cash back cards:** Flat-rate (Citi Double Cash 2%, Wells Fargo Active Cash 2%, Fidelity 2% into brokerage). Rotating categories (Discover It 5%, Chase Freedom Flex 5% on quarterly categories). Category-specific (Blue Cash Preferred 6% groceries, Amazon Prime 5% Amazon/Whole Foods). Simple, no point valuation complexity.
- **Secured cards:** For building/repairing credit. Requires cash security deposit (typically $200–$500, refundable after responsible use 6–12 months). Graduates to unsecured card. Best: Discover secured (cashback rewards after 8 months), Capital One Platinum secured (no annual fee).
- **Balance transfer cards:** 0% intro APR for 12–21 months on transfers. Typically 3–5% transfer fee. Best for paying down existing high-interest debt. Examples: Citi Simplicity, Wells Fargo Reflect, BankAmericard. **Do not use for new purchases** (purchase APR may apply).
- **Student cards:** Designed for limited credit history. Issued by Discover, Capital One, Chase. Lower credit limits ($500–$1500). Rewards + "good grade" bonuses. No annual fee typical.

## Credit Utilization Impact

- **Definition:** Total revolving credit used ÷ total credit limit (per card + overall). FICO scoring treats both individual and aggregate utilization.
- **Important thresholds:** Under 30% = good. Under 10% = excellent (highest scores). Above 50% = significant negative impact. 0% utilization is slightly worse than 1–10% (no recent usage = no active account data).
- **Timing matters:** Most issuers report statement balance to bureaus. Paying balance BEFORE statement closes = low utilization reported. "AZEO" method (All Zero Except One): report $2–$10 on one card, $0 on others = maximizes score.
- **Utilization has NO memory:** Score penalty for high utilization disappears once lower utilization is reported next month. Quick fix before mortgage/auto loan application.
- **Credit limit increases (CLI):** Request every 6–12 months (soft pull from some issuers). Higher limits = lower utilization. No CLI request = slow growth. Some issuers auto-increase after responsible use.

## APR vs Annual Fees

- **APR (Annual Percentage Rate):** Variable (prime rate + margin). Purchase APR: 18–30% typical. Penalty APR can be 30%+ (triggered by 60+ day late payment). Balance transfer APR: intro 0% then regular. Cash advance APR: higher (24–28%) + immediate interest (no grace period) + fee (3–5% of amount).
- **Annual fees:** Range $0–$695. Fee waived first year on many cards. Reasonable if benefits > fee. Examples: Chase Sapphire Preferred $95 (effective $0 with $50 hotel credit). Amex Platinum $695 (effective ~$200+ with credits). Paying $95 for a card earning 3× travel is fine. Paying $95 for a card you use $10/mo on is waste.
- **No annual fee cards:** Citi Double Cash (2%). Chase Freedom Unlimited (1.5%). Chase Freedom Flex (5% categories). Discover It (5% categories). All solid everyday cards.
- **Grace period:** Time between statement closing and payment due date (typically 21–25 days). If paid in full by due date, no interest charged on new purchases. Grace period does NOT apply to cash advances or balance transfers (interest accrues immediately).
- **Late payment penalty:** First late fee ~$30, subsequent ~$40 within 6 months. Late payment also triggers penalty APR on some cards (requires 6+ months on-time payments to revert).

## Rewards Optimization

- **Category bonuses:** Cards with bonus categories (groceries 4–6%, dining 3–4%, gas 3–5%, travel 3–5%). Activate quarterly on rotating category cards (Chase Freedom, Discover It). Set calendar reminders. Stack category bonuses with store-specific apps.
- **Sign-up bonuses (SUB / welcome offer):** Most important factor for total rewards value. Typically spend $500–$5000 in 3 months for bonus worth $150–$1000. Check terms: "not available to those who have or have had this card" (Chase 48-month rule for Sapphire). Apply for new cards at strategic times (before large purchases).
- **Transfer partners:** Move points from flexible currencies (Chase UR, Amex MR, Citi TYP, Capital One Miles) to airline/hotel partners for outsized value. Often 1.5–5× return vs cash redemption. Best partners: Chase→Hyatt (high hotel value), Amex→Delta/Avianca/British Airways (transatlantic). Capital One→Air Canada/Virgin Atlantic.
- **Bonus stacking:** Use a card with category bonus for purchase + shopping portal (Rakuten, TopCashback) + store rewards program + Amex Offers / Chase Offers. Example: Amex Gold 4× groceries + Rakuten 5% + Amex Offer $10 off = effective ~12% return.
- **Minimum spend optimization:** Strategize SUB timing with known large purchases (insurance, tuition, property tax, home renovation). Prepay utilities or buy gift cards for routine spending. Do NOT overspend just to meet SUB.
- **Points valuation guide:** Redeeming for cash: $0.01/point or less. Statement credit: same. Gift cards: $0.008–0.01. Travel portal: $0.0125–0.015 (Chase Sapphire Preferred/Reserve). Transfer to partner: $0.015–$0.05+ (aspirational luxury travel). Always compare redemption options.
- **Churning / heavy strategy:** Apply for multiple new cards per year for SUBs. Requires good credit (750+). "5/24 rule" (Chase: auto-deny if 5+ new personal cards <24 months). Recommended: natural spend pace, 2–5 cards/year, no manufactured spending.

## Minimum Payments and Interest

- **Minimum payment:** Usually 1–3% of balance + interest + fees. Paying only minimum = decades of debt. Example: $5,000 at 22% APR, minimum $100/mo = 8+ years to pay off, ~$5,000+ total interest.
- **Daily interest calculation:** Interest = average daily balance × daily periodic rate (APR ÷ 365). If paid in full by due date, grace period = no interest. Trailing interest can appear after carrying a balance — even after paying off last statement (need to pay full balance + current charges to reset grace).
- **Avoid interest trap:** Once you carry a balance, new purchases start accruing interest immediately (no grace) until balance is paid in full. Use separate cards for spending vs debt payoff.

## Debt Repayment Strategies

- **Avalanche method (mathematically optimal):** Pay minimum on all cards, put extra toward highest APR card first. Saves most interest. Requires discipline — no psychological wins from paying off small balances.
- **Snowball method (behavioral):** Pay minimum on all, put extra toward smallest balance first. After paid off, roll that payment toward next smallest. Good for momentum/motivation (psychological wins). Higher total interest than avalanche.
- **Balance transfer:** Move debt to 0% intro APR card (12–21 months). Pay as much as possible during intro period. Fee 3–5% of transferred amount. Do not add new purchases. Keep old cards open (don't close — utilization impact). Consolidation loan alternative: SoFi, LightStream, Upstart (fixed rate, 2–7 yr terms).
- **Debt management plan (DMP):** Nonprofit credit counseling agency (NFCC.org). Negotiates lower interest rates (8–10%) with creditors. You make single monthly payment to agency. Closes all cards (but debt paid faster). Impacts credit score short-term.
- **Bankruptcy:** Chapter 7 (liquidation, discharge unsecured debt, 7–10 yr on credit report). Chapter 13 (3–5 yr repayment plan, less severe). Credit cards dischargeable. Student loans not. Last resort.

## Responsible Card Management

- **Number of cards:** 3–5 recommended for optimal credit score (thick file). Average age of accounts (AAoA) matters. Don't open too many in short bursts (lowers AAoA, raises inquiries). Keep oldest cards open (even if unused).
- **Closing a card:** Affects utilization (lowers total credit limit) and AAoA (if closed, continues to age for 10 years on FICO, then drops off). Better to keep open with small recurring charge (Netflix, subscription) set to autopay.
- **Card stacking / combining:** Can you have multiple from same issuer? Yes (except rules like Chase 5/24, Amex one-per-lifetime SUB per card type). Multiple cards from one bank share credit limit (combined risk).
- **Fraud protection:** Zero liability on unauthorized charges (Regulation E for debit, Reg Z for credit — but credit gives best protection). Set up purchase alerts. Enable 2FA / biometric login. Don't store card numbers on untrusted sites. Review statements monthly.
- **Autopay:** Always set autopay to at least minimum payment (statement due date). To maximize grace period: schedule payment a few days before due date (not too early). Paying early shows low utilization if balance paid before statement closes.
