# Financial Independence, Retire Early (FIRE)

## The Core Math
FIRE = accumulating enough invested assets to cover living expenses without working. The target is driven by your savings rate, not your income.

```
savings rate = (income − expenses) / income
years to FI  ≈ log( (1 − sr) / (wr − sr) ) / log(1 + r)   — sr=savings rate, wr=withdrawal rate, r=return
```

Higher savings rate compounds faster because both the invested amount and the reduced target grow together. Cutting expenses beats chasing raises.

## Savings Rate → Years to FI
Assuming ~5% real (inflation-adjusted) returns and a 4% withdrawal rate. The relationship is non-linear: small rate bumps early save many years.

| Savings Rate | Years to FI |
|--------------|-------------|
| 10% | 51 yr |
| 25% | 32 yr |
| 50% | 17 yr |
| 65% | 11 yr |
| 75% | 7 yr |

Doubling rate from 25%→50% nearly halves the timeline. Past ~70% the gains flatten as you approach the minimum survival spend.

## The 4% Rule / 25x
The Trinity study found a 4% inflation-adjusted withdrawal had ~95% success over 30-year periods. This gives the FI target.

```
FI number = annual expenses × 25        — the "25x" rule (inverse of 4%)
monthly need = FI / 25 / 12             — reverse it to set a savings goal
example: $40k/yr spend → $1,000,000 FI target
```

For early retirees with 40–50 yr horizons, many use 3.5–3.75% (28–29x) for safety. Sequence-of-returns risk is highest in the first 5 years.

## Safe Withdrawal Example
Concrete math on a $1M portfolio, $40k spend:

```
year 0:  balance $1,000,000, withdraw $40,000 (4.0%)
year 1:  +5% growth = $1,008,000, withdraw $40,000 → $968,000
year 2:  −10% drop  = $871,200,  withdraw $40,000 → $831,200
guardrail: if balance < 20× spend, cut discretionary 10–20%
```

Flexible spending (cut in downturns) dramatically raises success vs rigid 4%. A "cash buffer" of 1–2 yr expenses absorbs bear markets without selling low.

## Expense Tracking & Low-Cost Investing
Know the spend before optimizing it.

```
track 3 months         — categorize every outflow; find the top 3 leaks
automate savings       — transfer to investments the day you're paid
index funds            — VT, VTI, VOO; expense ratios ~0.03–0.10%
avoid timing           — lump-sum beats DCA ~2/3 of the time; just invest
rebalance yearly       — restore target allocation; enforces buy-low
```

The biggest cost drag is fees and taxes, not market timing. A 1% fee can cost ~20% of final wealth over 30 yr.

## Account Order: Taxable vs Tax-Advantaged
Fill tax-advantaged space first, then taxable brokerage.

```
1. employer 401(k) match   — free money, always max the match
2. HSA (if eligible)        — triple tax advantage; invest, don't spend
3. Roth IRA / Traditional   — income-dependent choice
4. remainder 401(k)         — up to annual limit
5. taxable brokerage        — for early-retirement bridge before 59½
```

Early retirees use " Roth conversion ladders" or SEPP/72(t) to access 401(k) funds penalty-free before 59½.

## Avoiding Lifestyle Creep
Raise in pay should raise savings, not spending. This is the single biggest FIRE accelerator.

```
bank half of every raise   — automate the increase to investments
keep housing ≤ 25–30% net  — the largest fixed cost; don't upgrade reflexively
define "enough"            — a written annual spend cap prevents drift
separate fun money         — guilt-free allowance avoids all-or-nothing burnout
```

FIRE fails most often from undefined wants, not low income. A clear "enough" number makes the math concrete.
