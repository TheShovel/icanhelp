# Small Business & Entrepreneurship

## Starting a Business
- **Validate the idea**: before investing time/money, talk to potential customers — what problem does it solve? Will they pay?
  - Minimum Viable Product (MVP): simplest version that delivers value — test, learn, iterate. Don't build full product before anyone uses it
  - "If you're not embarrassed by your first product, you launched too late" — Reid Hoffman (LinkedIn founder)
- **Business plan**: executive summary, problem, solution, market, competition, business model, team, financial projections
  - Lean canvas: simpler 1-page alternative — problem, solution, key metrics, unique value proposition, unfair advantage, channels, customer segments, cost structure, revenue streams
  - Most investors don't read full business plans — pitch deck (10-15 slides) is what you present
- **Legal structure**: Sole Proprietorship (simplest, personal liability), LLC (limited liability, pass-through taxation — most common for small businesses), S-Corp (tax savings for higher income, more paperwork), C-Corp (investors, double taxation, preferred by VC startups)
  - LLC: protects personal assets from business debts — strongly recommended if you have any liability risk or significant assets
  - Business license, permits, zoning — depends on location and type (food service needs health permits, construction needs contractor license)
  - EIN (Employer Identification Number): free from IRS — needed for bank account, taxes, employees

## Pricing
- **Cost-plus**: cost + markup (50-100% retail, 100-200% restaurant food, 200-400% jewelry/gift items). Know ALL costs (materials, labor, overhead, fees)
  - Retail keystone pricing: buy for $10, sell for $20 (100% markup, 50% margin)
- **Value-based**: price based on what customer is willing to pay — higher margin, more market research needed
  - Example: software that saves company $10,000/year can be priced $1,000-3,000/year (value capture)
- **Competitive**: match/beat competitors — race to bottom (commodity trap, low margin)
- **Psychological**: $19.99 > $20.00, $97 > $100. Charm pricing (ending in .99/.97) works for budget shoppers, round numbers for luxury
  - Anchoring: show expensive option first (makes others seem reasonable). Decoy pricing: add third option to steer choice
- **Raise prices**: small businesses often underpriced — test 10-20% increase, most customers won't leave. You can always add value rather than compete on price

## Marketing on a Budget
- **Content marketing**: blog posts, videos, social media — provides value, builds trust, attracts customers. Cost: time. Best for: B2B, education, services
- **Email list**: most cost-effective channel — $0-30/month, 20-40% open rates for good lists, $1 earned per $0.10 spent (average ROI for email: $36 per $1 spent). Start building on Day 1
  - Lead magnet: free download (checklist, template, ebook, guide) in exchange for email. Segment list by interest
- **SEO (Search Engine Optimization)**: optimize website for Google — long-term play (3-6 months to see results), free traffic, best ROI over time
  - Basics: keyword research (what do customers search?), on-page (title tags, H1, content), technical (site speed, mobile-friendly, sitemap), off-page (backlinks)
  - Local SEO: Google Business Profile (free), local citations, customer reviews — critical for brick-and-mortar
- **Referral program**: reward customers who bring new customers (10-20% discount, free product, credit). Most trusted marketing = word of mouth
  - Referral programs: Dropbox (extra storage for both), Uber (ride credit), Airbnb (travel credit) — incentive both sides
- **Social media organic**: consistent posting, engage with followers, partnerships with similar businesses — free but time-intensive

## Accounting & Bookkeeping
- **Track everything**: income, expenses, receipts. Mixing personal and business is #1 mistake (legal issues + tax nightmare)
  - Open separate business bank account + credit card from Day 1 — even for sole proprietor
- **Software**: QuickBooks (industry standard, $15-30/month), Wave (free, good for very small businesses), FreshBooks (service businesses), Xero (cheaper than QB), or simple spreadsheet + shoebox for receipts (scale up when complex)
  - Apps: Expensify, Receipt Bank (auto-scan receipts), Melio (pay vendors/free ACH)
- **Taxes**: quarterly estimated taxes (federal + state + self-employment tax 15.3%), annual filing. Deductions: home office (if exclusive + regular use), vehicle mileage (67¢/mi 2024), equipment (Section 179 — deduct full cost first year), software/subscriptions, travel (50% meals), health insurance, retirement (SEP IRA), education, professional fees
  - Work with a CPA (especially for first year) — saves more than it costs, catches deductions you'd miss
  - Sales tax: if selling physical products (or digital in some states), need to collect and remit — varies by state, marketplace facilitator laws (Amazon collects for you in most states)

## Hiring & Team
- **When to hire**: when you're turning down work OR bottleneck is you (can't grow without delegation)
  - Hire for bottlenecks, not busy-ness — first hire should be whatever you're worst at or hate doing (admin, bookkeeping)
- **Independent contractor vs employee**: IC (1099) = control their own schedule, use own tools, can work for others. Employee (W-2) = you control when/where/how they work. Misclassification is risky (IRS fines, back taxes + penalties)
  - General rule: IC gives you result, employee follows your process
- **Remote team**: hire from anywhere (wider talent pool, lower cost in many cases). Overcommunicate, async-first, results-oriented
  - Tools: Slack (chat), Notion/Google Docs (docs), Asana/ClickUp/Trello (tasks), Zoom (meetings), Loom (async video), Gusto/Paychex (payroll)

## Customer Service
- **Respond fast**: same day for email, within minutes for chat, 24 hours for social media (shorter = better). Customers expect: email < 12 hours, social < 1 hour, phone < 5 min hold
- **The customer is not always right**: but they are always the customer — listen, empathize, solve the problem without being a doormat
  - Fire toxic customers: if a customer costs more to serve than they pay, and makes your team miserable, fire them (refund + suggest alternative)
- **Follow up**: after solving a problem, check back 24-48 hours later — turns a negative into a lifelong customer
- **Upset customers**: acknowledge their frustration (apologize for their experience), don't blame them or make excuses, solve the problem, offer something extra (discount, free shipping)
  - "I understand how frustrating that must be. Let me fix this for you right now."
- **Reviews**: respond to ALL reviews (good + bad). Thank for positive. For negative: apologize publicly, take it offline ("please email me at..." - shows you're responsive), solve the problem. Don't argue or get defensive
  - 88% of consumers trust online reviews as much as personal recommendations (BrightLocal)

## Growth & Scaling
- **Systems**: document processes (SOPs — standard operating procedures) — so business runs without you. "If you can't be gone for a week without it falling apart, it's not a business — it's a job"
  - License to your business: a business you can sell = runs without you. A job that you own = doesn't sell (buyers buy systems, not your time)
- **Outsource/grow**: as revenue grows, delegate non-core tasks (bookkeeping, customer service, fulfillment, social media, admin)
  - Virtual assistants (VA): $5-20/hour (Time Etc, Belay, Fancy Hands, Upwork). Specialized help: Upwork, Toptal, LinkedIn
- **Funding options**: bootstrapping (self-funded = slow but full control), small business loans (SBA, banks, credit unions), grants (federal/state/nonprofit — free money, competitive), angel investors (early stage, $25k-500k), venture capital (high growth, $1M+, expect 10x return, heavy dilution)
  - Most businesses don't need VC — VC is for high-growth tech/startups that can return entire fund. For main street businesses (restaurant, store, service): SBA loan or personal savings
- **Pivot**: be willing to change direction based on what customers tell you — don't fall in love with your idea, fall in love with the problem you're solving
  - Examples: Slack (started as game company), Twitter (started as podcast platform), YouTube (started as dating site), Instagram (started as check-in app — Burbn, pivoted to photos)

## Work-Life Balance
- **Set boundaries**: business hours, no email after 7 PM, take Sundays off. The business will NOT collapse if you rest
  - The fire will always be there — choose which fires to put out (urgent vs important)
- **Avoid burnout**: most common in first 2 years. Schedule breaks, outsource, say no to opportunities that don't align
  - When working 80 hours/week makes you 10% more inefficient than 50 hours, you're achieving less with more time
- **Pay yourself**: take a salary from the start (even if small) — business must prove it can support you. Don't work for free indefinitely
- **Mental health**: entrepreneurship is lonely, high-stress, high-failure (50% fail in 5 years). Have support (mentors, peers, family)
  - Failure = tuition (you learned what doesn't work). Most successful entrepreneurs failed multiple times before success
