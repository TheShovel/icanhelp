# Relationships & Communication

## Communication Basics
- **Active listening**: fully concentrate, understand, respond, then remember — don't plan your response while they're talking
- Reflective listening: "So what I'm hearing is..." — confirms understanding, shows you care
- "I" statements: "I feel [emotion] when [situation] because [reason]" — avoids blame
- Avoid: "you always" / "you never" (absolutes trigger defensiveness)
- Nonverbal: 55% body language, 38% tone, 7% words (Mehrabian's rule for emotional communication)
- Eye contact: 60-70% of time during conversation (too little = disinterest, too much = aggression)
- Paraphrase: repeat back what you understood — clarifies and validates

## Conflict Resolution
1. Stay calm — take 5-10 min break if emotions are high (agree to return to conversation)
2. Each person speaks without interruption (use talking stick/timer if needed)
3. Acknowledge the other person's perspective (don't need to agree, just understand)
4. Identify the real issue (often not what the surface argument is about)
5. Brainstorm solutions together (not "my way vs your way")
6. Agree on a plan, including what happens if it doesn't work
- Goal: solve the problem, not win the argument
- Attacking character ("you're selfish") vs addressing behavior ("when you don't text, I worry")
- Repair attempts: "I'm sorry", "tell me more", "let's take a break" — de-escalate before damage

## Friendships
- Quantity time → quality relationships: regular contact builds bonds
- Friendships need: consistency, vulnerability, reciprocity, shared experiences
- To maintain: check in regularly, remember important dates, show up during hard times
- Deepening: share something vulnerable → other reciprocates → trust builds
- It's normal for friendships to change with life stages (moving, kids, career changes)
- Friendship breakups are real and painful — it's OK to outgrow people
- Good friends: celebrate your wins, support during lows, honest with you, respect boundaries

## Romantic Relationships
- **Love languages** (Gary Chapman): words of affirmation, quality time, receiving gifts, acts of service, physical touch
- Know your partner's primary love language (and yours) — we often give what we want to receive
- **Attachment styles**: secure (comfortable with intimacy), anxious (fear abandonment, need reassurance), avoidant (fear closeness, value independence)
- **Gottman principles**: turn toward bids for connection, build love maps, manage conflict, create shared meaning
- The "magic ratio": 5 positive interactions for every 1 negative during conflict
- Four horsemen (relationship killers): criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling
- Contempt (sarcasm, eye-rolling, disgust) is the #1 predictor of divorce
- Healthy relationships: trust, respect, communication, boundaries, shared values, independence
- Unhealthy: controlling behavior, jealousy, gaslighting, emotional/physical abuse, constantly walking on eggshells

## Boundaries
- Types: physical (personal space, touch), emotional (what you share), time (when available), material (lending items/money)
- How to set: "I need [boundary] because [reason]" — calm, direct, not apologetic
- How to enforce: restate boundary, state consequence, follow through
- You don't need to justify your boundaries — "No" is a complete sentence
- Healthy boundaries: flexible when appropriate, firm when needed
- Signs of weak boundaries: saying yes when you mean no, oversharing, taking responsibility for others' feelings
- Signs of rigid boundaries: never asking for help, emotional isolation, difficulty trusting

## Assertiveness
- Assertive: stands up for self while respecting others. Passive: ignores own needs. Aggressive: ignores others' needs
- DEAR MAN technique (DBT):
  - Describe: facts of situation
  - Express: your feelings/opinion
  - Assert: what you want clearly
  - Reinforce: positive outcome if they agree
  - Mindful: stay on topic, don't get distracted
  - Appear confident: eye contact, firm voice
  - Negotiate: be willing to compromise

## Empathy
- Cognitive empathy: understanding someone's perspective (I understand how you feel)
- Emotional empathy: feeling what they feel (I feel your pain)
- Compassionate empathy: understanding + feeling + wanting to help
- Empathy ≠ agreement — you can understand without agreeing
- Empathy ≠ fixing — often people just need to be heard, not solved
- "The opposite of empathy is not apathy — it's certainty" (Brene Brown)

## Online Communication
- Tone is easily misread in text — assume good intent, clarify if unsure
- Avoid all-caps (yelling), excessive punctuation, sarcasm without /s
- Emojis help convey tone but use sparingly in professional contexts
- Response time expectations vary by relationship — communicate yours
- Group chats: don't share others' personal info without permission
- Texting: if it's complex/emotional, call instead
