# Assertiveness & Communication

## Communication Styles
- **Passive**: avoid conflict, prioritize others' needs, don't express own feelings/rights. Apologize excessively. Outcome: resentment builds, needs unmet, taken advantage of. Body language: hunched, avoiding eye contact, quiet voice
- **Assertive**: express thoughts/feelings honestly while respecting others. "I feel X when Y, I need Z." Stand up for self without trampling others. Outcome: mutual respect, clean relationships. Body language: open, steady eye contact, firm voice
- **Aggressive**: express feelings at others' expense — demanding, hostile, blaming. "You always..." "You never..." Outcome: others feel attacked, relationships damaged, short-term win long-term loss. Body language: pointing, leaning in, loud, invading space
- **Passive-aggressive**: indirect resistance — sarcasm, silent treatment, procrastination, "fine" when not fine. Saying yes but sabotaging. Outcome: confusion, erodes trust, solves nothing. Driven by fear of direct conflict but desire to express anger
- **Assertive is the goal**: it's a skill, not personality — anyone can learn it. Start in low-stakes situations and build up.

## I-Statements
- **Structure**: "I feel [emotion] when [specific situation] because [impact]. I need/would like [request]."
- **Correct**: "I feel frustrated when meetings start late because it disrupts my schedule. I'd like us to start on time."
- **Wrong (you-statement)**: "You're always late and you don't respect my time." — triggers defensiveness
- **Benefits**: expresses ownership of feelings, doesn't blame, invites cooperation, hard to argue with (can't deny someone's feelings). Defuses conflict
- **Practice**: take any complaint and rephrase as I-statement. Avoid "You make me feel..." (still blaming). Stick to "I feel... when..."

## Setting Boundaries
- **Types**: time (how long you'll spend), emotional (what you'll discuss), physical (touch, space), material (lending money/things), digital (response time expectations, off-hours contact)
- **How to set**: be specific, direct, calm. "I can't take calls after 7pm." "I'm not comfortable discussing that." "I can help for 30 minutes, then I need to leave."
- **What happens**: boundary pushback is normal — people used to you saying yes. Don't over-explain (jade). "No" is a complete sentence. Repeat boundary calmly (broken record technique)
- **Guilt**: feeling guilty ≠ doing something wrong. Protect your boundaries — self-care isn't selfish. Start with small boundaries, build tolerance for others' disappointment

## Saying No
- **Direct**: "No, I can't do that." "That doesn't work for me." "I'm not available."
- **Soft (for relationships)**: "I appreciate you asking but I can't right now." "I wish I could, but I have other commitments." "Let me check and get back to you" (buy time, then say no)
- **No apology necessary**: don't say "I'm sorry but..." when there's nothing to apologize for. Save apologies for actual mistakes
- **Delay tactic**: "Let me look at my schedule and get back to you." Gives time to decide, avoids pressure. Then decline if needed
- **No sandwich**: positive-no-positive weakens the no. "I'd love to help but can't — maybe next time" is fine, but don't bury the no in flattery
- **Reasons not required**: "No" doesn't need justification. Over-explaining = opening for negotiation. If you give a reason, keep it brief and don't lie

## DEAR MAN (DBT Interpersonal Effectiveness)
- **D**escribe: state facts without judgment. "We agreed the report was due Friday."
- **E**xpress: share feelings/opinions using I-statements. "I'm concerned because it's still incomplete."
- **A**ssert: ask directly for what you need. "I need the report by Monday morning."
- **R**einforce: explain positive outcome of getting needs met. "If I get it by Monday, I can review it before the client meeting."
- **M**indful: stay focused on goal, don't get sidetracked. Repeat if needed (broken record). Ignore attacks/irrelevant arguments
- **A**ppear confident: maintain eye contact, good posture, firm tone. Even if anxious — fake it
- **N**egotiate: be willing to find middle ground. "What deadline works for you? Can we compromise on Tuesday?"
- **Application**: use for tough conversations — asking boss for raise, declining invitation, roommate conflict, setting boundaries with family

## Conflict Resolution Steps
1. **Cool down**: if emotional, delay conversation. "I need to think about this. Can we talk in an hour?"
2. **Choose time/place**: private, neutral, enough time (not before bed/running out door). Both people ready
3. **Describe situation without blame**: use I-statements and facts. "When I heard you say X in the meeting..."
4. **Listen actively**: paraphrase what you heard. "It sounds like you felt..." Ask questions, don't interrupt
5. **Validate**: acknowledge their feelings even if disagree. "I can see why you'd feel that way."
6. **Identify needs**: what does each person actually need? (versus positions/stances)
7. **Brainstorm solutions**: multiple options, no judging yet. Look for win-win
8. **Agree on action**: specific, time-bound, clear who does what
9. **Follow up**: check in later. Unresolved issues fester

## Body Language for Assertiveness
- **Posture**: stand/sit upright, shoulders back (not rigid), head level. Open chest, not crossed arms
- **Eye contact**: steady but not staring. Look at eyes or between eyebrows. 60-70% of time while speaking, more while listening. Break occasionally to avoid staring
- **Voice**: steady pace, moderate volume, clear articulation. Not too fast (anxiety) or too slow (condescending). Drop pitch at end of statements (not up — uptalk sounds uncertain)
- **Gestures**: open palms (honesty), hands visible. No finger-pointing, no fidgeting, no covering mouth. Emphasize points with purposeful gesture
- **Space**: stand at arm's length — not too close (aggressive) or too far (submissive). Match others' distance

## Dealing with Criticism
- **Determine validity**: is there truth? Who is it coming from? What's their motive? Even poorly delivered criticism may contain useful feedback
- **Assertive response**: "Thank you for the feedback. I'll think about that." "Can you give me a specific example?" "I see your point, here's my perspective..."
- **Don't**: get defensive immediately, counter-criticize, shut down, apologize excessively, dismiss outright
- **Constructive vs destructive**: constructive = specific, behavioral, offers solution. Destructive = personal, vague, attacks character. Assertively reject destructive: "I disagree with that characterization."
- **Separate action from identity**: "I made a mistake" ≠ "I am a failure." Feedback is about what you did, not who you are

## Workplace Assertiveness
- **Speak up in meetings**: prepare points beforehand. Say them early. Frame as contribution, not question. "I'd like to add that..." "One thing to consider is..."
- **Credit**: acknowledge your contributions. "I led that project." "I implemented the solution." Don't minimize — women particularly prone to this
- **Disagreeing with superiors**: "I see it differently. Can I share my perspective?" "What about [alternative]?" "I'm concerned about [specific risk]." Focus on data/outcomes
- **Negotiation**: always negotiate salary/offers — it's expected. Do research (Glassdoor, Levels.fyi). State number first (anchoring). Justify with specific accomplishments
- **Delegating**: other people's urgent ≠ your emergency. "I can prioritize that if you want, but that means X will slip." "Who else could take this on?"
- **Email**: clear subject, direct ask, one ask per email. "As discussed..." for accountability. CC strategically (bring in manager for support, not as threat)
