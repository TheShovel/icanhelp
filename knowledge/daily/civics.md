# Civics & Civic Engagement

## How Government Works — US Levels
- **Local**: counties, cities, towns, school districts. Handle: zoning, police/fire, parks, schools, water, trash, local courts, public transit. Most direct impact on daily life. Attend city council meetings, school board meetings — often have public comment period
- **State**: governors, state legislatures, state courts. Handle: education funding, state highways, state police, driver's licenses, professional licensing, Medicaid. States have significant power (10th Amendment). Governor's signature or veto on state bills. State legislatures pass state laws and budgets
- **Federal**: three branches. Legislative (Congress — House + Senate) makes laws. Executive (President + agencies) enforces laws. Judicial (Supreme Court + federal courts) interprets laws. Handles: national defense, interstate commerce, immigration, patents/copyright, federal taxes, Social Security, Medicare, foreign policy
- **Separation of powers**: each branch checks the others. President vetoes bills (Congress can override 2/3). Senate confirms judges. Supreme Court strikes down laws (judicial review — Marbury v. Madison). Congress impeaches/removes president and judges. President appoints judges. House starts all revenue bills
- **Federalism**: power divided between national and state governments. Some powers exclusive to federal (declare war, coin money), some shared (tax, courts), some reserved to states (education, elections). Supremacy Clause: federal law trumps state law when they conflict (Const. Art. VI)

## Voting
- **Registration**: required in most states (ND doesn't require). Deadlines vary (10-30 days before election in most states). Register online (vote.gov), by mail (National Voter Registration Form), or in-person (DMV, public assistance agencies). Motor Voter Law: DMV must offer registration
- **Election day**: first Tuesday after first Monday in November (federal general elections). Primaries/caucuses earlier in year. State/local elections often in off-years (lower turnout). Check your state's primary type: open (anyone), closed (party members only), semi-closed
- **Voting methods**: in-person on election day, early voting (many states), absentee/by-mail (all states allow with excuse, some no-excuse, some all-mail like CO/OR/WA/UT/HI). Drop boxes increasingly available
- **Voter ID laws**: vary by state — some require photo ID, others accept non-photo ID/bills. 36 states have some form of voter ID requirement. Check your state's rules at vote.org
- **Voter rights**: Voting Rights Act of 1965 (Section 2 bans racial discrimination, Section 5 preclearance for jurisdictions with history — weakened by Shelby County v. Holder 2013). National Voter Registration Act (NVRA). Help America Vote Act (HAVA 2002). You have right to: vote if registered, accessible polling place (ADA), provisional ballot if name not on roll, language assistance (if covered jurisdiction)
- **Provisional ballot**: if name not on roll or ID missing — vote provisionally, election officials verify eligibility later. Always ask for one if problems

## Contacting Elected Officials
- **Who to contact**: local issue → mayor/city council. State → state rep/senator/governor. Federal → US Representative (House) + both US Senators. Find at usa.gov/elected-officials
- **Most effective**: physical letter (personalized). Phone call (brief, to the point). Email (next best). In-person meeting (best — district office visit). Form letters/forwarded emails carry less weight
- **Template**: identify yourself as constituent (address). State specific bill/issue by number (e.g., H.R. 1234, S. 567). Say your position clearly. Explain personal impact (2-3 sentences). Request specific action ("Vote YES on..."). Thank them. Keep to one page
- **Phone call**: "Hi, I'm [name], a constituent from [city/zip]. I'm calling to urge [Senator/Rep] to vote [yes/no] on [bill number/issue]. [Brief reason]. Thank you." Leave voicemail if staff doesn't answer
- **Timing**: call during legislative session and before key votes. Best: Monday morning, Wednesday. Avoid: Friday afternoon, after votes are already decided. Build relationship with staff (they brief the official)
- **Follow up**: if they vote as requested, thank them. If not, hold accountable (remember for next election)

## Public Comment
- **City council / school board / planning commission meetings**: almost always have public comment period (usually 2-3 min per person). Sign up in advance or at meeting
- **Effective**: arrive early, sign up. Prepare written statement (read it, bring copies for board + media). State name + where you live. Be specific ("I oppose rezoning 123 Main St because..."). Cite facts/data. Offer alternative if possible. Be respectful — even when angry
- **Not effective**: reading from phone, rambling, attacking individuals, repeating what others already said, going over time limit. 30+ comments on same side → organize, send fewer but stronger representatives
- **Virtual**: many meetings now hybrid. Same rules apply. Test tech beforehand

## Jury Duty
- **Summoned**: random selection from voter registration + DMV records. Must appear or get excused (hardship, medical, prior service, age >70 in some states). Failure to appear = contempt of court / fine
- **Selection (voir dire)**: judge + attorneys question potential jurors. Can be dismissed "for cause" (bias, hardship) or peremptory challenge (no reason needed, limited number per side). Be honest — lying disqualifies
- **Trial types**: criminal (unanimous verdict in most states) — beyond reasonable doubt. Civil (unanimous or supermajority depending on state) — preponderance of evidence. Grand jury (decides indictment, not guilt)
- **Rights**: employer cannot fire you for jury service (federal + most states). Not all states pay jurors (or pay very little — $5-50/day). Some states pay mileage. Some employers pay difference
- **Pro tip**: bring reading material (lots of waiting). Dress comfortably but respectfully. Pay attention — judge will instruct on law. Deliberate seriously — it's a constitutional duty

## Community Organizing
- **Steps**: identify issue → research existing efforts → find affected people (1-on-1 meetings) → build core team → develop strategy → take action (meetings, rallies, petitions, media) → evaluate + adjust
- **Power mapping**: identify who has power to give you what you want (decision-maker). Then identify who influences them (supporters, opponents, swing). Target both
- **Coalition building**: partner with existing groups (churches, nonprofits, unions, neighborhood associations). More people = more power. Share credit. Agree on common ask
- **Tactics**: meetings with officials, public comment, rallies, petitions, media (op-eds, press releases, press conferences), social media campaigns, direct action (marches, sit-ins, boycotts), lawsuits. Legal observers if risk of arrest
- **Digital tools**: ActionNetwork, Mobilize (events), Google Sheets (tracking), Signal/WhatsApp (group chat), Google Groups/email lists. Petitions: Change.org, MoveOn. Phone banking: CallHub, Hustle. Texting: Spoke, ThruText

## Nonprofits vs Advocacy Groups
- **501(c)(3)**: charitable, educational, religious. Tax-deductible donations. CANNOT endorse political candidates or engage in substantial lobbying (limited). Examples: Red Cross, ACLU education arm, food banks
- **501(c)(4)**: social welfare organizations. Can lobby heavily and endorse candidates (but campaigning must not be primary activity). Donations NOT tax-deductible. Examples: Planned Parenthood Action Fund, ACLU advocacy, AARP, Sierra Club
- **527 organizations**: political committees — PACs, Super PACs. Unlimited fundraising/spending on elections (but must disclose donors). Cannot coordinate with candidates. Super PACs (Citizens United v. FEC 2010) can raise unlimited funds from anyone
- **Action vs advocacy**: direct service (food bank, shelter) vs systemic change (policy reform, legislation). Both needed. Many orgs do both via separate (c)(3) and (c)(4) arms

## Understanding Legislation
- **How a bill becomes law (federal)**: Introduced in House or Senate → committee (hearings, markup, vote) → if passes → full chamber vote (simple majority) → other chamber (same process) → conference committee (reconcile differences) → both chambers vote on final version → President signs (or vetoes → 2/3 override)
- **Bill numbers**: H.R. = House bill, S. = Senate bill, H.Res/S.Res = simple resolution (one chamber), H.Con.Res/S.Con.Res = concurrent resolution (both chambers, no force of law), H.J.Res/S.J.Res = joint resolution (same as bill). Look up at congress.gov
- **Tracking**: congress.gov (official), govtrack.us, popvox, OpenSecrets (campaign finance). Each bill page shows: text, summary, sponsors, committee assignments, votes, related bills
- **State level**: 49 states have bicameral legislatures (NE has unicameral). Process similar to federal. State house + state senate. Find bills at state legislature website. Many have simpler naming (e.g., "AB 123" in CA)
- **Regulations**: laws → agencies write regulations (detailed rules). Proposed regulations published for public comment (Federal Register at regulations.gov). Final rules = force of law. Public comments DO matter — agencies respond to them
