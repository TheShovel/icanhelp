# Camping, Hiking & Outdoor Skills

## Trip Planning
- Choose destination appropriate to skill level: novice (well-marked trails, campgrounds), intermediate (longer, moderate elevation), advanced (backcountry, navigation required, technical terrain)
  - Research: trail conditions, weather forecast, water sources, wildlife concerns, permits needed
  - Leave trip plan with someone: route, expected return time, vehicle info, emergency contacts
- **Permits**: national parks (backcountry permits often required, sometimes lottery), wilderness areas, fire restrictions
  - Some popular trails (Half Dome, Whitney, Enchantments) require lottery months in advance
- **Check fire danger**: many areas ban campfires during dry season — use camp stove instead

## Hiking
- **Pace**: start slow, maintain steady, adjust for terrain — should be able to hold conversation (if breathing too hard to talk, slow down)
- **Rest step**: fully extend leg, lock knee for split second, relax muscles between steps — saves 15% energy on uphills
- **Descending**: shorter steps, don't lock knees, lean forward slightly, use trekking poles (reduce knee stress 25%+)
- **Hiking poles**: reduce joint impact by 25-30%, improve balance, assist on uphills and downhills, clear spider webs
  - Adjust height: elbows at 90° when poles vertical, tips on ground
- **Navigation**: GPS on phone (download offline maps — Gaia GPS, AllTrails, CalTopo) + PAPER MAP (phones die) + compass (know how to use!)
  - Always carry paper map — even if you have GPS. Electronics fail (cold drains battery, drop it in river, breaks)
  - Know how to read: contour lines (close = steep, far = flat), declination (magnetic vs true north), UTM grid coordinates
  - Mark your starting point and waypoints on the map
- **Leave No Trace** principles:
  1. Plan ahead and prepare
  2. Travel and camp on durable surfaces (stay on trail, camp on rock/sand/dry grass, not vegetation)
  3. Dispose of waste properly (pack it in, pack it out — including toilet paper!)
  4. Leave what you find (leave rocks, plants, artifacts)
  5. Minimize campfire impacts (use camp stove, existing fire rings, keep fires small, burn to ash)
  6. Respect wildlife (observe from distance, never feed, store food properly)
  7. Be considerate of other visitors (yield to uphill hikers, keep noise down, control pets)

## Camping
- **Tent site selection**: flat, elevated (not valley/drainage — cold air + water pools there), away from dead trees/ branches (widowmakers), 200 ft from water (allow wildlife access), afternoon shade if hot
  - Orient tent door away from wind
- **Sleep system**: sleeping bag (rated 10-15°F lower than expected temp), sleeping pad (R-value: 1-2 summer, 3-4 3-season, 5+ winter), pillow (inflatable or stuff clothes in stuff sack)
  - CCF (closed cell foam) pad: cheap, indestructible, less comfortable. Inflatable: comfortable, packable, can puncture
  - R-value: insulation measure. Higher = warmer. 1-season: 1-2, 3-season: 3-4, winter: 5+
  - Cold camping: sleep in dry base layers (not sweaty!), warm hat, eat fatty food before bed, fill water bottle with hot water for foot of bag
- **Cooking**: camp stove (propane/isobutane canister for car camping, white gas for winter/extended), cookware, camp sink (wash 200 ft from water, strain food particles and pack out)
  - Bear canister (required in many areas): stores ALL scented items (food, toothpaste, deodorant, trash). Hang properly (10 ft up, 6 ft from trunk, 4 ft from limb) or use bear box
  - Bear spray: accessible (not packed deep!), know how to use (aim slightly down, 2-3 second burst at 30 ft when bear charges). NOT bear deterrent for aggressive bears — practice drawing canister
- **Camp hygiene**: hand sanitizer before every meal, don't wash dishes in lakes/streams, pack out all trash + toilet paper
  - Poop: 200 ft from water/trail/camp, 6-8" deep cathole, pack out TP (WAG bags in sensitive areas)
  - In desert/arid areas: no catholes (dig in organic soil or pack out). In high traffic areas: use restrooms or pack out

## Clothing (Layering System)
- **Base layer** (against skin): merino wool (best — warm even when wet, odor resistant) or synthetic (polyester, nylon — cheaper, dries fast)
  - NEVER cotton — "cotton kills" (absorbs moisture, stays wet, causes hypothermia)
- **Mid layer** (insulation): fleece (most breathable), puffy jacket (down = warmest/lightest but useless when wet, synthetic = less warm/heavier but insulates when wet)
  - Add/remove layers before you sweat — sweating = wet = cold
- **Outer layer** (shell): waterproof + breathable (Gore-Tex, eVent, or equivalent) — blocks wind + rain while letting sweat out
  - "Water-resistant" ≠ "waterproof" — for serious rain, you need a hardshell jacket
  - Pit zips: essential for ventilation without taking jacket off
- **Footwear**: trail runners (lighter, dry faster, good for day hikes/well-maintained trails) vs boots (ankle support, durable, heavy — good for rough terrain, heavy packs)
  - Break in new boots BEFORE trip (wear around house, short walks, then longer)
  - Wool socks (Darn Tough, Smartwool) — change mid-day if feet get wet. Dry sock break at lunch (take shoes off, let feet air dry)
  - Treat feet: moleskin/leukotape at first sign of hot spot (don't wait for blister!), change socks, let feet dry

## Food & Water
- **Water**: 0.5-1 liter per hour hiking (more in heat, altitude, heavy pack)
  - Purification: filter (Sawyer Squeeze, Katadyn BeFree, Grayl — removes bacteria + protozoa), UV (SteriPEN, treats viruses), tablets (Aquamira — weight, slow), boil (1 min at sea level, 3 min at altitude — uses fuel)
  - Giardia ("beaver fever"): most common waterborne illness from untreated water — symptoms 1-2 weeks after exposure (cramps, diarrhea, nausea — NOT fun on trail)
  - Viruses: rare in North American backcountry (human waste) but present in developing countries — need filter + chemical or UV
  - Deserts: carry ALL water (1 gallon/day, none found on trail)
- **Food**: high calorie/weight ratio (nuts, trail mix, dried fruit, tortillas, cheese, salami, peanut butter, instant mashed potatoes, ramen, energy bars)
  - Calorie needs: 3,000-5,000+ calories/day backpacking (hard to eat enough, eat continuously)
  - Before trip: check food allergies in group, meal prep at home (dehydrate, pack into daily bags)

## Safety & Emergencies
- **Hypothermia**: early signs (shivering, loss of coordination, confusion, apathy) — immediate action: get out of wind/rain, add insulation, warm fluids, no alcohol
  - Severe: shivering stops (BAD), unconscious — emergency, evacuate ASAP. Hypothermia kills
  - Prevention: stay dry, layer before you're cold, eat enough, don't push too hard
- **Heat exhaustion**: heavy sweating, pale/clammy, headache, nausea, weak pulse, dizziness — shade, water, electrolytes, cool compresses
  - Heat stroke (emergency): hot/red/dry skin, confusion, unconsciousness — IMMEDIATE cooling + evacuate
- **Lightning**: if you hear thunder, get off ridges/exposed areas, avoid tall isolated trees, water, caves (lightning can travel through ground)
  - Position: crouch on insulative pad, away from metal poles. Wait 30 min after last thunder
  - Most dangerous: being above tree line in afternoon thunderstorm (common in mountains)
- **Falls**: leading cause of hiking injury/fatality. Watch footing, don't step where you can't see, take breaks when tired
  - Downhill accidents more common (fatigue, less control) — slow down, use poles, pay attention
  - If injured alone: stay put if you can (easier to find you), blow whistle 3 times (universal signal), call for help if service
- **Wildlife encounters**: give them space, don't run (trigger chase instinct), make noise to avoid surprising (bear bells don't work — yell "hey bear" every 30 seconds in brushy areas)
  - Bear encounter: identify as human (wave arms, speak calmly, don't stare directly into eyes). Back away slowly. Do NOT run. Bear spray ready
  - Grizzly charge: stand your ground (grizzlies often bluff charge). If contact (they hit you): play dead — face down, hands clasped behind neck, legs spread (harder to roll you)
  - Black bear charge: DO NOT PLAY DEAD — fight back aggressively (aim for face/eyes), use rocks/sticks/bear spray. Most black bear attacks are predatory (not defensive)
  - Moose: more dangerous than bears (aggressive, especially in spring with calves). Give LOTS of space, climb a tree if charged, get behind something solid
  - Mountain lions: stand tall, make yourself big, maintain eye contact, don't run, fight back if attacked (protect neck/throat)

## Ten Essentials (Always Carry)
1. Navigation (map + compass + GPS/phone with offline maps)
2. Headlamp/flashlight + extra batteries
3. Sun protection (sunscreen, lip balm, hat, sunglasses)
4. First aid kit (including blister care)
5. Knife/multi-tool
6. Fire (lighter + waterproof matches in waterproof case)
7. Emergency shelter (emergency bivvy, space blanket — 2 oz, cheap, can save your life)
8. Extra food (1+ day extra)
9. Extra water (or water treatment)
10. Extra clothes (insulation layer + rain shell)
