# Grief, Loss & Coping

## Types of Grief
- **Normal grief**: sadness, anger, guilt, numbness, longing — comes in waves, gradually less intense over time
- **Anticipatory grief**: grieving before loss (terminal illness, impending divorce)
- **Complicated grief**: intense, persistent (>12 months), interferes with daily functioning — may need professional help
- **Disenfranchised grief**: loss not socially recognized (pet loss, miscarriage, non-marital partner, estranged family)
- **Collective grief**: shared societal loss (public figures, tragedies, climate grief)
- **Ambiguous loss**: no closure (missing person, dementia, addiction where person is alive but changed)

## Five Stages (Kubler-Ross)
1. **Denial**: "This can't be happening" — numbs shock, buffers immediate pain
2. **Anger**: "Why me?" — anger at God, doctors, self, deceased — masks deeper pain
3. **Bargaining**: "If only I had..." — guilt, what-ifs, attempts to negotiate with reality
4. **Depression**: deep sadness, withdrawal — acknowledges loss fully, necessary for healing
5. **Acceptance**: not "okay with it" but learning to live with the new reality
- Stages are NOT linear — people bounce between them, skip some, revisit

## What Helps
- **Let yourself feel**: cry, scream, rest — suppressing emotions prolongs grief
- **Talk about it**: share memories, tell stories — keeps connection alive
- **Rituals**: light a candle, visit gravesite, create memory box, annual tradition
- **Write**: journal to the person who died, grief journal, unsent letters
- **Physical outlets**: walking, yoga, exercise — grief lives in the body too
- **Avoid numbing**: alcohol, drugs, excessive work/gaming/social media — delays processing
- **Support groups**: others who've experienced similar loss (local, online) — reduces isolation
- **Professional help**: if grief feels stuck, overwhelming, or you can't function after months

## Supporting Someone Who's Grieving
- **DO**: say "I'm sorry", "I'm here", "Tell me about them", bring food, check in consistently (not just first week)
- **DON'T**: say "they're in a better place", "everything happens for a reason", "you should be over it by now", "at least..."
- Practical help: cook meals, do laundry, walk dog, run errands, help with paperwork
- Remember anniversaries (death date, birthday) — they matter
- Listen more than talk — don't try to fix it
- Keep checking in after the first month (most support fades, grief doesn't)

## When Grief Requires Professional Help
- Can't perform basic self-care for extended period
- Substance use to cope
- Thoughts of self-harm or suicide (call 988/emergency)
- Complete isolation, inability to leave house
- Intense guilt or self-blame that won't ease
- Feeling stuck in anger or numbness with no relief
- Traumatic loss (violence, suicide, accident) may need trauma-specific therapy

## Major Life Transitions
- **Divorce/separation**: grieve the relationship, future plans, shared identity — allow yourself to mourn
- **Job loss**: grieve identity, routine, community — loss of work is loss of structure
- **Empty nest**: grief for daily parenting role, reconnect with partner/self
- **Health diagnosis**: grieve old abilities, future assumptions, body changes
- **Moving**: grieve place, community, familiarity — takes 1-2 years to feel at home
- **Retirement**: grieve purpose, social connections, identity — pre-plan for this

## Miscarriage & Pregnancy Loss
- Very real grief (often disenfranchised) — affects all family members
- Common (15-20% of known pregnancies) — that doesn't make it less painful
- Partners grieve too, often silently
- Support groups, memorial rituals, naming the baby can help
- No timeline for "trying again" — each person is different
- Professional counseling specifically for pregnancy loss exists

## Pet Loss
- Valid grief — for many, pet is family, daily companion, source of unconditional love
- People often hide pet grief (fear of being judged) — seek pet loss support groups
- Rituals: burial, photo album, paw print keepsake, planting a tree
- Guilt about euthanasia decisions is common — making the kindest choice is the hardest
- No rush to get a new pet — grieving fully first leads to healthier future relationship
