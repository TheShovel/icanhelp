# Public Speaking & Presentation

## Overcoming Fear
- Glossophobia (fear of public speaking) is the #1 fear — more than death (Jerry Seinfeld: "at a funeral, most people would rather be in the coffin than give the eulogy")
- **Adrenaline is good**: that nervous energy = performance energy. Reframe "I'm nervous" as "I'm excited" (studies show this improves performance)
- **Preparation**: knowing your material is the #1 anxiety reducer — practice until you can't get it wrong
- **Beta blockers**: propranolol (prescription) blocks physical symptoms of anxiety (shaky voice, pounding heart) — discuss with doctor
- **Start small**: speak at team meetings first, then larger groups, build up gradually
- **Breathe**: slow deep breaths before starting (4 in, 4 hold, 6 out) — calms nervous system

## Preparation
- **Know your audience**: what do they care about? What do they already know? What do they need?
- **One big idea**: what's the single thing you want people to remember? Everything supports that
- **Structure**: tell them what you'll say → say it → tell them what you said
  - Opening: hook (question, story, surprising fact, quote)
  - Body: 3 main points max (rule of three is memorable)
  - Close: call to action or memorable takeaway
- **Outline, not script**: bullets on notecards, not word-for-word — scripts sound rehearsed, bullets sound natural
  - If you MUST script: memorize opening and closing sentences, know the rest as ideas
- **Slides**: visual aid, NOT your script. One idea per slide. 10 words max per slide
  - Images > text. No reading slides (audience reads faster than you speak)
  - Font: 30pt minimum. No clipart. Consistent design

## Delivery
- **Opening**: first 30 seconds set the tone — start strong (story, question, bold statement, silence)
  - Never start with "Hi, I'm [name] and I'm going to talk about..." (boring)
  - Introduce yourself AFTER the hook (after you've grabbed attention)
- **Eye contact**: 3-5 seconds per person, scan room, don't stare at one spot or read slides
  - Triangle method: look at left, center, right sections of room — everyone feels included
- **Voice**: vary pitch (monotone kills), slow down (nerves speed you up), pause for emphasis
  - Silence is powerful — don't fill with "um", "uh", "like", "you know"
  - Record yourself — you'll hear fillers you don't notice live
- **Hands**: gestures add energy, but don't flail or keep hands in pockets
  - Rest position: hands at sides (not clasped in front, not behind back)
  - Use gestures to emphasize: count on fingers, indicate size, point to slide
- **Movement**: step forward for emphasis, step sideways between sections, don't pace
  - Stand still for key points, move during transitions

## Visual Aids
- **Slides**: dark text on light background (best contrast). Light text on dark = dramatic but harder to read
  - 1 slide per minute (10 min talk = 10 slides max)
  - Animations: use sparingly — dissolve/reveal are fine, spinning/flying = unprofessional
- **Props**: memorable and effective (Steve Jobs's envelope with MacBook Air)
- **Whiteboard**: good for spontaneous explanations, showing process — practice writing straight
- **Handouts**: give AFTER the talk (before = audience reads instead of listening)

## Handling Q&A
- Repeat the question (ensures everyone heard, buys you thinking time)
- If you don't know: "I don't know, but I'll find out and get back to you" — never bluff
  - Follow up within 24 hours (gives credibility)
- If question is hostile: stay calm, find common ground, "That's one perspective, here's another"
  - "That's a great question" sounds dismissive if the question actually isn't — just answer it
- If someone talks too long: "Let me stop you there and make sure I understand. Your question is [paraphrase]?"
- Redirect to audience: "Does anyone have experience with that?"
- Bridge back: "That's a good point. But returning to my main point..." (stay on message)
- Close Q&A on a high note: have a strong closing statement ready regardless of last question

## Remote Presentations (Zoom, Teams)
- **Lighting**: face a window or ring light (in front, not above — no raccoon eyes)
- **Camera at eye level**: stack books under laptop — looking down = unflattering
- **Sound**: earbuds/headset > laptop mic. Mute when not speaking
- **Eye contact**: look at camera, not yourself on screen — put speaker view near camera
- **Share screen**: clean up desktop (no personal files), close notifications, have windows open before sharing
- **Engaging**: chat for questions, polls, reactions, call on people by name
  - Use hand raise feature — don't interrupt
- **Pace**: slightly slower than in-person (lag, processing time)
- **Background**: clean or virtual (not messy real room)

## Storytelling
- **Stories > data**: data tells, stories sell. Use data to SUPPORT the story, not be the story
- **Personal stories work best**: vulnerability builds connection — appropriate discomfort shows courage
  - "I failed at X and learned Y" is more compelling than "Here's how to succeed"
- **Structure**: character (someone relatable) → problem (challenge they faced) → discovery (what they learned) → transformation (how they changed) → takeaway (what audience learns)
- **Details**: specific (the blue binder, Tuesday at 3 PM, the smell of coffee) > vague (one day)
- **Emotion**: make them feel something — joy, surprise, sadness, inspiration — emotional content is remembered 2x more

## Toastmasters Method
- Table topics: impromptu speaking (1-2 min on random topic) — practice thinking on feet
- Evaluations: every speech gets constructive feedback (what worked + what to improve)
- Pathways: structured program with specific projects (persuasive speaking, technical topics, storytelling)
- Find a club: toastmasters.org — $60-120/year, weekly meetings
- Even 6 months of Toastmasters dramatically improves speaking ability for most people

## Technology & Backup
- Have a backup: PDF of slides on USB, handouts, know your material well enough to speak without slides
- Test equipment before: projector, laptop connection, clicker, audio, internet (for remote)
- Arrive early (30 min minimum) to set up and test
- Ask: is there a microphone? Handheld, lapel, or podium? Use it (even if you're loud — recorded for remote)
- If tech fails: "Looks like technology isn't cooperating. Let me tell you about..." — have a no-slides version ready
