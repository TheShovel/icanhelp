# News & Media Literacy

## How to Evaluate a News Source
- **Check the source**: who published it? Do they have a reputation for accuracy? (AP, Reuters, BBC, NPR, WSJ, NYT, Washington Post have editorial standards)
- **Check the author**: are they a journalist or an op-ed writer? Do they cover this beat?
- **Check the date**: is this old news being shared as current?
- **Check the evidence**: are claims backed by sources, data, quotes? Or just assertions?
- **Check other outlets**: are other reputable outlets reporting the same story?
- **Check for bias**: all outlets have bias — understand where they lean (AllSides, Media Bias/Fact Check)
- **Check if it's news or opinion**: opinion/editorial is not straight news — different standards
- **Check the URL**: lookalike domains (cnn-trending.com vs cnn.com), fake sites mimicking real ones
- **Check images**: reverse image search (Google Images, TinEye) — images often taken out of context or AI-generated

## Types of Misinformation
- **Misinformation**: false information shared without intent to harm (someone genuinely believes it)
- **Disinformation**: false information deliberately created and spread to deceive
- **Malinformation**: true information shared with intent to cause harm (doxxing, out-of-context quotes)
- **Clickbait**: exaggerated/ misleading headlines to get clicks — "You won't BELIEVE what happened"
- **Propaganda**: information (true or false) spread to promote a political agenda
- **Deepfakes**: AI-generated video/audio that looks/sounds real — increasingly convincing
- **AI-generated text**: ChatGPT and similar can produce convincing but false content
- **Satire**: humorous fake news (The Onion, Babylon Bee) — meant to entertain, not deceive

## Logical Fallacies in Media
- **Ad hominem**: attacking the person instead of the argument
- **Straw man**: misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack
- **False dichotomy**: presenting only two options when more exist
- **Slippery slope**: claiming one step leads to extreme outcome without evidence
- **Appeal to authority**: "experts say" without naming experts or evidence
- **Cherry-picking**: selecting only data that supports your position, ignoring contrary data
- **False balance**: giving equal weight to both sides when one side has overwhelming evidence (climate change, vaccines)
- **Correlation = causation**: two things happen together, one doesn't necessarily cause the other (ice cream sales and drowning both increase in summer — not causal)
- **Anecdotal evidence**: "my uncle smoked and lived to 95" doesn't disprove that smoking causes cancer

## How Algorithms Shape What You See
- Social media algorithms prioritize: engagement > accuracy — outrage, fear, and confirmation bias get more clicks
- Filter bubble: algorithm shows you content aligned with your beliefs, hides opposing views
- Echo chamber: surrounded by only like-minded people/opinions → views become more extreme
- Radicalization pipeline: YouTube/TikTok can progressively recommend more extreme content
- Break the filter: actively seek out diverse perspectives, follow people you disagree with, use media bias charts
- Algorithmic literacy: understand that "trending" means many people engaged, not that it's true or important

## Verifying Information (SIFT Method)
1. **Stop**: before sharing or believing, pause
2. **Investigate the source**: who wrote/produced this? What's their reputation and agenda?
3. **Find trusted coverage**: what do other reputable sources say? Use "news search" not just search
4. **Trace claims, quotes, and media back to the original context**: is this being shared out of context?
- Fact-checking sites: Snopes, FactCheck.org, PolitiFact, Reuters Fact Check, AP Fact Check

## Responsible Sharing
- Read the article before sharing (headlines can be misleading)
- Don't share if you only read the headline
- Don't share if it makes you angry (emotional manipulation by design)
- Wait 24 hours before sharing breaking news (initial reports are often wrong)
- Correct your own mistakes publicly when you share something wrong
- If you're not sure, don't share — verify first

## Understanding Statistics
- **Relative vs absolute risk**: "doubles your risk" sounds scary — but if baseline risk was 1 in 10,000 → 2 in 10,000, that's still very low
- **Mean vs median**: "average salary $200k" with a few billionaires skews the mean, median is more representative
- **Small samples**: any study with <100 participants should be taken very cautiously
- **No control group**: can't claim effectiveness without comparing to no treatment
- **P-hacking**: running many statistical tests until one comes up as "significant" (like flipping a coin 100 times, you'll get 10 heads in a row eventually)
- **Survivorship bias**: looking at successful people/companies and ignoring all the failures
- **Base rate fallacy**: ignoring how common something is in the general population (rare diseases — positive test often wrong)

## Conspiracy Theories
- Common features: claims of secret plots, no evidence accepted as proof against it ("that's what THEY want you to think"), all evidence is interpreted to fit the theory, evil powerful groups
- Why people believe: need for certainty, feeling of special knowledge, distrust of institutions, social belonging
- How to talk to someone in a conspiracy: don't ridicule, ask questions about evidence, offer alternative explanations, don't expect immediate change
- When it's harmful: refusing medical treatment, violence, isolation from family, financial scams

## Health Information
- Peer-reviewed studies > news articles about studies > blog posts about news articles
- Single study ≠ scientific consensus (look for meta-analyses and systematic reviews)
- Check: who funded the study? (Tobacco company study on smoking = skeptical)
- Correlation ≠ causation — see above
- Extraordinary claims need extraordinary evidence
- Reliable sources: WHO, CDC, NIH, major medical journals (NEJM, Lancet, BMJ, JAMA)
- Be skeptical of: "miracle cure", "doctors hate this", one weird trick, testimonials instead of data
