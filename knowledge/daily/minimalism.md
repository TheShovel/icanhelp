# Minimalism & Simple Living

## Core Principles
- **Minimalism**: intentionally living with only what you need and what brings joy — less stuff, more life
- NOT about deprivation, empty rooms, or owning <100 items (that's extreme minimalism)
- About asking: "Does this add value to my life?" before acquiring or keeping things
- The benefits: less cleaning, less clutter, less debt, less decision fatigue, more time, more money, more focus

## Getting Started
1. **30-day minimalism game**: day 1 remove 1 item, day 2 remove 2 items... 30 days = 465 items
   - Don't trash everything — sell (eBay, FB Marketplace, Craigslist), donate, give away, recycle
2. **One-in-one-out**: for every new item, one old item leaves
3. **No-buy period**: 30-90 days with no non-essential purchases (food, toiletries, bills excluded)
4. **Declutter by category** (KonMari method): clothes → books → papers → miscellaneous → sentimental
5. **The container method**: limit each category to a specific container (e.g., one drawer for T-shirts, if it overflows, something goes)

## Room-by-Room
- **Closet**: keep only what fits, flatters, and gets worn. If you haven't worn it in a year, it goes
  - Uniform approach: capsule wardrobe (30-40 items total, mix and match)
  - Quality over quantity: 5 good shirts > 20 cheap ones
- **Kitchen**: do you use it? Do you have a duplicate? Do you even know what it is?
  - Keep: daily essentials, quality tools. Donate: single-use gadgets (unless you actually use it regularly)
  - Most people use 20% of kitchen items 80% of the time
- **Living room**: surfaces clear, visible storage minimal, only meaningful decor
  - Books: keep favorites, donate rest (public libraries still exist)
  - Electronics: cable management, hide what isn't used daily
- **Digital**: clean desktop, organized files, unsubscribe from emails, delete unused apps, organized photos

## Financial Minimalism
- Spend on experiences > things (experiences bring more lasting happiness)
- Buy for quality and longevity, not trend
- Borrow rarely-used items (books from library, tools from neighbor, camping gear from friend)
- Avoid: storage units (paying to store stuff you don't need), buying in bulk (unless you use it), "might need someday" items
- Maintenance cost: every physical item costs time (clean, organize, repair, move) — factor that in

## Digital Minimalism (Cal Newport)
- **Digital declutter**: 30 day break from optional technology, then reintroduce intentionally
- Social media: unfollow noise, schedule usage, delete apps from phone (use browser only)
- Notifications: turn off all non-essential (most apps don't need your attention)
- Phone: grayscale display (reduces addictiveness), no apps on home screen except essentials
- Single-task: close tabs, one window at a time, no multitasking
- Scheduled low-quality leisure: don't fill every gap with scrolling — be bored, it's good for creativity

## Mindful Consumption
- **30-day rule**: if you want to buy something non-essential, wait 30 days. Usually the urge passes
- **Cost-per-use**: $100 coat worn 100 times = $1/wear. $20 coat worn 3 times = $6.67/wear
- **Before buying ask**: Do I need it? Do I have something that already does this? Will I use it in 6 months? Where will I store it?
- **Rent vs buy**: tools, camping gear, formal wear, party supplies
- **Secondhand first**: thrift stores, FB Marketplace, Craigslist, eBay — better for planet, better for wallet

## Downsizing
- If moving to smaller space: measure first, be ruthless about what makes the cut
- Sentimental items: take photo, keep one or two meaningful pieces, let rest go
- Family heirlooms: ask relatives if anyone wants them — if not, let them go (they're not obligated to keep them either)
- Digitize: photos, documents, artwork, letters — keeps memory without physical bulk
- Gift obligation: you're not obligated to keep things people give you — the gift was the giving, not the keeping

## Benefits Beyond Stuff
- **Time**: less shopping, cleaning, organizing, maintaining, deciding what to wear
- **Money**: fewer purchases, smaller home possible, less storage, less maintenance
- **Focus**: visual clutter is mental clutter — empty space is calm
- **Freedom**: ability to move, travel, change jobs without being weighed down
- **Environment**: less consumption = less waste, less manufacturing, less shipping

## Common Criticisms & Responses
- "Minimalism is for privileged people" — minimalism saves money (spend less), it's not about buying expensive minimalist decor
- "But I NEED all my stuff" — most people genuinely need far less than they think. Try boxing things you think you need for 30 days
- "What if I need it someday?" — ask yourself: can I borrow it? Buy it used? Rent it? Replace it for under $20 in 20 minutes? If yes, let it go
- "My stuff makes me happy" — if it genuinely brings you joy, keep it. Minimalism is about intentionality, not suffering
- "I can't because I have kids/family" — kids adapt and often benefit (less clutter, more attention, more creativity with fewer toys)
