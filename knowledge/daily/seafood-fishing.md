# Fish, Seafood & Fishing Basics

## Seafood Selection & Sustainability
- **Sustainability ratings**: Monterey Bay Aquarium Seafood Watch (free app/guide — "Best Choice", "Good Alternative", "Avoid")
  - Best choices: farmed mussels/oysters/clams, Pacific salmon (wild), Alaskan cod, domestic catfish, farmed rainbow trout, sardines, anchovies
  - Avoid: Atlantic salmon (farmed = concerns), imported shrimp (farmed), Chilean sea bass (overfished), bluefin tuna (overfished, critically endangered), shark, orange roughy, Atlantic cod (still recovering)
- **Fresh fish signs**: smells like ocean (not fishy), firm flesh (springs back), clear eyes (not cloudy), red gills (not brown), shiny skin, no gaping flesh
  - Buy from reputable fishmonger. If it smells "fishy" = old (fishy = TMAO breaking down into trimethylamine)
- **Fish to avoid eating raw**: freshwater fish (parasites), some marine fish (use sushi-grade/sushi-quality = frozen to -4°F for 7 days to kill parasites). Know your source

## Fish Identification
- **White fish** (mild, versatile): cod, haddock, halibut, tilapia (farmed, mild), pollock (used in fish sticks/imitation crab — surimi), catfish (mild, slightly sweet), sea bass, grouper, snapper, flounder, sole
  - All cook similarly: bake, pan-fry, steam, poach. Cook until flaky (internal temp 145°F)
- **Rich fish** (higher fat, stronger flavor, heart-healthy omega-3s): salmon (Atlantic, Pacific — sockeye, coho, king, pink), tuna (bluefin, yellowfin/ahi, skipjack), mackerel, sardines, anchovies, herring, trout
  - Omega-3s (EPA + DHA): highest in salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring, anchovies — 2 servings/week reduces heart disease risk
- **Shellfish**: shrimp (most popular US seafood), crab (king, snow, Dungeness, blue, stone), lobster (Maine/American, spiny/rock), scallops (bay = sweet, small; sea = large, meaty), clams (littlenecks, cherrystones, chowder), mussels (farmed = sustainable), oysters (east coast = briny, west coast = creamy/sweet — eat raw or cooked)
  - Buying: live shellfish should be closed or close when tapped. Discard any that stay open (dead = unsafe)
  - Mussels: debeard (pull fibrous beard before cooking). Clams: soak in salt water 20 min to purge sand

## Cooking Seafood
- **General rule**: fish cooks fast (10 min per inch of thickness at 400-450°F). Overcooking = dry, rubbery
  - Fish is done when: flesh flakes easily with fork (cook until just opaque). For salmon: medium = translucent in center (some prefer fully cooked through)
  - Carryover cooking: fish continues cooking after removed from heat — pull it slightly before perfect
- **Pan-searing**: pat dry (critical!), season, high heat, oil, cook skin-side down first (crisp), don't move fish until skin releases (if it sticks, it's not ready), flip once
  - Skin-on: scores through skin (prevents curling), press down with spatula for even contact
- **Baking**: 400°F, 10-15 min depending on thickness. En papillote (in parchment packet with herbs/veg/butter/wine — steams, Infused flavor)
- **Grilling**: firm fish only (salmon, tuna, swordfish, halibut), oil well, clean hot grill, cook 4-6 min per side. Use fish basket for delicate fish
- **Steaming**: delicate fish, keeps moist, subtle flavor. Season fish, place on steamer basket over simmering water, cover, 8-10 min
- **Poaching**: submerse in simmering liquid (court-bouillon, wine, milk, water + herbs), 160-180°F (not boiling), 8-12 min
- **Ceviche**: raw fish "cooked" in citrus acid (lime, lemon) — fresh fish essential, marinate 15-30 min (longer = firmer/whiter), serve immediately
- **Shrimp**: peel/devein (optional), cook until pink + C-shaped (overcooked = O-shaped, rubbery). 2-3 min per side pan-fried, 3-5 min boiled
- **Mussels/clams**: steam in white wine + garlic + herbs, covered, until open (5-8 min). Discard any that don't open
- **Lobster/crab**: boil whole (lobster 8-15 min depending on size), serve with melted butter. Or steam for more flavor, less waterlogged texture

## Fishing Basics
- **Types**: freshwater (lakes, rivers, streams — bass, trout, catfish, panfish, pike), saltwater (ocean — striped bass, flounder, blues, tuna, marlin, snapper), fly fishing (artificial flies, trout/salmon, technique-heavy)
  - License required (state-specific, money goes to conservation). Know regulations (size limits, bag limits, seasons)
- **Gear**: rod (power/action: light for panfish/trout, medium for bass, heavy for big game), reel (spinning = beginner-friendly, baitcasting = accurate/higher learning curve, fly = specialized)
  - Line: monofilament (stretchy, cheap, beginner), fluorocarbon (invisible underwater, less stretch), braided (strongest, thin, no stretch)
- **Tackle**: hooks (circle = fish-friendly/hooks corner of mouth, J-hook), sinkers/weights, bobbers/floats (show bite), swivels (prevent line twist), leaders (abrasion-resistant, bite protection)
  - Lures: soft plastic worms (versatile bass bait), crankbaits (diving, imitate minnows), spoons (flashy, for casting/trolling), spinners (blade spins for flash/vibration), jigs (lead head + skirt, bounce off bottom), flies (dry = float on surface, wet = underwater, nymph = imitate aquatic insects)
- **Bait**: live (worms, minnows, crawfish, nightcrawlers, leeches, grasshoppers — most effective but need to keep alive), cut bait (chunks of fish for catfish), artificial (plastics, spinners, scented baits)
- **Catching**: cast where fish are (structure: rocks, logs, weed lines, drop-offs, points — fish hold near cover). Retrieve at different speeds. Feel for bites (tap/tug). Set hook (firm upward snap). Reel, keep rod tip up

## Fish Cleaning & Filleting
- **Scaler**: remove scales (scrape tail → head, under running water). Sharp knife: make cut behind gills to backbone, turn knife flat, cut along backbone to tail, lift fillet, remove rib cage with tip, remove skin (hold tail, knife between skin and flesh, pull)
  - Practice on whole fish — YouTube essential for technique
- **Pan-dressing**: for smaller fish (panfish, trout) — scale, gut (cut belly from vent to gills, pull out innards), remove head (optional), fins. Cook whole (bones removed at table)
- **Pollution concerns**: mercury (larger/older predator fish: shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish — limit especially pregnant/nursing), PCBs/dioxins — vary by location
  - General: smaller fish (sardines, anchovies, mackerel) lower in contaminants, high in omega-3s

## Seasonal Guide (Northern Hemisphere)
- **Spring**: trout, walleye, crappie, shad — fish moving to shallow after winter (spawning season). Best time for fly fishing
- **Summer**: bass, catfish, bluegill, salmon, tuna, mackerel — early morning/evening best (fish avoid midday heat), deeper water during day
- **Fall**: salmon run (Great Lakes, Pacific Northwest), striped bass, blues, albacore, walleye — fish feeding up for winter, aggressive
- **Winter**: ice fishing (perch, walleye, pike, crappie — drill hole in ice, jig). Still fishing (deep water for trout/walleye in warmest part of day). Many species go deep/less active — slow presentation, smaller baits
