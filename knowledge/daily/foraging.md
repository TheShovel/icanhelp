# Foraging & Wild Edibles

## Safety Rules
- **100% positive identification** before eating — use multiple guides, don't guess. Many edibles have toxic look-alikes. "When in doubt, throw it out." Learn from experts (local foraging groups, guided walks). Start with 5-10 easy-to-ID plants (dandelion, chickweed, stinging nettle, wild violet, lambs quarters, wood sorrel — mushrooms need expert identification)
- **Avoid contaminated areas**: roadsides (heavy metals, road salt, exhaust). Agricultural fields (pesticides, herbicides). Industrial sites (contamination). Dog walking areas (parasites). Drainage ditches. Pick >100 ft from roads, >500 ft from industrial sites. Avoid private property without permission
- **Allergy test**: try small amount first (some people react to new foods). Use Universal Edibility Test if unsure (but better to know before). Some plants cause photosensitivity (St. John's wort, giant hogweed — severe burns when exposed to sun). Don't eat raw new-to-you wild foods; cook when possible
- **Sustainable harvesting**: take <10% of a patch, leave roots (unless harvesting root), don't harvest rare/ endangered species (ramps/wild leeks: take ONE leaf per plant, not whole plant — slow to regrow). Check local regulations (state parks, national forests have rules). Harvest from abundant populations, not first/only plant you see. Re-seed or spread spores (for mushrooms — tap mature mushroom to disperse spores)

## Spring Edibles
- **Dandelion** (Taraxacum officinale): entire plant edible. Leaves (spring: mild, best raw; summer: bitter, blanch before use). Flowers: fritters, wine, jelly. Roots: roasted as coffee substitute. Look-alikes: none dangerous (catsear/hawkweed similar but also edible). Nutrition: high vitamin A, C, K, iron, calcium, antioxidants
- **Stinging nettle** (Urtica dioica): young tops (first 4-6 leaves) best. Wear gloves to pick. Cooking destroys sting completely (minute of heat). Taste: spinach-like, rich. Tea (dried nettle leaves = mineral-rich). High protein, iron, calcium, magnesium, vitamin K
- **Chickweed** (Stellaria media): mild, sweet, crisp. Raw in salads. Pairs well with other greens with stronger flavour. Tiny white star-shaped flowers. Look-alike: mouse-ear chickweed (not as good). Distinguish: chickweed has single line of hairs, opposite leaves, tiny white flowers
- **Morels** (Morchella): prized mushroom. Honeycomb cap, hollow inside. Season: spring when ground 50-60°F. Look-alikes: false morel (Gyromitra) — wrinkled, brain-like cap, NOT hollow. Distinguish: morel cap attached to stem at bottom — false morel cap hangs free. True morel = hollow from top to bottom. False = cottony/stringy inside. COOK ALL MUSHROOMS before eating
- **Wild violet** (Viola): purple/white flowers, heart-shaped leaves. Flowers: candied, tea, jelly (decorative). Leaves: salad, pesto. High vitamin C. Can be quite mild
- **Ramps/wild leeks** (Allium tricoccum): two broad leaves, garlic smell. Overharvested — dig only when abundant, replant root divisions, ONE leaf per plant

## Summer Edibles
- **Wild berries**: blackberries/raspberries (thorny canes, safest for beginners — no poisonous look-alikes). Blueberries (lowbush in rocky/wooded areas). Elderberries (cook only — raw cause nausea, edible flowers: make fritters, cordial, tea). Mulberries (white/red/black fruit; look exactly like blackberries but grow on trees — unlike blackberries on canes). Wineberries (invasive, sweet, like raspberries — best picked young, less seed). Serviceberries (Juneberries: small, blueberry-like tree fruit, great raw + jam)
  - **Never**: white berries (90%+ poisonous). Poison ivy/oak/sumac: don't eat! Most native red berries in bogs are safe; red + white in woodland are usually dangerous
- **Purslane** (Portulaca oleracea): succulent leaves, grows in gardens (prostrate on ground). Taste: lemony, crunchy. Highest plant-based source of omega-3 (alpha-linolenic acid). Great raw in salads, cooked like spinach. Thick reddish stems + small teardrop leaves, tiny yellow flowers. Look-alike: prostrate spurge (toxic) — spurge has milky sap, purplish spots, leaves in pairs, opposite. Purslane leaves are alternate, stem fleshy, not milky
- **Lamb's quarters** (Chenopodium album): "wild spinach." Triangular leaves with white powder coating (especially at growing tip). Tastes like spinach (cook like it). High in iron, calcium, protein, vitamin A + C. Better after cooking (raw too much oxalic acid). Look-alike: none really — distinctive pollen/meal coating
- **Wild grapes (Vitis spp.)**: woody vine, tendrils, opposite leaves similar to domestic grapes. Edible fruit inside skin (wild grapes more seedy, tarter). Use: jelly, juice, wine. Look-alike: Canada moonseed (poisonous!) — no tendrils, single leaf per node (not opposite), crescent-shaped seed inside (moonseed, not grape seeds). Grape = woody climbing vine. Moonseed = non-woody stem? Actually moonseed is woody in age but not with tendrils
- **Daylily** (Hemerocallis): flowers = edible (stuff, fritter, salad). Buds = like green beans. Tubers = potato-like. Eat STOLONS? Actually only flowers + buds. All parts edible but moderation (some may be laxative). Do NOT confuse with true lilies (which are toxic to cats — but daylilies are actually edible for humans). Daylily: each flower lasts one day

## Fall Edibles
- **Acorns** (oak): high tannin (bitter) — must leach (soak in hot/cold water until no brown, changing water regularly). Leaching: hot (boil, change water when turns brown — faster, 15 min). Cold (soak in cold water, change daily for 2-7 days — better flavor). Then dry + grind into flour. Oak types: White oak (less tannin, best). Red/black oak (higher tannin, need more leaching). Acorn flour: substitute up to 30% in baked goods
- **Rose hips** (Rosa): fruit of wild roses, after frost (sweeter). High vitamin C (20x oranges). Use: tea, syrup, jelly, jam. Remove seeds (hairs inside = GI irritant — fine sieve when cooking). Dried: break open, remove seeds + hairs, steep dried hips as tea
- **Hickory nuts**: difficult to crack (hard shell), sweet, good flavor (like pecan, same family). Black walnut: strong, earthy flavor, hull stains clothes yellow-brown (use for dye), very hard shell. Butternut: most mild, thin shell, best for eating. Nut cracking tools: heavy hammer + rock/ concrete
- **Persimmons** (Diospyros virginiana): native persimmon (must be SOFT ripe — astringent hard). Only eats when mushy (pulp sweet + custard-like). American persimmon smaller than Asian, grows wild in SE US. After frost: sweetest. Use: pudding, bread, cookies, smoothies. Unripe: bitter puckering tannins (can cause bezoar)
- **Jerusalem artichoke/sunchoke** (Helianthus tuberosus): sunflower tubers (grows like weed, prolific). Nutty, sweet, crunchy (raw) or potato-like (cooked). High in inulin (fiber = gas/bloat for some). Pairs well with apples (pectin reduces gas). Can eat raw (sliced thin), roast, boil, steam. Good for diabetics (inulin doesn't spike blood sugar)

## Spring Mushrooms (for experts)
- **Morel** (Morchella): hollow, honeycomb cap. Cook thoroughly
- **Oyster** (Pleurotus): white/fan-shaped, grows on dead wood, gills run down stem (decurrent gills). No poisonous look-alike. Mild + versatile, easy to ID
- **Chicken of the woods** (Laetiporus): bright orange bracket fungus on trees (oak). Taste: chicken-like. No toxic look-alike. Cook thoroughly. Individuals can react even after eating before — test first. Raw = GI upset. Only eat young tender edges
- **Hen of the woods / maitake** (Grifola frondosa): at base of oak trees, clustered gray brown caps. Fronds: one central base structure, 50+ caps. Edible, prized
- **Chanterelle** (Cantharellus): golden, wavy cap, forked ridges running down stem (not true gills). Fruity apricot smell. Look-alike: false chanterelle (Hygrophoropsis aurantiaca) — thinner, true gills, darker center. Distinguish: true chanterelle has false gills (blunt ridges, not knife-like). Poisonous look-alike: Jack-O-Lantern (Omphalotus olearius) — bioluminescent (gills glow in dark), true gills, same color, grows in clusters on wood (not ground). Chanterelle: solitary on ground
