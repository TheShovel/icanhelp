# Body Language

## Posture
- **Open vs closed**: open = uncrossed arms/legs, visible torso, facing toward — signals confidence, receptiveness, honesty. Closed = crossed arms/legs, turned away, barriers (bag, phone on table) — signals defensiveness, discomfort, disinterest (or just cold room — check context)
- **Leaning**: lean toward = interest, engagement. Lean away = disinterest, discomfort, disagreement. Mirror lean of conversation partner for rapport
- **Shoulders**: back + relaxed = confidence. Hunched/rolled forward = insecurity, low energy, submission. Shrugs = uncertainty ("I don't know"), minimizing
- **Head**: tilted = interest, listening, curiosity (also flirtation). Chin up = confidence/defiance. Chin down = evaluation, suspicion, or reading glasses. Head nodding = agreement/encouragement (exaggerated = impatience "get on with it")
- **Sitting**: forward = engaged. Reclined = relaxed/dominant. On edge = anxious. Legs spread = dominance (men). Ankles crossed = closed/tense. Feet under chair = submissive. Foot tapping = nervousness/impatience

## Eye Contact
- **Normal range**: 60-70% of time during conversation. More while listening, less while speaking. Breaking eye contact every 5-10 seconds prevents staring
- **Excessive** (>80%): staring, aggressive, intimidating, romantic interest. Make people uncomfortable — suspect lying or attraction depending on context
- **Too little** (<30%): nervous, submissive, disinterested, evasive, or culturally normed. Can signal deception (but many liars overcompensate with too much)
- **Gaze patterns**: business: look at forehead/eyes triangle. Social: eyes to mouth triangle (wider). Intimate: face to chest/body triangle. Pupil dilation = attraction/interest (hard to fake)
- **Blinking**: normal ~15-20/min. Increases: stress, attraction, deception, dry air. Decreases: concentration, threat (predator stare). Rapid blinking = anxiety
- **Looking up/away**: when thinking/recalling (not necessarily lying — contrary to myth). Right-handed people often look up-right when constructing lies (not reliable enough for evidence). Better indicator: look at timing — liars look away more when asked difficult questions

## Hand Gestures
- **Open palms**: honesty, openness, submission. "I have nothing to hide." Used in sincerity, welcoming, pleading. Palms down = authority, control, emphasis, "stop"
- **Steepling**: fingertips touching, pointing up. Signals confidence, authority, knowing. Common in experts, leaders. Used during explaining/asserting
- **Hand-to-face**: chin stroking = evaluating/thinking. Hand on cheek = interest (if vertical) or boredom (if horizontal/supported). Temple rub = doubt/frustration. Nose touch = discomfort/deception (allergic too though)
- **Arms**: crossed = defensive/closed. Arms behind back = confidence/authority ("hands tied" when nervous too). Hands on hips = readiness/assertive ("akimbo"). Hands clasped in front = submissive/polite. Hands in pockets = casual/hiding something (informal contexts)
- **Finger pointing**: aggressive/accusatory. Use whole hand or open gesture instead for assertiveness without hostility. Thumb pointing over shoulder = dismissive
- **Self-soothing**: touching neck, rubbing arm, playing with hair, adjusting clothes, touching face — signals nervousness, stress, discomfort. Increases when lying or under pressure

## Facial Expressions
- **Universal emotions** (Ekman: cross-cultural): happiness (smile, crow's feet), sadness (frown, inner brows up), anger (brows down, eyes glare, lips narrow), fear (brows raised+ pulled together, eyes wide, mouth open), surprise (brows up, eyes wide, jaw drop), disgust (nose wrinkle, upper lip raise), contempt (lip curl, one side). These are recognized across all cultures
- **Microexpressions**: full-face expressions lasting 1/25 to 1/5 sec. Often reveal true emotion before suppression. Hard to spot — requires slow-motion or training. Paul Ekman's METT training tool
- **Smile types**: genuine (Duchenne — crow's feet, eyes crinkle, involuntary — uses zygomatic + orbicularis oculi). Fake/polite (mouth only, no eyes — social smile). Sneer/smirking (asymmetrical — contempt or superiority). Tight-lipped = withholding, discomfort
- **Mouth**: lips pressed together = tension, anger, withholding. Lip biting = anxiety, self-restraint. Lip pouting = disagreement, sadness. Lip pursed = evaluation. Licking lips = nervousness or dry mouth. Yawning = tired OR stress (cooling brain)
- **Eyebrows**: raised = surprise, fear, interest (also greeting recognition). Knitted/down = anger, concentration, confusion. One raised = skepticism, query (popular myth: this alone isn't reliable). Lowered + head down = submission

## Personal Space / Proxemics
- **Zones** (Hall): Intimate (0-1.5 ft): close relationships, whispering, touch. Personal (1.5-4 ft): friends, family, good acquaintances. Social (4-12 ft): business, strangers, formal interactions. Public (12+ ft): audiences, public speaking. Adjust based on relationship and culture
- **Violations**: entering intimate zone uninvited = defensive reaction (lean back, cross arms, increase distance). Apologize if you invade space. In crowded spaces (elevator, train), compensate: avoid eye contact, minimal movement, face forward
- **Culture**: Latin/Southern European, Middle Eastern, Latin American: smaller personal space (1-2 ft), more touch. Northern European, East Asian, North American: larger space (2-4 ft), less touch. East Asian: less eye contact = respect (not evasion). Middle Eastern: more eye contact = sincerity (North Americans may find intense). These are generalizations — individual differences matter
- **Territoriality**: claiming space with objects (jacket on chair, phone on table). Invasion of workspace = stress response. Desk proximity: know your coworker's comfort zone

## Mirroring
- **Matching**: subtly copying posture, gestures, tone, speech pace of the other person. Builds rapport — people like those similar to them. Subconscious when done naturally, obvious when exaggerated
- **How**: match body orientation, arm/leg position, leaning, gestures (with delay, not simultaneous). Match voice pace and volume. Lead gradually: change posture slightly → if they follow, rapport is strong
- **Deliberate**: mirror for 5-10 seconds, then break. If they shift, wait then mirror new position. Don't mimic every move (looks mocking). Mirror only positive signals — don't mirror closed/aggressive body language
- **Anti-mirroring**: to disengage or create distance, break mirroring deliberately — shift posture, turn away, change pace. Useful in negotiation to signal independence

## Deception vs Nervousness
- **Myths**: liars don't "look away" reliably (many maintain eye contact). No single "lying tell" exists. Polygraph tests are not reliable (inadmissible in most courts). Honest people also show nervousness under accusation
- **Better indicators**: verbal-nonverbal mismatch (saying yes while shaking head no). Speech: pauses, stuttering, higher pitch, fewer details, distancing language ("that person" instead of "my friend"). More blinking. Lip pressing. Shoulder shrugs while denying. Increased self-soothing gestures
- **Baseline**: first establish person's normal behavior (casual conversation). Then watch for deviations. Someone always fidgety — fidgeting when accused means less than someone who never fidgets suddenly fidgeting
- **Truthful behavior**: more details, more consistent story (repeated), willing to correct themselves, spontaneous corrections ("actually it was Tuesday, not Monday"). Liars over-rehearse — story sounds too smooth
- **Cognitive load**: lying is mentally harder. Signs: delayed response, less complex language, more verbal fillers (um, uh, like), contradictory details later. Ask them to tell story backwards — liars struggle

## Power Poses
- **High power**: expansive posture — occupy more space, arms out/up, legs apart, chest out, chin up. Associated with testosterone increase and cortisol decrease (Carney, Cuddy, Yap 2010 — though replication mixed). Use before high-stakes situations (interviews, presentations)
- **Low power**: collapsed, small, arms wrapped, legs crossed tight, head down, touching self/neck. Signals submission, low status. Can reinforce feelings of powerlessness
- **Practical**: stand tall before interview, meeting, difficult conversation. Sit with good posture. Claim your physical space (but don't invade others'). Power posing effect is real even if hormonal claims are debated — posture affects self-perception
- **Context**: power poses appropriate in competitive/performance settings. In collaborative settings — open but not dominant. Adapt to culture and relationship

## Reading the Room
- **Group scan**: look at postures — are people open facing each other? Or closed arms, turned away? Energy level: leaning in (engaged) or back (disengaged). Eye contact: watching speaker or phones/laptops
- **Pacing**: if you're speaking, watch for signs of losing attention — glazed eyes, phone checking, fidgeting, glancing at door. Adjust: speed up, add energy, ask a question, take a break, change format
- **Emotional tone**: what's the dominant mood? Tired Friday afternoon vs energetic Monday morning. After bad news: subdued. Before holiday: antsy. Adjust your energy and message accordingly
- **Power dynamics**: who holds the room? Where do people look when someone speaks? Who interrupts whom? Who sits at head of table / by door? Who's quiet but influential? Work the room accordingly
- **Intervention**: if energy is low — stand up, move around, change the discussion format. If tension is high — acknowledge it, take a break, use humor carefully. If confused — check in, recap, clarify
