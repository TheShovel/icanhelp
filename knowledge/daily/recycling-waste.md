# Recycling & Waste Management

## Recycling Symbols — Plastics #1-7
- **#1 PET/PETE** (polyethylene terephthalate): clear plastic bottles — soda, water, peanut butter, salad dressing. Widely recycled. Downcycled into fiber (carpet, fleece, polyester fabric) or new bottles. Recycling symbol = 1 in triangle
- **#2 HDPE** (high-density polyethylene): opaque jugs — milk, detergent, shampoo, bleach, yogurt tubs. Most easily recycled. Made into bottles, pipe, plastic lumber, recycling bins. Best plastic to buy (highest recycling rate)
- **#3 PVC/V** (polyvinyl chloride): pipes, shower curtains, cling wrap, blister packs, some squeeze bottles. Rarely recycled — check local. Contains chlorine (dioxin risk if burned). Avoid buying when possible
- **#4 LDPE** (low-density polyethylene): plastic bags, shrink wrap, bread bags, six-pack rings, squeeze bottles. Not usually curbside — return to store collection bins. Recycled into trash can liners, shipping envelopes, floor tiles
- **#5 PP** (polypropylene): yogurt cups, ketchup bottles, medicine bottles, straws, bottle caps, takeout containers. Increasingly recyclable curbside (check local). Made into signal lights, battery cables, brooms, ice scrapers
- **#6 PS** (polystyrene): Styrofoam cups, takeout containers, egg cartons, meat trays, packing peanuts. Difficult to recycle — most curbside programs don't accept. Check for drop-off. Avoid when possible. Polystyrene doesn't biodegrade
- **#7 Other** (miscellaneous): polycarbonate, acrylic, nylon, mixed plastics, bioplastics (PLA). Rarely recycled. Includes many baby bottles, 5-gallon water jugs, sunglasses. BPA concern (polycarbonate). Avoid unless clearly recyclable in your system
- **General rule**: smaller numbers (#1, #2) = most widely recycled. Larger numbers = harder or impossible. Reduce use of #3, #6, #7.

## Plastic Recycling Practicalities
- **Check local**: recycling rules vary massively by municipality. What's accepted in one city may be trash in another. Look up your local hauler's website
- **Clean and dry**: rinse containers (no food residue), dry (water contaminates paper recycling). Remove caps (#5 PP often recyclable separately). Flatten cardboard
- **Wishcycling**: putting non-recyclables in bin hoping they'll be recycled. Contaminates batches — entire truckload can be sent to landfill if contamination >25%. When in doubt, throw it out
- **Plastic degradation**: most plastic downcycles (loses quality each time). PET → fiber (eventually landfill). True closed-loop recycling is rare. Reducing plastic use is more effective than recycling
- **Not recyclable curbside**: plastic bags (clog machinery — take to store drop-off), straws, utensils, coffee cups (wax/plastic lining), flexible pouches (stand-up pouches — multilayer), black plastic (sensors can't sort), dirty containers

## Composting Basics
- **Hot composting**: active aerobic decomposition at 130-160°F. Fast (2-4 months). Needs: 3:1 brown:green ratio (carbon:nitrogen), adequate moisture (like wrung sponge), frequent turning (aeration). Kills weed seeds, pathogens. Pile/bin minimum 3×3×3 ft to maintain heat
- **Cold composting**: passive, slower (6-12 months), lower temps. Pile and let sit. Easier but may not kill weed seeds. Less maintenance. Best for yard waste
- **Green materials (nitrogen-rich)**: fruit/vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, grass clippings, fresh leaves, manure (horse, cow, chicken — not dog/cat). Greens feed microorganisms
- **Brown materials (carbon-rich)**: dry leaves, straw/hay, wood chips, cardboard (non-glossy, no tape), paper, sawdust. Browns provide structure and energy
- **Do NOT compost**: meat/fish/bones (attracts pests, smell), dairy (same), oily foods, diseased plants, dog/cat waste (pathogens), treated wood, weeds gone to seed (unless hot compost), "compostable" plastics (most require industrial facility)
- **Methods**: bin (enclosed, tidy), tumbler (easy turning, faster), pile (simple, cheap), trench (dig scraps directly into garden), worm bin (vermicomposting — red wigglers, indoor-friendly, fast), Bokashi (anaerobic fermentation, pickles food waste, then bury)
- **Uses**: mature compost = dark, earthy-smelling, crumbly. Use as soil amendment (dig in), top dressing for lawn, mulch around plants, mix with potting soil. Finished in 2-12 months

## Hazardous Waste Disposal
- **What's hazardous**: paints, solvents, cleaners (bleach, ammonia), pesticides/herbicides, motor oil/antifreeze, batteries (household + car), fluorescent bulbs, electronics, propane tanks, nail polish remover, medications (unused/expired), pool chemicals
- **Never**: pour down drain, put in regular trash, dump on ground, burn. Contaminates water, soil, air. Can harm sanitation workers, damage septic/sewer
- **Disposal options**: household hazardous waste (HHW) collection events (many cities hold quarterly), permanent HHW facilities, retail takeback (Home Depot for batteries/CFLs, auto parts stores for oil/batteries), pharmacy drop boxes (medications), mail-back programs (batteries, bulbs)
- **Batteries**: single-use alkaline (check local — many now safe for trash in small quantities), rechargeable (NiMH, Li-ion, NiCd) = MUST recycle (Best Buy, Home Depot, Staples). Car batteries: auto parts stores take free. Lithium batteries: tape terminals, DO NOT trash (fire risk)
- **Electronics**: Best Buy, Staples, local e-waste recyclers (check certifications — avoid export to developing countries). Manufacturers: Apple, Dell, HP take back. Data: wipe hard drives or physically destroy before recycling

## E-Waste Recycling
- **Why**: electronics contain toxic materials (lead, mercury, cadmium, brominated flame retardants) AND valuable materials (gold, silver, copper, rare earth elements). Recycling recovers resources, prevents pollution
- **What can be recycled**: computers/laptops, monitors, phones, tablets, printers, TVs (flat panel + tube), cables/chargers, small appliances, game consoles. Nearly anything with a plug or battery
- **Data security**: factory reset phones, wipe hard drives (DBAN, Darik's Boot and Nuke, or secure erase), remove SIM/storage cards. Physical destruction: drill through hard drive platters (for high-security). Use certified recycler (e-Stewards, R2 certification)
- **Find recyclers**: Call2Recycle (batteries), EPA's electronics donation/recycling locator, Earth911, local city/county programs. Avoid: unlicensed scrap yards, export to developing countries, "free" pickup services that may dump
- **Donation vs recycling**: working devices (< 5 years old) → donate (Goodwill Dell Reconnect, The Salvation Army, schools). Broken/old → recycle. Some libraries accept used devices for digital divide programs

## Reducing Single-Use Plastics
- **Top swaps**: reusable water bottle (replace 300+ bottles/yr), reusable shopping bags, produce bags (mesh/cotton), reusable coffee cup, stainless straw, beeswax wrap (instead of plastic wrap), silicone food bags (instead of Ziploc), bar soap instead of liquid (less plastic), bulk bin shopping (bring own containers)
- **Refuse first**: decline straw, plastic bag, single-use cutlery, condiment packets, receipt (often coated, not recyclable). Say "no thanks" — simplest reduction
- **Packaging**: choose glass/metal/cardboard over plastic when possible. Buy concentrated products. Avoid individually wrapped portions. Buy larger sizes (less packaging per unit). Bring your own container to deli/butcher (some stores allow)
- **Microplastics**: synthetic clothes (polyester, nylon, acrylic) shed microfibers in wash. Solutions: wash less, use Guppyfriend bag or Cora Ball, full loads, cold water, air dry. Microplastics found in human blood, lungs, placentas — avoid where possible

## Zero Waste Principles
- **The 5 Rs** (Bea Johnson, Zero Waste Home):
  1. **Refuse**: what you don't need (freebies, junk mail, plastic bags)
  2. **Reduce**: what you do need (buy less, choose durable)
  3. **Reuse**: what you consume (repair, thrift, reusable containers)
  4. **Rot**: compost organic waste
  5. **Recycle**: only what you can't refuse/reduce/reuse
- **Circular economy**: products designed to be reused, repaired, remanufactured, or composted — no "waste." Opposite of linear take-make-waste model. Companies moving toward this: repair programs, refill stations, product-as-service models
- **Zero waste to landfill**: ambitious goal. Realistic approach: incremental reduction. 80% reduction beats perfectionism that leads to giving up. Start with easy swaps, build habits over time

## Municipal Waste Systems
- **Collection types**: single-stream recyclables (all in one bin — convenient, higher contamination), dual-stream (paper separate from containers — cleaner, higher quality), pay-as-you-throw (bag/sticker fee incentivizes waste reduction)
- **Landfills**: modern lined landfills (clay + plastic liner, leachate collection system, methane capture for energy). Older unlined = groundwater contamination risk. Landfill gas: 50% methane, 50% CO2 — captured for energy or flared (methane = 25x more potent greenhouse gas than CO2)
- **Incineration / waste-to-energy**: burn waste (at 1800°F+), generate electricity. Reduces volume by 90%. Concerns: air pollution (dioxins, heavy metals — modern scrubbers reduce), ash disposal (fly ash = hazardous), discourages recycling (need trash to burn). Common in Europe, Japan, parts of US Northeast
- **Recycling economics**: recyclables are commodities — prices fluctuate based on global market (China's National Sword 2018 banned most imported recyclables → US recycling crisis). Low contamination = higher value. Dirty recycling costs more to process than it's worth — reason some cities reduced/stopped recycling
- **Organic waste**: some cities mandate food waste separation for composting (SF, Seattle, NY, CA statewide SB 1383). Diverts 30-40% of waste from landfill. Anaerobic digestion: food waste → methane captured for energy → digestate used as fertilizer
- **Landfill alternatives**: community swap events, repair cafes, freecycle/Buy Nothing groups, "trash nothing" apps — keep items in use before considering disposal
