# Hosting Parties & Gatherings

## Types of Gatherings
- **Dinner party**: 4-10 people, sit-down meal, more formal, planned seating
  - Best for: deeper conversation, celebrating, impressing
  - Effort: high (cooking, table setting, timing multiple courses)
- **Cocktail party**: 10-30 people, standing mingling, finger foods, drinks
  - Best for: mixing different friend groups, networking, less pressure
  - Effort: moderate (drinks setup, snacks that don't require plates/silverware)
- **Potluck**: everyone brings a dish — less work for host, more variety, community-building
  - Coordinate to avoid duplicates (sign-up sheet: main, side, salad, dessert)
  - Good for: large groups, casual, new community
- **Game night**: 4-15 people, board games/card games/video games
  - Best for: interactive fun, groups that know each other, low alcohol focus
  - Have variety: party games (Codenames, Telestrations, Wavelength), strategy (Ticket to Ride, Catan), quick (Love Letter, Sushi Go)
- **Movie night**: casual, low interaction, projector or big TV
  - Pick movie everyone can agree on (or let group vote)
  - Provide popcorn, blankets, dim lighting
- **BBQ/backyard**: outdoor, casual, grilled food, daytime or evening
  - Best for: families, warm weather, large groups
  - Have shade and seating for everyone, bug spray, music at conversation level

## Planning
- **Guest list**: consider who will get along, mix of personalities, don't invite people who actively dislike each other
  - Size: for first time hosting, start small (4-8 people)
  - Even numbers help conversation, odd numbers can work too
  - One truly good gathering > trying to do too many and burning out
- **Invitations**: 1-2 weeks in advance for casual, 3-4 weeks for formal/holiday
  - Include: date, time, location (with address), what to expect (dinner? drinks? potluck?), dress code if any, RSVP by date
  - Digital: Partiful, Paperless Post, Evite. Text for casual. Paper for formal.
  - Follow up with non-responders 2-3 days before
- **Menu**: make ahead as much as possible — day-of cooking should be minimal (reheat, assemble, garnish)
  - Dietary restrictions: ask in advance (vegetarian, vegan, gluten-free, allergies — at least 1 option for most common)
  - If cooking: prep ingredients day before, set table morning of, cook what you know (not the time to experiment)
  - For 8 people: 3-4 appetizers, 1 main + 2 sides + salad, 1-2 desserts

## Setting the Scene
- **Lighting**: dimmer > bright overhead. Candles (real or LED), string lights, floor lamps
  - Warm lighting (2700K) makes everyone look better, room feel cozier
- **Music**: background, appropriate volume (conversation level), genre matching mood
  - Playlist ready before guests arrive (acoustic/jazz for dinner, upbeat for cocktail, chill for after)
  - No music too distracting or with explicit lyrics
  - Streaming preset playlists: Dinner Party, Cocktail Hour, Chill Vibes, Classic Rock
- **Seating**: enough seats for everyone, arrange for conversation (circle > line against wall)
  - For dinner: name cards optional but thoughtful — place guests who'd get along near each other
  - Separate couples (they already talk to each other — let them talk to others)
  - Conversation starter next to each place: a question card, or simply seat interesting people together
- **Temperature**: slightly cool is better than too warm (people warm up with bodies + alcohol)
- **Bathroom**: extra toilet paper visible, hand soap, clean towel, air freshener, plunger nearby (embarrassing but essential)

## As Host
- **Greeting**: answer door within 30 seconds, welcome warmly, take coat, offer drink immediately
  - If someone arrives who doesn't know anyone, introduce them around personally
- **Circulating**: don't stay in kitchen all night — make rounds, check on everyone, facilitate conversations
  - "Have you met X? You both love hiking!" — connector of people is your role
  - If conversation lags, restart it ("How do you all know the host?")
- **Refilling**: check drinks, offer more food, clear empty glasses/plates regularly
  - Don't let anyone's glass stay empty long (but also don't push alcohol on non-drinkers)
- **Pacing**: dinner served when most guests arrived + 30 min (not waiting for latecomers past 45 min)
  - Multiple courses = smaller portions, don't overwhelm guests with quantity
- **Photos**: ask permission before posting (some people don't want to be on social media)
  - Group shot at the end, not interrupting the flow
- **The host's job**: make sure everyone is having a good time, not have the best time yourself
  - You'll enjoy it more if prep is done and you're not stressed — this takes experience

## Managing Different Situations
- **Late guests**: wait 15-20 min for dinner, start without them if they're more than 30 min late (they can eat when they arrive)
- **Overstaying guests**: start cleaning up, yawn, "I have an early morning" — most people take the hint
  - Direct but kind: "It's been so lovely having you, but I need to start winding down"
- **Dietary restrictions**: ask when inviting, label dishes, at least one option for common restrictions
  - If you have a vegan: making a good vegan main > expecting them to eat side dishes
- **Sober guests**: have non-alcoholic options beyond water (soda, sparkling water with lime, mocktail, NA beer)
  - Don't pressure anyone to drink — "why aren't you drinking?" is invasive, don't ask
- **Conflict**: if guests argue, gently redirect, separate if needed — "let's change the subject" / "more wine anyone?"
- **Intoxicated guest**: cut off drinks, offer water/food, arrange safe ride home (Uber/Lyft/cab)

## Hosting Style by Type
- **Casual**: paper plates, self-serve drinks, "make yourself at home", potluck or takeout
  - Good for: new friends, low pressure, not having a perfect home
- **Semi-formal**: sit-down dinner, set table, printed menu, wine pairings, planned seating
  - Good for: celebrating, impressing, special occasions
- **Themed**: taco night, sushi making, wine tasting, Mediterranean, 90s party, costume
  - Fun because it narrows choices, gives conversation topics, creates experience
- **Outdoor**: backyard BBQ, park picnic, campfire, beach day
  - Advantage: cleanup is easier, people entertain themselves more, relaxed vibe

## Post-Party
- **Leftovers**: send guests home with food (goody bags: cookies, leftover main dish, wine)
  - Have to-go containers ready (don't make guests ask)
- **Cleanup**: enlist partner/roommate, tackle food storage first, dishes can wait until tomorrow
  - Crash clean: lights off, dishes in sink, bedding on guest bed — you'll be exhausted
- **Thank-you**: guests should thank you, but you can also text "so glad you came, it was wonderful to have you"
- **Reflect**: what worked? what would you change? guest number right? food enough? more/less structure?
