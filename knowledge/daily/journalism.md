# Journalism & Media Writing

## Types of Journalism
- **News**: factual reporting of events (who, what, when, where, why, how). Inverted pyramid: most important info first, then supporting details, least important at end. Readers may stop reading at any point — put crucial facts in lead (first paragraph). Structure: 5W1H lead → elaboration → background → less essential details. Objectivity: present facts, multiple perspectives, let readers decide
  - Hard news: breaking events (disasters, elections, crime, policy changes). Timeliness critical. Straightforward, concise
  - Soft news: feature stories, human interest, lifestyle. More narrative freedom. Can use scene-setting, literary techniques
- **Feature writing**: longer, narrative, storytelling. Ledes: anecdotal (opens with story), scene-setting, descriptive, or startling statement. Creative freedom within factual bounds. Structure: lead → nut graf (thesis: what story is about, why now) → body (backed with quotes, facts, narrative) → ending (circle back to lead, kicker — a closing image or quote that resonates)
  - Profile: in-depth piece about a person. Show DON'T tell — use scenes, quotes, observations. Hybrid: News feature = explanatory but with story-telling
- **Investigative**: deep research, documents, public records (FOIA — Freedom of Information Act), interviews. Expose wrongdoing, systemic issues. Typically weeks to months. Must be airtight fact-checked. Pulitzer prizes often go to investigative work. Watergate (Washington Post, 1972-1974): Woodward + Bernstein bring down Nixon through anonymous source "Deep Throat" → cover-up leads to resignation
  - Sources: primary (original documents, direct witnesses), secondary (books, articles analyzing events). Anonymous: last resort — require corroboration, explain in story why anonymity granted. Verify ALL sourcing
- **Opinion/editorial**: columnist's viewpoint, clearly labeled (op-ed, editorial, commentary). Editorials = newspaper's institutional view (unsigned). Op-eds = external writers. Should include: clear thesis, evidence, argument structure, counterarguments addressed. NOT news — labeled as opinion
- **Broadcast**: writing for ear, not eye. Shorter sentences, conversational tone, present tense. Sound bites (B-roll: background video, main subject: sync/interview). Write to video: "This house is now a pile of rubble" (show it, don't describe in detail if video already shows)
  - More repetition than print (listeners can't re-read). Simple words. One idea per sentence — less complex structure than print
- **Digital/online**: searchable (SEO), scannable (subheadings, bullet points, short paragraphs), shareable (social media). Hyperlinks for sources. Multimedia: integrate photos, video, graphics, embed tweets. SEO best: keyword in title, meta description, URL, first 100 words, and H2/H3 tags
  - Listicles: popular online format, but overused. Best listicles: have a strong unifying thesis (not just "10 things" but "10 surprising facts about X that change how we think about Y")
  - Mobile-first: short paragraphs (1-2 sentences max), larger font, responsive images

## Reporting Process
1. **Pitch**: angle, hook, why now, sources, what's the story? Include nut graf, outline of reporting. Editor/buyer approval needed before proceeding for freelance. Some newsrooms assign, some accept freelance pitches. Keep pitch to ~1 page, include conflict/ why readers care
2. **Research**: background, context, previous coverage. Gather documents, data (public records: FOIA requests, data.gov, census, SEC filings). Identify sources: primary (people directly involved), expert (scholars, analysts), secondary (reports, studies), archival (prior interviews, past coverage)
3. **Interviews**: prepare questions (open-ended: "Can you walk me through what happened?" vs closed: "Did you do it?" — open for storytelling + quotes, closed for yes/no + confirmation). Record + take notes (ask permission). Don't let subjects review article before publication — but can verify direct quotes. Build rapport. Cold email/message professionals for sourcing
4. **Writing**: structure (inverted pyramid or narrative arc). Lead (most important or most compelling). Nut graf (why this matters NOW). Body: evidence, quotes, scenes, data, context. End: kicker (quote, detail, or forward-looking sentence that leaves an impression). Best: often ends with signpost for future/next step
5. **Editing**: trim (remove adjectives, tighten, unnecessary words). Verify quotes (accurate: if you paraphrase, don't use quotes). Fact-check (names, dates, numbers, context, EVERYTHING). Check libel risks: truth = defense, but must be proven. Also: defamation. Opinion is protected, but misrepresenting facts is not
6. **Legal**: libel (false statement harms reputation — plaintiff must prove falsehood + harm + negligence/malice for public figures). Libel defense: truth, opinion (cannot be false), fair comment, privilege (reporting official proceedings accurately). Invasion of privacy (intrusion, public disclosure of private facts, false light, appropriation). Copyright: fair use limited. Shield laws: protect journalists from revealing confidential sources (vary by state)

## News Values (What makes a story?)
- **Timing**: now, today, breaking. If it happened yesterday: still timely? If last week: dead unless new angle
- **Proximity**: close to audience (geographic or emotional). A fire in town is more newsworthy than identical fire across continent
- **Impact**: how many people affected? Disaster, policy change, scientific breakthrough
- **Prominence**: famous people = news (celebrity, politicians, athletes). "Man bites dog" vs "dog bites man" — unusual events have higher news value
- **Conflict**: controversy, disagreement, competition. Elections, wars, court cases. Readers love conflict-driven stories
- **Novelty/oddity**: unusual, surprising, remarkable. "First," "largest," "only"
- **Human interest**: emotion, empathy, inspiration. People stories behind data/news. Feature stories: all about human interest angle

## Style & Structure
- **AP Style** (Associated Press): industry standard for US journalism. Numbers: spell out 1-9, digits 10+. Percent: %. State abbreviations (standard 2-letter: not "Calif." but "CA" using AP style specific list: "Ala.", "Ariz.", "Ark.", "Calif.", "Colo.", "Conn.", "Del.", etc. Full list for datelines; 2-letter codes for addresses). Titles: Dr., Gov., Rep., Sen. before names. Months: abbreviate if with date (Jan., Feb.; March, April, May, June, July don't abbreviate). Time: 4 p.m., 9:30 a.m., noon. Dateline: CITY (AP abbreviation), State or country — story originates
  - Punctuation: single space after period. Commas in series (serial comma optional AP doesn't use it: "red, white and blue" — no comma before and). Quotation marks: curly/smart. Attribution: "said" (not "stated," "noted," "pointed out"). Always attribute quotes + facts you didn't personally witness
- **Lead types**: summary (who, what, when, where, why, how in 1-2 sentences for hard news). Anecdotal (story to hook reader, then nut graf). Scene-setting (describe a moment for feature). Question (be careful — done poorly it's cliché). Contrast (before/after). Staccato (short, punchy sentences for drama). Direct address ("You probably don't know about X..."). Blind lead (anonymous "he" or "she" revealed later)

## Ethics
- **Accuracy**: verify facts, check sources, correct errors promptly (publish correction with explanation prominently). Never plagiarize
- **Fairness**: give all relevant parties opportunity to respond. Include context, don't cherry-pick. Represent views accurately even if you disagree
- **Independence**: avoid conflicts of interest (don't cover friends/family, don't accept gifts from sources). Don't use position for personal gain. Reveal potential conflicts to editor
- **Accountability**: correct errors prominently. Explain corrections. Transparency about methods, corrections, funding. Accept feedback from public
- **Minimize harm**: especially for vulnerable subjects (children, victims of crime/tragedy, mentally ill). Identify when to name vs withhold names (victims of sexual assault generally not named without consent). Use sensitivity with traumatic events. Balance: public's right to know vs individual privacy
- **SPJ Code of Ethics**: Seek Truth and Report It. Minimize Harm. Act Independently. Be Accountable and Transparent
- **Objectivity ≠ neutrality**: fairness + accuracy + context ≠ giving equal weight to false claims. Journalists should not be neutral on facts. "If your reporting is factually correct and both sides hate it, you're probably doing it right"
