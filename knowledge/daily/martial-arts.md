# Martial Arts Basics

Overview of major martial arts styles and self-defense principles.

## Striking Arts
- **Boxing**: Punches only (jab, cross, hook, uppercut) — footwork, head movement, combinations. Best for hand speed and distance management
- **Muay Thai**: Eight limbs (fists, elbows, knees, shins) — clinch work, teeps (push kicks), roundhouse kicks. Best for stand-up striking with clinch
- **Taekwondo**: High kicks, spinning kicks, fast footwork — Olympic sport. Best for kicks and flexibility. Less effective for self-defense (limited punches)
- **Karate**: Linear strikes, powerful stances, kata (forms) — Shotokan, Goju-Ryu, Kyokushin (full contact). Kyokushin emphasizes conditioning
- **Kickboxing**: Western boxing + kicks — no elbow strikes, no clinch. Popular for fitness and competition

## Grappling Arts
- **Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu (BJJ)**: Ground fighting — positional hierarchy (mount, side control, guard, back mount), submissions (chokes, joint locks). Size doesn't dominate — leverage-based
- **Judo**: Throws and takedowns — use opponent's momentum against them. Olympic sport. Best for getting opponent to ground without striking
- **Wrestling**: Takedowns, control, pins — folkstyle (collegiate), freestyle, Greco-Roman. Best athletic base for MMA
- **Sambo**: Russian martial art — combines Judo + wrestling + striking. Combat Sambo adds strikes

## Mixed & Hybrid Arts
- **MMA (Mixed Martial Arts)**: Combination of striking, grappling, wrestling — fight in cage/ring. Requires proficiency in at least striking + takedown defense + ground game
- **Krav Maga**: Israeli self-defense — no sport aspect, no rules. Focuses on real threats (knife defense, gun defense, multiple attackers). Target vulnerable areas (eyes, throat, groin). Military/ law enforcement origins
- **Jeet Kune Do**: Bruce Lee's philosophy — "use what works, discard what doesn't" — no fixed forms, incorporates elements from many styles

## Self-Defense Principles
- **Avoidance first**: Best fight is the one you avoid — de-escalation, situational awareness, leaving
- **Situational awareness**: Scan environment, identify exits, notice potential threats before they're close
- **De-escalation**: Calm voice, non-threatening body language (hands visible, open palms), "I don't want any trouble"
- **Distance management**: Maintain 6-8 feet from potential threat — most attacks close this distance in <1 second
- **Targets**: Eyes (gouge/throat punch), throat (strike), groin (knee/kick), knees (side kick), shins (stomp) — soft targets on larger attacker
- **Yell**: "BACK OFF!" or "FIRE!" not "HELP!" — draws attention, releases adrenaline, startles attacker
- **Run**: After creating space, sprint away — no honor in staying to fight if you can escape

## Training Considerations
- **Consistency > intensity**: 3x/week for 1 hour beats 8 hours on Saturday — skill acquisition requires repetition over time
- **Gym culture**: Look for clean mats, respectful students, no ego sparring, structured curriculum
- **Sparring safety**: Light technical sparring 80% of time — hard sparring 1-2x/month max (CTE risk)
- **Cross-training**: Striking + grappling essential for complete self-defense — boxing + BJJ is effective combination
- **Age considerations**: Teens can train any style; 30+ focus on technique over athleticism; 50+ BJJ (knee-friendly), Tai Chi, boxing (light pad work only)

## Common Techniques
- **Jab-cross-hook** (striking combo): Lead hand quick punch → rear hand straight → lead hook. Base of all boxing
- **Rear naked choke**: Back control → arm around neck → squeeze — safest submission, puts opponent to sleep in seconds, no permanent damage if released
- **Double leg takedown**: Level change → drive through opponent's hips → lift and slam. Wrestling fundamental
- **Armbar**: Isolate arm across your body → hips up → hyperextend elbow. BJJ fundamental submission
- **Guillotine choke**: Front headlock → arm wrapped around neck → squeeze — counters bad takedown attempts

## Physical Conditioning
- **Cardio**: Roadwork (3-5 mile runs), jump rope, burpees, assault bike — fighting requires high aerobic capacity
- **Neck strength**: Neck bridges, wrestler's bridge — prevents whiplash from strikes, essential for grappling
- **Grip strength**: Dead hangs, farmer's carries, gi pull-ups — controls opponents in grappling
- **Flexibility**: Dynamic stretching before training, static after — hip flexibility critical for kicks and guard retention
