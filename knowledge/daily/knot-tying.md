# Knot Tying

Essential knots for camping, boating, climbing, and everyday use.

## Terminology
- **Standing end**: The static part of rope, not used in tying
- **Working end**: The active part you're tying with
- **Bight**: Folded section of rope — U-shaped curve, not crossing itself
- **Loop**: Circle of rope that crosses itself
- **Turn**: Wrap around an object (one turn = 360°)
- **Round turn**: Two full wraps (720°)
- **Friction hitch**: Hitch that grips rope, adjustable under load (tension loosens it)
- **Tail/bitter end**: Short end after the knot

## Essential Knots
- **Bowline**: Fixed loop at end of rope — "king of knots". Used for: rescue (loop around person), mooring, lifting. Does not slip or jam. "Rabbit comes out of hole, around tree, back down hole"
- **Figure-8 follow-through**: Standard climbing knot for tying into harness. Figure-8 on bight then follow working end through. Backup knot (fisherman's) ~2" from 8
- **Clove hitch**: Quick temporary attachment to pole/railing. Used for: fender ties (boat), tarps, starting lashes. Can jam under load — backup with half hitch
- **Trucker's hitch**: Mechanical advantage for tightening cargo lines. Use loops, then get 2:1 or 3:1 purchase. Essential for securing loads on roof racks/trailers
- **Sheet bend**: Joining two ropes of different diameters. Webbing or paracord to thick rope. Double sheet bend for extra security
- **Reef knot (square knot)**: Binding — tying two ends of same rope. Used for: bandages, packages, shoelaces (first part of bow). NOT for joining two ropes (spills under load). NEVER use for climbing
- **Prusik hitch**: Friction hitch on climbing rope — slides when loose, grips when weighted. For ascending rope, self-rescue. 5-6mm cord on 9-11mm rope. Triple wrap standard
- **Taut-line hitch**: Adjustable loop that holds tension. Used for: tent guy lines, tarp tensioners. Slips up/down but holds under tension
- **Alpine butterfly**: Loop in middle of rope. Used for: isolating damaged section, creating clip-in point on rope, equalizing anchors. Strong in all directions
- **Double fisherman's knot**: Joining two ropes (especially for prusik loops). Two fisherman's knots back-to-back. For climbing rescue, cordelettes. Most reliable bend for small diameter cord
- **Stopper knot**: Backs up other knots, prevents rope end from slipping through. Double overhand stopper or figure-8 stopper. Essential on all climbing ropes
- **Timber hitch**: For dragging logs or cylindrical objects — simple, tightens under load. Wrap end around object, twist around own standing part 2-3 times

## Rope Types
- **Static rope**: Low stretch (<2%) — rescue, ascending, hauling. Not for lead climbing (too rigid, no shock absorption)
- **Dynamic rope**: Stretches 6-10% — absorbs fall energy. For lead climbing. Single (9-10.5mm), half (8-9mm — used in pairs), twin (7-8mm — clipped together)
- **Kernmantle**: Kern (core load-bearing fibers) + Mantle (outer sheath protects core). Most common climbing rope construction
- **Accessory cord**: 3-8mm, for prusik loops, anchor building, not for life support alone. Lower strength than proper climbing rope
- **Paracord**: 550 (550 lb breaking strength). General purpose: lashing, gear repair, survival bracelets. NOT for climbing or load-bearing life support
- **Natural fiber**: Manila (stiff, strong, UV resistant — historic climbing), hemp, cotton (weak, stretchy — decoration/projects)
- **Marine rope**: Polyester (best for marine — low stretch, UV resistant), Polypropylene (floats — ski ropes, tow lines), Nylon (shock absorbent — dock lines, anchor rodes)

## Rope Care
- **Inspect regularly**: Abrasion, cuts, discoloration, stiffness, soft spots, lumpiness, chemical damage. Climbing rope: retire if you take a hard fall (factor 1.5+) or every 5 years (even unused)
- **Cleaning**: Cold water, mild soap (no fabric softener, bleach, detergent with brighteners). Hand wash or machine gentle cycle in rope bag. Air dry away from direct sun (UV degrades fibers)
- **Storage**: Coil loosely (not tight bends), hang or store in rope bag. Cool, dry, dark place. Keep away from chemicals, gas, oil, battery acid
- **Coiling**: Mountaineer's coil (over shoulder), butterfly coil (over hand — best for storage, tangle-free deployment)
- **Cutting / fusing**: Melt synthetic ends (cut with hot knife or sharp blade + carefully melt with flame). Natural fiber: whip ends (small cord tightly wound around end)

## Common Mistakes
- **Wrong knot for job**: Figure-8 for climbing (not bowline — bowline can shake loose). Clove hitch not for heavy end-load (shakes loose)
- **No tail length**: Leave 6+ inches of tail for all critical knots. Too short = shakes out
- **Dress the knot**: All strands should lie parallel, not cross — smooth and tight before loading. Crossed strands reduce strength 20-40%
- **Overcomplicating**: A few well-learned knots > many half-remembered ones. Master 6-8 knots for most scenarios
- **Not testing**: After tying and before loading, pull on knot to check. Trust but verify
