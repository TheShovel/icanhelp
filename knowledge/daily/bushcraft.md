# Survival Skills & Bushcraft

## Fire by Friction
- **Bow drill**: spindle (straight, dry, dead wood — softwoods: cedar, yucca, willow, cottonwood, aspen, balsam fir, basswood, poplar). Hearth board (same type wood as spindle — soft enough to generate dust, hard enough to resist burning through). Bow (curved branch, string: shoelace, paracord inner strand, natural fiber cordage). Bearing block (hard wood/ stone/shell/bone, lubricate with green leaf/grease). Tinder nest (dry grass, shredded bark, cattail fluff, thistle down, birch bark — roll into bird nest shape). Technique: start notch (V-cut in hearth board), place ember tray (leaf) under notch. Bow perpendicular to spindle. Long smooth strokes, increasing speed. Smoke → dark brown dust → coal (glowing ember in dust pile). Transfer to tinder nest, cup gently, blow into coal (gentle sustained breath until flame). Practice before you NEED it. Expect multiple failures — learn what wood species work in your environment (cedar/juniper excellent)
  - Hand drill: faster, no bow needed (directly roll spindle between palms, apply downward pressure). Requires very dry, soft wood (yucca, mullien, thistle, cottonwood). More difficult than bow drill for most. Takes calluses
- **Fire piston**: bamboo/antler tube with tight-fitting piston → compresses air → adiabatic heating creates hot enough tinder (char cloth). Works in wet conditions but relies on precise seal. Not primitive — requires manufacturing skill but can be improvised (sections of bamboo)
- **Ferro rod**: synthetic rod of iron + mischmetal (cerium, lanthanum). Striking with steel/ceramic → 3,000°C+ sparks. Works wet (rod + striker both wet, still works — scrape/scrape pad in sharp 90-degree angle to produce showers). Practice: one-handed draw, collect sparks into tinder nest, blow into flame. Back up skill only if primitive fails

## Water Sourcing
- Find water: morning dew (tie bandana around ankles, walk through tall grass → wring into container). Clean: tie shirt/bandana to tree or soak overnight — wring water out (cotton or linen hold 10+ times their dry weight). Solar still: dig hole, place container in center, cover with plastic, weigh down center, weight under plastic over cup → condensation collects. Can produce 500ml/day in dry conditions. Not enough for survival
  - Transpiration bag: tie clear plastic bag over leafy tree branch (not conifer — pine sap toxic). Sun causes transpiration → water condenses in bag. Gets 1-2 cups per bag per day. Use broadleaf species only (avoid walnut, oleander, yew, poison ivy/oak/sumac). Drinkable but taste like leaves
- Ground still: same as solar but line hole with wet vegetation + plastic. Less productive. Prefer finding stream/seep

## Shelters
- **Debris hut**: ridge pole (main beam) between crotch of two trees or branch supported on forked uprights. Ribs (branches angled from ridge to ground). Cover: debris (leaves, grass, ferns, moss, pine needles — small debris for insulation gets picked up to 1-2 ft thick maximum). Enter (crawl into small opening). Close entrance with debris behind you. Body heat: trapped in leaf litter — minimal air flow. Without good debris cover and insulating layer between you and ground (pile of debris 18 inches thick): hypothermia risk. Inside temp only ~10-20°F above outside, but little wind + body heat can keep you alive. With -10°F outside and good debris: 30°F inside
- **Wickiup / Teepee frame**: tripod of long poles, lean more poles around. Cover with bark, sod, grass, branches with leaves, tarp, poncho. Larger = longer to build but more room. Central fire: smoke rises through hole in top. Good rain protection with overlapping bark/grasses: layers. Better than debris hut for wet climates: fire inside possible if roof tall enough
- **Lean-to**: ridge pole between trees/forked sticks. Poles leaned from ridge to ground. Cover with debris. Open side faces away from wind + toward fire (if building one). Not as warm as debris hut but easier to enter + exit

## Trapping & Snaring
- **Simple snare**: wire/cord loop at correct height (~hand's width from ground for rabbit). Use locking mechanism (loop through slip knot). Place along animal trail (natural narrow passage or funnel with brush). Trigger: bent sapling keeps tension (need spring pole to snap prey up — otherwise they may chew through/escape). Practice setting triggers (figure-4 deadfall + snare + trigger notch + bait stick)
  - Wire: brass picture hanging wire (soft, holds loop shape, reusable). 22-24 gauge. Paracord inner strands (weak but quick). Snare loop: rabbit ~2.5-3" diameter, at head height off ground (~3-4"). Place along trail: under branches, between rocks, in runways
- **Deadfall trap**: Paiute deadfall (weight supported by trigger stick + notched vertical + bait stick). Signal: stick + bait triggers mechanism to drop large rock/log on animal. Practice: set with small stick that falls easily. Not efficient for survival (time/effort vs calories). Better for mental engagement (and potential large payoff)

## Plant Identification
- **Universal Edibility Test**: separate plant into parts (leaves, stem, root, flower, fruit). Test each part for 3+ hours total (skin contact → lip → chew → swallow small amount → wait 8 hours). If any adverse reaction at any step: discard. DON'T rely on this in emergency without good plant guide — some deadly plants (water hemlock = one bite kills)
  - Better: learn a few reliable edible plants in your region (dandelion, plantain, chickweed, clover, cattail, acorns [leach tannins first for edibility], burdock, purslane, lamb's quarters, wood sorrel, stinging nettle [cook first — destroys sting]. Pinon pine nuts)
- **Poisonous look-alikes**: water hemlock (most toxic plant in N. America) vs queen Anne's lace (carrot family). Water hemlock: purple spots on stem, stalks in clusters, single stem. Queen Anne's lace: hairy stem, smells like carrot, one umbrella. Never eat unknown carrot-family plants unless 100% sure
- **Pine**: edible inner bark (cambium layer — scrape off outer bark, eat inner white layer raw or roasted, high vitamin C. Not very calorie dense but life-saving vitamin source). Needles for tea (high vitamin C, good for colds). Seeds (pine nuts — high fat, high calorie). Resin: waterproofing, glue (mixed with charcoal), fire starter

## Useful Knots for Survival
- **Bowline**: non-slip loop (rescue, harness, throwing line). "Rabbit comes out of hole, around tree, back down hole"
- **Taut-line hitch**: adjustable loop on tent line (slide to tighten, holds under tension). Works as hitch but not for heavy loads (pulling rope)
- **Prusik**: friction hitch for climbing rope (ascending, self-belay). Slides when no load, grips under tension. Needs smaller diameter cord around larger one
- **Sheet bend**: joining two ropes (good for different thicknesses). Double sheet bend for slippery ropes (paracord/nylon)
- **Clove hitch**: quick tie to post/tree (start of lashing). Can slip without tension both sides — secure with half hitch
- **Trucker's hitch**: tighten rope as much as possible (camping, tarp, load). Creates mechanical advantage (2:1). Release by pulling at loop

## Edible Insects
- Grasshoppers/crickets: in grass, under logs, at base of plants. Remove head + wings + legs, roast on hot rock. Taste like nuts/shrimp. Nutrition: ~20g protein/100g, high fat
- Ants: edible raw (sour from formic acid). Dip into water before eating to wash. Roasted: like popcorn. Avoid red ants (more formic acid — sour, not dangerous). Avoid fire ants (leave bite). Find nest under log or rock
- Mealworms/beetle larvae: in rotten wood, leaf litter. Roast. High fat, good survival food. Not safe raw (can carry parasites)
- Termites: in dead wood, mounds. Nutty flavor. Pick them from under bark or top of termite mound. Roast

## Navigation Without GPS
- **Sun**: rises east, sets west. North: at noon, sun is south (northern hemisphere). Stick shadow method: push stick into ground, mark tip of shadow, wait 15 min, mark again — line between marks roughly east-west direction (first = west, second = east). More accurate: combine over 1-2 hours
- **Stars** (northern hemisphere): Polaris (North Star) — find Big Dipper, last two stars of cup point to Polaris (about 5x distance between them). If Big Dipper low, use Cassiopeia (W shape) — Polaris is between them. Southern Cross (bottom+top) line points south in Southern Hemisphere
- **Moon**: crescent moon shaped like a bow? First quarter moon sets west around midnight, waxing crescent in west after sunset points to sun position. Crescent tips point roughly north/south. At new moon: moon is on same side as sun (day sky)
- **Moss**: grows more on north side of trees in northern hemisphere (shade). Unreliable (shade can be any side). Better: more reliable on tree trunks — look for dense side. Not a quick trick, requires looking at multiple trees

## Signaling
- **Whistle**: 3 short blasts (universal distress). Space: 1-2 seconds between bursts. Respond with 2 short blasts (acknowledged). Keep whistle accessible from pocket
- **Signal mirror**: flash sunlight toward plane/helicopter. Hold mirror near eye, reflect spot onto hand, then adjust until spot is on target. Visible for miles (up to 10+ miles in clear conditions, 50+ miles if aimed properly at search aircraft)
- **Ground signals**: SOS (3 short, 3 long, 3 short) — can be made with logs, rocks, trampled snow, digging trench. International distress signal: V (vs. N/N = "need assistance") — "X" = need medical. But "SOS" wider known and recognized worldwide. Use high contrast (dark rocks on pale soil, cut green branches on snow, contrast with shade, trample dark soil). Size: 10 ft+ per symbol or letter (for aerial visibility). Best: 6 ft height for altitude
- **Flares**: daytime hand flare (red smoke) vs nighttime (red flare). Aerial flare (short-range pistol, star parachute). Beware fire risk in dry brush. Age: flares degrade, replace every 4 years
