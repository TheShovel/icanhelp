# Leadership & Management

## Leadership vs Management
- **Manager**: plans, organizes, controls, focuses on systems/processes, authority from position — "doing things right"
- **Leader**: inspires, influences, sets vision, focuses on people/purpose, authority from trust — "doing the right things"
- You can be a manager without being a leader, and a leader without being a manager. Best = both
  - People join companies and leave managers — good management is HARD. Most people who quit jobs quit because of their direct supervisor, not the company or role

## Leadership Styles
- **Autocratic**: leader decides alone. Fast, clear, good for crises/emergencies — but demotivating, kills creativity
- **Democratic**: team input → leader decides. Slower but better buy-in, good for complex problems, builds engagement
  - Consensus: everyone agrees (slow, can't always reach/achieve in large groups). Consent: everyone can live with it (faster, practical)
- **Transformational**: inspire through vision, motivate through purpose, develop people. High engagement, innovation — but requires strong vision, charisma, constant energy
- **Servant leadership**: leader serves team first — remove obstacles, provide resources, support growth. Builds trust, strong teams — but can be slow, indecisive, sacrificed for urgent tasks
  - "The goal: make everyone around you better." — focuses on employee growth and well-being. Challenge: can lead to leader burnout (always giving)
- **Laissez-faire**: hands-off, let team self-manage. Works with highly skilled, motivated teams — fails without clear direction or accountability. Dangerous if team needs guidance
- **Situational**: adapt style to situation + team maturity. Novice team: more directive (telling). Expert team: more delegative (trusting)
  - Hersey-Blanchard model: telling, selling, participating, delegating based on follower readiness and task complexity

## Building & Managing Teams
- **Hiring**: hire for attitude, train for skill (skills can be taught, attitude hard to change)
  - Look for: curiosity, ownership, communication, problem-solving. Ask behavioral questions ("Tell me about a time you...")
  - Structured interviews: same questions for ALL candidates → fairer, more predictive of success (unstructured "chat" interviews have near-zero predictive validity for job performance)
  - Involve team members in hiring — they'll work with them, they should have say
- **Onboarding**: first 90 days critical — clear expectations, assign buddy/mentor, regular check-ins, define success criteria. Set clear 30-60-90 day plan
  - New hires decide if they'll stay in first 3 months — make them feel welcome + productive fast
- **1:1s**: weekly or biweekly, 30 min, no agenda (their time) — NOT status updates (those are for standups)
  - Ask: "What's on your mind?", "What do you need from me?", "What should we start/stop/continue?"
  - Topics: career growth, blockers, feedback, well-being. Listen > talk. Take notes, follow up
- **Feedback**: specific, actionable, timely, kind. "I noticed X, which had Y impact. In future, could you try Z?"
  - SBI model: Situation, Behavior, Impact. "In yesterday's meeting (situation), when you interrupted me (behavior), I felt unheard (impact)."
  - Praise in public, correct in private. Regular feedback > annual review. Continuous, low-stakes, immediate > batched/delayed

## Delegation
- **What to delegate**: tasks that develop others (stretch assignments!), tasks you don't need to do, routine/recurring work
  - If someone can do 80% as well as you: DELEGATE (they improve with practice)
- **What NOT to delegate**: vision/strategy, confidential (compensation, personnel), team culture/values, crisis, performance feedback
- **How**: describe the WHAT (outcome) not HOW (process) — give autonomy. Provide resources + authority. Set clear expectations (quality, timeline, decision rights). Check in at milestones (not micromanage)
  - The "hand-off conundrum": telling someone HOW step-by-step = you're not delegating, you're assigning yourself the oversight
- **Trust**: give people room to fail small. "If you never let them make decisions, they'll never learn to make good ones" — builds competence and confidence
  - "Mistakes are tuition paid for learning." Create safe space for calculated risks

## Communication
- **Clear > clever**: simple language, short sentences, say what you mean. "I need this by Friday" not "Whenever you get a chance" (if you need it by Friday)
- **Overcommunicate**: repeat key messages 3-7 times before they stick. Different channels (meeting, email, Slack, 1:1, all-hands)
  - Especially true for vision/strategy changes — you'll be tired of saying it before it's heard
- **Transparency**: share context (WHY behind decisions), admit mistakes, share uncertainty. Builds trust
  - Openly sharing decision-making process helps people accept outcomes they don't personally prefer
- **Difficult conversations**: prepare (know what you want to say, anticipate response), direct + compassionate, don't delay (avoidance makes it worse), focus on behavior not person
  - Start with intention: "I want to talk about something that will help us work better together"
- **Active listening**: listen to understand, not to respond — paraphrase, ask follow-up questions, don't interrupt

## Conflict Resolution
- **Address early** — small disagreements fester into big problems. "The longer you wait, the harder it gets" — true for conflict
- **Focus on interests, not positions**: "You want the corner office (position) because you need quiet for calls (interest). Maybe we can solve the need differently?"
- **Separate people from problem**: attack the issue, not each other. Describe impact without blame
- **Mediation**: if two team members have conflict, hear each separately, then facilitate conversation together
  - Set ground rules: no interrupting, no personal attacks, one person speaks at a time
- **When to escalate**: legal issues, harassment, policy violations, when team member can't/won't change

## Performance Management
- **Set clear goals**: OKRs (Objectives + Key Results) or SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound)
  - OKRs: ambitious, measurable, transparent. Objectives = what you want to achieve. Key Results = how you measure progress. 3-5 per quarter. Google, Intel, many tech companies use
- **Regular feedback** (not just annual reviews): continuous, real-time, forward-looking. 360-degree feedback helps
  - "Annual performance reviews" increasingly being replaced by ongoing feedback loops (Adobe, Microsoft, many others eliminated them for more frequent check-ins)
- **Addressing poor performance**: 1. Name the gap (specific behavior/outcome vs expectation). 2. Understand root cause (skill? motivation? resources? personal issue?). 3. Create plan together. 4. Follow up with clear timeline. 5. Escalate if no improvement
  - PIP (Performance Improvement Plan): formal process, documented, specific goals + timeline. Should be genuine opportunity to improve (not just paper trail for firing)
- **Promotions**: based on demonstrated impact at CURRENT level + readiness for NEXT level, not tenure
  - Peter Principle: people promoted until they reach level of incompetence. Promotion to management should not be the only career path (individual contributor track equally valid!)

## Meeting Culture
- **Only meet if necessary**: agenda distributed before, clear outcome, invite only necessary people, start/end on time
  - Status update meetings: replace with async (Slack, project management tool, shared doc)
  - Decision meetings: bring recommendation, discuss alternatives, decide
- **Meeting types**: stand-up (daily, 15 min, what did I do / what will I do / blockers). Planning (weekly/biweekly, prioritize, assign). Retro (after project/milestone — what went well, what to improve, what to try). All-hands (monthly, company-wide updates, Q&A)
  - Decision: 1-2 people present options, 1 decision-maker, rest are optional
  - Brainstorm: separate idea generation (divergent) from evaluation (convergent) — no criticism in generative phase
- **Async-first**: document decisions, share written updates, use recorded video for broadcasts — respect deep work time
  - Write things down: making a decision doc forces structured thinking, and it's a record others can reference later
  - Over-communicate: for async, more context is better than less — share why, not just what

## Leading Through Change
- **Why change fails**: lack of urgency, no clear vision, poor communication, not addressing resistance, declaring victory too soon (Kotter 8 steps)
  - Kotter 8-Step Change Model: 1. Create urgency. 2. Build guiding coalition. 3. Form strategic vision. 4. Enlist volunteers. 5. Enable action by removing barriers. 6. Generate short-term wins. 7. Sustain acceleration. 8. Institute change
- **People go through**: denial → anger → bargaining → depression → acceptance (Kubler-Ross change curve)
  - NOT just for grief — it's the normal human response to major change. Leaders need to meet people where they are in this curve
- **Communicate**: WHY (the burning platform / opportunity), WHAT (the change), HOW (the plan), repeatedly
  - "People don't resist change. They resist being changed." — involve them in the process
- **Listen to resistance**: often signals real problems or good ideas — address concerns, don't dismiss
  - Asking "What are you worried about?" or "What could go wrong?" surfaces blockers early
