# Rock Climbing Basics

Fundamentals of rock climbing — types, gear, safety, and technique.

## Climbing Types
- **Bouldering**: Short climbs (10-20ft) without ropes — crash pads for falls. Grades V0-V17 (V-scale) or Font 3-9A. Most accessible, great for technique
- **Sport climbing**: Lead climbing on bolted routes — clip quickdraws into pre-placed bolts. Grades 5.0-5.15d (YDS). Requires rope, draws, belay device
- **Trad climbing**: Place own protection (cams, nuts, hexes) into cracks — no pre-placed bolts. Grades same YDS + R/X for danger. More gear, higher commitment
- **Top-roping**: Rope anchored at top of climb — belayer takes slack. Safest, best for beginners. Grades 5.0-5.15d
- **Free soloing**: Climbing without any protection — single fall = death. Expert-level, high consequence
- **Aid climbing**: Using gear to pull up on — for routes too hard/blank for free climbing. Grades A0-A5

## Essential Gear
- **Climbing shoes**: Tight (snug, no dead space in toebox) — leather stretches 0.5-1 size. Beginners: moderate arch, flat last. Advanced: aggressive downturn for steep
- **Harness**: Leg loops + waist belt — adjustable for layers. Important: gear loops for carrying draws, belay loop for belay device
- **Belay device**: ATC (tube-style) or Grigri (auto-locking cam). ATC simpler, Grigri safer for catching falls
- **Quickdraws**: Two carabiners (straight gate + bent gate) connected by dogbone. Sport climbing: 12-18 draws
- **Rope**: Dynamic rope — 9.4-10.2mm diameter, 60-70m length for single-pitch sport. Dry-treated for multipitch/ice
- **Chalk + bag**: Magnesium carbonate — absorbs sweat, improves grip. Liquid chalk for first application
- **Crash pad** (bouldering): Foam pad, 3-4" thick — land flat on back, spotter redirects to pad

## Safety Fundamentals
- **Partner check**: Before climbing, check — harness threaded correctly, double-back buckle, rope through tie-in points, knot tied properly (figure-8 follow-through), belay device loaded correctly, carabiner locked
- **Commands**: "On belay?" → "Belay on" → "Climbing" → "Climb on" → "Take" (take slack) → "Slack" (give slack) → "Falling!" (warning) → "Lower" → "Lowering"
- **Catching falls**: Watch climber, not rope — give soft catch (slight jump as rope catches, reducing jerk) — don't lock off too early
- **Holding the brake**: Always hold brake strand (the rope tail) — never let go of brake hand, even for a second
- **Lowering**: Slow, controlled — lower with both hands on brake strand, one controlling speed

## Technique
- **Feet first**: Most beginners pull too much with arms — push with legs, use big leg muscles
- **Three points of contact**: Always move one limb at a time — keep two hands + one foot or two feet + one hand stable
- **Straight arms**: Keep arms straight — let skeleton support weight, not muscles. Hang from bones, rest on straight arm
- **Hips close to wall**: Hips in = better balance, less energy. Drop knee to turn hip into wall
- **Flagging**: Extend free leg to balance body — counterbalance to keep center of gravity over feet
- **Smearing**: Press shoe rubber against flat rock — friction holds you on when no foothold exists
- **Heel hook**: Heel on hold to pull body in — for overhangs, keeps hips close to wall
- **Drop knee**: Rotate one knee down, turning hip into wall — reaches distant holds, works on steep terrain

## Grading Systems
- **Yosemite Decimal (YDS)**: 5.0-5.7 = easy, 5.8-5.10 = intermediate, 5.11-5.12 = advanced, 5.13-5.15 = elite. Subgrades a,b,c,d (5.10a → 5.10d)
- **V-scale (bouldering)**: VB = beginner, V0-V2 = entry, V3-V5 = intermediate, V6-V8 = advanced, V9+ = expert, V17 = world's hardest
- **French (sport)**: 3 (easy) to 9c (elite). 5a ≈ 5.9, 6a ≈ 5.10b, 7a ≈ 5.11d, 8a ≈ 5.13b

## Common Injuries
- **Pulley strains**: A2 pulley (base of ring finger) most common — from crimping. Rest 4-6 weeks, tape for support. Prevent: open-hand grip > crimp
- **Tendonitis**: Elbow (golfer's/climber's elbow) — from overuse, poor technique (too much pulling, not enough pushing). Stretch, antagonist training
- **Shoulder**: Labrum tears, dislocations — from dynamic moves, poor shoulder engagement. Strengthen rotator cuff
- **Skin**: Flappers (skin tears) — file calluses, moisturize, don't climb with open wounds

## Training for Climbing
- **Antagonist training**: Push-ups, dips, overhead press — balance pulling muscles, prevent muscle imbalances
- **Hangboarding**: Dead hangs on finger edges — for finger strength. Beginners: wait 1 year before hangboarding
- **Core**: Leg raises, planks, hollow body holds — keeps feet on wall in steep terrain
- **ARC training**: Aerobic Rest + Capillarity — continuous easy climbing 20-30 min — builds endurance, capillary density
