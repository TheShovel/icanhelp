# Boating & Water Sports

## Types of Boats
- **Powerboats**: fishing boats (center console, bay boat, bass boat), runabouts (family skiing/wakeboarding), cabin cruisers (overnight), deck boats (entertaining, shallow draft), pontoon boats (stable, party/relaxation, great for lakes), center console (fishing, open deck, walk-around), bowrider (family, seating front+back), cuddy cabin (small cabin, overnights), speed boats/cigarette boats (speed, offshore racing), personal watercraft (Jet Ski, Sea-Doo, WaveRunner)
- **Sailboats**: daysailer (under 20 ft, simple), cruising sailboat (20-50 ft, liveaboard, long passages), catamaran (two hulls, stable, wide, great for shallow/anchoring), trimaran (three hulls, very fast), racing sailboats (spartan, fast)
- **Kayaks/Canoes**: recreational (stable, short trips, calm water), touring (longer, faster, ocean bays), whitewater (shorter, maneuverable, rockered), fishing (wide, stable, rod holders), inflatable (portable, storage, less durable)
- **Stand-up paddleboard (SUP)**: all-around (wide, stable, good for flatwater), touring (longer, faster, tracking), surfing (shorter, maneuverable in waves), yoga (very wide, stable), inflatable (iSUP = portable, durable, most popular)

## Boat Safety
- **Life jackets**: legally required (1 per person, readily accessible, proper size/type for activity). Must be USCG-approved
  - Type I: offshore life jacket, turns unconscious face-up (bulky, best for open/ocean water where rescue may be delayed. Required for offshore)
  - Type II: near-shore buoyant vest, turns some people face-up (less bulky, good for calm inland waters)
  - Type III: flotation aid (for conscious users, good for watersports — most comfortable, must be USCG Type III)
  - Type IV: throwable (ring buoy, cushion — must be on board for boats 16ft+). NOT wearable, for secondary/rescue
  - Children under 13: MUST wear life jacket while underway (most states), law varies by state. Children: Type II or V (rated for child weight), proper fit (tug shoulders up — shouldn't ride above chin. Crotch strap important!)
  - Wear it, don't stow it (most drownings happen on boats — people didn't put it on fast enough. Capsizing = you don't have time to find and put on jacket)
- **Kill switch / engine cut-off**: lanyard attached to operator (if operator thrown from helm, engine stops — prevents boat running over them). Required on most boats (federal law for smaller powerboats)
- **Navigation lights**: red (port/left), green (starboard/right), white (stern/masthead). Required sunset to sunrise and in restricted visibility. Check before leaving dock
- **Fire extinguisher**: required for boats with engine (inboard/outboard). Type B-I (smaller boats <26ft), Type B-II (larger). Inspect annually, check pressure gauge, replace if expired
- **Flares / visual distress signals**: required for coastal waters/Great Lakes (or any waters >2 miles offshore). Type: hand-held red flare (day+night), orange smoke (day), parachute flare (high visibility, stored in dry box). Check expiration dates. Daytime only: orange signal flag
- **Sound signals**: whistle/air horn (required for smaller boats, 3-4 sec duration, audible 1/2 mile). Bell for larger boats (>12m). Signals: 1 short = pass starboard, 2 short = pass port, 5+ rapid = danger/uncertain

## Operation Basics
- **Pre-departure checklist**: weather (NOAA marine forecast, wind/wave planning), fuel level (1/3 out, 1/3 back, 1/3 reserve), bilge (dry + pump working), drain plug (IN! — most common sinking cause is forgot drain plug), battery (fully charged, terminals clean), kill switch + lanyard attached, PFDs accessible, flares/fire extinguisher onboard, VHF radio/charged phone (waterproof bag), tool kit + spare parts (spark plugs, basic tools, duct tape, zip ties, hose clamps)
- **Docking**: approach SLOWLY, wind/current determine approach, bump boat in neutral, brief use of throttle in gear, have fenders out, lines ready. Practice in calm conditions
  - Docking tips: NEVER put hand/fingers between boat and dock (crush hazard). Use boat hook. Come in at shallow angle, shift into neutral, coast in
  - Spring line: essential for docking — cleat on boat, wraps around dock cleat/piling, back to boat cleat. Helps control boat in current/wind
- **Anchoring**: scope (anchor line length) = 5-7:1 (depth + freeboard) minimum (7:1 for windy, 10:1 for storm). Set anchor by backing down (reverse gently until anchor digs in). Check set with landmarks (transit bearings). Use anchor ball (day shape = anchored)
  - Rode: chain (heavy, protects against abrasion, holds lower in water) + line (nylon, stretches/shocks). Chain length: minimum length of boat
  - Types: Danforth/fluke (sand, mud, popular), plow/CQR (mixed/sand/shell, sets well, popular for cruising), Bruce/claw (rocky, muddy, sets fast), mushroom (permanent mooring), grapnel (rocky, reef, small boats)
- **Man overboard (MOB)**: yell "MAN OVERBOARD!", point at person (never lose sight), hit MOB button on GPS, throw cushion/ring, turn boat (recommended: figure-8 or crash stop), approach from downwind (let wind blow boat to them), shut off engine before reaching them, get them aboard (boarding ladder has webbing step, horseshoe life ring)
  - Most important: never stop pointing at the person in water. Practice MOB drill before needed

## Navigation
- **Charts**: NOAA nautical charts (digital + paper). Depth, hazards, buoys, beacons, lights. Know how to read: depth in fathoms/feet, nav aids (lateral system: red-right-returning), buoys/beacons meanings and colors
  - Navigational marks: red (even numbers) = starboard side returning from sea, green (odd) = port side. "Red right return" — red buoys stay on your right (starboard) when ENTERING from open sea
- **GPS/chart plotter**: essential (phone with Navionics/ iSailor works, but have backup — phone battery/water/exposure). Mark waypoints, set route, track
- **VHF radio**: channel 16 (emergency + hailing). Channel 13 (bridge-to-bridge, ship movements). Channel 09 (recreation/hailing). Channel 22A (Coast Guard broadcast). Weather channels (WX 1-7)
  - Emergency: Mayday (life-threatening), Pan-Pan (urgent, not life-threatening), Securite (navigational safety message). Protocol: channel 16, give Mayday x3, boat name, position, nature of emergency, number on board, assistance needed
- **AIS** (Automatic Identification System): tracks other vessels. Class A (required for larger ships), Class B (smaller vessels). Valuable for collision avoidance in shipping channels
- **Rules of the road**: power gives way to sail, overtaking gives way, port gives way to starboard (if same tack approaching each other, boat on PORT side gives way), windward gives way to leeward (sailboats same tack)
  - Stand-on vessel: maintain course and speed. Give-way vessel: take early and substantial action to avoid (change course, slow, stop)

## Weather on the Water
- **Check before going**: marine forecast (wind speed + direction, wave height, chance of storms, visibility)
  - Thunderstorms: develop quickly over water — if dark sky/thunder/lightning. Head in early! Lightning = #1 weather danger on water (boat is tallest object). Disconnect electronics, drop anchor if stuck
- **Wind + waves**: small craft advisory (winds 25-33 knots, waves 4-7 ft) — experienced boaters only, small boats stay in
  - Gale warning (34-47 knots). Storm warning (48-63 knots). Hurricane force (64+ knots). Know forecast before departure. Inland lakes: thunderstorm wind gusts more dangerous than forecast sustained winds
- **Lightning on water**: squat low in center of boat, stay away from metal (mast, rigging, steering wheel electronics), drop fishing rods, outboard engine may provide some grounding

## Watersports
- **Skiing/wakeboarding**: observer required (by law in many states), flag (skier down flag — orange/red, displayed when skier in water), proper rope length (75 ft beginner slalom, 60-65 ft wakeboard), signal to driver (speed up = thumb up, slow down = thumb down, stop = cut throat, OK = circle with hand, go back to dock = pat head, turn = point direction)
  - Slalom (two skis → one ski/slalom). Combo skis: beginner. Wakeboard: both feet on board, started from sitting position. Kneeboard: lower impact, easier for kids
- **Tube**: separate tow rope (shorter, dedicated for tubes), low speed (15-20 mph), calm water, watch direction, release handle if falling
- **Kayaking/SUP**: wear life jacket, know wind forecast (wind against tide = rough), leash (SUP: ankle leash, kayak: leash for paddle, boat connection), navigation lights at night, dress for water temperature (not air temp — cold water = incapacitation in minutes)
  - Self-rescue: practice wet exit + reentry (kayak: paddle float self-rescue), SUP: jump back on without letting board flip, swim beside to end and climb on

## Boat Maintenance
- **Engine**: fresh water flush after saltwater use (ear muffs on outboard or bucket, flush with fresh water). Oil change (4-stroke: every 50-100 hours/annually. 2-stroke: oil injection system check). Fuel stabilizer (if stored more than 30 days, add stabilizer to tank, run engine to circulate). Change fuel/water separator filter (annual). Zinc anodes (check/replace annually - prevent galvanic corrosion in salt water)
- **Winterization**: for cold climates — drain engine block (hoses/manifolds), fog oil (protect cylinders), antifreeze (pink RV antifreeze in fresh water system), remove batteries (store indoors on maintainer), shrinkwrap/safeguard cover, leave drain plug OUT (collect rainwater drains out). Winterize BEFORE first freeze
- **Battery**: marine deep-cycle (starting + trolling). Check water level (distilled water), keep terminals clean (dielectric grease), fully charged when stored, maintain monthly (battery tender/maintainer). Group size and CCA must match engine spec
- **Hull**: clean bottom (fouling: barnacles/algae = drag = 10-20% worse fuel economy). Bottom paint (antifouling paint for salt water). Check sacrificial anodes (zincs). Wax topsides (gelcoat protection). UV damage = oxidation = chalky surface. Use marine wax 2x/year

## Legal
- **Boat registration**: required (state-specific, numbered). Display registration numbers (3" block letters, separated by hyphen). Registration decals current. Documentation (federal documentation for larger vessels for interstate/international, financing, tonnage-based)
- **Boating license/education**: many states require boating safety course + certificate (especially for operators under certain age). Online course ($30-50, NASBLA-approved)
- **BUI (Boating Under Influence)**: same BAC limits as driving (0.08% — .08 -.10% varies slightly by state). Penalties similar to DUI (fines, jail, license suspension). Federal law for all waterways. MORE enforcement on holidays (July 4th, Memorial Day, Labor Day)
