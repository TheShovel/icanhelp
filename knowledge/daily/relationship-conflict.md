# Relationship Conflict & Communication

## Conflict Dynamics
- **Four Horsemen** (John Gottman): criticism (attacking character — "you're so lazy" vs "I feel frustrated when you don't do dishes"). Contempt (sarcasm, eye-rolling, mockery, name-calling — most destructive, predicts divorce with 93% accuracy). Defensiveness (making excuses, counter-complaining — "you always do that too!"). Stonewalling (shutting down, withdrawing, walking away — usually after emotional flooding)
  - Antidote: criticism → gentle startup (I feel... about... I need...). Contempt → build appreciation culture. Defensiveness → take responsibility. Stonewalling → self-soothe (take a break, 20+ min to let physiological arousal decrease)
- **Flooding**: physiological overwhelm (heart rate >100 bpm, adrenaline, loss of rational thought). When flooded: can't listen, problem-solve, or empathize. Need a 20-minute break to let body calm down. NOT "I'm leaving" — "I'm flooded and need a break. I love you. I'll be back in 30 minutes." During break: DON'T ruminate about the fight (read, walk, play music). Goal: distract, not rehash. After: return + reconnect
- **Soft startup**: how you start a conversation determines how it ends. "Honey, I need to talk about something" vs "You never help with the kids!" Gentle, without criticism or accusation = better outcome 96% of the time (Gottman). No blame: "I" statements, talk about YOUR feelings, frame as a problem you both need to solve

## Repair Attempts
- Any statement/action that prevents conflict from escalating. "I'm sorry," "Can we take a break?", "Tell me more," "I hear you," "I see your point," "I appreciate you saying that," "We're OK," — these create connection and de-escalation. Successful if partner responds (they can choose to accept). Unsuccessful if partner continues attacking
  - Gottman: couples who stay happy use repair attempts effectively. Unhappy couples: repair attempts ignored or rejected. You can learn to make repairs + partner can learn to recognize them. Signs repair is needed: voice raised, criticism, stonewalling — initiate repair early

## Problem-Solving Structure
1. **Soft startup**: "I need to talk about schedules. Can we find a time?" (Gentle opening)
2. **Describe feelings + needs**: "I feel overwhelmed when I'm the only one tracking appointments" (No blame, share your experience. Use "I" statements about YOUR lived experience)
3. **Partner reflects**: "So you're saying you feel overwhelmed by managing the calendar alone?" (Validate, show you understand — don't rebut or explain or deny)
4. **Partner shares perspective**: "I've been feeling the same way. I need help too. I thought you preferred doing it because you're better at it." (No defensiveness, share your real experience)
5. **Find common ground**: "We both want the calendar managed." (Agree on shared problem/goal)
6. **Brainstorm solutions**: "What if we each take half the appointments? Or we do a weekly Sunday check-in? Or we use a shared calendar app?" (Propose concrete, actionable, make-it-work-able solutions)
7. **Commit to plan**: "Let's try a shared Google Calendar and check in Sunday evenings for 10 minutes. We'll try it for 2 weeks then revisit." (Specific, time-boxed, follow-up)

## Deep Listening
- **Active listening**: paraphrase what you heard ("So you're saying that when I come home late, you feel unimportant"). Ask follow-up questions. Don't plan your response while they're talking (you're not listening — you're composing your rebuttal)
- **Empathic listening**: reflect the EMOTION, not just the content. "You sound sad about that" "That seems frustrating." "I hear you saying that was exciting." This validates their experience. When someone feels heard, they're more open to hearing you. Don't try to fix their feelings. "When I hear you out, you feel better even if the problem is unsolved" — because they feel understood and less alone
- **Curiosity**: ask questions from genuine desire to understand. "What's that like for you?" "Can you tell me more?" "What do you wish I understood?" (Open-ended, curious, no "why" questions which sound accusatory)
- **Time to listen**: "Listen long enough that the other person has expressed their full thought — then you can respond. Not just until you have your reply ready."

## When to Seek Help
- Couples therapy: Gottman Method, EFT (Emotionally Focused Therapy — best evidence for couples, 70-75% recovery rate). Prepare for improvement if both willing. Seek BEFORE crisis (not when divorce papers are drafted). Average couples wait 6 years after problem appears
- Signs you need a therapist: same fights for years, contempt/defensiveness habits, stonewalling most conversations, infidelity recovery needs help, considering divorce, emotional withdrawal, one partner feels lonely
- Individual therapy: for your own anxiety, depression, triggers, past trauma. Individual work is YOUR responsibility to show up and do it

## Trust & Repair After Betrayal
- **Rebuilding trust**: betrayer must take full responsibility (no excuses, no blaming partner). Remorse (not guilt). Transparency (open phone, location, schedule). Apologize repeatedly (as long as partner needs to hear it). Changed behavior over time (months/years). Patience (betrayed may cycle through anger, sadness, doubt — normal. May need professional help)
  - Gottman Trust Revival Method: attunement + apology + changed behavior. Attunement: "I'm sorry, I see how much that hurt you. I let you down."
- **Apology languages** (Gary Chapman): expressing regret ("I'm sorry"), accepting responsibility ("I was wrong"), making restitution ("What can I do to make this right?"), genuine repentance ("I will change"), requesting forgiveness ("Will you forgive me?"). Know yours + partner's preferred apology style
- **Forgiveness**: not required, not forgetting, not reconciling. Forgiveness is FOR YOU (release resentment, move forward). Reconciliation is a separate decision (if trust can be rebuilt + relationship is healthy enough to rebuild). You can forgive and still choose to leave
- **When to leave**: abuse (physical, emotional, verbal, financial, psychological). Chronic infidelity without remorse. Addiction without treatment. Irreconcilable values (kids, money, lifestyle). You've done therapy, both tried, still miserable. "A relationship ending doesn't mean it failed" — many relationships end while both have mutual respect and have learned, just can't continue together

## Love Languages (Gary Chapman)
- **Words of affirmation**: compliments, "I love you," encouragement, verbal appreciation. "I'm proud of you" goes a long way for this language
- **Quality time**: undivided attention — conversation, walks, date nights, shared activities. Watching TV together doesn't count
- **Receiving gifts**: thoughtful presents, not expensive. Symbol of love + being thought of. Small things count (flower from the garden, a favorite chocolate). For this person: the thought IS the gift
- **Acts of service**: doing chores, running errands, fixing things, making coffee. Actions speak louder than words — empty if they're not done. Resentment can build if one partner feels they're the only one doing acts of service
- **Physical touch**: hugs, kisses, sex, holding hands, cuddling. Not just sexual intimacy. Every touch is an expression of love
- Everyone has a primary language. Give love in THEIR language, not yours. Ask: "What makes you feel most loved?" and observe what they complain about most (tell you what they value). Also: if they repeatedly criticize you for something, that's likely their love language not being spoken
