# Backpacking & Long-Distance Hiking

## Gear Selection
- **Backpack**: 40-60L for 3-5 day trips, 60-70L for longer. Ultralight: 30-50L (frameless, under 2 lbs). Traditional: 50-80L (frame, 3-5 lbs). Internal frame (close to back, stable) vs external frame (carries load better, more ventilation — rare now). Fit: hip belt on hip bones (not waist), 80% of weight on hips. Shoulder straps should go around shoulders, not dig into armpits
- **Sleep system**: quilt (lighter than bag, no zipper, 10-30°F rating). Sleeping bag ratings: comfort (lower limit for women), limit (lower limit for men). Down (warmest/lightest, loses insulation when wet, $$$). Synthetic (bulkier, heavier, insulates when wet, cheaper, compresses poorly)
  - Sleeping pad: R-value = insulation. CCF (closed-cell foam): R-1-2, cheap, indestructible, uncomfortable. Inflatable: R-2-5.5, comfortable, can puncture (carry patch kit). For 3-season: R-4+ minimum. Ultralight: NeoAir XLite (R-4.5, 12oz)
- **Shelter**: tent (1p 2-3 lbs, 2p 3-5 lbs). Tarp (lightest 5-12oz, requires trekking poles, less bug protection). Hammock (off-ground, comfortable, need underquilt for warmth). Trekking pole tent (lightest shelter for weight-conscious hikers, uses poles instead of tent poles). Bug net required in summer
- **Stove**: canister stove (BRS-3000T 0.9oz, MSR PocketRocket 2, Soto WindMaster). Alcohol stove (penny stove, lighter, slower, fewer regulations? Some areas ban alcohol due to fire risk). No-cook (cold soaking: ramen, instant potatoes, couscous, dried beans soak in jar — saves stove weight). For genuine backpacking: canister is best weight/cooking speed tradeoff

## Lightweight Packing
- **Base weight** (everything except food, water, fuel): target <10 lbs for ultralight, 10-15 lbs for light, 15-20 lbs for traditional. Lower base weight = less fatigue, more miles, more enjoyable
  - Example UL kit: backpack 12oz, shelter 16oz, sleeping bag 20oz, pad 12oz, pad+bag 30oz = 5 lbs before clothes + extras. Goal: 8-10 lbs base weight
- **Big 3 weight**: backpack + shelter + sleeping bag = heavy. Drop 3 lbs from these = huge difference. Spend money on lighter version of these three items
- **Water**: 2.2 lbs/liter. Carry 1L in dry areas, filter from sources (~2L capacity max on trail). Carry only 1L between water sources. In deserts: rent a water cache or haul 6L+ (12 lbs extra)
- **Food**: ~2 lbs/day (2,500-4,000 cal/day). Calorie density: nuts (6,000 cal/lb) > granola (5,000) > chocolate (4,500) > cheese (4,000) > freeze-dried meals (2,000). Dehydrate: hummus, refried beans, fruit leather. Per day on trail: 1.5-2 lbs if high cal/oz

## Water Crossings
- **Rivers**: look for wide, shallow, slow sections. Avoid fast narrow sections (current stronger, deeper near middle). Unbuckle hip belt (easier to escape pack in current). Use trekking poles for stability. Face upstream, shuffle sideways. Strong current: cross facing upstream, lean into current with two legs spread wider. Worst case: try to keep boots on (bare feet less grip, rocks slippery). In swift water (fast current over ankle depth): don't cross, find alternate route or wait for water to recede
- **Snowmelt**: afternoon highest flow (wait for morning). Spring: most dangerous (cold water = loss of dexterity + hypothermia risk). Cross early AM

## Health & Safety
- **Blisters**: prevention (moleskin/leukotape at first hot spot). Stop immediately, don't "walk it off." Method: clean area, apply leukotape directly (stays on days). Pop dirty blisters? Only if painful and will continue walking — clean, lance with sterilized needle at edge, leave skin on, apply antiseptic, cover with moleskin donut. Hot spots: leukotape BEFORE blister forms
- **Chafing**: prevention (body glide, Vaseline, diaper rash cream). Under arms, groin, between thighs. Fresh socks mid-day
- **Giardia** (beaver fever): waterborne, causes explosive diarrhea + cramps. Incubation 1-2 weeks. Treat: antibiotics (tinidazole, metronidazole). Prevention: filter or treat ALL water (even crystal clear streams). Sawyer Squeeze: 0.1 micron filter removes bacteria + protozoa (giardia + cryptosporidium). Iodine tablets or AquaMira drops (chlorine dioxide) effective against viruses
- **Altitude sickness**: >8,000 ft. Prevention: gradual ascent, climb high sleep low, acetazolamide (Diamox). Symptoms: headache + nausea + fatigue. Stop ascent until resolved. Descend if severe

## Trail Etiquette
- **Leave No Trace**: pack it in, pack it out (ALL trash, including toilet paper). No campfires in sensitive areas or when fire ban. Use existing campsites, walk through puddles not around (widening trails). Leave what you find
- **Trail courtesy**: uphill hiker has right of way (they're working harder). Step to downhill side when yielding. Bikes yield to hikers, hikers yield to horses (move to downhill side, stand still, talk quietly). Horses spook easily — announce yourself from a distance before they round a bend
- **Campsite selection**: 200 ft from water + trail. Use existing sites (don't create new). No cutting switchbacks. In bear country: store food in bear canister 100 ft from tent, hang in some areas. Cook 100 ft from tent
- **Group size**: keep small (4-8 max). Spread out on trail (single file, not widening trail). Designate someone for water crossings. Quiet hours: respect quiet after dark

## Resupply (Long Trails)
- **Mail drops**: send box to post office/general delivery (write "HOLD FOR THRU-HIKER" + expected arrival date). Self-resupply: pack food in advance, mail from home or from trail town grocery. Post offices: limited hours (closed weekends/holidays)
- **Trail towns**: buy food at local grocery (limited selection, expensive). Resupply boxes: easier if picky/vegetarian/special diet (buy in advance, mail). Gas stations often have extra freeze-dried meals?
- **Zero days**: zero miles hiked (rest day). Resupply, laundry, shower, eat town food. Cost: $30-60/night motel, $10-20 hostel. Typical every 5-8 days. Many hikers budget $100-200/day in town (motel + food). On trail: $3-8/day food cost
- **Shuttles**: hikers help each other between trail + town. Pay drivers $20-40 per ride. Many trail towns have "hiker angels" who offer free rides (or low-cost). Hitches: stick out thumb, hold sign with location

## Popular Long Trails
- **Appalachian Trail (AT)**: 2,200 miles, Georgia → Maine, 5-7 months, 3-season. Well-marked (white blazes), frequent shelters (every 8-10 miles), many road crossings, lots of hikers. Terrain: moderate, but very up+down. Typical thru-hike cost: $5,000-8,000
- **Pacific Crest Trail (PCT)**: 2,650 miles, Mexico → Canada (CA, OR, WA). 4-6 months. Desert (CA) → Sierra (snow) → Cascades. Requires permit (PCTA only 50/day? Actually no quota for northbound, limited for southbound?). Best: start April-May (SoCal desert), hit Sierra in June (snow melt). Snow/ice crossing in Sierra (need ice axe + microspikes). More remote than AT, longer water carries
- **Continental Divide Trail (CDT)**: 3,100 miles, Mexico → Canada (NM, CO, WY, ID, MT). Most remote + least developed — navigation skills needed. 5-6 months. Lowest completion rate of Triple Crown (15% vs 25% AT, 30% PCT)
- **Colorado Trail**: 500 miles, Denver → Durango. 4-6 weeks. High altitude (most above 10,000 ft). Well-maintained, beautiful
- **John Muir Trail (JMT)**: 211 miles, Yosemite → Mt. Whitney. 2-4 weeks. ~8,000 ft elevation gain per segment. Requires lottery permit for Yosemite (highly competitive). Most beautiful 200 miles in US
