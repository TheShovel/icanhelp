# Entomology

## Insect Classification — Major Orders
- **Coleoptera (beetles)**: largest order (~400,000 species). Hard forewings (elytra) covering membranous hindwings. Chewing mouthparts. Complete metamorphosis. Examples: ladybugs (beneficial — eat aphids), fireflies (bioluminescence), weevils (plant pests — distinctive snout), Japanese beetle (invasive pest), dung beetles (ecologically important decomposers). Largest family: Curculionidae (weevils, ~50,000 species). Found in nearly every terrestrial habitat
- **Lepidoptera (butterflies & moths)**: ~180,000 species. Scales on wings (color patterns). Coiled proboscis (sucking mouthparts — except some adult moths lack mouthparts entirely). Complete metamorphosis (caterpillar → pupa → adult). Antennae: clubbed in butterflies, feathery/filiform in moths. Examples: monarch (migration, milkweed host — endangered), silkworm moth (Bombyx mori — domestic, flightless), luna moth, painted lady (most widespread butterfly). Moth vs butterfly: most moths nocturnal, hold wings flat at rest (butterflies fold up), thicker bodies and feathery antennae
- **Hymenoptera (bees, wasps, ants, sawflies)**: ~150,000 species. Two pairs membranous wings (linked by hamuli). Chewing or chewing-lapping mouthparts. Complete metamorphosis. Social and solitary. Females have ovipositor (modified into stinger in many). Examples: honey bee (Apis mellifera — eusocial, pollination, honey), bumble bee (social, buzz pollination), paper wasps (social), yellowjackets (scavengers, aggressive), ants (eusocial, diverse roles), parasitic wasps (important biological control agents). Haplodiploid sex determination: males haploid (unfertilized eggs), females diploid (fertilized). This genetic system (sisters share 75% relatedness vs 50% in diplodiploidy) drives evolution of eusociality (kin selection theory — Hamilton's rule, rB > C)
- **Diptera (flies, mosquitoes, gnats)**: ~160,000 species. One pair of wings (hindwings modified into halteres — gyroscopes for flight stability). Sucking/sponging mouthparts (mosquitoes: piercing-sucking). Complete metamorphosis. Larvae = maggots. Examples: house fly (Musca domestica — disease vector: mechanically transports >100 pathogens including E. coli, Salmonella, Shigella), mosquito (Anopheles transmits malaria, Aedes: dengue/Zika/chikungunya, Culex: West Nile), fruit fly (Drosophila melanogaster — genetics model: key in identifying homeotic/Hox genes, segments, chromosomes), tsetse fly (sleeping sickness — vector of Trypanosoma brucei), horse/deer flies (painful bites, mechanical vectors of tularemia and anthrax)
- **Hemiptera (true bugs)**: ~100,000 species. Piercing-sucking mouthparts (stylet bundle — plant or animal feeding). Incomplete metamorphosis (nymphs resemble adults). Forewings: basal half thickened, apical half membranous (hemelytra — uniquely identifying feature). Examples: aphids (plant pests, produce honeydew — waxy excretion, farmed by ants, parthenogenesis + viviparity, complex life cycles with host alternation), cicadas (periodical — 13/17 year life cycles, synchronized emergences — periodical cicadas endemic to eastern US), stink bugs (invasive brown marmorated stink bug = agricultural pest, defensive chemicals), bed bugs (Cimex lectularius — blood feeders, resurgence from insecticide resistance and international travel, no disease transmission but severe dermatitis), assassin bugs (some transmit Chagas disease via Triatoma/Kissing bugs)
- **Other orders**: Orthoptera (grasshoppers, crickets, katydids — jumping hind legs, sound production stridulation, incomplete metamorphosis). Blattodea (cockroaches + termites — termites now classified in Blattodea, eusocial, digest cellulose via gut symbionts or protists). Odonata (dragonflies, damselflies — aquatic naiads, terrestrial adults, voracious predators, incomplete metamorphosis, among most ancient insect lineages). Siphonaptera (fleas — laterally compressed, jumping, blood-feeding ectoparasites, complete metamorphosis, vectors of plague (Yersinia pestis), typhus). Phthiraptera (lice — wingless ectoparasites, incomplete metamorphosis: head/body/crab lice). Thysanoptera (thrips — tiny, rasping-sucking mouthparts, plant pests, also vectors of tospoviruses). Dermaptera (earwigs — pincers/cerci, scavengers, maternal care). Isoptera (now infraorder of Blattodea — termites: extremely important decomposers, major structural pests in buildings)

## Metamorphosis
- **Complete (holometabolous)**: egg → larva → pupa → adult. ~85% of insects (beetles, butterflies, bees, flies, wasps, moths). Each stage completely different form/habitat. Larva: feeding, growing (multiple instars — growth stages between molts, number varies: 3-8 typically). Pupa: non-feeding, metamorphosis inside cocoon/chrysalis/puparium. Holometabolous larvae and adults avoid competition by using different resources (caterpillar eats leaves, butterfly drinks nectar)
- **Incomplete (hemimetabolous)**: egg → nymph → adult (no pupal stage). Nymphs resemble adults (gradual changes: wing buds, compound eyes, reproductive organs). Develop through molts (instars). Examples: grasshoppers, cockroaches, true bugs, dragonflies, mayflies, stoneflies. Aquatic nymphs (naiads): dragonfly naiads breathe via rectal gills, catch prey with extendable labium (modified jaw — prehensile, captures prey with lightning speed)
- **Ametabolous**: egg → juvenile → adult. Very primitive — no metamorphosis, only size increase. Found in wingless insects: silverfish, bristletails (order Zygentoma). Juveniles identical except size and reproductive maturity. Continuous molting even as adults
- **Hormonal control**: prothoracicotropic hormone (PTTH — from brain) → stimulates prothoracic gland → ecdysone (molting hormone, triggers molt). Juvenile hormone (JH — from corpora allata) keeps insect in juvenile stage. High JH = larval molts. Declining JH → pupation (holometabolous) or adult (hemimetabolous). Synthetic JH analogs (methoprene) used as insecticides — disrupt metamorphosis, kill before adulthood. Ecdysone agonists (tebufenozide) trigger premature, lethal molt

## Insect Behavior
- **Social insects (eusocial)**: three traits: cooperative brood care, reproductive division of labor (queen + workers), overlapping generations. Hymenoptera (honey bees, bumble bees, ants, some wasps) and Isoptera (termites). Honey bees: queen (only fertile female, 1000+ eggs/day, lives 2-5 years), workers (sterile females, 4-6 weeks in summer — roles: nurse → comb builder → food storage → forager), drones (males from unfertilized parthenogenetic eggs, mate then die). Termites: king + queen (pair remains monogamous lifelong, queen can live 30+ years in some species), workers (both sexes — unlike Hymenoptera where only females work), soldiers (defend colony with enlarged mandibles or chemical spray)
- **Communication**: pheromones — sex (attract mates, often species-specific blends — codling moth uses precise ratios), alarm (alert colony — honey bee isopentyl acetate = "attack" pheromone, triggers stinging), trail (ants — leave chemical trail from abdomen to food source: follow longest-lasting strongest, decision-making via evaporation dynamics), aggregation (bark beetles — mass attack overwhelms tree defenses), queen substance (9-oxo-2-decenoic acid in bees — suppresses worker ovary development, attracts drones). Honey bee waggle dance: distance and direction to food source (angle relative to sun, duration = distance, round dance for nearby <50m, figure-8 waggle dance for further). Cricket/grasshopper stridulation: file + scraper (files on forewing, scraper on other wing; species have unique songs or dialects). Firefly bioluminescence: species-specific flash patterns (males flash searching, females respond with species-specific flash timing — females of predatory Photuris species mimic other species' flash to attract males for predation, "femme fatale" fireflies)
- **Foraging**: optimal foraging theory — animals maximize energy per unit time. Central place foragers (bees, ants — return to nest). Recruitment communication (honey bee dance, ant trail pheromones). Resource defense (dragonflies defend territories with high prey density). Host selection (herbivores): host plant detection via visual + chemical cues, females choose oviposition sites, Hopkins' host selection principle: adults prefer the host they developed on (debatable). Caterpillar feeding guilds: leaf chewers, leaf miners (live inside leaf, serpentine/blotch patterns), gall makers (induce plant tumor, modify plant metabolism), borers (stem, root, trunk), root feeders

## Beneficial Insects
- **Pollinators**: honey bees (most important managed pollinator — $15 billion annual crop value in US), bumble bees (better for certain crops — tomatoes/buzz pollination/floriculture), solitary bees (mason bees, leafcutter bees, sweat bees — often more efficient than honey bees per flower visit, important for native plants), butterflies (less efficient but important for some wildflowers), flies (hoverflies/syrphids — also eat aphids as larvae, important pollinators for many small flowers like carrots/onions), beetles (ancient pollinators, magnolias, water lilies). Pollination syndrome: flower traits adapted to specific pollinators (bee flowers: blue/yellow, UV patterns, nectar guides; moth flowers: white, strong evening scent, tubular — long proboscis). Crop pollination dependency: 75% of leading global crops benefit from animal pollination

- **Predators**: ladybugs (Coccinellidae — adults and larvae eat aphids, scale insects, mealybugs — 50-60 aphids/day for adults, 400 total lifetime for larvae), lacewings (Chrysopidae — aphid lions, eat up to 200 aphids/week as larvae in 2-3 weeks before pupation), praying mantis (eat any insects including beneficial — not ideal biocontrol), ground beetles (Carabidae — eat caterpillars, slugs, other soil pests), predatory wasps (hunting wasps — paralyze prey, lay eggs on them — caterpillar hosts for larval food), hoverfly larvae (eat aphids before pupation), assassin bugs (generalist predators on many pest insects). Gardening: plant dill, fennel, yarrow, cosmos, alyssum, parsley, cilantro for attracting beneficials (provide nectar and pollen for adult beneficials)
- **Parasitoids**: insects that develop on/in a single host and kill it. Ichnetmonidae (giant Ichneumon wasps — huge diversity, specialized on specific hosts, long ovipositors to reach wood-boring larvae). Braconidae (attack caterpillars — tomato hornworm pupae on hornworm's back, common in gardens). Trichogramma (tiny wasps, lay eggs in pest insect eggs — sold commercially for biological control, <1mm). Tachinidae (parasitoid flies — lay eggs on caterpillars, beetles, bugs; larvae burrow inside and feed). Many are highly host-specific — excellent biological control agents
- **Decomposers**: dung beetles (Scarabaeidae — bury dung, improve soil, reduce fly breeding, sequester carbon), carrion beetles (bury dead animals, nutrient recycling), termites (digest cellulose via gut symbionts— break down wood, crucial in tropical ecosystems), flies (maggots consume dead matter, forensic entomology uses insect succession on corpses to estimate time of death)
- **Other beneficial**: silkworms (Bombyx mori — domestication for 5000+ years, silk production), lac insects (shellac), cochineal (carmine red dye — Dactylopius coccus, scale insects on cactus — used for centuries as natural red food and textile dye), forensic entomology (blow flies, flesh flies arrive first at carcass — use life stage to estimate minimum PMI/postmortem interval, measuring accumulated degree hours or days), edible insects (entomophagy: crickets, mealworms, grasshoppers — high protein, low environmental impact compared to livestock feed conversion, ~2 billion people worldwide consume insects as part of traditional diet)

## Pest Management (IPM)
- **Integrated Pest Management**: monitoring + prevention + intervention only when needed. Economic threshold: pest density where control cost = damage cost. Economic injury level: pest density causing damage equal to control cost. Four tiers:
  1. **Cultural control**: crop rotation (disrupt pest life cycles, deplete soil pathogens), intercropping (confuse pests, attract beneficial), resistant varieties (plant breeding, GMO — Bt corn reduces pesticide use by >40%), sanitation (remove infected plants/plant debris), proper planting time (avoid pest emergence windows)
  2. **Biological control**: classical (import natural enemy — ladybug for aphids, Neochetina weevil for water hyacinth, Cactoblastis moth for prickly pear cactus in Australia). Augmentative (release mass-reared beneficials — Trichogramma wasps, predatory mites in greenhouses). Conservation (protect existing natural enemies — reduce broad-spectrum insecticides, maintain hedgerows and wildflower strips, provide overwintering habitat)
  3. **Mechanical/physical control**: traps (sticky, light, pheromone), barriers (row covers, tree bands, netting), tillage, high-pressure water spray (for aphids, spider mites, whiteflies on plants), vacuuming (for pests in greenhouses, bed bugs). Exclusion: window screens, sealing cracks, floating row covers for crops
  4. **Chemical control**: last resort. Selective pesticides (Bt — Bacillus thuringiensis — produces toxin specific to caterpillars/beetles/fly larvae, kills only target group, safe for mammals — widely used in organic farming and Bt crops). Insect growth regulators (methoprene, pyriproxyfen — disrupt JH signaling or chitin synthesis). Narrow-spectrum vs broad-spectrum (organophosphates/neonicotinoids = broad, kill beneficials). Resistance management: rotate modes of action, limit applications, use mixtures. Botanicals: neem oil (contains azadirachtin — insect growth regulator, repellent, antifeedant), pyrethrin (from Chrysanthemum flowers — fast knockdown neurotoxin, but breaks down quickly in sunlight)
- **Pesticide resistance**: over 600 arthropod species resistant to one or more pesticides. Mechanisms: target-site insensitivity (knockdown resistance/kdr in mosquitoes = mutation in sodium channel gene reduces pyrethroid binding — common worldwide), metabolic detoxification (increased esterase, P450, GST activity), behavioral avoidance, reduced penetration, sequestration. Resistance evolves rapidly in insects due to short generation times, large population sizes, strong selection pressure. Rotation of modes of action delays resistance (Insecticide Resistance Action Committee/IRAC groups numbered by mechanism)

## Insect Anatomy
- **Exoskeleton (cuticle)**: chitin (polysaccharide, long polymer of N-acetylglucosamine) + proteins. Layers: epicuticle (waxy, waterproof — thin outermost, protects from desiccation), exocuticle (hard, sclerotized/rigid), endocuticle (flexible). Cuticle secreted by epidermis. Provides protection, support, water barrier, muscle attachment. Apodemes: internal projections for muscle attachment (like endoskeleton). Molting (ecdysis): old cuticle shed periodically — controlled by ecdysone hormone, new cuticle secreted under old, insect swallows air/water to expand new cuticle before it hardens (sclerotization — tanning process crosslinks proteins, use of catecholamines such as dopamine and N-acetyldopamine)
- **Body segments**: three tagmata — head, thorax, abdomen
- **Head**: compound eyes (thousands of ommatidia — individual visual units. Each ommatidium has its own lens and photoreceptor cells, provides mosaic image, excellent motion detection, polarization sensitivity, UV vision). Ocelli (simple eyes — light intensity detection, 3 usually, in triangular pattern). Antennae (sense: smell/olfaction/antennal lobes, touch, humidity, vibration, CO₂ detection, some detect sound — Johnston's organ in mosquitoes detects wing-beat frequency for mates). Mouthparts: chewing (beetles, grasshoppers mandibles/crushing — most primitive), piercing-sucking (mosquitoes/labrum+mandibles+maxillae stylets, flies/sponging+rasping), siphoning (butterflies/proboscis coiled, uncoiled when feeding, labial palps present), chewing-lapping (bees — mandibles for manipulation, glossa tongue for nectar, collects nectar into crop — honey stomach)
- **Thorax**: three segments — pro, meso, metathorax. Each typically bears one pair of legs. Meso- and metathorax each bear a pair of wings (or modified halteres or elytra). Legs: coxa, trochanter, femur, tibia, tarsus (1-5 tarsomeres), pretarsus (claws, sometimes pulvilli pads — allow sticking to surfaces, arolium expands for adhesion on smooth surfaces). Leg modifications: jumping (grasshoppers — enlarged femur with large extensor muscle), swimming (diving beetles — paddle-like, hairs fringe hind legs, rowing), grasping (praying mantis — raptorial front legs with spines to catch prey), digging (mole cricket — front legs expanded, tibial teeth for digging), pollen-carrying (honey bees — corbicula/tibia with hairs on hind legs, packed with pollen)
- **Abdomen**: 11 segments (reduced in many groups). Contains digestive, reproductive, excretory (Malpighian tubules/osmoregulation — blind tubes attached at midgut-hindgut junction, absorb nitrogenous wastes (uric acid) from hemolymph, excrete as uric acid or guanine = water conservation for terrestrial insects, waste removed in feces or as metabolic end product — insects don't have a urea cycle like mammals), respiratory systems. Ovipositor (egg laying tube, segments 8+9 — modified into stinger in aculeate Hymenoptera). Cerci (paired appendages on segment 11 — sensory). Spiracles (openings to tracheal system — up to 10 pairs (2 thoracic, 8 abdominal), can open/close with valves to regulate water loss and gas exchange)
- **Internal systems**: open circulatory system — hemolymph pumped by dorsal heart (along dorsal vessel, ostia valves allow inflow) into hemocoel (body cavity). No oxygen transport (respiratory system separate — insects don't use hemoglobin, except Chironomus midges larvae = hemoglobin). Respiratory: tracheal system — branching tubes (tracheae) delivering O₂ directly to cells. Tracheae end in tracheoles (<1µm diameter, fluid-filled at rest, fill with air during activity via osmotic changes). Spiracles regulate gas exchange and water loss. Diffusion + active ventilation (abdominal pumping). Digestive: foregut (mouth, crop/storage, proventriculus for grinding), midgut (gastric ceca — digestion and absorption), hindgut (ileum, colon, rectum — water reabsorption). Malpighian tubules (excretion — produce uric acid). Nervous: ventral nerve cord (paired longitudinal cords along length of body) + brain (protocerebrum: vision; deutocerebrum: antennae; tritocerebrum: mouthparts, connects to stomatogastric system/frontal ganglion). Subesophageal ganglion (mouthparts). Ganglia in each segment. Insect brain ~10^5 to ~10^6 neurons (compared to 10^11 in humans, but capable of remarkable computation — e.g., honey bee navigation, bumble bee learned tool use)
- **Sense of smell**: antennae covered in sensilla (hairs with pores), olfactory receptor neurons project to antennal lobe (insect olfactory processing center analogous to vertebrate olfactory bulb). Odor coding: each odorant activates unique combination of olfactory receptor neurons — combinatorial coding. Pheromone detection: extremely sensitive (male silkworm moth can detect single molecule of bombykol — detecting a single female from up to 10 km away in ideal conditions)

## Collecting & Preserving
- **Methods**: aerial net (butterflies, dragonflies — swing through specimen, flip net to close, fold over to trap). Sweep net (heavy canvas, swing through vegetation — catches many insects in one motion, good for surveys). Beat sheet (white cloth under branches, hit branch with stick — collected beetles and caterpillars fall onto sheet, use aspirator/sucking device for collection). Pitfall trap (cup in ground, covered, with preservative or dry — carabid beetles, ground-dwelling arthropods). Light trap (UV light at night — attracts moths, beetles, true bugs — sheet or bucket with killing agent, very effective using mercury vapor UV bulbs). Malaise trap (tent-like mesh — intercept flying insects, collect in alcohol jar at top of trap). Berlese funnel (leaf litter/soil → heat from top drives arthropods down into collection jar — collects springtails, mites, small beetles, insect larvae in soil). Bait traps: fruit bait (butterflies, fruit flies), pheromone lures for specific pest monitoring (gypsy moth, codling moth, boll weevil)
- **Killing**: kill jar (potassium cyanide — dangerous, use with caution, controlled substance in some jurisdictions) or ethyl acetate (safer, non-lethal to humans in exposure but can be recovered but kills insects gradually, some books prefer ethyl acetate because it doesn't harden specimens as much, less harmful) or freezer (kill by freezing — non-toxic but allows recovery in some insects/caterpillars, requires 24-48 hours). Smaller insects pinned directly or stored in ethanol (80-95% ethyl alcohol). Lepidoptera: careful pinching of thorax between fingers to crush before pinning. Large beetles: inject alcohol or ammonia into abdomen with fine syringe to soften and prevent decay long-term
- **Pinning**: #1-#7 pins (fine to coarse). Position: pin through right side of thorax (mesothorax) for beetles, bees, flies — centered between wing bases. Lepidoptera: pin through center of thorax. Spread wings (spreading board with groove), paper strips hold wings in position. Legs and antennae arranged symmetrically. Labels: locality/date/collector, identification label with determiner's name, bar code/genitalia dissection label. Standard pinning height: labels 20-25mm below pin head, specimen raised on pin with pinning block. Drying: 1-2 weeks at room temperature, prevent pests (dermestid beetles, booklice damage specimens). Large beetles need internal cleaning: remove viscera, stuff with cotton to prevent greasy decay
- **Storage**: insect boxes (Schmitt box/unit tray system, Cornell drawer system — stacking cases). Specimens kept in Cornell drawers or Schmidt boxes. Museum standard: pinned specimens in airtight drawers (museum cabinets or drawer system). Pest control: paradichlorobenzene (PDB) or naphthalene mothballs (carcinogenic concerns — many museums switching to freezing at -20°C for 1 week, or -40°C for 3 days, repeating twice). Pinned collection stored in low humidity, dark, stable temperature (~18-20°C, 35-50% relative humidity). Ethanol-preserved collection: replace with fresh 70% EtOH periodically, keep in dark. Damaged specimens: relaxed in humidity chamber (wet sand with fungicide, sealed container, 1-3 days for relaxed insects), repaired with insect pins and glue (water-soluble glue preferred). Documentation: field notebook with date, location (GPS coordinates), habitat, host plant, behavior, weather, collector identification
