# Birds: Ornithology & Birdwatching

## Bird Identification
- **Size/shape**: compare to common birds (robin = medium, sparrow = small, crow = large, hawk = mid-large). Shape: rounded vs pointed wings, forked vs square tail, long vs short neck, slender vs stout bill
- **Bill shape**: conical (seed eater: finches, sparrows). Thin/pointed (insect eater: warblers, vireos). Hooked (bird of prey: hawks, owls — but also shrikes). Flattened (flycatcher — broad base, catch insects midair). Specialized: crossbill (pry open pine cones), avocet (curved up, sweep through water), pelican (pouch for fish), flamingo (filter feeder). "Bill shape tells you what they eat"
- **Plumage**: breeding vs non-breeding (many ducks: drakes = bright breeding, drab eclipse plumage in summer). Juvenile vs adult. Male vs female (sexual dimorphism: many male birds brighter, females camouflaged). Seasonal differences: warblers in spring = bright breeding, fall = drab. Molt: feathers replaced ~1-2x/year, often asymmetrical flight feathers
- **Behavior**: feeding (on ground, in trees, hover, catch in air). Flight pattern (undulating: finch, woodpecker; soar: hawk, vulture; straight: swift, swallow). Song/call (diagnostic: chickadee-dee-dee, cardinal whistle, crow caw). Walking (hop: sparrow; waddle: duck). Head-bobbing (pigeon, dove). Tail bobbing (phoebe, wren). Wing flicking (titmouse, wren, kinglet)
- **Habitat**: forest, field, wetland, coast, desert, urban. Some birds restricted to specific microhabitats (marsh wren = cattails, Blackburnian warbler = high in conifers, wood thrush = deep forest). Learn bird calls for ID (Merlin Bird ID app can identify by sound — a revolutionary tool for beginners to identify birds by song alone. Uses computer vision/audio identification)

## Common North American Birds
- **Backyard**: Northern Cardinal (male: bright red, female: tan, crest. Song: cheerful whistle. Whistle: "birdie, birdie, birdie"). American Robin (orange breast, gray back, early spring arrival, song: cheerful "cheer up cheerily"). Blue Jay (blue crest, noisy, mimics hawks). Black-capped Chickadee (small, black cap + bib, active, friendly, "chicka-dee-dee-dee"). Mourning Dove (plump, gray, mournful cooing). Tufted Titmouse (crested, gray, active at feeders). Downy Woodpecker (small, black + white, male has red spot). Northern Mockingbird (gray, long tail, mimics other bird songs). House Finch (male: red/orange head, streaked belly). Goldfinch (bright yellow, black wings). Dark-eyed Junco (slate gray, white belly, "snowbirds" — appear in winter)
- **Waterfowl**: Mallard (most common duck — green head male, brown female, blue speculum). Canada Goose (black neck, white cheek, loud honk, aggressive, highly common). Wood Duck (beautiful male, iridescent, crested, nests in tree cavities). Great Blue Heron (4' tall, gray, hunts fish in shallow water — stands motionless until fish approaches, then strikes with bill)
- **Birds of prey**: Red-tailed Hawk (broad, rounded wings, brick-red tail, "kee-eeer" call. Most common hawk, often seen perched on phone poles/power lines). Bald Eagle (white head, brown body, large, sometimes seen near water — symbol of US, recovered from near extinction; wingspan 6-8'). Peregrine Falcon (fastest animal — 240 mph dive, nesting on city skyscrapers). Great Horned Owl (large, ear tufts, hoots, powerful predator)
- **Songbirds**: American Goldfinch (bright yellow/summer, drab olive/winter, "po-ta-to-chip" flight call). Eastern Bluebird (bright blue back, orange chest, prefers open fields + nesting boxes). Barn Swallow (deeply forked tail, nests in barns/under eaves, insectivore, acrobatic). Red-winged Blackbird (marsh, red+ yellow shoulder patch on male)
- **Warblers**: tiny, often bright, insectivorous, many species (Yellow-rumped Warbler = most common, easy to start). Yellow Warbler: all yellow, red streaks on male's chest, "sweet sweet sweet, I'm so sweet"

## Birdwatching Tips
- **Optics**: binoculars: 8x42 (most versatile — good field of view + brightness). 10x42 (more zoom, harder to hold steady, dimmer in low light). Better: waterproof, nitrogen-purged (prevents fogging). Brands: Nikon Monarch, Vortex Diamondback, Celestron Nature, Swarovski EL (top of the line). Spotting scope: 20-60x zoom, tripod required, for shorebirds/waterfowl at distance, digiscoping (attach phone camera to scope)
- **Field guide**: Sibley Guide to Birds (standard North American, detailed paintings/descriptions). Merlin Bird ID (free app, photo ID, sound ID, location-based suggestions — revolutionary). Audubon app (good, photos, range maps). eBird (track sightings, real-time bird sightings near you, contributes to citizen science). iBird (cue cards with range maps)
- **Technique**: look with naked eyes, scan for movement, raise binoculars slowly. Listen for calls — learn 5-10 common species calls first. Bird call memories: "chickadee-dee-dee for Black-capped Chickadee," "cheer-up, cheerily" for robin, "tea-kettle, tea-kettle, tea-kettle" for Carolina Wren. Time: early morning (dawn chorus), spring migration (April-May) = best. Fall mild too (September-October). Hot midday: slow
- **Ethics**: stay on trail, don't disturb nests, limit use of recorded calls (stress for birds, especially during breeding). Never approach nesting birds. Report rare bird sightings to local listserv/ eBird. Don't interfere with fledglings on ground (leave them — parents are nearby)
- **eBird**: track your sightings, explore hotspots near you. Global citizen science project (Cornell Lab of Ornithology). Provides data for ornithology research. Hotspots: public birding locations with counts. "Bar charts" show what species are being seen where + when — plan trips around migration windows

## Bird Biology
- **Flight feathers**: primaries (on hand — 10 per wing, provide thrust). Secondaries (on forearm, provide lift). Coverts (overlap + streamline). Tail feathers (steering, braking, display). Feathers = lightweight (hollow shaft), replaceable (molt), waterproof (preen oil from uropygial gland at base of tail). Diurnal vs nocturnal, single vs multiple molt patterns. Feather function: insulation, waterproofing, flight, display (male peacock, bird of paradise), camo, sound production
- **Migration**: routes (flyways — Atlantic, Mississippi, Central, Pacific). Orientation: magnetic (magnetite in head/ beak, light-dependent magnetic sensing — compass sense for direction, map sense for location), celestial (stars — use Earth's rotation axis relative to star field), visual landmarks (rivers, coast, mountains — see from high altitude), olfactory (pigeons: smell-guided). Distance: Arctic Tern = longest migration (pole to pole, ~25,000 miles, sees more daylight than any creature). Ruby-throated Hummingbird: 500 mile nonstop across Gulf of Mexico. Bar-tailed Godwit: longest nonstop flight of any bird (9-day, 7,000 mile flight from Alaska to New Zealand)
  - Timing: day length + weather cues. Fat storage: double body weight before migration for some (Ruby-throated hummingbird: 2g→4g. Blackpoll warbler: 11g→20g). Stopover: migratory birds need habitat to rest + refuel; habitat loss along flyways threatens populations
- **Song**: mostly male, to defend territory + attract mate. Song learning: some species inherit melody (innate: flycatchers), some learn from adults (year-round: song sparrow, some learn song in critical period first year: zebra finch only learns as juvenile before sexual maturity). Regional dialects exist (White-crowned Sparrow has dialects). Each human "dialect" zone? Song sparrow learns from neighbors = local dialects. Females choose males with better song: more complex, larger repertoire
- **Nesting**: types (cup: robins, warblers; cavity: bluebirds, woodpeckers; platform: eagles, ospreys; pendant: orioles; ground: killdeer, many shorebirds, grouse; burrow: kingfishers, puffins). Eggs: colored/camouflaged (open nests = cryptic eggs), white (cavity nests = no need for camouflage). Incubation: mostly female, sometimes male (many shorebirds), or both (pigeons, raptors). Hatchlings: altricial (born blind, helpless, naked — need extensive parental care, primarily songbirds) vs precocial (born eyes open, downy, leave nest quickly — ducks, chickens)
