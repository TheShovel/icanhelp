# Botany

Plant biology — structure, physiology, reproduction, and ecology.

## Plant Cell Structure
- **Cell wall**: Cellulose + hemicellulose + pectin + lignin (structural support, rigid shape). Primary wall (growing cells), secondary wall (thicker, after growth ceases). Plasmodesmata: channels connecting adjacent cells
- **Chloroplasts**: Photosynthesis site — thylakoid membranes (light reactions), stroma (Calvin cycle). Contains own DNA (endosymbiotic origin). Chlorophyll a and b (green pigments), carotenoids (red/orange/yellow, accessory pigments)
- **Vacuole**: Central large vacuole (up to 90% of cell volume). Functions: storage (water, nutrients, pigments, waste), turgor pressure (cell rigidity), digestion (hydrolytic enzymes). Tonoplast membrane surrounds vacuole
- **Plastids**: Chloroplasts (photosynthesis), chromoplasts (pigment storage — fruits, flowers), amyloplasts (starch storage — roots, tubers), leucoplasts (colorless — oil, protein storage)

## Plant Tissues
- **Meristems**: Undifferentiated cells capable of division — apical (tips of roots/shoots — primary growth/length), lateral (cambium — secondary growth/width), intercalary (grasses — internode growth)
- **Dermal tissue**: Epidermis (outer protective layer — cuticle, stomata, root hairs), Periderm (bark — cork cambium replaces epidermis in woody plants)
- **Vascular tissue**: Xylem (water + mineral transport — tracheids, vessel elements, dead at maturity, continuous tubes, transpiration pull). Phloem (sugar transport — sieve tube elements + companion cells, living, bidirectional flow)
- **Ground tissue**: Parenchyma (most common — photosynthesis, storage, secretion, healing), Collenchyma (living, flexible support — celery strings), Sclerenchyma (thick, lignified, dead at maturity — fibers, sclereids — nut shells, pear grit)

## Photosynthesis
- **Light reactions**: Thylakoid membrane — chlorophyll absorbs photons → excite electrons → electron transport chain → ATP + NADPH + O₂ (from water splitting). Photosystem II and photosystem I in series. Non-cyclic (produces ATP + NADPH + O₂) vs cyclic (ATP only)
- **Calvin cycle (dark reactions)**: Stroma — CO₂ fixation: RuBisCO adds CO₂ to RuBP (5C) → unstable 6C → 2x 3-PGA (3C). Reduction: ATP + NADPH → G3P (3C sugar). Regeneration: G3P → RuBP. Requires 3 CO₂ molecules → 1 G3P net (6 turns of cycle)
- **C3 plants**: Most plants. CO₂ fixed directly by RuBisCO. Photorespiration: RuBisCO uses O₂ instead of CO₂ when hot/dry (wastes energy, releases CO₂). Problem: stomata close to save water → O₂ builds up → photorespiration increases
- **C4 plants**: Spatial separation — fix CO₂ in mesophyll cells into 4C compound (oxaloacetate, then malate/aspartate), transport to bundle sheath cells, release CO₂ for Calvin cycle. Concentrates CO₂ at RuBisCO — reduces photorespiration. Corn, sugarcane, sorghum
- **CAM plants**: Temporal separation — fix CO₂ at night (stomata open, cool), store as malic acid in vacuole. Day: release CO₂ for Calvin cycle (stomata closed, conserve water). Cacti, succulents, pineapple, agave

## Water & Nutrient Transport
- **Transpiration**: Water evaporates from leaf stomata → tension in xylem → pulls water column up from roots (cohesion-tension theory). Water molecules cohesive (H-bonds) → continuous column from roots to leaves. Transpiration rate affected by: humidity, temperature, wind, light, leaf area
- **Root pressure**: Positive pressure from roots pushes water up xylem — guttation (water droplets on leaf edges in morning). Minor contribution compared to transpiration pull
- **Mineral uptake**: Active transport into root hairs (H+ pump creates electrochemical gradient). Essential elements: macronutrients (N, P, K, Ca, Mg, S — needed in large amounts), micronutrients (Fe, Mn, Zn, Cu, Mo, B, Cl, Ni — trace amounts). Deficiency symptoms crop-specific (yellow leaves = N deficiency, purple tint = P deficiency, leaf edge burn = K deficiency)
- **Phloem transport**: Pressure flow hypothesis — sugars loaded into sieve tubes at source (leaves) → osmotic water flow → pressure pushes sap to sink (roots, fruits, growing tips). Bidirectional possible. Sieve plates (porous walls) connect sieve tube elements

## Reproduction
- **Angiosperm life cycle**: Flower → pollination (pollen on stigma) → pollen tube grows → double fertilization (sperm + egg = zygote, sperm + polar nuclei = endosperm) → seed + fruit. Double fertilization unique to angiosperms
- **Flower structure**: Sepals (outer, protective), Petals (attract pollinators), Stamens (male — anther + filament), Carpels (female — stigma + style + ovary containing ovules). Complete: all four whorls. Incomplete: missing parts
- **Pollination**: Biotic (bees, butterflies, birds, bats — flowers have colorful petals, nectar, scent), Abiotic (wind — grasses, trees — small, plain, large stigmas, abundant pollen). Self-pollination vs cross-pollination (cross = genetic diversity)
- **Seed dispersal**: Wind (dandelion pappus, maple samara), Animal ingestion (berries pass through digestive tract), Animal attachment (burrs — hook structures), Mechanical explosion (touch-me-not, pea pods), Water (coconut). Each tied to specific habitat
- **Fruit types**: Simple (one ovary — apple, cherry, pea pod), Aggregate (multiple ovaries one flower — raspberry, strawberry), Multiple (multiple flowers — pineapple, fig). Dry (nuts, grains) vs fleshy (berries, drupes, pomes)

## Growth & Development
- **Primary growth (apical meristems)**: Root cap → zone of cell division → elongation → differentiation → maturation. Shoot apical meristem produces leaves, buds, nodes, internodes. Protoderm → epidermis, procambium → vascular tissue, ground meristem → ground tissue
- **Secondary growth (lateral meristems)**: Vascular cambium produces secondary xylem (wood, inward) and phloem (outward). Cork cambium produces bark (periderm). Annual rings visible (earlywood = large cells, fast spring growth; latewood = small cells, dense summer growth)
- **Plant hormones**: Auxin (cell elongation, apical dominance, root initiation, phototropism), Cytokinins (cell division, shoot growth, delay senescence), Gibberellins (stem elongation, seed germination, fruit growth), Abscisic acid (stress response, stomatal closure, dormancy), Ethylene (fruit ripening, leaf abscission, senescence)
- **Photoperiodism**: Plants measure day length (phytochrome photoreceptor). Short-day (flower when days < critical length — chrysanthemum, poinsettia), Long-day (flower when days > critical length — spinach, wheat), Day-neutral (flower regardless — tomato, cucumber)
- **Tropisms**: Phototropism (grow toward light — auxin redistribution), Gravitropism (roots grow down, shoots grow up — statoliths sediment), Thigmotropism (touch response — tendrils curl, Venus flytrap), Hydrotropism (roots grow toward moisture)
