# The Universe & Cosmology

## Big Bang Theory
- **Evidence**: cosmic microwave background (CMB, 1965 Penzias+Wilson — uniform 2.7K radiation from ~380,000 years after BB, predicted by Gamow). Expanding universe (Hubble, 1929 — galaxies move away, farther = faster: v = H₀d, Hubble constant ~70 km/s/Mpc). Abundance of light elements (Big Bang nucleosynthesis: 75% H, 25% He, trace Li — matches predictions accurately. 4He ~25% by mass predicted, found). Large-scale structure (cosmic web: filaments + voids matching simulations of initial quantum fluctuations expanded by inflation)
  - Timeline: Planck epoch (<10⁻⁴³ sec, quantum gravity, unknowable). Inflation (10⁻³⁶-10⁻³² sec, exponential expansion 10²⁶× in ~10⁻³² sec. Solves horizon + flatness problems. Horizon problem: why is CMB same temperature in opposite directions? They were in causal contact before inflation, then inflated apart). Particle production (quarks + leptons formed). Nucleosynthesis (3-20 min, protons + neutrons form H + He + Li). Recombination (380,000 yr, neutral atoms form, CMB released). Dark ages (stars + galaxies not yet formed). First stars + galaxies (200-400 million yr). Today (13.8 billion yr)
- **Cosmic Microwave Background** (CMB): snapshot of baby universe at 380,000 years old (surface of last scattering). Temperature 2.725 K (± 0.001K). Dipole anisotropy: Earth moving relative to CMB (~370 km/s). Tiny fluctuations (ΔT/T ~ 10⁻⁵) correspond to density seeds that grew into galaxies + clusters. Measured by COBE (1992), WMAP (2003), Planck (2013+)
- **Inflation**: explains uniformity + flatness + origin of structure via quantum fluctuations stretched to cosmic scales. Multiple models (slow-roll inflation, eternal inflation, string theory landscapes). Testable prediction: B-mode polarization pattern in CMB (gravitational waves from inflation). BICEP2 (2014) claimed detection, retracted (galactic dust contamination). Still searching

## Dark Matter & Dark Energy

### Dark Matter
- **Evidence**: galaxy rotation curves (stars at edges orbit too fast — more gravity than visible matter suggests. Vera Rubin 1970s). Gravitational lensing (light bends more than visible mass predicts — e.g., Bullet Cluster: mass centroid separated from galaxy centroid after collision = most mass follows collision-less dark matter, not gas). CMB fluctuations (amount of matter in universe ~5% ordinary, ~27% dark matter). Large-scale structure formation (CDM (cold dark matter) simulations match observed cosmic web)
- **Candidates**: WIMPs (weakly interacting massive particles — predicted by SUSY, not found at LHC). Axions (very light, abundant — solves strong CP problem, undetected still). Sterile neutrinos (heavier, rarer). MACHOs (massive compact halo objects like black holes, neutron stars, brown dwarfs — ruled out by microlensing surveys). Primordial black holes (some evidence, unlikely to explain all dark matter)
  - Must be: massive, electrically neutral, interact via gravity + possibly weak force. Not baryonic (ordinary matter — limits from Big Bang nucleosynthesis). Not hot (fast-moving, would wash out small structures) — dark matter must be "cold" (slow-moving) for structure formation
- **Modified gravity** (MOND — Modified Newtonian Dynamics): alternative — modify gravity at low acceleration to explain galaxy rotation curves. Problem: doesn't explain Bullet Cluster, CMB, or large-scale structure across all contexts

### Dark Energy
- **Evidence**: distant type Ia supernovae (Perlmutter, Riess, Schmidt — 1998 Nobel. They found expansion accelerating, not decelerating as expected). CMB (flat universe = total density = critical, ordinary + dark matter ~32% — rest must be dark energy). Baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO: standard ruler from early universe, measured galaxy clustering — consistent with accelerating expansion)
- **Nature**: Einstein's cosmological constant Λ (vacuum energy density, simplest model ΛCDM — "standard model of cosmology"). Quintessence (dynamic scalar field — changes over time). Phantom energy (increasingly strong, could end in "Big Rip"). Modified gravity (could mimic dark energy at cosmic scales). Evidence: Λ consistent with observations, quintessence not ruled out
- **ΛCDM model**: "Cold Dark Matter with Cosmological Constant" — the standard model of cosmology. Parameters: H₀ ~70 km/s/Mpc, Ω(matter) ~0.3 (5% baryonic + 25% CDM), Ω(Λ) ~0.7, age 13.8 billion years. Very successful at explaining most observations

## Large-Scale Structure
- **Cosmic web**: filaments of galaxies + dark matter. Voids (vast empty regions ~100 Mly across). Clusters (100-1,000 galaxies). Superclusters (chains of clusters, Laniakea = our supercluster, Virgo cluster at center, 520 Mly across, 100,000 galaxies). Great attractor (gravitational center of Laniakea, dense region of galaxies pulling our Local Group)
- **Galaxy formation**: hierarchical assembly — small galaxies merge → larger. Gas falls into dark matter halos → cooling → star formation. Mergers trigger starbursts + active galactic nuclei. Feedback: supernovae + supermassive black holes regulate star formation (prevent too-fast star formation, heat gas). Two main types: main sequence star-forming blue galaxies (spirals, live in lower density, still forming stars) vs red-and-dead galaxies (ellipticals, in dense clusters, no new stars — quenched by mergers/AGN feedback)

## Stars & Stellar Evolution (Lifecycle)
- **Formation**: molecular cloud collapses (gravity > gas pressure) → fragment → protostar → main sequence. Timescale: ~100,000 years for massive stars, ~50 million years for sun-like. Minimum mass for fusion: ~0.08 M☉ (brown dwarf: can't fuse H, only deuterium, faint, cools over time — between planet + star transition mass 13-80 Jupiter masses)
- **Life stages**: main sequence (H→He fusion, 90% of stellar life — mass determines how long. High mass: hotter, brighter, shorter life ~10 million years. Low mass (red dwarf): can fuse for trillions of years). Red giant (H exhausted → core contracts → shell burns H → outer layers expand → He fusion). Red supergiant (for massive stars >8 M☉: core burns He → C → O → Ne → Mg → Si → Fe — onion-layered structure. The fusion chain stops at iron; iron fusion consumes not produces energy)
- **Death**: low-mass (sun-like: planetary nebula + white dwarf [Earth-sized, supported by electron degeneracy pressure, cools over billions of years]). Massive >8 M☉: supernova II (core collapse → bounce → explosion. Remnant: neutron star [city-sized, ~1.4-2 M☉, supported by neutron degeneracy pressure, magnetic field ~10¹⁵G, rotates up to 700 times/sec — pulsars]) or black hole [if >~3 M☉ remnant, event horizon forms]. White dwarf accretion in binary: can exceed Chandrasekhar limit (1.4 M☉) → thermonuclear explosion = Type Ia supernova — used as standard candle for cosmology

## Black Holes
- **Types**: stellar-mass (3-100 M☉, from core collapse — Cygnus X-1, LIGO mergers, many). Supermassive (millions to billions M☉, at center of MOST galaxies — Sagittarius A* Milky Way 4.3 M☉). Intermediate (100-100,000 M☉, uncertain origin, rare). Primordial (hypothetical from early universe, could be any size)
  - Event horizon: point of no return (Schwarzschild radius r_s = 2GM/c²). Inside: nothing escapes, not even light. Singularity: all matter crushed to infinite density (mass ends at point at center). Information paradox: does information about matter falling in escape? Hawking radiation (1974): black holes slowly emit particles, eventually evaporate — suggests information may be preserved in outgoing radiation. Still unresolved
- **Detection**: indirect (stellar orbits around center of Milky Way — Ghez+Gen-zel Nobel 2020; accretion disk X-ray emission; gravitational waves from BH mergers — LIGO 2015, mapped recoil plus final BH spin). Direct (Event Horizon Telescope — first image of M87* 2019, Sgr A* 2022)

## Exoplanets
- **Detection methods**: transit (Kepler, TESS — planet passes in front of star, dims by ~1% for Jupiter, ~0.01% for Earth, duration hours. Kepler found 5,000+ planets). Radial velocity (Doppler shift of star from planet's gravity, measures mass). Direct imaging (nearby+big planets, young systems). Microlensing (rare, distant planets, can detect free-floating). Astrometry (star wobble, needs extreme precision — Gaia detects)
  - Key discoveries: Kepler-186f (first Earth-sized planet in habitable zone of red dwarf). TRAPPIST-1 (7 Earth-sized planets orbiting ultra-cool dwarf, 3 in habitable zone). Proxima b (closest exoplanet, 4.2 ly). Hot Jupiters (massive planets very close to star — surprising early discovery, changed planet formation theories)
  - Habitable zone (Goldilocks zone): right distance from star for liquid water on surface. Depends on star temperature + brightness. For Sun: 0.9-1.7 AU. For red dwarf (M-star): much closer (0.1-0.4 AU). Problem: tidal locking (one side always facing star = permanent day/night), flares (M-stars flare frequently, could strip atmosphere). Multiplicity: ~5% of Sun-like stars have Earth-sized planet in habitable zone → billions in Milky Way
