# Paleontology & Prehistoric Life

## Major Animal Groups (Prehistoric)

### Dinosaurs
- **Classification**: Saurischia (lizard-hipped — theropods + sauropodomorphs). Ornithischia (bird-hipped — hadrosaurs, ceratopsians, stegosaurs, ankylosaurs, pachycephalosaurs). Birds are theropod dinosaurs, not descended from; they ARE theropods (feathered theropods, evolved from small meat-eaters around Jurassic). "Dinosaur": defined by specific hip socket anatomy (perforated acetabulum, upright stance — legs directly under body, not sprawling like lizards)
- **Theropods** (carnivores, bipedal): Tyrannosaurus rex (Late Cretaceous N. America — 40 ft, ~12-14 ft at hip, massive teeth, bite force 12,000+ lbs. Arms: tiny, 2 fingers? No strong use, maybe holding struggling prey? Or for standing up from sleep? Feathers: juveniles likely had downy protofeathers). Spinosaurus (largest known carnivorous dinosaur? ~50 ft, sail on back, semi-aquatic — paddle tail, dense bones, upright in water. Lived in North Africa swamps, ate fish). Velociraptor (4 ft, feathered, not "Jurassic Park" size— inaccurate: JP had Deinonychus model under wrong name). Allosaurus (Jurassic, 28 ft, apex predator before T. rex. Stegosaurus had thagomizer points on tail that could wound Allosaurus: one fossil Allosaurus has a hole from thagomizer)
  - Birds are the only surviving theropods! (feathers: originally for display/thermoregulation before flight. Flight evolution: Velociraptor relative Archaeopteryx (first known bird, 150 mya) has feathers + teeth + tail. Microraptor: four wings — feathers on all four limbs, likely glider)
- **Sauropods** (largest land animals ever): Brachiosaurus (long neck taller at shoulder than hip, front legs longer), Diplodocus (whiplash tail, horizontal neck). Argentinosaurus (heaviest: 70-100 tons, 100+ ft long). Dreadnoughtus (known from ~70% skeleton not just fragments — rare for a sauropod). Patagotitan (102 ft, 70 tons). Long necks: efficient grazing high trees + low energy cost of moving head side to side (each step = low energy). Air sacs in bones (like birds) for light weight + efficient respiration
- **Ornithischians**: Triceratops (3 horns + frill, lived alongside T. rex. Frill: defense + display+species recognition). Stegosaurus (plates on back for thermoregulation + display — highly vascularized; thagomizer: 4 tail spikes for defense). Ankylosaurus (heavy armor + club tail — 100+ lb club, could break leg bones of a T.rex). Parasaurolophus (hollow crest on head — could have been used for sounds, produced at very low frequencies: dinosaur calls). Hadrosaurs (duck-billed dinosaurs — most common herbivores of Late Cretaceous: large herds, hundreds of strong teeth in "dental batteries"). Pachycephalosaurus (dome-headed, debated head-butting vs flank-butting — dome could withstand 40mph force, as in Pachycephalosaurus+Stygimoloch? Actually, fossils of big dome+spikes: Pachycephalosaurus juveniles have horns — Pachycephalosaurus may be same species as Dracorex + Stygimoloch = different growth stages of same animal!)

### Marine Reptiles (not dinosaurs)
- **Ichthyosaurs**: dolphin-shaped, fish eaters, live birth (fossils: mother + baby). Large eyes (Ichthyosaurus ~4 inch diameter; Temnodontosaurus ~10 inch — largest eye of any known animal). Extinct before end-Cretaceous (replaced by mosasaurs?)
- **Plesiosaurs**: long neck (Elasmosaurus: 70 vertebrae in neck = 2/3 body length — flexible but slow). Four flippers (unique propulsion: both pairs used for "underwater flight"? Or "row-like" vertical stroke?). Pliosaurs (short neck, huge jaws — Kronosaurus, Liopleurodon: large marine predators, at least 25-50 ft). Plesiosaurs: possibly live birth (fossil adult + baby inside)
- **Mosasaurs**: Late Cretaceous apex marine predators (not dinosaurs — giant marine lizards, related to modern monitor lizards and snakes). Tylosaurus: up to 50 ft. Distinctive double-hinged lower jaw + second row of teeth in pterygoid area (inside mouth). Powerful tail fins. Extinct 66 mya with all other non-avian dinosaurs

### Pterosaurs (not dinosaurs, flying reptiles)
- **Quetzalcoatlus**: largest flying animal ever — 36-40 ft wingspan, 500 lbs (stood as tall as giraffe on ground — 16-18 ft). Bone structure: air-filled/wall thickness as thin as eggshell, stress lines for reinforcement. Warm-blooded (likely covered in pycnofibers — fuzzy body covering). Not flapping constantly: likely soared + maybe launched from all fours pushing off front limbs. Fed like stork: walked on ground, probed for small prey
- **Pteranodon**: 20-23 ft wingspan, distinctive crest (males large, females small). Toothless beak. Diet: fish — swoop down to water, scoop up with lower jaw. Not flying start: pterosaurs were quadrupedal launchers — jump + wing-flap. Crest size signaling for mate attraction

## Key Fossil Sites
- **Burgess Shale (Canada, 508 mya)**: Cambrian explosion fossils — soft-bodied preservation. Anomalocaris (apex predator, weird "shrimp" — first large predator: ~1 meter. Radiodont: mouth parts circle of plates, appendages with spines to grab prey). Opabinia (5 eyes, feeding proboscis — hallucigenia's spines? No, it had a "feeding claw" extending out from head). Hallucigenia (walking spines on top? Actually: dorsal spines up, legs down). Wiwaxia: scaly slug-like organism with spines. Chordates: Pikaia — early similar to primitive vertebrates (has notochord, precursor to backbone). Exceptional preservation: fine-grained mud, anoxic bottom, rapid burial — keeps soft body impression
- **Solnhofen (Germany, 150 mya)**: Archaeopteryx (first known feathered dinosaur bird — fossil skeletons with feather impressions). Tithonian: lithographic limestone, extremely fine-grained: 8 birds? 12? actually 12 specimens, feathered wings + skeleton with claws. Also: pterosaurs (Rhamphorhynchus, Pterodactylus — long+short tails), small dinosaurs (Compsognathus — ~3ft, theropod), jellyfish, dragonflies, early mammal with hair
- **Hell Creek (Montana, 66 mya)**: end-Cretaceous ecosystem. T. rex, Triceratops, Edmontosaurus, Ankylosaurus, Pachycephalosaurus, etc. Covers KT extinction layer: iridium spike (from Chicxulub impactor). Types of fossils: teeth, bones, eggshells, coprolites (dinosaur poop with fish scales). Last 2 million years of dinosaurs. Above the layer: few dinosaurs — sudden disappearance
- **Dinosaur Provincial Park (Alberta, Canada, 75 mya)**: one of richest dinosaur fossil sites in the world (Late Cretaceous). Over 150 complete skeletons from 35 species. Horned dinosaurs: Centrosaurus (bonebeds: mass death, hundreds), Styracosaurus. Duckbills: Corythosaurus, Lambeosaurus. Tyrannosaurs: Daspletosaurus, Gorgosaurus. Albertosaurus (smaller fast tyrannosaur — pack hunter? Bonebed: 13+ individuals at one site = evidence of pack behavior)

## Mass Extinctions
- **The Big Five**:
  1. **End-Ordovician (443 mya)**: 85% species, two pulses (glaciation + sea level drop then rise). Marine extinction (most life still in ocean). Brachiopods, trilobites, conodonts hit hardest
  2. **Late Devonian (372 mya)**: 75% species, prolonged episode (multiple events over 25 my). Ocean anoxia (dead zones). Reef ecosystems devastated. Trilobites, ammonites, jawless fish decline. What caused? Global cooling + plant colonization increased nutrient runoff + ocean anoxia
  3. **End-Permian "Great Dying" (252 mya)**: Earth's worst extinction — 96% of marine species, 70% of terrestrial vertebrates. Siberian Traps volcanism (1-4 million sq km of lava across Siberia — lasted 1-2 million years). CO₂ + methane released → extreme global warming (10-15°C?), ocean acidification, anoxia. It took 10+ million years for recovery. No reefs for most of Triassic recovery (no frame-building corals until Middle Triassic, 8 million years after extinction)
  4. **End-Triassic (201 mya)**: 80% species. Mass volcanism (Central Atlantic Magmatic Province) as Pangea split → CO₂, warming. Dinosaurs survive + become dominant in Jurassic (they had already diversified before extinction, but extinction removed many non-dinosaurian archosaur competitors like crurotarsans)
  5. **End-Cretaceous "KT Extinction" (66 mya)**: 75% species (all non-avian dinosaurs, pterosaurs, mosasaurs, plesiosaurs, ammonites). Asteroid impact (Chicxulub, Yucatan, 6-10 mile diameter, released energy equivalent to 100 teratons TNT. Crater: 112 miles diameter, 12 miles deep. Immediate: fireball, earthquakes, tsunami. Soot + dust blocked sun → "impact winter" — photosynthesis stopped for years. Acid rain from sulfur + nitrogen oxides. Survivors: small, burrowing, generalist, or aquatic (mammals, birds, crocodiles, turtles. All were small — largest survivors chicken-sized). The survivors were less than 25 kg

## Human Evolution (Detailed Timeline)
- **Sahelanthropus tchadensis** (7 mya, Chad): oldest known hominin. Brain ~350cc (chimp-sized). Small canine. Foramen magnum position suggests bipedalism? (skull found in 2001 by Michel Brunet, from Sahel — only one skull known, may be female, possibly more ape-like face; bipedalism debated)
- **Orrorin tugenensis** (6 mya, Kenya): femur indicates bipedalism (femoral head like biped, unlike apes). Brain: still chimp-like. Teeth: small canines (key for human status — indicates reduction in canine size). Strong climber with ape-like finger bones
- **Ardipithecus ramidus** (4.4 mya, Ethiopia): "Ardi" ~50% skeleton, 4' tall, brain 300-350cc. Bipedal (pelvis + foot) but also climbed trees (opposable big toe, long arms, curved fingers. Adapted for both walking upright + tree climbing). Hand: not knuckle-walking like chimps/gorillas — suggests human+chimp ancestor was not knuckle-walking
- **Australopithecus afarensis** (3.9-2.9 mya): "Lucy" (1974, 40% skeleton, Ethiopia). 3'6" tall, brain 375-500cc. Fully bipedal (knee + pelvis adapted, Laetoli footprints 3.6 mya: three individuals walking through volcanic ash, big toe aligned). Sexual dimorphism (males ~100lb, females ~60lb). Still climbed (curved finger bones, ape-like shoulders)
- **Paranthropus** (2.7-1.0 mya): "robust australopith" — huge molars + sagittal crest (chewing muscles anchor on top of skull). Specialized for tough foods (seeds, roots, nuts). NOT human ancestor — side branch (extinct ~1 mya). Three species: P. aethiopicus, P. boisei ("Nutcracker Man" — most robust), P. robustus (South Africa)
- **Homo habilis** (2.4-1.4 mya): first Homo — brain 500-800cc, larger brain, smaller teeth. First stone tools (Oldowan: simple flakes + cores — sharp edges for cutting meat, pounding bone — cut marks on animal bones alongside tools evidence of early butchery). "Handy man" — named for association with tools. Height: 3'5-4'5", weighed ~70lb. Long arms still suggest climbing ability
- **Homo erectus** (1.9 mya - 108,000 ya? ~1.8 mya Java, maybe to 108,000 ya in Java): first to leave Africa (Dmanisi, Georgia: 1.77 mya). Brain 600-1,100cc. Modern body proportions (long legs, short arms). Made Acheulean handaxes (symmetrical, teardrop shape, standard design over 1.5 million years — oldest known human "technology standard"). First to use fire (1.5 mya-800,000 ya?). Height 5'10", very active (thick bones, long legs). Possible language? (Broca's area, hyoid bone, but not advanced speech). Could be: protolanguage. Survived until 108,000+ years ago in Indonesia (Ngandong, Java)
- **Homo heidelbergensis** (700,000-300,000 ya): common ancestor of Neanderthals + H. sapiens? Also possibly: Denisovans? Height 5'9-6', brain 1,100-1,400cc. Hunted large game (Schöningen spears: 400,000 ya, 7 ft wooden throwing spears, sharpened tips, designed for thrusting? Actually designed for throwing — earliest clear hunting weapons). Shelter building (Terra Amata, 380,000 ya — structure with post holes). Stone tools: Mode 3/Levallois technique — prepared core, detached predetermined flake shapes. Social: division of labor, caring for elderly (Atapuerca, Spain: 430,000 ya — evidence of healing from severe trauma, care for disabled)
- **Neanderthals** (400,000-40,000 ya): Europe + Asia. Brain 1,200-1,900cc (larger than H. sapiens! Relative to body: similar). Stocky, barrel-chested (cold adaptation, higher muscle mass). Tools: Mousterian (Levallois technique, varied specialized tools). Buried dead (La Chapelle-aux-Saints, Shanidar: burial with flowers, pollen on Shanidar IV grave — evidence of ritual: 60,000 ya). Abstract art (cave art: Spanish caves, possible Neanderthal origin — red pigment dating disputes. Gibraltar cave carving 37,000 ya — crosshatch pattern on rock). Red ochre use. Boats? Possibly. Speech? (hyoid bone, FOXP2 same as humans). Extinct ~40,000 years ago (Homo sapiens arrived ~45,000 ya, overlap 5,000 years. Contributing factor: competition, climate change, interbreeding, possibly lower fertility rates, possibly smaller population sizes). Interbred: 1-4% Neanderthal DNA in non-Africans (Europeans 1-2%, East Asians 2-3%, higher in some). Some Neanderthal genes advantageous (immunity genes, hair/skin adaptation to cold). Some linked to modern diseases: depression, blood clotting, smoking behavior, susceptibility to COVID-19 severe illness (Neanderthal region on chr 3)
- **Denisovans** (300,000-30,000 ya?): known only from DNA + a few fossils (finger bone + tooth + modified jawbone from Baishiya Karst Cave, Tibetan Plateau). 3-5% DNA in Melanesians, Aboriginal Australians, Philippine Negritos (Aeta). High-altitude adaptation gene (EPAS1) from Denisovans → Tibetans adapted to high altitude (shared DNA with Denisovans from extinct population). Range: Siberia, East Asia, possibly SE Asia
