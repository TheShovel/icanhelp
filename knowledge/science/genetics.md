# Genetics & DNA

## DNA Structure
- **Double helix**: two strands of nucleotides, antiparallel (5'→3' and 3'→5'), connected by hydrogen bonds
- **Nucleotides**: A (adenine) pairs with T (thymine) — 2 bonds. G (guanine) pairs with C (cytosine) — 3 bonds
  - AT/GC ratio varies between species — GC base pairs are stronger (3 bonds), more stable
- **Base pairs**: human genome = ~3 billion base pairs, ~20,000-25,000 genes
  - Only ~2% of DNA codes for proteins — the rest is regulatory, structural, "junk" (though much has function we're still discovering)
- **Chromatin**: DNA wrapped around histone proteins (like thread around spools) — organized into chromosomes
  - Histones can be chemically modified (acetylation, methylation) to turn genes on/off — this is epigenetics
  - Euchromatin: loosely packed (accessible, active). Heterochromatin: tightly packed (silenced)
- **Telomeres**: protective caps at ends of chromosomes — shorten with each cell division
  - Telomere length = biological aging marker (not perfect but correlated)
  - Telomerase: enzyme that rebuilds telomeres — active in stem cells and cancer cells (keeps dividing), inactive in most adult cells

## Gene Expression (Central Dogma)
1. **DNA → RNA** (transcription): gene's DNA is copied into pre-mRNA in nucleus
   - Promoter region (upstream of gene) controls when and how much a gene is transcribed
   - Transcription factors: proteins that bind to promoter and regulate transcription (turn genes on/off)
2. **RNA processing**: introns (non-coding) spliced out, exons (coding) joined — alternative splicing generates multiple proteins from one gene
   - ~95% of human genes undergo alternative splicing — one gene can code for many proteins
   - mRNA gets a 5' cap and 3' poly-A tail (stability, export from nucleus)
3. **RNA → Protein** (translation): ribosome reads mRNA codons (3-base segments), tRNA brings corresponding amino acids, builds protein chain
   - Start codon: AUG (Methionine). Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA
   - Genetic code is nearly universal (all life uses same codon→amino acid mapping) — strong evidence of common ancestry

## Inheritance Patterns
- **Mendelian**: dominant (needs only one copy to express) vs recessive (needs two copies)
  - Autosomal dominant: Huntington's disease, Marfan syndrome — 50% chance to inherit from affected parent
  - Autosomal recessive: cystic fibrosis, sickle cell disease, Tay-Sachs — 25% chance if both parents are carriers. Carrier frequency is high for some conditions (1 in 25 Caucasians carry CF mutation)
  - X-linked recessive: hemophilia, color blindness, Duchenne muscular dystrophy — mostly affects males (one X), females are carriers
- **Non-Mendelian**:
  - Incomplete dominance: red + white = pink flowers (blending)
  - Codominance: both traits expressed (AB blood type — both A and B antigens)
  - Epistasis: one gene masks another (baldness, coat color in animals)
  - Polygenic: many genes contribute (height, skin color, intelligence, most complex traits)
  - Mitochondrial inheritance: only from mother (mitochondria have their own DNA)
- **Heritability**: percentage of variation in a trait due to genetic differences
  - Height: ~80% heritable. IQ: ~50-80% (varies by age, environment). Personality traits (Big Five): ~40-60%. Political views: ~30-40%

## Mutations
- Types: point mutation (single base change: silent, missense = different amino acid, nonsense = stop codon)
  - Insertion/deletion (indel): can cause frameshift (changes all downstream codons) — often more damaging
  - Copy number variation (CNV): gene duplicated or deleted — surprisingly common (everyone has some)
  - Chromosomal: large structural changes (translocation, inversion — Down syndrome: extra chromosome 21)
- **Causes**: spontaneous (replication errors), radiation (UV → thymine dimers, X-rays → breaks), chemicals (smoking, asbestos, some food compounds), viruses (HPV causes cervical cancer)
- **DNA repair**: cells have multiple repair mechanisms (proofreading during replication, mismatch repair, excision repair for UV damage, double-strand break repair)
  - Cancer can result when repair systems fail — mutations accumulate (typically need 5-10 driver mutations)
- **Mutation rate**: ~1-2 × 10⁻⁸ per base pair per generation — each human inherits ~30-60 new mutations not present in parents
  - Most mutations are neutral, some harmful, rare beneficial (evolution's raw material)

## Genomics & Technology
- **PCR** (Polymerase Chain Reaction): amplify specific DNA sequence — essential for COVID testing, forensics, research
  - Heat to denature (94°C) → cool for primer binding (55-65°C) → heat for extension (72°C) — repeat 30-40 cycles, amplifies target exponentially
- **CRISPR-Cas9**: gene editing tool — Cas9 enzyme cuts DNA at specific location guided by RNA, cell repairs cut (can disable gene or insert new sequence)
  - Potential: cure genetic diseases (sickle cell, cystic fibrosis), improved crops, eliminate malaria mosquitoes
  - Ethical concerns: germline editing (changes passed to children — heritable), off-target effects, designer babies
  - First CRISPR-based therapy approved (2023 UK, 2024 US) for sickle cell disease and beta-thalassemia
- **DNA sequencing**: determine exact order of nucleotides
  - Sanger sequencing (gold standard, short reads, accurate). Next-gen (NGS): massively parallel, cheaper, whole genomes
  - Human Genome Project (1990-2003): cost $3 billion. Today: whole genome ~$600-1000, soon $100
- **Genome-wide association studies** (GWAS): scan genomes of thousands of people to find variants associated with traits/diseases
  - Has found thousands of genetic variants linked to diseases, but most have very small effects
  - Polygenic risk scores: combine many small-effect variants to predict disease risk (still limited utility)
- **Direct-to-consumer genetics**: 23andMe, Ancestry.com — test specific SNPs, provide ancestry and health risk information
  - Limitations: tests only known variants (misses rare or unknown ones), false reassurance possible, privacy concerns

## Epigenetics
- **Changes in gene expression WITHOUT changing DNA sequence**
  - DNA methylation: adding methyl group to cytosine (usually silences gene)
  - Histone modification: acetylation (activates), methylation (activates or silences depending)
  - Non-coding RNA: RNA molecules that regulate gene expression (microRNA, lncRNA)
- **Influenced by**: diet, stress, exercise, smoking, pollution, social environment, aging
  - Dutch Hunger Winter (1944-1945): famine during pregnancy → children had higher rates of obesity, schizophrenia, heart disease — epigenetic changes persisted for generations
- **Transgenerational**: some epigenetic marks can be inherited by offspring (controversial how much)
- **Cancer epigenetics**: many cancers have abnormal methylation patterns (tumor suppressor genes silenced)
- **Epigenetic therapy**: drugs that reverse abnormal methylation/histone modifications (some approved for cancer)

## Evolutionary Genetics
- **Comparison with other species**: humans share
  - ~98.8% of DNA with chimpanzees
  - ~85% with mice
  - ~60% with fruit flies
  - ~50% with bananas
  - ~30% with yeast
- **Human evolution**: ~6 million years since last common ancestor with chimpanzees
  - Neanderthal DNA: 1-4% in non-African populations (interbreeding ~50,000 years ago)
  - Denisovan DNA: found in Melanesian and Aboriginal Australian populations
  - Some borrowed genes were adaptive: Neanderthal genes involved in immunity, skin/hair adaptation to cold, high-altitude adaptation in Tibetans (Denisovan EPAS1 gene)
- **Natural selection at work**: lactase persistence (ability to digest milk in adulthood, evolved independently in Europe and Africa ~7,000 years ago), sickle cell trait (protects against malaria), skin pigmentation (vitamin D synthesis vs UV protection), high-altitude adaptation in Tibetans and Andeans
- **Genetic drift vs selection**: most genetic differences between populations are neutral (genetic drift, not adaptation) — "race" is a social construct, not biological (more genetic variation within any "race" than between them — 85% of human genetic variation is within populations)
