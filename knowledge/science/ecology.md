# Ecology & Environmental Science

## Ecosystems
- **Ecosystem**: community of organisms + their physical environment (soil, water, air, climate) — functions as a unit
  - Biotic (living): producers (plants), consumers (herbivores, carnivores, omnivores, decomposers)
  - Abiotic (non-living): sunlight, water, temperature, soil, nutrients, pH
- **Energy flow**: sun → producers (photosynthesis) → primary consumers (herbivores) → secondary (carnivores) → tertiary (top predators) → decomposers
  - Only ~10% of energy transfers to next trophic level (rest lost as heat/metabolism)
  - This is why top predators are rare — it takes 10,000 kg of plants to make 1 kg of top predator
- **Biogeochemical cycles**: carbon (CO₂ → organic → CO₂), nitrogen (N₂ → NH₃ → NO₃⁻ → N₂), phosphorus (no gas phase, from rock), water (evapo-transpiration → precipitation → runoff)
  - Humans have massively altered these cycles (carbon: burning fossil fuels + deforestation, nitrogen: fertilizer production exceeds natural fixation)

## Biodiversity
- **Three types**: genetic diversity (within species), species diversity (between species), ecosystem diversity (between habitats)
  - All three are crucial for ecosystem resilience (ability to recover from disturbance)
- **Hotspots**: 36 global biodiversity hotspots (defined as 1,500+ endemic plant species, 30%+ habitat lost) — cover 2.4% of land but contain 50% of plant species and 42% of vertebrate species
  - Examples: Madagascar, Andes Tropical, Mediterranean Basin, Indo-Burma, Caribbean Islands, California Floristic Province, Cape Floristic Region (South Africa)
- **Keystone species**: species with disproportionately large effect on ecosystem — removing them causes collapse
  - Examples: sea otters (control urchins → protect kelp forests), wolves (control elk → forest regeneration), beavers (create wetlands), elephants (maintain savanna), bees (pollination), starfish (intertidal predators)
- **Ecosystem services**: benefits humans get from ecosystems ($125-145 trillion/year globally)
  - Provisioning: food, water, timber, medicine, fuel
  - Regulating: climate (carbon sequestration), water purification, pollination, flood control, disease regulation
  - Supporting: nutrient cycling, soil formation, photosynthesis
  - Cultural: recreation, spiritual, aesthetic, education

## Biomes & Their Ecology
- **Tropical rainforest**: warm/wet year-round, most productive and diverse biome — nutrient cycling is fast (nutrients in biomass, not soil)
  - Deforestation rates: ~10M hectares/year (size of Iceland), mainly for cattle/palm/soy
  - Carbon: clearing rainforest releases stored carbon (plus eliminates future carbon capture)
- **Coral reefs**: "rainforests of the sea" — 25% of marine species on 0.1% of ocean area
  - Threats: warming (bleaching — corals expel symbiotic algae when stressed), acidification (slows growth), pollution, overfishing
  - 50% of coral reefs lost since 1950, most of remainder at risk by 2050
- **Wetlands**: marshes, swamps, bogs — filter water, flood control, carbon storage (peatlands store twice as much carbon as all forests combined!)
  - Destroyed: 50%+ of world's wetlands lost in last century (drained for agriculture/development)
- **Tundra**: permafrost (permanently frozen ground below surface) — stores massive carbon (twice as much as atmosphere)
  - Thawing permafrost releases methane (CH₄), 80x more potent than CO₂ as greenhouse gas over 20 years — warming feedback loop

## Pollution
- **Air pollution**: 7M+ premature deaths/year globally — #1 environmental health risk
  - PM2.5 (particulate matter <2.5 microns): penetrates deep into lungs → enters bloodstream → heart attacks, strokes, lung cancer, respiratory disease
  - Sources: vehicle exhaust, coal power plants, industrial emissions, wood burning, agriculture (ammonia), dust
  - Indoor air pollution: cooking with solid fuels (wood/charcoal) — 2.5M deaths/year, mostly women/children in developing countries
- **Plastic pollution**: 400M+ tons produced/year, 40% single-use, <10% recycled
  - 11M tons enter ocean yearly — if trends continue, more plastic than fish in ocean by weight by 2050
  - Microplastics: found everywhere — Arctic ice, deep sea, human placenta/lungs/blood/testes — health effects being studied (inflammatory, endocrine disruption, cytotoxicity)
  - Most ocean plastic comes from 10 rivers (8 in Asia, 2 in Africa — inefficient waste management in rapidly industrializing regions)
- **Light pollution**: disrupts circadian rhythms (human health), migration/navigation (birds, sea turtles hitting buildings/disoriented), night-blooming plants
- **Noise pollution**: chronic stress in humans and animals, disrupts predator/prey dynamics (bats can't hear insects, owls can't hear mice), interferes with marine mammal communication (sonar harmful to whales)

## Conservation Strategies
- **Protected areas**: national parks, nature reserves, marine protected areas — currently 17% of land, 8% of ocean (target: 30% by 2030 — "30×30" goal)
  - Effectiveness varies by management quality, funding, enforcement
- **Rewilding**: restore ecosystems by reintroducing keystone species and letting natural processes resume
  - Successes: wolves in Yellowstone (rebalanced elk, regenerated forests, increased biodiversity), beaver reintroductions
  - Pleistocene rewilding: controversial idea to reintroduce analogs of extinct megafauna (elephants to North America as mammoth proxy)
- **Corridors**: connect isolated habitat patches — allow migration, gene flow, range shifts with climate change
- **Ex-situ conservation**: zoos, botanical gardens, seed banks (Svalbard Global Seed Vault — 1M+ seed varieties, Arctic permafrost ensures cold storage)
  - Captive breeding programs: California condor (from 22 to 500+), black-footed ferret (from 18 to 1000+)
- **Community-based conservation**: local communities manage and benefit from conservation
  - More effective than "fences and fines" approach (fortress conservation) — when locals benefit from wildlife (tourism, sustainable harvest), they protect it
- **Restoration ecology**: actively restore degraded ecosystems — tree planting (right species, right location), wetland restoration, coral gardening, mine reclamation
  - Tree planting: native species, diverse, in deforested areas (NOT planting trees in grasslands/savannas — different ecosystem). "Tree planting" ≠ "carbon offset" — young trees take 20+ years to absorb meaningful carbon

## Sustainability
- **Planetary boundaries**: 9 boundaries within which humanity can safely operate (climate change, biodiversity loss, nitrogen/phosphorus cycles, land use, freshwater, ocean acidification, ozone depletion, aerosol loading, chemical pollution)
  - 6 of 9 boundaries already crossed (climate, biodiversity, land, nitrogen/phosphorus, freshwater, chemical pollution)
- **Carrying capacity**: Earth's maximum sustainable human population — estimates range 8-16 billion (currently 8.1B)
  - Ecological footprint: if everyone lived like average American, we'd need 5 Earths. Like average Indian: 0.7 Earths
  - Math: Earth = 1.6 global hectares/person. US uses 8.0 gha/person. World average: 2.7 gha/person
- **Circular economy**: eliminate waste and pollution, keep products/materials in use, regenerate natural systems — vs linear "take → make → dispose" economy
  - Cradle-to-cradle: products designed to be fully recyclable or biodegradable from start
  - Planned obsolescence: designing products with limited lifespan → wastes resources (some jurisdictions requiring "right to repair" laws)
- **Degrowth**: intentional reduction of economic production/consumption in wealthy countries to achieve sustainability and well-being (controversial — challenges core assumptions of economic growth)
  - Alternative: "green growth" (decouple economic growth from resource use/emissions through technology — so far, relative decoupling occurring but not absolute decoupling)
