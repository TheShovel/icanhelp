# Geology: Rocks, Minerals & Resources

## Mineral Classification

### Silicates (90% of Earth's crust)
- **Feldspar** (60% of crust): most abundant mineral group. Orthoclase (pink, potassium feldspar). Plagioclase (white-gray, sodium/calcium feldspar). Albite NaAlSi₃O₈ to Anorthite CaAl₂Si₂O₈. Most common in igneous rocks (granite = quartz + feldspar + mica)
- **Quartz** (SiO₂): 2nd most abundant (~12%). 6-sided crystals, conchoidal fracture, glassy luster, piezoelectric (charges under pressure, scratches glass). Varieties: amethyst (purple, Fe impurities, heat treated to make citrine). Citrine (yellow, heat-treated amethyst or natural). Rose quartz (pink, Ti/Fe impurities). Smoky quartz (brown, natural irradiation). Agate (banded microcrystalline). Jasper (opaque, microcrystalline, colored). Chert/flint (microcrystalline SiO₂). Milky quartz (white, fluid inclusions). Rock crystal (clear, colorless)
- **Mica**: biotite (black, iron-rich, dark mica). Muscovite (colorless/white mica, light mica, transparent sheets). Perfect basal cleavage — splits into thin flexible sheets (used for windows in medieval times before glass)
- **Pyroxene**: augite (most common, dark green/black). Found in basalt, gabbro. Single-chain silicate (inosilicate)
- **Amphibole**: hornblende (dark green/black, good cleavages at 60°/120° vs pyroxene's near-90° cleavage). Double-chain silicate. Found in diorite, andesite, amphibolite
- **Olivine** (Mg,Fe)₂SiO₄: green, glassy, in basalt + peridotite. Gem: peridot. High pressure form: wadsleyite, ringwoodite (found deep in mantle transition zone 410-660 km)
- **Garnet**: deep red/brown, cubic crystals, used as abrasive (garnet sandpaper) + gem (almandine, pyrope). Common in metamorphic rocks (schist, gneiss)

### Carbonates
- **Calcite** (CaCO₃): fizzes with dilute HCl (strong acid reaction). Main component of limestone + marble + chalk. Double refraction: clear calcite splits light. Used as building stone, cement (Portland cement = limestone + clay heated to 1450°C, then ground with gypsum)
- **Dolomite** (CaMg(CO₃)₂): fizzes weakly with acid only when powdered. Forms dolostone rock. Similar to limestone
- **Aragonite**: same chemistry as calcite, different crystal structure (orthorhombic). Formed by marine organisms (coral, mollusk shells). Unstable at surface — converts to calcite over millions of years

### Oxides
- **Hematite** (Fe₂O₃): red, most important iron ore. Streak: red-brown. Rust = hematite. Banded iron formations (BIFs) = ancient ocean deposits (2.5-1.8 billion years old, most iron ore mined today)
- **Magnetite** (Fe₃O₄): magnetic (lodestone), black. Important iron ore. Ferrimagnetic, direction points to earth's magnetic pole at time of formation — used for paleomagnetic dating
- **Corundum** (Al₂O₃): extremely hard (9 on Mohs scale). Ruby (red, containing Cr). Sapphire (blue, Fe+Ti). Industrial uses: abrasives, watch bearings, sandpaper, laser components (synthetic corundum)
- **Bauxite** (Al(OH)₃): not a single mineral — mix of gibbsite, boehmite, diaspore. Ore of aluminum (primary ore). Formed by weathering of aluminum-rich rocks in tropical climates

### Sulfides
- **Pyrite** (FeS₂): "fool's gold" — brass-yellow, cubic crystals, sparkle. Common in hydrothermal veins, sedimentary pyrite nodules. Oxidizes to acid mine drainage (sulfuric acid + iron hydroxide = yellow/orange acidic stream water — environmental disaster in many mining regions). When struck: spark / sulfur smell
- **Galena** (PbS): lead ore, heavy, cubic cleavage (cubic crystal + perfect cubic cleavage). Often contains silver (as inclusion). Largest lead ore
- **Sphalerite** (ZnS): zinc ore, brown/black, resinous luster, cubic cleavage. Often with galena. Cd + Ge + In trace elements (minor metals sourced from it)
- **Chalcopyrite** (CuFeS₂): copper ore, brass-yellow (often tarnished green/iridescent). Most important copper ore mineral. Harder than gold
- **Cinnabar** (HgS): red, highly toxic (Hg). Ore of mercury. Mined for elemental mercury. Red pigment (vermilion) used in Renaissance art, Chinese lacquerware. Toxic — absorbs through skin

## Economic Geology

### Ore Deposits
- **Magmatic**: crystallized from magma — chromite, platinum, nickel. Bushveld Complex (South Africa): world's largest PGE (platinum group elements) deposit + chromite. Layered ultramafic intrusion
- **Hydrothermal**: hot fluids deposit minerals in fractures — gold veins, copper porphyries (largest source of copper: low-grade (0.3-1.5% Cu) porphyry deposits in subduction zones — Andes, SW US, Chile. Bingham Canyon, Chuquicamata. Gold: Carlin-type (Nevada, fine-grained gold disseminated in carbonate rocks — world's largest gold-producing region). Quartz veins with native gold, crushed, cyanide extraction. Epithermal gold: shallow <1km, boiling fluids, contain Au with silver + base metals
- **Sedimentary**: placer (panning for gold in river gravel — gold is very dense, gathers in stream beds: Sn, Ti, diamonds, gold, platinum). Banded iron formations (BIFs, 2.5-2.0 bya). Evaporites (salt, gypsum, potash from dried-up seas)
- **Residual**: bauxite (Al from tropical weathering). Laterite (Ni laterite — weathe olivine/ultramafic rock in tropical climate, Ni concentrated). Kaolin (clay from feldspar weathering). Supergene enrichment: groundwater percolates, concentrates copper/gold/silver at water table (secondary enrichment blanket, makes low-grade deposit minable)

### Mining Methods
- **Surface**: open pit (like Bingham Canyon — 0.75 mile deep, 2.5 miles across. Largest human-made excavation). Strip mining (coal, phosphate). Mountaintop removal (Appalachian coal — removes entire mountain top, valley fill = streams and biodiversity lost. Extremely environmental damage, energy intensive)
- **Underground**: room and pillar (coal, salt, uranium — leave support pillars). Longwall (coal — more efficient, ~80% of coal seam recovered). Block caving (large low-grade copper deposits: deepest underground mine in world, South Africa's Mponeng gold mine 2.5 miles deep — hot, dangerous, costly ventilation). Cut-and-fill (fill mined areas with tailings to support roof)
- **In-situ leaching**: pump solution into ground to dissolve minerals, collect via wells (Uranium ISL: CO₂ + O₂ + water oxidized and dissolves U → well. Gold cyanide heap leaching: spray crushed rock with cyanide, leach gold out, collect pregnant solution). Less surface disturbance, but groundwater contamination risk. In-situ uranium mining is common; used for 45% of world's uranium

### Fossil Fuels
- **Coal**: peat → lignite (brown, low heat, 25-35% carbon) → bituminous (soft, high heat, most common, 45-86% C) → anthracite (hard, clean burning, highest heat, 86-97% C). Formed in swamp environments, Carboniferous period (360-300 mya). Coal types: rank = heat + pressure over time. US: largest coal reserves, but coal production declining (transition to natural gas + renewables). Clean coal: scrubbers (SO₂ removal), CO₂ capture (expensive, not commercially widespread). Coal ash: contains arsenic, mercury, lead — environmental hazard (disposal in ponds, failures like Kingston 2008 spill)
- **Oil & natural gas**: formed from marine organic matter (plankton, algae) buried + heated (50-150°C oil window/ thermogenic — peak generation at 90-120°C). Kerogen: organic matter partially broken down by heat in source rock. Types of kerogen: Type I (algal, oil-prone; II (marine plankton, oil- and gas-prone; III (woody, gas-prone; IV (reworked inertinite, little potential). Source rock (organic-rich shale → oil/gas generated). Reservoir rock (porous sandstone/carbonate → oil/gas accumulates). Trap (structural/stratigraphic prevents escape). Seal (impermeable cap rock: shale, salt, anhydrite — prevents upward migration). Oil & gas accumulate in structural (fault, anticline) and stratigraphic (pinch-out, unconformity) traps. Conventional: pool of oil/gas that flows easily. Unconventional: oil/gas trapped in tight rocks (shale, tight sandstone, coalbed methane — need fracking). Shale gas revolution: horizontal drilling + hydraulic fracturing (fracking) cracked US gas production — from shortage (1990s) to world's largest producer. Hydraulic fracturing: high-pressure water + sand + chemicals (pump into wellbore at 1000s of PSI to fracture shale, sand holds fractures open, gas/oil flows out). Concerns: groundwater contamination (methane migration in cement sheaths, not fracking fluids migrating up from depth), induced earthquakes (wastewater injection into deep wells ~M3-4 max, not fractures themselves), water use (2-5 million gallons per well — significant in arid regions). Solutions: recycle flowback water. Natural gas: methane CH₄, burns cleaner than coal (50% less CO₂, less SO₃/NOₓ). But methane leaks = potent GHG (20x CO₂ over 100 years? 85x over 20 years). Methane leaks during extraction + transport (lost to atmosphere) — need to minimize

## Gemstones
- **Precious**: diamond (C, hardest 10 Mohs, pure C. Four Cs: cut (determines sparkle), color (D colorless to Z yellow), clarity (inclusions), carat weight (1 carat = 0.2g). Most diamonds = artificial (synthetic diamonds for industrial use: hard, heat conductive — used for abrasives, cutters, heat sinks, semiconductors, drill bits, diamond windows). Synthetic gem-quality diamonds increasingly available, lab grown: cheaper, conflict-free, chemically identical. Ruby (corundum, red = Cr). Sapphire (corundum, blue = Fe+Ti, any color not red — pink = Cr, yellow = Fe). Emerald (beryl, green = Cr/V). Also: alexandrite (chrysoberyl, changes color: green in daylight, red in incandescent). Pearl (organic, calcium carbonate from mollusks)
- **Semi-precious**: amethyst (quartz, purple). Citrine (quartz, yellow). Topaz (yellow/blue/pink, 8 Mohs, piezoelectric). Opal (SiO₂·nH₂O, precious: play of color from silica spheres diffracting white light). Turquoise (blue-green, Cu/Al phosphate, porous, often treated). Lapis lazuli (deep blue, metamorphic rock — lazurite: used for ultramarine pigment, ancient Egyptians + Renaissance). Garnet (deep red, many colors). Peridot (olivine, green). Jade (nephrite: tough, creamy/ green. Jadeite: precious green jadeite = emerald green — more intense, rare). Tanzanite (blue/violet zoisite, found only in Tanzania — at base of Mt. Kilimanjaro. Rare, supply depleting predicted. Heated to produce blue/violet color). Tourmaline (many colors — watermelon pink+green)
