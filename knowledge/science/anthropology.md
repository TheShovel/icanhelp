# Anthropology & Human Origins

## What is Anthropology
- The study of humans, past and present — "what it means to be human"
- **Four fields** (US tradition):
  1. **Cultural anthropology**: living cultures, customs, beliefs, social organization — ethnography (participant observation, field work)
  2. **Archaeology**: past human societies through material remains (artifacts, structures, bones, trash)
  3. **Biological/physical anthropology**: human evolution, primatology, genetics, forensic anthropology
  4. **Linguistic anthropology**: language, communication, how language shapes thought and culture

## Human Evolution Timeline
- **~7-6 million years ago**: last common ancestor of humans and chimpanzees (Sahelanthropus tchadensis)
  - Bipedalism is the defining human trait — evolved BEFORE large brains
  - Why bipedal? Theories: free hands (carry food/tools), efficient walking (savanna), thermoregulation (less sun exposure), display
- **~4.4 mya**: Ardipithecus ramidus ("Ardi") — bipedal but also climbed trees, small brain (~300cc)
- **~3.2 mya**: Australopithecus afarensis ("Lucy") — fully bipedal, 3'6" tall, brain ~400cc (ape-like), lived 3.9-2.9 mya in Africa
- **~2.5 mya**: Homo habilis ("handy man") — first Homo species, made stone tools (Oldowan), brain ~600cc
  - Oldowan: simple stone flakes for cutting meat/scraping — earliest known stone tool industry
- **~1.8 mya**: Homo erectus — first to leave Africa, used fire, more complex tools (Acheulean handaxes), brain ~900cc
  - Survived until ~100,000 years ago! Most successful human species by duration
  - First to have body proportions similar to modern humans (long legs, shorter arms)
- **~400,000-300,000 years ago**: Homo sapiens appears in Africa (Jebel Irhoud, Morocco ~315,000 years ago)
  - Brain size: ~1300-1500cc (slightly larger on average than modern humans, but we've shrunk ~10% in last 20,000 years)
  - Key adaptations: complex language, symbolic thought (art, burial, jewelry), cooperation at scale
- **~100,000 years ago**: Homo sapiens leaves Africa (multiple waves). Meets Neanderthals in Europe/Asia, Denisovans in Asia
  - Interbreeding happened: 1-4% Neanderthal DNA in non-Africans, 3-5% Denisovan in Melanesians/Aboriginal Australians
- **~70,000-50,000 years ago**: "Great Leap Forward" — explosion of symbolic behavior (cave art, beads, complex tools, long-distance trade, seafaring)
  - Possibly linked to development of fully modern language, population growth, or cognitive changes
- **~40,000 years ago**: Homo sapiens reaches Europe. Neanderthals go extinct (~40,000 years ago) — likely outcompeted, interbred, and were absorbed
- **~15,000 years ago**: humans reach Americas (Bering land bridge from Asia, then along coast or through ice-free corridor)
- **~12,000 years ago**: Agricultural Revolution — domestication of plants and animals, settlements, private property, inequality, civilization

## Key Human Adaptations
- **Bipedalism**: freed hands, efficient long-distance walking (persistence hunting: humans can outrun horses over long distances via sweating)
- **Large brain**: 3x expected size for body — expensive (20% of energy at 2% of body weight)
  - Trade-off: bigger brain → narrower birth canal → more painful childbirth → born less developed (altricial) → longer childhood need care → social bonds
- **Language**: vocal tract evolved for speech (lowered larynx), Broca's and Wernicke's areas for language processing
  - Language allows cooperation at unprecedented scale (groups of 100+ instead of ~50 like other primates)
  - FOXP2 gene: important for speech and language (not the only one, but mutation in humans)
- **Cooperation**: humans cooperate with unrelated strangers (unique among animals) — basis of society
  - Evolved through: shared intentionality, social norms, punishment of free-riders, language (gossip as social bonding)
  - Dunbar's number: ~150 = maximum stable social relationships humans can maintain (~5 close friends, ~15 good friends, ~50 friends, ~150 acquaintances)
- **Culture**: cumulative knowledge passed between generations (not just genes) — allows rapid adaptation

## Archaeology
- **Dating methods**: radiocarbon (up to ~50,000 years, organic materials), potassium-argon (volcanic rock, millions of years), dendrochronology (tree rings), thermoluminescence (pottery, burned flint), optically stimulated luminescence (sediment)
- **Key sites**: Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania — early human fossils), Çatalhöyük (Turkey — early city 9,500 years ago, 8,000 people), Göbekli Tepe (Turkey — first temple 11,000 years ago, pre-agriculture), Stonehenge (England, 5,000 years ago, astronomical alignment), Pompeii (Roman city frozen in time by Vesuvius 79 CE)
- **Underwater archaeology**: shipwrecks, submerged cities (Port Royal, Jamaica; Alexandria, Egypt)
- **Industrial archaeology**: recent past (factories, mills, mines, railways)

## Primatology
- Our closest living relatives: chimpanzees and bonobos (share ~98.8% DNA)
  - Chimps: hierarchical, male-dominated, territorial, can be violent (group warfare, infanticide) — BUT also cooperative, empathetic
  - Bonobos: matriarchal, conflict resolution through sex, more peaceful — bonobos demonstrate our capacity for cooperation
  - We didn't evolve from chimps — we share a common ancestor who was neither chimp-like nor bonobo-like
- **Other primates**: gorillas (largest, gentle, family groups led by silverback), orangutans (solitary, arboreal, highly intelligent, tool use), gibbons (monogamous, singing)
- **Tool use in animals**: chimps (termite fishing, nut cracking, leaf sponges), orangutans, crows (hook tools, multiple steps), octopuses, dolphins (sponges on nose for protection), sea otters (rock anvil)
- **Culture in animals**: chimpanzees have regional traditions (different tool use, grooming styles, food preferences) passed between generations
- **Language in animals**: Kanzi (bonobo learned ~400 symbols, understood spoken English), Koko (gorilla, ~1,000 signs), Alex (parrot, ~150 words, understood concepts like color/shape/number), dolphins and whales have complex communication systems

## Anthropology of Religion
- **Animism**: spirits inhabit natural objects and phenomena (trees, rivers, animals, weather) — earliest known religious belief
- **Totemism**: clan symbolically associated with animal/plant, sacred relationship
- **Shamanism**: part-time religious specialist communicates with spirit world (healing, divination, rituals)
- **Polytheism**: multiple gods (Ancient Greece/Rome, Hinduism, Shinto)
- **Monotheism**: single god (Judaism, Christianity, Islam)
- **Syncretism**: blending of religious traditions (Santeria, Vodou, Candomblé — African + Catholic)
- **Secularism**: non-religious worldviews — ~16% of world population is unaffiliated

## Applied Anthropology
- **Medical anthropology**: cultural factors in health, illness, healing (why some groups have higher rates of certain diseases, culturally appropriate healthcare)
- **Development anthropology**: works with communities on development projects — ensures projects fit local culture, don't cause harm
- **Forensic anthropology**: identify human remains for legal purposes — age, sex, ancestry, trauma analysis
- **Business anthropology**: understand consumer behavior, workplace culture, improve product design
- **Digital anthropology**: how technology and digital culture shapes human experience (social media, remote work, AI)
