# Oceanography & Marine Science

## Ocean Basics
- **Coverage**: 71% of Earth's surface, 97% of Earth's water. Average depth: 3,688m (12,100 ft). Total volume: 1.332 billion km³. Weight: 1.4 × 10²¹ kg. Mean temperature: 3.5°C (38°F). Pressure: 1 atmosphere per 10m depth. At 4,000m: 400+ atm. Below 1,000m: aphotic zone (no sunlight)
- **Oceans**: Pacific (largest, deepest — Mariana Trench 11,034 m/36,200 ft. Contains 50% of ocean volume). Atlantic (2nd, 1/2 area of Pacific). Indian (3rd, north of Antarctic convergence). Southern (Antarctic — current, nutrients, unites Atlantic+Indian+Pacific south of 60°S). Arctic (smallest, mostly ice-covered, shallow — ~1,200m average depth)
- **Ocean zones (by depth)**: Epipelagic (sunlight, 0-200m, photosynthesis, 90% marine life). Mesopelagic (twilight, 200-1,000m, faint light, bioluminescence, 900+ species). Bathypelagic (midnight, 1,000-4,000m, no light, high pressure, 4°C constant). Abyssopelagic (4,000-6,000m, near freezing, 6,000 atm). Hadalpelagic (6,000-11,000m, deep-sea trenches, only specialized life)

### Ocean Currents
- **Thermohaline circulation**: "Global conveyor belt" — driven by density (temperature + salinity). Warm surface water flows toward poles, cools, sinks (downwelling in North Atlantic and Southern Ocean), flows deep toward equator, upwells. Takes ~1,000 years to complete cycle. Atlantic conveyor: Gulf Stream warm water to Europe, sink in Greenland/Norwegian Seas (forms North Atlantic Deep Water), southward deep current, upwell in Pacific/Indian. Important for global heat distribution + carbon cycling
  - AMOC (Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation): weakened ~15% since 1950 (due to Greenland ice melt → freshwater → reduces density → less sinking). Potential collapse = major climate shift (colder Europe, stronger sea level rise on US East Coast, drought in Africa/India). Loss of AMOC within next century = existential scenario — tipping point could be triggered by 2-3°C warming
- **Surface currents**: wind-driven (trade winds, westerlies). Coriolis effect deflects currents right (northern hemisphere) producing gyres (clockwise N, counterclockwise S). Gyres: N Pacific, S Pacific, N Atlantic, S Atlantic, Indian. Sargasso Sea: center of N Atlantic Gyre, Sargassum seaweed floats, area of calm. Eddies: spinning currents peeling off main current (warm-core vs cold-core. eddies carry heat, nutrients, and carbon across ocean basins)
- **Upwelling**: deep nutrient-rich water brought to surface (western coasts: California, Peru, NW Africa, Southwest Africa). Wind pushes surface water offshore → replaced by deep water. High productivity (most of world's fish catch from upwelling zones, which comprise <1% of ocean surface). El Niño disrupts upwelling (warmer Pacific → less nutrients → fish crash)

### Tides
- **Causes**: moon's gravitational pull (twice as strong as sun's — moon's proximity overrides sun's mass). Sun adds spring/neap cycle. Spring tides (new + full moon — sun+ moon aligned, maximum range). Neap tides (first + third quarter moon — sun+moon at right angles, smaller range). Diurnal (one high/low daily), semidiurnal (two equal — most Atlantic), mixed (two different — Pacific coast). Tidal range: <1m (Mediterranean) to 16m (Bay of Fundy, Nova Scotia)
  - Tidal bulge: water pulled toward moon = high tide on that side AND on opposite side (centrifugal). Earth rotates under these bulges → two high tides daily
- **Tidal energy**: turbines (barrage, tidal stream, tidal lagoon). Predictable (unlike wind/solar). Biggest tidal range = highest potential. Turbines in tidal races (narrow passages where tidal flow accelerates to 10-15 knots). Environmental concerns: fish kill, changed sediment transport. Only a few viable sites globally

### Waves
- **Wind waves**: generated by wind — fetch (distance wind blows over water) + wind speed + wind duration. Sea (choppy, developing) → swell (organized, traveling). Fully developed sea: waves limited by fetch. Significant wave height: average of highest 1/3 waves (what mariners report, ~1.6× raw mean). Rogue waves: >2× significant wave height, rare but documented (Draupner wave 1995, 26m). Breaking waves: depth <1.3× wave height → waves break (surf zone). Longshore current: wave energy at angle moves water along beach. Rip current: water returning seaward through break in sandbar — narrow, fast (up to 8 ft/sec)
  - Tsunami: displacement of water column (earthquake, landslide, volcanic eruption, asteroid). Speed: 500-800 km/h in deep ocean (like jet). Wave height: <1m in deep, 10-30m+ at shore when slowing from friction and squeezing into shallows. Travel time: 15-30 min from distant epicenter (Hawaii, California), 5-15 min from local. Warning: don't wait for official warning in local tsunami — if you feel earthquake near coast, move to high ground immediately. Wave train: may have multiple pulses, largest not always first. Drawback: ocean recedes before first wave (like low tide at extreme speed) — if you see this, RUN. 2004 Indian Ocean (227,000 dead, no warning system — $5M Thailand now has system). 2011 Tohoku Japan (18,000 dead, Fukushima disaster — new sea wall engineering after)

## Marine Life

### Coral Reefs
- **Formation**: coral polyps (animals) + zooxanthellae (algae) symbiosis. Algae photosynthesize → provide 90% of coral's energy → coral provides shelter + CO₂. Coral bleaching: high temperature (1°C above normal for 4+ weeks) → algae expelled → coral turns white → dies if prolonged. 50% of world's coral reefs lost since 1950 — end-century: up to 90% loss at 1.5°C warming, 99%+ at 2°C. Bleaching events now annual in tropics. Reef types: fringing (shoreline), barrier (offshore, separated by lagoon — Great Barrier Reef is largest living structure on Earth 2,300 km), atoll (ring-shaped, reef island over sunken volcano — Darwin's theory). Additional threat: ocean acidification slows coral calcification (growth, skeleton formation). CO₂ absorbed into ocean forms carbonic acid, pH drops 0.1 since industrial revolution = 30% increase in acidity
- **Biodiversity**: 25% of all marine species on 0.1% of ocean area. "Rainforests of the sea." One reef may host 3,000+ species. Biggest threats: climate change (bleaching), overfishing (removes key fish — herbivorous parrotfish that clean algae off coral), pollution (runoff: fertilizer → algae overgrow coral. Sediment from deforestation smothers coral), crown-of-thorns starfish (outbreak eats coral)

### Deep Sea
- **Hydrothermal vents**: chimneys on seafloor — superheated water (400°C) rich in minerals, precipitates as "black smokers" (sulfide minerals). Extremophiles (organisms survive at extreme conditions — heat, pressure, no light). Chemosynthesis: bacteria use chemical energy (sulfur, methane) instead of sunlight to produce organic matter. Unique ecosystems: tube worms (Riftia — no mouth, no gut, bacteria inside), giant clams, shrimp (no eyes — dorsal light-sensing organ). Vent communities: extinct annually at individual vent, new forming elsewise
- **Cold seeps**: methane + sulfide seep through seafloor — tube worms, mussels, clams. Lower temperature than vents, slower growth
- **Bioluminescence**: chemical reaction in animals — light produced by luciferin + luciferase enzyme. Deep-sea fish (anglerfish dangling bioluminescent lure to attract prey in total darkness), jellyfish, squid, dinoflagellates. Uses: attract mates, lure prey, warn predators, counter-illumination (flashlight fish match ambient downwelling light to hide silhouette from predators). 90% of deep-sea creatures produce light

### Fisheries
- **Overfishing**: 34% of fish stocks overexploited (FAO), 60% at maximum. Bluefin tuna: down 96%+ from unfished levels (since 1970s). Blue whale: 90%+ decimated (hunted to near extinction for oil). Cod: Grand Banks cod fishery collapsed 1992 (40,000 jobs lost, still hasn't recovered after 30+ year moratorium). Atlantic swordfish recovered after catch limits (1990s). Patagonian toothfish (Chilean sea bass): severely overfished, illegal fishing (driftnets banned internationally; IUU — illegal, unreported, unregulated still a major problem). MSC certification: sustainable fisheries certified by Marine Stewardship Council
  - Bycatch: 40% of global catch discarded (bycatch — unintended — dolphins, turtles, sharks, seabirds, juvenile fish). Shrimp trawling has highest bycatch ratio (1:3-1:10, shrimp:fish). Solutions: turtle excluder devices (TEDs), circle hooks (reduce turtle mortality in longline), whale-safe gear types (weak hooks in crab pot lines) and area-time closures
- **Aquaculture**: fastest-growing food sector — now exceeds wild-caught for human consumption? ~50% of fish for human consumption from aquaculture — yes, since 2013). Environmental concerns: pollution (fish waste, uneaten feed → algal blooms), disease (spread to wild fish), escapees (interbreed with wild, reduce genetic fitness), wild fish caught for feed (small pelagics like anchovies, sardines — some fishmeal produced from bycatch). Better: filter feeders (mussels, oysters) = low impact, some benefit water quality (absorb nutrients). Land-based recirculating systems: better pollution control, energy-intensive, expensive but possible. Open-ocean aquaculture: further offshore = less coastal impact, but still challenges with storms + feeding + disease

## Marine Megafauna

### Whales & Dolphins
- **Baleen whales** (filter feeders): humpback (annual migration 10,000+ mi, complex songs — each population sings a unique evolving song. Males sing, change song over time, all males in a population sing same version). Blue whale (largest animal ever — 100 ft, 200 tons, heart = size of a small car). Gray whale (migrating 12,000 miles round trip — longest mammal migration). Right whale (slow, near extinction, critically endangered 350 remain in North Atlantic). Fin whale (2nd largest, 80 ft, endangered)
- **Toothed whales**: sperm whale (largest toothed predator — 60 ft, dive 2 hrs to 3,000 ft for giant squid. Uses echolocation — huge nose organ (spermaceti organ) focuses sound). Killer whale/orca: apex predator in all oceans — pods have family-specific dialects & hunting techniques. Eat: fish, seals, or other whales depending on pod. Whale pods: matriarchal, live 50-90 yrs, females stop reproducing in mid-life (menopause — like humans only 3 other species). Pod members teach each other hunting, recognize calls from neighboring pods. Dolphin: high intelligence, large brain relative to body size (neocortex), self-recognition in mirror, complex social relationships, tool use (marine sponges on nose to protect while foraging), names (signature whistles — each dolphin invents its own signal as a calf, maintains for life)
- **Whale songs**: humpback males sing during breeding season. Song evolves over time (all males in population learn new song together). So complex they have repeated phrases, themes, cycles at multiple scales (like musical composition). Fin whales have the lowest frequency sound of any animal: 20 Hz. Sound travels long distances underwater (acoustic thermometry: 20Hz sound can cross ocean basin). Ship noise interferes with whale communication; believed to hinder mating/feeding

### Sharks & Rays
- **Types**: great white (largest predatory fish — 20 ft, 2.5 tons, counter-shaded, warm-blooded (regional endothermy). Annual migration across Pacific (up to 12,000 mi). Attacks: ~10 unprovoked fatalities/year vs 70M sharks killed annually by humans). Whale shark (largest fish — 40 ft, 20 tons, filter feeder). Hammerhead (cephalofoil: ampullae of Lorenzini across enhanced for electroreception — head shape enhances ability to find rays buried in sand). Manta ray (largest ray — 25 ft, filter feeder, brain-to-body ratio similar to dolphins, complex social behavior). Megalodon (extinct 3.6 mya, 50-60 ft, bite force 18 tons, largest shark ever)
  - Cartilaginous fish (no bones). No swim bladder (must swim to breathe, sink if stop). Electroreception (ampullae of Lorenzini — detect electrical fields of prey). Lateral line senses water movement. Can go months between meals. Most species are declining (70% of oceanic shark species threatened). Finning: fins cut off at sea, body discarded (cruel + wasteful). Shark fin soup drives 70M+ deaths/year. Bans on finning (US, EU, many countries) but enforcement difficult at sea. Many shark species slow-growing, later maturity, few pups = very vulnerable to overfishing. Great whites protected in many areas; some populations recovering (e.g., US West Coast; East Coast recovering slowly)
