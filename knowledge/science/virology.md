# Virology

## Virus Structure
- **Capsid**: protein shell protecting viral genome. Icosahedral (20 triangular faces — most efficient, e.g. adenovirus, herpes, polio), helical (rod-shaped — tobacco mosaic virus, rabies, measles, Ebola), complex (bacteriophage heads + tails, poxviruses). Capsid self-assembles from capsomeres (identical protein subunits). Capsid symmetry determined by virus — t = triangulation number for icosahedral viruses (how many capsomeres: T=1, T=3, T=7 etc). Enveloped: outer lipid bilayer from host cell membrane (acquired during budding). Non-enveloped: naked capsid only
- **Envelope**: lipid membrane studded with viral glycoproteins (spikes). Functions: receptor binding and membrane fusion. More fragile — inactivated by drying, heat, detergents, acid (enveloped viruses = easier to kill with hand sanitizer/soap). Examples: HIV (gp120), influenza (HA, NA), SARS-CoV-2 (spike protein). Envelope derived from: plasma membrane (HIV, flu), ER (coronavirus, dengue), Golgi (herpes), nuclear membrane (some herpes). Non-enveloped: more resistant, survive on surfaces, alcohol less effective — need bleach/heat (norovirus, rhinovirus, adenovirus, HPV, poliovirus, rotavirus)
- **Genome types**: DNA (dsDNA — herpes, adenovirus, poxvirus, hepadnavirus / ssDNA — parvovirus, circovirus), RNA (dsRNA — rotavirus, reovirus / +ssRNA — can act as mRNA: coronavirus, polio, HCV, dengue, Zika, norovirus / -ssRNA — need RNA-dependent RNA polymerase to make mRNA: flu, rabies, Ebola, measles, RSV). Segmented genome: influenza (8 segments — reassortment causes pandemics). Circular: HBV, circoviruses, some plant viroids. Ambisense: some bunyaviruses, arenaviruses (both positive and negative sense on same segment)
- **Virion**: complete infectious virus particle. Some have additional structures: tegument (matrix layer between capsid and envelope in herpesvirus), internal proteins (flu M1 matrix protein), polymerases packaged inside (RNA viruses carry RdRp), accessory proteins to block host defenses. Viroids: smallest known infectious agent — naked circular RNA (~250-400 nt), only plants, no proteins. Virusoids: like viroids but encapsulated by helper virus. Satellite viruses: require helper virus for replication

## Replication Cycles

### Lytic Cycle
- **Steps**: (1) Attachment/Adsorption: virus binds to specific host cell receptor. Receptor determines tropism (which cells infected). HIV: CD4 + co-receptor (CCR5/CXCR4). SARS-CoV-2: ACE2. Flu: sialic acid. Rabies: acetylcholine receptor/nAChR, then spread to nervous system. Some viruses use multiple receptors or co-receptors
- (2) Entry: three mechanisms — direct fusion (enveloped viruses fuse envelope with plasma membrane — HIV, measles, herpes, paramyxoviruses — releases capsid into cytoplasm), receptor-mediated endocytosis (most common — virus taken into endosome, low pH triggers fusion — flu, coronaviruses, dengue, adenovirus, many others), injection (bacteriophage — only injects genome, capsid stays outside)
- (3) Uncoating: capsid disassembles, releases genome. Can occur: at plasma membrane, in endosome, in cytoplasm, in nucleus (most DNA viruses). Some viruses (reovirus) only partially uncoat — transcribe inside capsid
- (4) Replication/Transcription: genome replicated and viral mRNA transcribed. DNA viruses (except poxvirus) replicate in nucleus, use host RNA pol II for transcription, host or viral DNA polymerase for replication. RNA viruses replicate in cytoplasm (except influenza, bornavirus). +ssRNA: directly translated → RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp) replicates. -ssRNA: RdRp transcribed first, then +mRNA. Retroviruses: RNA → DNA via reverse transcriptase → integrates into host genome as provirus. dsDNA viruses: immediate early → early → late gene expression cascade. The Baltimore classification groups viruses by genome type + mRNA synthesis strategy
- (5) Assembly: new virions assemble in cytoplasm (most RNA viruses, poxvirus), nucleus (herpes, adenovirus, parvovirus), or at membrane sites. Capsid assembles around genome (some package genome into pre-formed capsid). Enveloped viruses: nucleocapsid buds through membrane at assembly sites enriched with viral glycoproteins. Matrix protein (M) orchestrates assembly and budding — critical for efficient particle formation
- (6) Release: non-enveloped viruses lyse host cell (lysis — adenovirus, poliovirus, many bacteriophages, often cell death). Enveloped viruses bud from membrane without immediately killing cell (HIV, flu, coronaviruses — can cause persistent infection). Neuraminidase (flu NA) cleaves sialic acid to release virus from cell surface — target of oseltamivir (Tamiflu). Some viruses use viroporins (small ion channels) to alter membrane permeability and facilitate release. Some can also spread cell-to-cell via syncytia (fusion of infected cells with neighbors — RSV, measles, HIV) to avoid immune detection

### Lysogenic Cycle
- **Temperate phages** (e.g., λ phage): integrate genome into host chromosome (prophage) or exist as plasmid. Repressor protein (CI in λ) keeps lytic genes off — immunity to superinfection. Environmental stress (UV, damage) triggers SOS response → RecA activates prophage excision → lytic cycle. Transduction: host genes packaged with phage DNA and transferred to new host during lytic infection — important mechanism of horizontal gene transfer in bacteria
- **Latency (animal viruses)**: similar principle but more complex. Herpesviruses: HSV (trigeminal ganglia), VZV (dorsal root ganglia), EBV (B cells). Maintain genome as episome (circular DNA) with limited gene expression (latency-associated transcripts). Stress, immunosuppression, UV trigger reactivation. HIV: integrates into host genome (provirus), can remain latent in resting memory T cells — latent reservoir prevents cure. HBV: cccDNA (covalently closed circular DNA) in hepatocyte nucleus — current antivirals rarely clear it completely

## Transmission Routes
- **Respiratory**: droplets (>5 µm — flu, cold, COVID, RSV — travel ~6 ft, short range, heavy, fall quickly) vs aerosol (≤5 µm — measles, TB, chickenpox — float in air, long range, can remain airborne for hours). Droplet precautions: mask, distance. Airborne: N95, negative pressure room, HEPA filtration. Influenza: mainly droplets, some aerosol. SARS-CoV-2: primarily aerosol in poorly ventilated spaces. Measles: one of most contagious — R0 = 12-18, airborne up to 2 hrs. Tuberculosis: exclusively airborne, requires N95
- **Fecal-oral**: norovirus (leading cause of acute gastroenteritis — extremely contagious, 10 virions sufficient for infection, survives on surfaces for weeks), hepatitis A/E, rotavirus (major cause of childhood diarrhea — vaccine available), poliovirus. Transmission: contaminated food/water, poor sanitation, fomites, direct contact. Handwashing (with soap) is critical — norovirus resists alcohol sanitizers
- **Sexual**: HIV, HSV-2 (genital herpes), HPV (most common STI — some types cause cervical cancer), HBV, syphilis, gonorrhea, chlamydia. Blood-borne: HIV, HBV, HCV (needle sharing, blood transfusion, occupational needlestick). Needlestick risk: HBV 30% (unvaccinated), HCV ~1.8%, HIV ~0.3% (mucous membrane: 0.09%). Mucous membrane exposure: standard HIV PEP
- **Vector-borne**: mosquito (dengue, Zika, chikungunya, West Nile, yellow fever, malaria — though that's protozoan), tick (Lyme disease, tick-borne encephalitis, Crimean-Congo hemorrhagic fever), sandfly (leishmania), flea (plague). Zoonotic (animal → human): rabies (dog/bat), Ebola (fruit bats), Nipah (bats → pigs), hantavirus (rodents), SARS (civet cats), MERS (camels). Rabies in unvaccinated: ~100% fatal once symptoms appear — PEP must be given before symptoms

## Immune Response to Viruses
- **Innate (immediate, non-specific)**: (1) Interferon (IFN) response — infected cells secrete type I INFs (IFNα/β) → neighboring cells enter antiviral state (upregulate PKR, OAS, MX proteins — degrade RNA, block translation). (2) NK cells — kill infected cells that downregulate MHC-I (viral evasive strategy). (3) Complement — some viruses directly activate. Macrophages, dendritic cells — phagocytose, present antigen. Many viruses evolved to block IFN signaling (NS1 of flu, NS5 of flaviviruses, V protein of paramyxoviruses, multiple proteins in herpesviruses)
- **Adaptive (specific, memory)**: Humoral — B cells produce antibodies that neutralize virus (block attachment, prevent entry). Antibodies: IgM (early), IgG (memory/long-lasting), IgA (mucosal). Neutralizing antibodies correlate with protection (vaccine efficacy). Monoclonal antibodies: used as treatment (REGN-COV for COVID, palivizumab for RSV prophylaxis in high-risk infants). Cell-mediated — cytotoxic T cells (CD8+) kill virus-infected cells via perforin/granzyme or FasL/Fas. Helper T cells (CD4+) help B cells and CD8+ — HIV targets CD4 cells → AIDS
- **Antibody-dependent enhancement (ADE)**: pre-existing non-neutralizing antibodies enhance viral entry into Fc receptor-bearing cells. Problem in dengue (heterotypic infection → severe disease), seen in vitro for other viruses including Zika, Ebola, coronaviruses (concern during COVID vaccine development). Antibody evasion: coronaviruses use shield of glycans around spike, flu undergoes antigenic drift

## Antiviral Drugs — Mechanisms
- **Entry/fusion inhibitors**: block viral attachment or membrane fusion. Maraviroc (CCR5 antagonist — HIV — blocks co-receptor binding), enfuvirtide (T-20, HIV fusion inhibitor — injectable, rarely used now). Ibalizumab (HIV — binds CD4, blocks entry). Palivizumab: monoclonal against RSV F protein (prophylaxis for high-risk infants). Arbidol (umifenovir): broad-spectrum, used in Russia/China
- **Uncoating inhibitors**: amantadine/rimantadine (flu — block M2 ion channel. No longer recommended — widespread resistance). Most effective influenza antivirals now are NA inhibitors
- **Polymerase inhibitors**: most common class. Nucleoside/nucleotide analogs: acyclovir (HSV — thymidine kinase phosphorylates activates, viral DNA polymerase incorporates → chain termination. Valacyclovir = prodrug with better oral bioavailability). Ganciclovir (CMV, needs viral kinase UL97). Remdesivir (SARS-CoV-2, Ebola — RdRp inhibitor). Tenofovir/emtricitabine (HIV — NRTI, incorporated into DNA, terminates). Sofosbuvir (HCV — NS5B polymerase inhibitor, revolutionised HCV treatment — >95% cure). Cidofovir (broad anti-DNA virus — CMV, others — nephrotoxic). Favipiravir (broad-spectrum RdRp inhibitor — mutagenesis, approved for flu in Japan, used for COVID in some countries). Non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NNRTIs — efavirenz, nevirapine — HIV, bind allosterically to RT). Hepatitis B: entecavir, tenofovir — suppress replication, rarely cure
- **Protease inhibitors**: HIV: atazanavir, darunavir, lopinavir (bind HIV protease active site, prevent gag-pol processing → immature non-infectious virions). HCV: boceprevir, simeprevir, grazoprevir (NS3/4A protease, early DAAs). SARS-CoV: nirmatrelvir (Paxlovid component — main protease Mpro inhibitor, oral, reduces hospitalization by 89%). Influenza: none currently in common use (baloxavir targets cap-dependent endonuclease of PA subunit — newer)
- **Neuraminidase inhibitors**: oseltamivir (Tamiflu — oral), zanamivir (Relenza — inhaled). Bind neuraminidase → virus can't release from cell. Most effective if started within 48 hours of symptoms. Reduce duration by ~1 day. Resistance: oseltamivir resistance in seasonal H1N1 in 2007-08 (H274Y mutation), but surveillance now. Peramivir (IV, single dose). Baloxavir marboxil (cap-dependent endonuclease inhibitor — newer, single oral dose, different mechanism)
- **Integrase inhibitors**: HIV integrase strand transfer inhibitors (INSTIs) — raltegravir, dolutegravir, bictegravir, elvitegravir. Prevent HIV cDNA from integrating into host genome. Dolutegravir = first-line treatment for HIV (high barrier to resistance, once daily, well tolerated)
- **Combination therapy**: essential to prevent resistance (especially HIV — highly active antiretroviral therapy HAART, 2 NRTI backbone + INSTI or NNRTI or boosted PI). HCV: typically 2-3 direct-acting antivirals. Flu: monotherapy possible due to short replication cycle, but resistance emerges

## Vaccine Types
- **mRNA vaccines**: synthetic mRNA encoding viral protein (spike for SARS-CoV-2) in lipid nanoparticle. Delivered to cells → translate protein → immune response. Advantages: rapid development (Moderna/Pfizer COVID ~11 months), no live virus, easy to update, strong cellular and humoral immunity (Th1-biased). Disadvantage: requires ultra-cold storage (historically), reactogenicity (fever, fatigue), novice technology (public skepticism). Platform adaptable to other viruses (flu, CMV, RSV in trials). Self-amplifying RNA (saRNA): includes replicase → amplifies mRNA → lower dose needed
- **Viral vector**: modified virus (adenovirus — J&J, AZ/ChAdOx1; vesicular stomatitis virus — Merck Ebola; modified vaccinia Ankara — MVA) carrying viral gene. Infects cells → expresses antigen → immune response. Advantages: strong T cell response, single dose sometimes sufficient, stable (refrigerator storage for some). Disadvantages: pre-existing immunity to vector may reduce efficacy (Ad5, Ad26), rare blood clotting with AZ/J&J (VITT — vaccine-induced immune thrombotic thrombocytopenia, frequency ~1:100,000). New approach: chimpanzee adenovirus vectors (ChAd) avoid human pre-existing immunity
- **Inactivated/killed**: virus grown, then chemically inactivated (formalin, β-propiolactone). Examples: polio (IPV/Salk), hepatitis A, flu (injectable), rabies, whole-cell pertussis (part of DTP). Advantages: safe (no reversion to virulence), stable, long track record. Disadvantages: require boosters, weaker cellular immunity (mostly humoral), sometimes less durable, may need adjuvant (alum). Production requires growing large quantities of live virus → biosafety
- **Live attenuated**: weakened virus that replicates minimally but doesn't cause disease. Examples: MMR (measles-mumps-rubella), varicella (chickenpox), polio (OPV/Sabin — still used in polio eradication), yellow fever (17D), flu (Flumist nasal), rotavirus (oral), BCG (TB — controversial efficacy). Advantages: strongest immunity (mimics natural infection), often lifelong after 1-2 doses, mucosal immunity. Disadvantages: cannot give to immunocompromised (reversion risk), requires cold chain, rare reversion to virulence (OPV → VAPP/VDPV ~1:2.4 million doses — reason US switched to IPV), harder to develop, teratogenicity concern in pregnancy
- **Subunit / protein-based**: purified viral protein (surface antigen). Examples: HBV (HBsAg), HPV (virus-like particle/VLP — L1 protein, the only licensed cancer-preventing vaccine), flu (Flublok, recombinant HA), acellular pertussis, shingles (Shingrix — recombinant VZV glycoprotein E + adjuvant AS01, >90% efficacy even in elderly). Advantages: very safe, can give to immunocompromised. Disadvantages: need adjuvant (largely humoral), multiple doses, manufacturing complex. VLP vaccines (HPV, HBV): self-assemble into virus-like particles — highly immunogenic (repetitive array of antigens)
- **Other platforms**: virus-like particles (VLPs), DNA vaccines (plasmid encoding antigen — few licensed for human use, mostly veterinary — COVID showed platform promise), protein nanoparticles (ferritin-based, computationally designed), T cell vaccines (induce CD8 response without neutralizing antibodies — needed for HIV, HCV, TB). Multivalent: cover multiple strains (Gardasil-9 covers 9 HPV types: 6,11,16,18,31,33,45,52,58, >90% of cervical cancers). Combination: MMR, DTaP-IPV-Hib (Pentacel), MMRV

## Emerging Viruses
- **Zoonotic spillover**: >60% of emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic. Driver: deforestation, wildlife trade, intensive farming, climate change, urbanization. Bat reservoir (particularly high-risk — live in dense colonies, fly, unique immune system tolerating viruses, shed virus without symptoms): Nipah, Hendra, Ebola, Marburg, SARS, MERS, COVID-19 suspected. Birds: avian influenza (H5N1, H7N9 — high lethality but limited human-human transmission). Pigs: influenza (mixing vessel for avian + human), Nipah (intermediate host). Mosquito expansion: dengue, chikungunya, West Nile, Zika expanding range with warming climate
- **Pandemic risk factors**: human-to-human transmission (respiratory especially concerning), asymptomatic/pre-symptomatic transmission (COVID, flu), novel antigen (no pre-existing immunity), high R0 (measles 12-18, COVID original ~3, Delta ~5-7, Omicron ~10, polio ~5-7, smallpox ~3-5), airborne transmission, long environmental survival, high mutation rate (RNA viruses especially). WHO priority pathogens: COVID-19, Crimean-Congo hemorrhagic fever, Ebola/Marburg, Lassa, MERS, Nipah, Rift Valley fever, Zika, Disease X (unknown future pandemic threat)
- **Surveillance**: Global Influenza Surveillance and Response System (GISRS, WHO). Next-generation sequencing for pathogen discovery. Wastewater monitoring (COVID era). GISAID (genome sharing — key for tracking variants). International Health Regulations (IHR 2005): mandatory reporting of public health events of international concern (PHEIC). Challenges: underreporting, delayed reporting, lack of lab capacity in many countries
- **Antiviral resistance**: influenza (oseltamivir resistance), HIV (multi-drug resistant strains, ~8-10% of new infections carry resistant strains), HSV (acyclovir-resistant in immunocompromised), CMV (ganciclovir-resistant), HBV (lamivudine resistance well-documented, entecavir resistance uncommon). Strategies: combination therapy, drug targets with high resistance barrier (dolutegravir), rotating drugs

## Prions
- **Infectious protein**: PrPSc (misfolded conformer of normal cellular PrPC). PrPC: normal function uncertain (copper binding, cell signaling, circadian rhythm?) — found on cell surface (GPI-anchored) mainly in neurons. PrPSc: β-sheet rich (vs α-helical PrPC) → aggregates into amyloid fibrils. Conversion: PrPSc templates conformational change of PrPC → exponential chain reaction. Extremely resistant to heat, UV, radiation, formaldehyde, nucleases (only protein, no nucleic acid). Infectivity: persists in soil for years (scrapie in sheep pastures). Iatrogenic transmission: contaminated surgical instruments, cadaveric growth hormone (causing variant CJD in younger patients), dura mater grafts. Prion diseases: Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD — sporadic 85%, familial 10-15%, iatrogenic <1%), variant CJD (from BSE-contaminated beef, peaked in UK in 1990s), Kuru (Fore people, Papua New Guinea — mortuary cannibalism), Gerstmann-Sträussler-Scheinker (familial, very rare), fatal familial insomnia (FFI — D178N mutation, PRNP gene), scrapie (sheep/goats — not zoonotic), bovine spongiform encephalopathy/BSE (mad cow — zoonotic → vCJD), chronic wasting disease (deer/elk — spreading in North America, zoonotic potential under study)
- **Diagnosis**: brain MRI (pulvinar sign in vCJD, cortical ribboning in sCJD), CSF 14-3-3 protein (elevated but not specific), RT-QuIC (real-time quaking-induced conversion — highly sensitive/specific, detects PrPSc in CSF/nasal brushings — used antemortem to diagnose sCJD with >90% sensitivity), tonsil biopsy (vCJD — PrPSc in lymphoid tissue). Definitive: brain biopsy or autopsy (spongiform change, PrPSc IHC, PrPSc western blot), spongiform degeneration (vacuoles in gray matter), astrogliosis, neuronal loss (no inflammation or fever). No proven treatment — uniformly fatal, median survival ~4-6 months for sCJD, longer for vCJD ~14 months
- **Decontamination**: prions NOT inactivated by standard autoclaving (121°C). Requires: 1N NaOH or sodium hypochlorite (2% bleach) for 1 hr, then autoclave at 134°C for 18 min in gravity displacement or 3 min in prevacuum (WHO protocol). Surgical instruments potentially used on suspected CJD patients are destroyed or quarantined. No known environmental degradation (persists in soil for years — CWD in deer poses environmental contamination challenge). No vaccine or treatment exists for any prion disease (experimental anti-prion compounds have failed in clinical trials)
