# Epidemiology

## Study Designs

### Observational Studies
- **Cohort study**: follow a group (cohort) forward in time, compare disease incidence between exposed and unexposed. Prospective (recruit now, follow forward — Framingham Heart Study, Nurses' Health Study) or retrospective (use existing records, go forward in time). Best for: determining incidence, studying multiple outcomes from one exposure, rare exposures. Measures risk ratio (RR) = incidence in exposed ÷ incidence in unexposed. Expensive, long follow-up, attrition bias. Nested case-control: within cohort, select cases + matched controls → saves cost. Examples: smoking → lung cancer (Doll & Hill, 1950s, British Doctors Study)
- **Case-control study**: select cases (with disease) and controls (without), look backward to measure odds of exposure. Odds ratio (OR) = odds of exposure in cases ÷ odds of exposure in controls. Best for: rare diseases, diseases with long latency, rapid to conduct. Susceptible to recall bias (cases remember exposures differently), selection bias (choosing appropriate controls), temporal ambiguity (exposure vs outcome timing). Controls must be from same source population. Matching: frequency (group) or individual. Example: investigating outbreak source — compare what cases ate vs controls ate
- **Cross-sectional study**: survey at one time point — measure exposure and outcome simultaneously. Prevalence, not incidence. Can't establish causation (temporal ambiguity). Good for: disease burden estimates, health surveys (NHANES), hypothesis generation. Quick, cheap. Measuring current disease count and current exposure simultaneously means you can't know which came first. Example: CDC's Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS)
- **Ecological study**: group-level (countries, cities, time periods), not individual. Correlation between average exposure and disease rate. Ecological fallacy: association at group level may not hold at individual level. Cheap, uses existing data, good for generating hypotheses. Example: correlation between national fat intake and breast cancer rates. Problem: the individuals eating fat aren't necessarily getting breast cancer — maybe other lifestyle factors differ

### Interventional Studies
- **Randomized controlled trial (RCT)**: gold standard. Randomly assign to treatment vs control (placebo or standard of care). Randomization balances known AND unknown confounders. Any difference in outcome = effect of intervention (within random error). Types: parallel (two groups simultaneously), crossover (each person gets both in random sequence, washout period), cluster (randomize groups — schools, clinics — for community interventions). Blinding: single (participant unaware), double (participant + investigator unaware), triple (also data analysts). Limits: expensive, ethical constraints (can't randomize harmful exposures), not generalizable for narrow eligibility criteria, long duration for some outcomes
- **Pragmatic vs explanatory trials**: pragmatic (real-world conditions, broad eligibility, measures effectiveness — does it work in real practice?) vs explanatory (ideal conditions, narrow eligibility, measures efficacy — can it work under ideal circumstances?). Both needed: explanatory first, pragmatic later
- **Non-randomized / quasi-experimental**: when randomization not possible. Before-after, interrupted time series, difference-in-differences (compare intervention group vs control group, before vs after), regression discontinuity. Weaker causal inference: susceptible to confounding, secular trends, regression to the mean
- **Systematic review/meta-analysis**: combines multiple studies. Systematic review: comprehensive, reproducible search, quality assessment. Meta-analysis: statistical combination of results. Forest plot: each study's effect size + confidence interval + pooled estimate. Heterogeneity (I² statistic): % variation due to true differences, not chance. Fixed effects (assume one true effect) vs random effects (true effects vary between studies). Publication bias: small negative studies less likely to be published → funnel plot asymmetry. Cochrane Collaboration gold standard

## Incidence vs Prevalence
- **Incidence**: NEW cases in population over time period. Measures risk of developing disease. Incidence rate = new cases ÷ person-time at risk. Cumulative incidence = proportion of at-risk population who develop disease over time period (e.g., 5-year risk). Person-time: sum of time each person contributes while disease-free. Example: 10 cases / 1000 person-years = incidence rate 10/1000 PY
- **Prevalence**: ALL existing cases at a point in time (point prevalence) or period (period prevalence). Snapshot of disease burden. Prevalence = (cases ÷ total population). Affected by: incidence rate × average disease duration. Chronic diseases (diabetes, arthritis) have HIGH prevalence even if low incidence (people live with them for years). Acute diseases (cold, flu) have low prevalence despite high incidence because duration short
- **Relationship**: Prevalence ≈ Incidence × Duration. Long duration = high prevalence even if incidence low. Short duration = low prevalence even if incidence high. For chronic diseases, incidence determines new cases; prevalence also reflects survival and cure rates, as well as changes in treatment and migration patterns
- **Uses**: incidence for studying causation and risk factors, evaluating prevention. Prevalence for healthcare planning (how many beds/clinics/docs needed), measuring disease burden. Incidence harder to measure (need follow-up), prevalence easier (single survey)

## Outbreak Investigation Steps
1. **Confirm outbreak**: is observed cases > expected? Verify diagnosis, rule out reporting artifact. Establish case definition (clinical + lab + time/place/person criteria)
2. **Describe data by person, place, time**: epidemic curve (epi curve — x-axis = time of onset, y-axis = cases). Point source: cases rise sharply then fall (single exposure — e.g., food poisoning at one meal). Propagated: successive waves (person-to-person — e.g., measles, flu cases spread across successive generations of transmission). Continuous source: plateau over time (ongoing exposure — e.g., contaminated water supply). Intermittent: irregular peaks (multiple exposures)
3. **Generate hypothesis**: who is at risk? What exposures differ? Interview cases + controls
4. **Evaluate hypothesis**: analytic study (case-control or cohort) to compare exposure between cases and non-cases. Calculate attack rates (cohort study of closed population — number ate food item/total at risk) and odds ratios (case-control comparing % cases exposed vs controls). P-value, confidence intervals
5. **Implement control/prevention**: remove contaminated food, close source, isolate/treat cases, prophylaxis for exposed, public education, vaccination (if available). For ongoing outbreaks, control measures may be implemented while investigation continues — you don't have to wait for statistical confirmation to act
6. **Communicate findings**: report to local/state/federal health authorities (CDC, WHO if international), public health advisory, scientific publication, media communication as needed. Maintain transparency. Prepared statement, media training, consistent messages. Honesty about uncertainty

## R₀ and Herd Immunity
- **R₀ (basic reproduction number)**: average number of secondary cases from one infected person in a fully susceptible population. Depends on: infectiousness, contact rate, duration of infectiousness. Examples: measles 12-18, pertussis 12-17, polio 5-7, smallpox 3-5, HIV 2-5, SARS-CoV-2 original ~3, Delta ~5-7, Omicron ~10, influenza ~1.5, Ebola 1.5-2, seasonal flu ~1.3, MERS <1. R₀ > 1 → epidemic grows. R₀ < 1 → epidemic dies out
- **Effective R (Rt/Re)**: actual reproduction number considering immunity and interventions. Rt = R₀ × proportion susceptible × effect of interventions. When Rt < 1, outbreak declines. Goal of control measures: reduce Rt below 1. Measured in real time during epidemics using case data, contact patterns, and modeling approaches (EpiEstim)
- **Herd immunity threshold**: proportion of population immune (vaccination or prior infection) to achieve Rt < 1. Formula: HIT = 1 - 1/R₀. For R₀ = 3, need 67% immune. For measles (R₀ = 15), need 93% immune. Achieved through vaccination (safe) NOT natural infection (causes illness, death). Not every disease has herd immunity: tetanus (not contagious), rabies (zoonotic, killed before spreading)
- **Herd immunity ≠ elimination**: elimination = no transmission in region (measles eliminated in US since 2000 — but imported cases). Eradication = global elimination (smallpox 1980, rinderpest 2011, polio close). Eradication requires: effective intervention, no animal reservoir, practical diagnosis, political will and funding. Smallpox: only human disease ever fully eradicated — successful due to visible rash (case detection), effective vaccine (robust to freeze-dried distribution), no animal reservoir, global commitment (WHO Intensified Smallpox Eradication Programme 1967-1980)
- **Vaccine efficacy vs effectiveness**: efficacy (RCT conditions) vs effectiveness (real-world). Population immunity requires high vaccine coverage + high efficacy. Vaccine efficacy formula: (AR in unvaccinated − AR in vaccinated)/AR in unvaccinated. VE 90% → 90% reduction in disease among vaccinated. VE 95% for measles vaccine, 90-95% for polio vaccine, 60-90% for seasonal influenza depending on match

## Confounding & Bias

### Confounding
- **Confounder**: variable associated with BOTH exposure and outcome, NOT on causal pathway between them. Distorts exposure-outcome relationship. Example: gray hair (exposure) and death (outcome). Age confounds — older people have more gray hair AND higher mortality. Gray hair doesn't cause death
- **Criteria**: (1) associated with exposure, (2) independent risk factor for outcome (not intermediate), (3) not caused by exposure. Confounding can: create false association, hide true association, reverse direction (Simpson's paradox — when aggregate data shows association that reverses when adjusting for confounder)
- **Control methods**: (1) Randomization (experiments — distributes confounders evenly between groups if sample size large enough). (2) Restriction (limit study to one level — all male, all non-smokers). (3) Matching (select controls with same confounder distribution — frequency or individual pair matching). (4) Stratification (analyze within strata of confounder — Mantel-Haenszel method: calculates pool of odds ratio or risk ratio across strata, weighted by inverse of variance, produces adjusted summary estimate). (5) Multivariable regression (statistically adjust — logistic, Cox, Poisson, or linear regression with covariates for confounders). Residual confounding: confounder measured imprecisely or unmeasured confounders remain

### Bias
- **Selection bias**: systematic error in who participates or is included. Berksonian/hospital bias (hospitalized patients differ from general population). Healthy worker effect (workers healthier than general population). Volunteer bias (health-seekers more likely to participate). Loss to follow-up (differential dropout). Self-selection bias (individuals choose their own exposure). Detection bias (diagnosis affected by exposure status). Prevention: careful sampling, high follow-up rates, sensitivity analyses
- **Information (measurement) bias**: errors in measuring exposure or outcome. Recall bias (cases remember exposures differently — especially for events or past exposures like diet or childhood trauma. This is a primary concern in case-control studies: cases with cancer may over-report smoking compared to healthy controls). Interviewer bias (interviewer probes differently for cases). Observer bias (differential measurement). Social desirability bias (underreport of smoking, alcohol). Misclassification: non-differential (same error in both groups — biases toward null, dilutes association — equal misclassification across groups makes it harder to find a true effect) vs differential (different error — can bias in either direction, may produce spurious associations. More serious than non-differential because direction of bias is unpredictable). Prevention: blinding, standardized protocols, objective measures, validated instruments, using biomarkers instead of self-report, memory aids like calendars/photos
- **Confounding by indication**: treatment decisions are based on patient prognosis. Treated group differs from untreated by indication for treatment. Example: people taking statins have worse cardiovascular health (indication) but also have lower CVD events than similar untreated — may appear statins reduce risk, but the sicker unmedicated group may appear worse OR the treated group may appear healthier because they were prescribed statins for high cholesterol. Solutions: use new-user designs, active comparator (compare to alternative treatment), propensity scores (model treatment selection probability), instrumental variables (use exogenous variation in treatment such as prescribing preference or geographic variation)
- **Lead-time bias**: earlier detection makes survival appear longer even if death occurs at same time. Critical in cancer screening. Example: screening detects cancer 5 years earlier, patient still dies at same age. Survival from diagnosis appears 5 years longer (but no benefit). Lead time is the time from diagnosis by screening to when it would have been diagnosed clinically. Correction: compare mortality rates (not survival times) between screened and unscreened groups. Length time bias: screening preferentially detects slower-growing, less aggressive cases (longer detectable preclinical phase while aggressive fast-growing tumors escape detection between screening intervals, become interval cancers and present between screening rounds). These slower-growing screen-detected cases have better prognosis regardless of screening benefit. This is why screening trials should measure mortality reduction not survival differences

## Screening Tests

### Sensitivity, Specificity, Predictive Values
- **Sensitivity**: P(test positive | disease present). True positive rate. "How good is the test at catching cases?" High sensitivity = few false negatives. SnNout: highly Sensitive test, if Negative → rules OUT disease. Formula: TP ÷ (TP + FN). Example: HIV ELISA has >99.5% sensitivity. A negative ELISA essentially rules out HIV (in appropriate window period). High-sensitivity tests used for screening (catch as many cases as possible)
- **Specificity**: P(test negative | disease absent). True negative rate. "How good is the test at ruling out healthy people?" High specificity = few false positives. SpPin: highly Specific test, if Positive → rules IN disease. Formula: TN ÷ (TN + FP). Example: HIV Western blot has >99.9% specificity. A positive Western blot confirms HIV (provided appropriate pretest probability). High-specificity tests used for confirmatory testing after positive screening tests
- **Positive predictive value (PPV)**: P(disease | test positive). Depends on disease prevalence. If prevalence low, even highly specific test gives many false positives. PPV = TP ÷ (TP + FP). Lower PPV in screening asymptomatic populations (low prevalence) vs testing symptomatic patients (higher pretest probability). Example: mammography PPV ~5-15% (first screening at age 40) meaning 85-95% of positive mammograms are false positives. This is expected and acceptable — the important thing is follow-up of positives
- **Negative predictive value (NPV)**: P(no disease | test negative). NPV = TN ÷ (TN + FN). High when prevalence low — negative test is reassuring. Low when prevalence high — negative test less reliable
- **Pre-test probability**: estimated disease likelihood before testing. Often based on: age, symptoms, risk factors, local prevalence. Bayesian reasoning: post-test probability depends on pre-test probability × test characteristics. A positive test increases probability but doesn't make it 100% (unless specificity = 100%). A highly sensitive test negative makes disease very unlikely. This subject area: use Fagan nomogram to calculate how test results move pre-test probability to post-test probability
- **Trade-off**: higher sensitivity → lower specificity (and vice versa). ROC curve plots sensitivity vs 1-specificity across thresholds. Area under curve (AUC): 0.5 (useless), 0.7-0.8 (fair), 0.8-0.9 (good), 0.9-1.0 (excellent). AUC does not directly describe clinical utility — depends heavily on context and cost of false positives/false negatives. Choose threshold based on relative costs of false positives vs false negatives. For screening: maximize sensitivity. For confirmation: maximize specificity. For population screening, aim for high specificity (reduce false positives) while maintaining adequate sensitivity (catch important cases)
- **Example**: HIV testing algorithm. First: ELISA (sensitivity >99.5%, specificity ~99%). If reactive → repeat in duplicate (1 false positive per 10,000). If repeatedly reactive → confirm with Western blot or nucleic acid test (specificity >99.9%). One rapid test alone is not diagnostic (PPV insufficient). This two-step or three-step testing strategy is standard in almost all public health and clinical guidelines (WHO, CDC)

## Causal Inference — Bradford Hill Criteria
- **Purpose**: guide for assessing whether association = causation. NOT checklist — these are considerations, not necessary conditions. Proposed by Sir Austin Bradford Hill (1965, in the Proceedings of the Royal Society of Medicine) for interpreting epidemiological evidence. Used by Surgeon General for smoking → lung cancer conclusion (1964)
- **1. Strength of association**: larger effect size = more likely causal (OR/RR > 3 more convincing than 1.2). Weak associations more likely due to confounding. Smoking → lung cancer: RR ~20 (very strong). Sitting → mortality: RR ~1.2 (weak). But weak association doesn't rule out causation — if confounders well controlled, weak association may be real (e.g., air pollution → lung cancer, RR ~1.1 but consistent across many studies)
- **2. Consistency**: replicated across different populations, study designs, settings. Single study rarely sufficient. Smoking → lung cancer consistent across >50 case-control and >10 cohort studies in many countries. Meta-analyses give consistency assessment
- **3. Specificity**: one cause → one effect (rare in chronic disease). Smoking causes many diseases (lung, bladder, heart, stroke). Helpful for infectious diseases (one pathogen → one disease) but not for multifactorial diseases. Hill: "If specificity exists, we may be able to draw conclusions; but if it doesn't, we cannot" (criteria 1-4 are most useful, 5-9 supportive)
- **4. Temporality**: cause must precede effect. Essential — only criterion absolutely required. Prospective cohort establishes temporality. Cross-sectional can't. Case-control: must ensure exposure before outcome
- **5. Biological gradient (dose-response)**: more exposure → more disease. Increases confidence. Example: more cigarettes/day → higher lung cancer risk. But not always — some exposures have thresholds or U-shaped curves (alcohol: moderate may be protective for CVD, heavy harmful). Absence of monotonic dose-response doesn't rule out causation; flat dose-response at high doses may indicate saturation or competing risks
- **6. Plausibility**: association coherent with existing biological knowledge. No contradiction, but absence of known mechanism doesn't rule out causation (mechanisms discovered later). Hill: "The observed association may be new to science, but the biological knowledge of the day may be insufficient to explain it" — e.g., Semmelweis handwashing and puerperal fever (germ theory not yet established). Pure plausibility without evidence is also insufficient as evidence
- **7. Coherence**: association doesn't conflict with known natural history of disease. Broad agreement with what is already known. Example: smoking and lung cancer coherent with rising lung cancer rates after smoking became common (temporal trends echoed each other), histopathological changes seen in smokers' lungs, ex-smokers have declining risk over time
- **8. Experiment**: evidence from controlled interventions (cessation studies, clinical trials). Remove exposure → reduced disease. Smoking cessation → reduced lung cancer risk (approximately 50% reduction after 10 years compared to continuing smokers). Clinical trials of sodium reduction → decreased blood pressure (using antihypertensive trials as possible experiments). Hard for harmful exposures (can't randomize people to smoke)
- **9. Analogy**: similar associations known for similar exposures. Thalidomide → birth defects (other teratogens known). Rubella → congenital defects (analogy supports other viral causes). Angiosarcoma and vinyl chloride (similar to other occupational cancers). Not essential but supportive
- **Modern views**: Rothman's sufficient-component cause model and counterfactual framework (potential outcomes: what would happen to same person with and without exposure — most rigorous causal framework, forms basis of modern causal inference with DAGs/directed acyclic graphs representing causal assumptions). Hill's criteria remain useful teaching/communication tool but not sufficient for rigorous causal inference. Current epidemiology uses directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) to identify confounders, mediators, colliders, and to determine minimal adjustment set. Causal diagrams formalize assumptions that Hill criteria left implicit. Causal criteria for the 21st century: do-calculus (Pearl 2009), target trial emulation (use observational data to mimic design of hypothetical randomized trial to avoid design flaws and common biases), instrumental variables, regression discontinuity, difference-in-differences
- **Common fallacies**: ecological fallacy (group-level association ≠ individual), Simpson's paradox (aggregate association reverses when adjusted for confounder), post hoc ergo propter hoc (after this therefore because of this — temporal sequence alone insufficient for causation: rooster crowing after dawn doesn't cause sunrise). Pyramid scheme: observational studies of association → animal models → mechanistic studies → human experiments (clinical trials) for true causality. For many environmental exposures, strong observational evidence + coherence may be the best available evidence, and requiring RCT-level evidence may be unethical or infeasible
