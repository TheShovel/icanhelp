# Mycology

## Fungi Classification
- **Taxonomy**: Fungi are a separate kingdom (closer to animals than plants). Major phyla: **Chytridiomycota** (chytrids — earliest diverging, flagellated spores/zoospores, mostly aquatic, some parasites — Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis causes amphibian chytridiomycosis, one of most devastating wildlife diseases, driving many frog species to extinction), **Zygomycota** (zygomycetes — old group, polyphyletic, bread mold Rhizopus stolonifer, black mold on strawberries, fast-growing, produce zygospores in sexual reproduction, common laboratory contaminants). **Glomeromycota** (arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi — form endomycorrhizae with 80% of land plants, obligate biotrophs, reproduce asexually, produce large multinucleate spores >100µm, critical for plant nutrient uptake, evolution of these associations ~450 mya facilitated colonization of land)
- **Ascomycota (sac fungi)**: largest phylum (~65% of described fungi). Produce sexual spores in asci (sac-like structures). Include: yeasts (Saccharomyces cerevisiae — baker's/brewer's yeast, model organism first eukaryote genome sequenced — 1996), morels (Morchella), truffles (Tuber — underground asci, symbiotic mycorrhizal with oaks/hazelnuts, prized in gastronomy, trained dogs/pigs for harvest), powdery mildews (plant pathogens), Penicillium (source of penicillin antibiotic, also cheese ripening — Roquefort, Camembert, Brie), Aspergillus (A. niger/citric acid, A. oryzae/sake soybean, A. flavus/aflatoxin/carcinogenic — produce flavus toxin, cause of aflatoxin contamination in peanut/corn/pistachio), Candida (opportunistic human pathogen), Neurospora (bread mold, model for circadian rhythm), Claviceps (ergot — produces LSD-like alkaloids, cause of ergotism/St. Anthony's fire in medieval Europe from contaminated rye, basis for LSD synthesis), Cup fungi (morels, saddle fungi, earth tongues, cup fungi with exposed asci)
- **Basidiomycota (club fungi)**: produce sexual spores on basidia (club-shaped structures). Include: mushrooms (Agaricus bisporus — supermarket button/crimini/portobello), puffballs, bracket/conk fungi (Ganoderma — reishi, Fomes fomentarius — tinder fungus historically used as tinder), stinkhorns (Phallus — foul-smelling, attract flies for spore dispersal, eggs edible in some cultures), earthstars (Geastrum), jelly fungi (Auricularia — wood ear, edible, tremella — use in herbal medicine), coral fungi (Clavarioid), chanterelles (Cantharellus — mycorrhizal with trees, distinct funnel-shaped caps with false gills running down stem), boletes (Boletus — porcini/king bolete, pores instead of gills, many edible, some toxic — avoid any with red pores), Amanita (includes deadly amanitins — death cap, destroying angel, also edible Caesar's amanita), Psilocybe (psilocybin-containing "magic mushrooms" — hallucinogenic, being researched for depression/anxiety therapeutic use at several universities, active compound psilocybin metabolized to psilocin, a 5-HT2A agonist)
- **Deuteromycota (imperfect fungi)**: artificial group for fungi with no known sexual stage. Many are actually Ascomycota whose sexual stage hasn't been observed. Includes: Penicillium (sexual stage found in some), Candida, many human pathogens. Modern classification uses molecular phylogeny to assign these properly

## Life Cycle
- **Spore**: haploid reproductive unit (sexual or asexual). Dispersed by wind (most common — millions to billions of spores, microscopic, can survive extreme conditions), water, insects, animals. Dormant until conditions favorable. Spore forms: sporangiospores (asexual, produced in sporangium = sac that holds spores, asexual spores formed at tip of hypha with mitotic nuclear division), conidia (asexual, produced on conidiophore exposed on surface). Sexual spores: zygospores (Zygomycota), ascospores (Ascomycota, in ascus), basidiospores (Basidiomycota, on basidium). Rust fungi produce up to 5 different spore types in complex life cycle alternating between two hosts (heteroecious — wheat stem rust Puccinia graminis: barberry + wheat, urediniospores = repeating stage causing epidemics, teliospores = overwintering, basidiospores from germinating teliospores infect alternate host)
- **Hyphae**: thread-like filaments (2-10 µm wide). Septate (cross-walls/dividing walls with pores allowing cytoplasm/nuclei flow — Ascomycota + Basidiomycota) vs coenocytic (no septa, continuous multinucleate — Zygomycota). Hyphae grow at tips (apical extension — Spitzenkörper/SV = vesicle supply center, vesicles delivering cell wall materials, actin cytoskeleton involved). Branched network forms mycelium. Hyphae can be: vegetative (absorptive, secrete enzymes for external digestion — saprobic fungi produce cellulases, lignin peroxidases, pectinases for breakdown of plant polymers), aerial (reproductive structures), rhizomorphs (thick, root-like cords — Armillaria, can travel meters underground, one clone in Oregon ~2400 years old, 9.6 km², largest living organism) 
- **Mycelium**: entire hyphal network. Can be microscopic or massive (honey fungus Armillaria ostoyae in Oregon ~3.5 square miles, 2400+ years old — this is one individual from a single spore, all mycelium genetically identical). Mycelium is the main body of the fungus — mushrooms are just fruiting bodies. Mycelium can be: monokaryotic (each cell contains one haploid nucleus — primary mycelium, from single spore) or dikaryotic (two compatible haploid nuclei per cell — secondary/n + n mycelium, forms when two genetically different monokaryotic mycelia fuse (plasmogamy) but nuclei delay fusion — this is the dominant mycelial stage for most Basidiomycota, can be very long-lived)
- **Fruiting body (mushroom)**: reproductive structure. Basidiocarp (mushroom): pileus (cap), lamellae/gills (where basidia produce basidiospores), stipe (stem), annulus (ring from partial veil, protects developing gills), volva (cup at base from universal veil, important for identification of Amanita species — deadly Amanita has white gills, white spores, volva and annulus present). Ascocarp (cup fungus): apothecium (cup-shaped, open — morels, cup fungi), cleistothecium (closed — powdery mildew), perithecium (flask-shaped with opening — ergot, some ascomycetes). Spore release: active (ballistospory in Basidiomycota — buller's drop mechanism, droplet of liquid on basidiospore triggered by surface tension, launches spore from basidium). Puffballs: spores released when mature fruiting body is hit by rain, animals — "puffing" cloud of billions of spores. Bracket/conk fungi: poroid hymenial layer (tubes/pores) underneath (perennial — add new layer each year). A single large puffball can produce 7 trillion spores

## Edible vs Toxic Mushrooms
- **Edible**: Agaricus bisporus (button/crimini/portobello — most cultivated mushroom worldwide, 90% of mushrooms consumed in US), shiitake (Lentinula edodes — cultivated on logs/sawdust, second most popular cultivated mushroom), oyster (Pleurotus ostreatus — easy to cultivate, grows on many substrates including coffee grounds and straw, also predatory: secretes toxin to paralyze and digest nematodes, helping supplement nitrogen in nitrogen-poor wood), enoki (Flammulina velutipes — cultivated in bottles, wild form is velvet foot, different appearance), king trumpet/king oyster (Pleurotus eryngii — meaty, thick stem), chanterelle (Cantharellus cibarius — wild-harvested, mycorrhizal, can't cultivate commercially yet, though some advances in China), porcini (Boletus edulis — mycorrhizal, wild-harvested, highly prized, dried intensifies flavor), morels (Morchella — wild, spring, can be cultivated in some controlled conditions after years of research), truffles (Tuber melanosporum/Périgord black, T. magnatum/Piedmont white — mycorrhizal, cultivated on inoculated tree roots since 1970s, trained dogs for harvest, white truffle can fetch $3000+/lb). Aspen bolete (Leccinum aurantiacum), honey mushroom (Armillaria mellea — edible but MUST be cooked thoroughly, causes GI upset raw, some people sensitive), chicken of the woods (Laetiporus sulphureus — bright orange bracket fungus, tastes like chicken, cooked thoroughly), hen of the woods/maitake (Grifola frondosa — also medicinal, immune modulating, cluster at base of oaks), wood ear (Auricularia auricula-judae — jelly fungus, used in Chinese hot and sour soup, often sold dried)
- **Deadly toxic**: Amanita phalloides (death cap — responsible for >90% of mushroom poisoning deaths worldwide. Contains amatoxins (α-amanitin) — heat-stable (survives cooking), inhibits RNA polymerase II (blocks transcription, irreversibly). Symptoms: 6-12 hr delay (asymptomatic period), then violent GI (cholera-like vomiting/diarrhea — massive fluid loss, dehydration, up to 24 hours symptom-free), then apparent recovery (1-2 days — liver begins failing silently), then liver/kidney failure (death in 5-8 days). Treatment: supportive care, IV fluids, activated charcoal, silibinin (milk thistle extract — blocks amatoxin uptake into hepatocytes), NAC, liver transplant if severe. No antidote. Found under introduced oaks and native pines. Looks similar to edible paddy straw mushrooms and some russulas. Death cap expanding range in North America and now found on both coasts
- **Amanita ocreata (destroying angel, West Coast)**: white, deadly, same amatoxins as death cap, found in oak woodlands or mixed forests. Galerina marginata (deadly Galerina — small brown mushroom, looks like some edible Psilocybe or Kuehneromyces, grows on wood, contains same amatoxins as death cap). Gyromitra esculenta (false morel — contains gyromitrin → monomethylhydrazine/MMH, rocket fuel, cooking reduces but not always sufficient, cumulative toxin, symptoms similar to amatoxin poisoning. Many cases in Europe, especially from people gathering for consumption. Known as beefsteak morel or "lumpy morel"). Cortinarius orellanus (fool's webcap — orellanine toxin, delayed nephrotoxicity 3-17 days after ingestion, can cause permanent kidney failure requiring transplant. First described in Poland in 1950s, mistaken for edible Cortinarius or Cantharellus. The Cortinarius gentilis — well known for having color similar to chanterelle but gill attachment and other structural features differ)
- **Toxic but rarely lethal**: Amanita muscaria (fly agaric — red with white spots, contains ibotenic acid + muscimol — psychoactive, not usually deadly. Symptoms: drowsiness, delirium, nausea, inebriation, vivid dreams. Used historically by Siberian shamans. Metabolized to muscimol which is more potent). Amanita pantherina (panther cap — similar toxins, stronger effects). Omphalotus illudens (jack-o'-lantern mushroom — bioluminescent (glows green under gills), poisonous but not deadly, severe GI distress. Looks similar to chanterelle — key difference: true gills (forked, decurrent) vs false gills/chiseled ridges of chanterelle). Entoloma sinuatum (poisonous Entoloma — raw flour smell, pink spores, causes severe GI, common in Europe). Paxillus involutus (brown roll-rim — immune-mediated hemolysis, previously considered edible but now known to cause severe autoimmune hemolytic anemia in some people, poisoning can occur after years of safe consumption. Contains antigen that triggers immune destruction of red blood cells)
- **IDs to learn**: cap shape/color, gill attachment (free/adnate/decurrent, notched etc), presence of ring (annulus), presence of volva (cup at base), stem (stipe) characteristics (texture, color, shape, whether it stains on bruising), spore print color (most important — place cap on paper, cover with glass, wait 4-12 hours. For gilled mushrooms: black/brown (Agaricus), pink (Entoloma, Pluteus), white (Amanita, Tricholoma, Russula), purple-brown (Psilocybe, Stropharia), yellow-brown (Cortinarius, many others). For boletes: brown, olive-brown, yellow. For russula and lactarius: white to cream). Always use multiple field guides and ID with an expert for confirmation. Absolutely never eat a mushroom unless you are 100% sure of identification. Poisonous and edible species grow side by side. First rule: when in doubt, throw it out

## Mycorrhizal Relationships
- **Ectomycorrhizae (ECM)**: hyphae wrap around root tips (Hartig net — grows between root cortical cells, forms network around cells but doesn't penetrate them). Associated with trees — pines, oaks, birches, beeches, willows, eucalyptus, dipterocarps. ~10% of plant families, mostly temperate/boreal trees. Fungus provides: water, nitrogen, phosphorus (especially important in acidic forest soils where P is limiting), micronutrients, pathogen protection (antibiotic compounds by sheath). Plant provides: 10-30% of fixed carbon (photosynthate — sugars/glucose). Mushrooms: boletes, Amanita, chanterelles, truffles, Russula, Cortinarius, Laccaria/trumpet, Lactarius (milk caps). Tree roots: often short, stubby, forked, colored mycorrhizal root tips (orange, yellow, white depending on fungal species). ECM fungal community: dozens of fungal species on single tree, with some degree of host specificity and succession (early colonizers: Laccaria, Hebeloma, Thelephora; late colonizers: Cortinarius, Russula, Boletus)
- **Arbuscular mycorrhizae (AM/endo)**: hyphae penetrate root cells (form arbuscules — tree-like branched structures inside root cortical cells, site of nutrient exchange). No mushroom fruiting bodies (Glomeromycota — produce large spores >100µm in soil, often up to 400 per 100g of soil). ~80% of land plants (most crops, grasses, tropical trees). Ancient association — fossils from 460 mya. Fungus provides: phosphorus (main benefit — up to 90% of plant P uptake in P-limited soils), nitrogen, trace elements, improved drought tolerance. Plant provides: ~20% of fixed carbon. Obligate biotrophs — can't grow without plant host (no known saprobic growth)
- **Ericoid mycorrhizae**: with plants in Ericaceae (blueberries, cranberries, rhododendrons). Hyphae penetrate root cells. Thrive in harsh, acidic, nutrient-poor soils. Fungus provides: nitrogen from organic sources (decomposes protein complexes directly, releasing simple amino acids — important adaptation in peatlands where N is almost entirely bound in organic matter). Helps with heavy metal tolerance (produces siderophores, metallothioneins). Mainly Ascomycota
- **Orchid mycorrhizae**: orchids have dust-like seeds with no nutrient reserves. Require fungal partner to germinate and provide carbon during early growth (myco-heterotrophic — <1mm seeds rely entirely on fungal infection for carbon for weeks to years before emerging as green plant or remaining subterranean achlorophyllous). Some orchids remain myco-heterotrophic throughout life (Monotropa/Indian pipe, Corallorhiza/coral root orchid). Fungal partners: various basidiomycetes (Rhizoctonia-related — Ceratobasidium, Tulasnella, Sebacina, many are saprobes or wood-rotters or mycorrhizal). Specificity varies: some orchids require very specific fungi, others are generalists. Study of orchid mycorrhizae crucial for orchid conservation and cultivation
- **Ecosystem importance**: mycorrhizal networks (common mycorrhizal networks/CMN) connect multiple plants via shared fungal hyphae. Can transfer carbon, nutrients, water, and defense signals between plants ("wood wide web" but current science debates extent and ecological significance of carbon transfer vs nutrient exchange). Lab experiments show: older trees can transfer carbon to shaded seedlings via networks, defense signaling in shared mycorrhizal networks (a plant attacked by pest sends chemical signal through network to neighbors). In practice: most transfer seems to be excess carbon not cost-free sharing, and many claims from popular science simplified

## Decomposition
- **White rot**: Basidiomycota (Phanerochaete chrysosporium — model white-rot, genome sequenced, found: has many lignin peroxidase genes, used in bioremediation for breaking down PCBs, dioxins, dyes, munitions, TNT, explosives). Decompose ALL wood components including lignin (the only organisms that can efficiently degrade lignin — a complex aromatic polymer that gives wood strength, makes up 20-35% of wood, very recalcitrant and difficult to break down. White-rots use class II peroxidases: lignin peroxidase (LiP) and manganese peroxidase (MnP), plus laccase (copper-containing enzyme) to generate free radicals that attack the polymer structure). Wood becomes pale, soft, spongy. Essential for forest carbon cycling — break down the most recalcitrant carbon in temperate and boreal forests, releasing CO₂ back to atmosphere
- **Brown rot**: Basidiomycota (Serpula lacrymans — dry rot in buildings, Gloeophyllum trabeum, Postia placenta — genomes sequenced — cause of major structural rot in North America). Decompose cellulose and hemicellulose but NOT lignin (modify lignin slightly via Fenton chemistry (Fe²⁺ + H₂O₂ → OH• hydroxyl radical) — they generate hydroxyl radicals to break open lignocellulose structure, then digest cellulose, leave brown, cubical, cracked lignin residue — brown, crumbly. Most common type of rot on conifers in northern forests (since conifer wood has recalcitrant lignin). Dominant decay type in boreal forests, while white rot more common in tropical and temperate deciduous forests. Brown rot represents only ~6% of wood-decaying basidiomycetes but dominates decomposition in cool northern coniferous forests where recalcitrant pine/spruce lignin remains intact
- **Soft rot**: Ascomycota and Deuteromycota. Decompose cellulose in high-moisture or extreme conditions (low oxygen, high nitrogen, pH extremes) where white/brown rot can't compete. Common in: wet wood, aquatic environments, soil, very dry wood. Cavity formation in secondary cell walls, eventually wood softens. More important in tropical soils and marine environments
- **Saprobic fungi**: primary decomposers in ecosystems. Substrate specialists: leaf litter (Collybia, Marasmius), dung (Coprinus — inky caps, self-digest via autodigestion into black spore-laden liquid to release spores from gills within 24 hours — dissolves its own gills after spores mature — rapid life cycle), wood, paper, cloth, leather, hair, other organic materials. Ecological succession on wood: first colonizers (sugar fungi/Zygomycota: Mucor, Rhizopus, utilize simple sugars — last weeks/months, ascomycetes including Phoma, Aspergillus), then primary decay (white rot/brown rot/fungi, decompose structural wood: months to years), then secondary decay later colonizers or mycorrhizal fungi that form fruiting bodies after wood decay (e.g. many Collybia, Armillaria, Mycena, Psilocybe appear after main decay). This succession driven by changes in substrate chemistry and microclimate

## Fungal Diseases
### Humans
- **Superficial**: dermatophytes (Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton) — ringworm/tinea (corporis/body, pedis/athlete's foot, capitis/scalp, unguium/nail), Candida (oral thrush, vaginal yeast, intertrigo/under-breast, diaper rash). Topical antifungals (clotrimazole, terbinafine, miconazole) usually enough. Oral: fluconazole, itraconazole, terbinafine (tablets) for extensive or resistant cases. Nail infections: oral terbinafine (12 weeks), topical amorolfine/lacquer but low efficacy. Predisposing factors: diabetes, obesity, immunosuppression, occlusive footwear, humid climate, frequent hand washing, use of public showers
- **Subcutaneous**: Sporothrix schenckii (sporotrichosis — rose thorn puncture, gardeners/rose thorn injuries, cat zoonotic transmission), chromoblastomycosis (Fonsecaea, Cladophialophora — tropical/subtropical, wood splinters or thorns, symptoms: slowly growing vegetative wart-like lesions on leg/foot). Treatment: itraconazole, saturated potassium iodide (KI — for sporotrichosis specifically), terbinafine, surgery — avoid incomplete excision
- **Systemic (dimorphic fungi — grow as mold at 25°C, yeast at 37°C)**: Histoplasma capsulatum (histoplasmosis — soil enriched with bat/bird droppings, Ohio/Mississippi River valleys, spelunking, chicken coops, bird roosts. Most infections mild/flu-like, but can be progressive in immunocompromised, disseminated Histoplasmosis = AIDS-defining illness). Coccidioides immitis/posadasii (valley fever — SW US desert soil, dust inhalation, arthroconidia/highly infectious. Most recover without treatment, but can be serious: chronic pulmonary, AIDs disease/disseminated/dissemination, treatment: fluconazole, amphotericin B). Blastomyces dermatitidis (blastomycosis — Great Lakes/Ohio/Mississippi River valleys, soil near waterways. Uncommon but very serious. Chronic cough, skin lesions, bone lesions. Treatment: itraconazole, amphotericin B for severe). Talaromyces marneffei (formerly Penicillium marneffei — SE Asia, bamboo rats, AIDS-defining illness. Treatment: amphotericin B then itraconazole)
- **Opportunistic**: Aspergillus fumigatus (invasive aspergillosis — most common in neutropenic patients/transplant, lung cavities, asthma: allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis/ABPA, pulmonary aspergilloma). Candida albicans (candidemia — 3rd most common hospital bloodstream infection in ICUs, fluconazole resistance increasing in C. glabrata and C. auris). Cryptococcus neoformans (meningitis — HIV/AIDS patients, soil/pigeon droppings. Treatment: fluconazole for mild cases, amphotericin B + flucytosine for meningitis). Pneumocystis jirovecii (PCP/Pneumocystis pneumonia — formerly classified as protozoan, now known as fungus — AIDS defining, prophylaxis TMP/SMX for CD4 < 200). Mucorales (mucormycosis — Rhizopus, Mucor — in diabetic ketoacidosis, neutropenia, trauma. Angioinvasive — very high mortality (50-80%), treatment: aggressive surgical debridement + high-dose IV amphotericin B, posaconazole for salvage therapy). During COVID-19 pandemic, India saw many cases of COVID-associated mucormycosis (CAM) linked to steroid treatment + diabetes

### Plants
- **Rusts**: Puccinia. Wheat stem rust (Puccinia graminis f. sp. tritici — Ug99 strain, emerged in Uganda 1999, virulent against major resistance gene Sr31, spread across Africa and Middle East, threatens global wheat supply, breeding for resistance ongoing). Many have complex life cycles with 5 spore types, alternate hosts (barberry for wheat stem rust). White pine blister rust (Cronartium ribicola — introduced from Europe, devastating 5-needle pines in North America, alternate host currant/gooseberry Ribes spp)
- **Smuts**: Ustilago. Corn smut (Ustilago maydis — huitlacoche/mexican truffle, edible delicacy in Mexico, prized for its earthy/smoky taste, mass-produced and canned in US as gourmet ingredient). Loose smut of wheat. Covered smut of barley
- **Mildews**: powdery mildews (Ascomycota — Erysiphe, Podosphaera, Sphaerotheca — white powdery coating on leaves, many host-specific, produce haustoria inside leaf epidermal cells, favored by high humidity but dry leaf surfaces). Downy mildews (not true fungi — Oomycota/water molds: Plasmopara, Peronospora, Phytophthora — more closely related to brown algae than to true fungi)
- **Crop impact**: Phytophthora infestans (late blight of potato — caused Irish Potato Famine 1845-1852, oomycete, not a true fungus but historically grouped with fungal diseases). Fusarium graminearum (head blight/scab on wheat, produces mycotoxin deoxynivalenol/DON — vomiting toxin, regulated by FDA: 1ppm finished wheat products). Botrytis cinerea (gray mold — wide host range: grapes/noble rot for sweet dessert wines in Sauternes/Tokaji, strawberries post-harvest rot, cannabis bud rot. In humid conditions, devastating. Noble rot when specific conditions allow controlled infection, concentrated sugars + flavor compounds). Magnaporthe oryzae (rice blast — most destructive rice disease, fungus infects leaves via appressorium (infection structure), can develop at high turgor pressure — turgor pressure inside appressorium can exceed 80 atmospheres, enough to rupture plant cuticle with mechanical force alone, uses melanized cell wall. Can also infect a wide range of grasses including wheat, causing wheat blast especially in South America/Bangladesh)
- **Ergot**: Claviceps purpurea — infects rye and other grasses, replaces grain with dark sclerotium (ergot body). Contains ergot alkaloids (ergotamine, ergometrine, ergocristine, ergocryptine — LSD-related compounds) → ergotism/St. Anthony's fire: convulsive (nervous system) or gangrenous (vasoconstriction → loss of limbs). Historical bread poisonings (Salem witch trials theory). Modern: controlled use in medicine — ergometrine (postpartum hemorrhage), ergotamine (migraine). Contamination limits in grain: FDA limits 0.05% ergot in rye, 0.1% in wheat

## Cultivation Basics
- **Substrate**: depends on species. Oyster mushrooms: straw, sawdust (hardwood: oak, beech, alder — avoid conifer: pine, spruce = resins inhibit growth, but some species can tolerate, such as P. pulmonarius), coffee grounds, paper. Shiitake: hardwood logs (oak, beech, sweetgum, maple) or sawdust blocks. Button mushrooms: composted horse manure + straw (pasteurized). Enoki: sawdust + rice bran in bottles. Lion's mane (Hericium erinaceus): hardwood sawdust. King oyster: sawdust blocks. Morels: specific soil conditions + fire ecology — most cultivation attempts unsuccessful until recent Chinese breakthroughs (reported 2018 successful indoor cultivation of Morchella rufobrunnea, followed by widespread M. sextelata/improved cultivation in Sichuan and Yunnan provinces, but still highly variable yields and trade secrets mostly kept by Chinese growers)
- **Sterilization vs pasteurization**: substrate must be free of competing organisms. Straw/coffee grounds: pasteurize at 160-180°F for 1-2 hours (hot water bath or steam) or cold water lime soak (calcium hydroxide Ca(OH)₂ pH 12-13 for 12-24 hours). Sawdust: pressure sterilize at 15 PSI/250°F for 90 min (autoclave or large pressure cooker). Logs: fresh-cut (within 2 weeks of felling, while still alive, not contaminated by competing wood-decomposing fungi). Supplementation: bran (wheat, rice, oat), soy hulls, corn meal added to sawdust for higher yields (standard: 20% bran at 60% moisture)
- **Spawn**: mycelium on grain (rye, millet, sorghum, birdseed, popcorn) or wooden dowels (for log inoculation). Purchase from reputable supplier (Field & Forest, Fungi Perfecti, spawnworks). Grain spawn: sterilize grain, inoculate with culture (liquid culture/mycelium in syringe, or agar wedge taken from a Petri dish), incubate at 70-80°F until fully colonized (typically 2-4 weeks). Shake at 30-50% to distribute mycelium. Testing: clean spawn should smell mushroomy, not sour/fermented (bacterial contamination) or sweet (yeast). Green/black molds = contamination, discard or bury outdoors far from clean grow area
- **Fruiting conditions**: trigger: drop temperature, increase fresh air exchange (FAE), increase humidity (90-95% RH), reduce CO₂ below 1000 ppm (higher CO₂ = long stems, tiny caps — "etiolation"). Light: ambient (not direct sun, mushrooms orient growth toward light, helps cap development, important for proper morphology, especially for oyster mushrooms needing 12 hr light cycle). Mist walls/floor. Provide diffused light (12 hours on/off). Pin formation: mycelium knots, then forms primordia (tiny pins), then matures into mushrooms. First flush: heavy harvest, then 1-3 more after soaking. For most cultivated species, between flushes soak substrate block in water for several hours to rehydrate — blocks lose 30% water weight each flush
- **Log cultivation**: drill holes (5/16" bit, 1-1.5" deep) in fresh-cut hardwood logs (3-6" diameter, 36-40" long, cut in late winter/early spring when sap is dormant). Inoculate with plug spawn/log spawn, seal with wax (cheese wax or food-grade paraffin wax, melted). Stack in shady, damp area (log pile, crib style — crisscross for good aeration, cover with shade cloth). Soak 20-30 minutes before fruiting. Shiitake: first harvest 6-12 months after inoculation, fruiting for 3-5 years per log (logs dry out eventually). Oyster: faster (3-6 months) but shorter production life on logs. Total yield from a shiitake log typically: 0.5-2 lbs per log per year depending on log size/species. Small-scale log production requires about 50 logs to supply a family with shiitake
- **Contamination**: green mold (Trichoderma — most common competitor in mushroom cultivation, especially T. harzianum in button/agaricus compost, T. aggressivum = green mold disease/compost mold that devastated Pennsylvania mushroom industry in 1990s). Blue-green mold (Penicillium — over-saturated substrate), black mold (Aspergillus — low pH, high starch), bacterial blotch (Pseudomonas tolaasii — causes brown spots/brown blotch on mushroom caps, looks like bacterial rot, high humidity + standing water on caps). Prevention: clean technique, proper sterilization, adequate fresh air, remove contaminated blocks immediately from grow area. Control: hydrated lime to adjust pH to 7-8 minimum, hydrogen peroxide diluted sprays for bacterial blotch, improved ventilation and reduced humidity for Trichoderma. Do not put contaminated blocks in your garden compost — spores will persist
- **Containers**: monotub (simple plastic tub with holes, polyfill filter, automated misting systems — lowest-tech but works, for home growers), grow bags (filters allow gas exchange, autoclavable), fruiting chambers (Martha tent — plastic shelving unit + humidifier + ventilation), commercial (tray system, shelf system, automated, climate-controlled rooms, 12,000 sq ft facilities for large production)

## Medicinal Mushrooms
- **Ganoderma lucidum (reishi/lingzhi)**: used in TCM for 2000+ years. Active: beta-glucans (immune modulation — activate dendritic cells, NK cells, macrophages, T cells), triterpenoids/ganoderic acids (anti-inflammatory, liver protection, anti-tumor in vitro and animal studies: inhibit cell proliferation, induce apoptosis, antimetastatic, but human clinical data limited for cancer efficacy). COX-2 inhibition reported. Adaptogenic. Forms: alcohol extract (triterpenoids) + hot water extract (beta-glucans) combined for full spectrum. Research: cholesterol reduction, hypertension, fatigue. Generally safe but caution with blood thinners (possible anticoagulant effect) and hypotension. Quality varies widely. Very bitter — most consume as double-extracted tincture (hot water + alcohol extraction recommended for full spectrum)
- **Hericium erinaceus (lion's mane/yamabushitake)**: nerve growth factor (NGF) stimulator — hericenones (from fruiting body, lipid-soluble) and erinacines (from mycelium, water-soluble and alcohol-soluble) cross blood-brain barrier, stimulate NGF synthesis in brain. Research: cognitive function (improvement in mild cognitive impairment — double-blind placebo-controlled study 2019: 30g/day for 16 weeks improved cognitive test scores in older adults), nerve regeneration, depression/anxiety (reduced depression/anxiety in menopausal women in pilot study), gastric health (protects gastric mucosa, inhibits H. pylori). Double extraction recommended. No serious side effects known. 1-3g/day powder or 1-3ml tincture 2-3x/day. Taste: mild, seafood-like, good in cooking or tea, can be cooked like any edible mushroom
- **Grifola frondosa (maitake/hen of the woods)**: beta-glucan D-fraction (MD-Fraction). Research: immune stimulation (increase NK cell activity + T cells + macrophages + cytokines including IL-12 and INF-gamma), blood sugar regulation (GLP-1 upregulation, metformin synergy in diabetic rats). Breast/prostate cancer research: in vitro and animal studies show anti-tumor, xenograft reduction, immune checkpoint synergy — clinical studies limited. Maitake SX-fraction for blood glucose. Generally safe, but some blood sugar interactions. Eating whole maitake mushroom is healthy but active beta-glucan content varies. D-fraction usually extracted and concentrated (standardized to 25-40% beta-glucans). Available in capsules or as extract powder. Standard dose: 500-1000mg whole mushroom or 200-500mg D-fraction extract
- **Trametes versicolor (turkey tail/yunzhi)**: polysaccharide-K (PSK/krestin) and polysaccharide-PSP (from related T. versicolor, used extensively in Japan as adjuvant cancer therapy, approved as prescription cancer drug for 40+ years, PSK — 3g/day). PSK and PSP are protein-bound polysaccharides (beta-glucans with peptide bonds). Research: improves survival in gastric, colorectal, breast, lung cancer (adjunctive to chemo — meta-analyses show 5-year survival increased RR 0.73 and 0.81 for gastric and colorectal cancers in adjuvant PSK-treated patients), improves immune function, reduces chemo side effects (fatigue, nausea, appetite loss). Approved in Japan (as krestin) since 1970s for post-operative combination cancer therapy — not approved in US/Europe as drug. Standard hot water extract or dual extract. Dose: 1-6g/day (extract standardized to 25-40% polysaccharides). For PSK: 3g daily. Safety: very safe; may interact with some chemo (check with oncologist). Studies of breast cancer (BC + Turkey Tail/QOL combined trial showed improved immune markers, NK cell function, and reduced fatigue)
- **Cordyceps sinensis / Ophiocordyceps sinensis**: parasitic fungus on caterpillar larvae (Tibetan plateau, at altitudes 3000-5000m). Rare, expensive ($10,000-50,000/lb, harvested by Himalayan communities — one of the most expensive biological materials by weight, over 1000x the price of silver, currently overharvested leading to 95% decline in some areas and listed in IUCN Red List). Active: cordycepin (3'-deoxyadenosine), adenosine, polysaccharides. Lab alternatives: Cordyceps militaris (cultivable, same compounds). Research: ATP production (improves cellular energy — increases VO₂ max, oxygen utilization, and aerobic capacity in exercise studies — meta-analysis 2019: 2.5% improvement VO₂max in trained athletes), anti-aging (possible anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, NAD+ protection, but all human clinical evidence is moderate), libido (traditional use as aphrodisiac — animal studies show improved testosterone, sexual behavior, human studies limited but promising for erectile function), kidney function. Most studies: on C. militaris (cultivated, standardized). Standard extract dose: 500-2000mg/day. Cordycepin is the most studied component — it's an analog of adenosine, has some anti-leukemia/anti-cancer potential in cell culture. Safety: caution with autoimmune disease, blood thinners, uncontrolled diabetes, and with gout (may increase uric acid levels in susceptible individuals)
