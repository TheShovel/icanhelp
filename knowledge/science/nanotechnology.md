# Nanotechnology

Materials and devices at the nanometer scale — properties, fabrication, and applications.

## Scale & Context
- **Definition**: 1-100 nanometers (1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m). DNA width ~2.5 nm. Virus ~100 nm. Human hair ~80,000 nm. Atom ~0.1 nm
- **Quantum effects dominate at nanoscale**: Quantum confinement (band gap increases as particle size decreases — quantum dots), tunneling (STM, flash memory), surface plasmon resonance (gold nanoparticles — color changes with size), superparamagnetism (magnetic nanoparticles lose permanent magnetization below critical size)

## Carbon Nanomaterials
- **Graphene**: Single atom-thick layer of carbon in hexagonal lattice (2D). Exceptional: strongest material known (130 GPa tensile), highest thermal conductivity (5000 W/mK), very high electron mobility (200,000 cm²/Vs), transparent (97.7% light transmission). Production: mechanical exfoliation (scotch tape — Nobel 2010, Geim/Novoselov), CVD (chemical vapor deposition — large area), graphene oxide (solution processable). Applications: composites (stronger + lighter), conductive inks, sensors, supercapacitors, desalination membranes
- **Carbon nanotubes (CNTs)**: Rolled graphene sheets — Single-walled (SWCNT, 1-2 nm diameter) or Multi-walled (MWCNT, multiple concentric tubes). Metallic or semiconducting depending on chirality (how sheet is rolled). Properties similar to graphene but 1D. Applications: structural composites (bike frames, tennis rackets), field-effect transistors (CNTFET), conductive films, water filtration
- **Fullerenes (C60, buckyballs)**: Spherical carbon cage molecules (60 atoms, soccer ball shape). Discovered 1985 (Smalley, Curl, Kroto — Nobel). Electron acceptor properties. Used in organic photovoltaics, lubricants

## Nanoparticle Types
- **Quantum dots**: Semiconductor nanocrystals (CdSe, InP, PbS). Size-tunable emission (smaller = bluer, larger = redder). Bright, photostable (don't bleach like organic dyes). Applications: QLED displays (Samsung — quantum dot TVs), biological imaging (cell labeling), solar cells (quantum dot solar cells)
- **Gold nanoparticles**: Spheres, rods, shells — color changes with size/shape (red, blue, purple). Surface plasmon resonance (absorb/scatter specific wavelengths). Applications: biomedical (photothermal therapy — laser heats nanoparticles to kill cancer), diagnostics (lateral flow assays — COVID tests), drug delivery, SERS (surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy)
- **Iron oxide nanoparticles (SPIONs)**: Superparamagnetic. Used in: MRI contrast agents (T2 contrast), magnetic hyperthermia (heat tumors), magnetic separation (DNA, proteins, cells), targeted drug delivery (magnetic field guides particles)
- **Silica nanoparticles**: Mesoporous (pores can carry drugs). Biocompatible, easy to functionalize. Used for: drug delivery, controlled release, biosensors
- **Liposomes**: Phospholipid bilayer vesicles. Used for: drug delivery (Doxil — chemotherapy), vaccines (mRNA vaccines use lipid nanoparticles), gene therapy

## Fabrication Methods
- **Top-down**: Carve from bulk material — lithography (photolithography, e-beam lithography — for microchips), etching, milling. Limits: diffraction limit (optical lithography ~10nm, e-beam ~5nm but slow). Used in semiconductor manufacturing
- **Bottom-up**: Build from atoms/molecules — self-assembly (molecules spontaneously organize — block copolymers, DNA origami), CVD, sol-gel (solution → gel → solid), molecular beam epitaxy (atom-by-atom deposition for thin films). More precise, scalable for some applications
- **Self-assembly**: Molecules organize into ordered structures without external direction. Block copolymers (form nanoscale patterns — lamellae, cylinders, spheres for lithography masks). DNA origami (fold DNA into 2D/3D shapes at 10-100nm scale — programmable)

## Applications
- **Medicine**: Targeted drug delivery (nanoparticles accumulate in tumors via EPR effect — enhanced permeability and retention). Theranostics (therapy + diagnostics combined). Imaging agents (MRI, PET, NIR fluorescence). Antibacterial (silver nanoparticles — antimicrobial coatings for wound dressings)
- **Electronics**: Smaller transistors (~3nm node in 2024. Next: 2nm gate-all-around FETs). Non-volatile memory (flash, MRAM, RRAM). Sensors (CNT gas sensors, nanowire biosensors — detect single molecule). Flexible electronics (CNT/graphene transparent conductors for bendable screens)
- **Energy**: More efficient solar cells (perovskite quantum dots, plasmonic enhancement). Better batteries (nanostructured anodes — silicon nanowires expand without cracking, higher capacity). Supercapacitors (graphene/CNT — fast charge/discharge, high power density). Catalysis (nanocatalysts — higher surface area, more active sites. Pt nanoparticles in fuel cells)
- **Environment**: Water filtration (nanofiltration membranes, CNT filters remove bacteria/viruses/heavy metals). Air purification (photocatalytic TiO₂ nanoparticles break down pollutants). Self-cleaning surfaces (superhydrophobic/ hydrophilic nanostructures — lotus effect). Catalytic converters (nanoparticle catalysts)
- **Cosmetics**: Sunscreen (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide nanoparticles — transparent, effective UV block — but safety concerns about nanoparticle penetration). Anti-aging (retinol/antioxidant-loaded nanoparticles for deeper skin penetration)

## Safety & Risks
- **Nanoparticle toxicity**: Small size → can enter cells → oxidative stress, inflammation, DNA damage. Factors: composition, size (smaller = more reactive), shape (rods/fibers more toxic than spheres? — asbestos-like for long CNTs), surface coating, dose, exposure route
- **Exposure routes**: Inhalation (most concerning — nanoparticles reach alveoli, enter blood), ingestion (food additives — TiO₂ nanoparticles as whitener), skin (limited penetration through intact skin, but can enter through cuts/abrasions)
- **Regulatory**: FDA, EPA, EU REACH have some nano-specific guidelines. No mandatory labeling for consumer products (except EU for cosmetics). Responsible development framework — "nano-specific" regulations still evolving
- **Environmental impact**: Nanoparticles from consumer products → wastewater → environment. Effects on aquatic organisms understood minimally. Bioaccumulation unclear. Need lifecycle assessment for each nanomaterial
