# Weather & Meteorology

## Atmosphere Layers
- **Troposphere** (0-12 km): weather happens here, temperature drops with height (~6.5°C/km), contains 80% of atmosphere's mass
  - Tropopause: boundary, temperature stops dropping, jet streams here
- **Stratosphere** (12-50 km): ozone layer absorbs UV, temperature rises with height (warmest at top), stable (commercial jets fly here to avoid turbulence)
- **Mesosphere** (50-80 km): coldest layer (~-90°C), meteors burn up here
- **Thermosphere** (80-700 km): temperature rises to 1500°C+ (but feels cold — density is extremely low), aurora borealis/australis here
- **Exosphere** (>700 km): transitions to space, atoms escape

## Weather Elements
- **Temperature**: measure of kinetic energy of air molecules — Celsius (water freezes 0°C, boils 100°C), Fahrenheit (freezes 32°F, boils 212°F)
  - Heat index: how hot it FEELS when humidity is added (body can't cool via sweat evaporation)
  - Wind chill: how cold it FEELS from wind removing body heat — only affects living things, not inanimate objects
- **Humidity**: water vapor in air
  - Relative humidity: % of saturation at current temperature (warm air holds more moisture)
  - Dew point: temperature at which air is saturated — better measure of "mugginess" than relative humidity
  - Dew point <50°F = dry, 50-60 = comfortable, 60-70 = humid, 70+ = oppressive
- **Air pressure**: weight of air column above — measured in millibars (mb) or inches of mercury (inHg)
  - High pressure (anticyclone): sinking air → clear skies, light winds
  - Low pressure (cyclone): rising air → clouds, precipitation, stronger winds
  - Standard sea level pressure: 1013.25 mb (29.92 inHg). Rapid pressure drop → approaching storm
  - Isobars: lines of equal pressure on weather maps — closer together = stronger winds
- **Wind**: air moving from high to low pressure — named by direction it comes FROM (north wind = from north)
  - Global patterns: trade winds (east to west near equator), westerlies (mid-latitudes), polar easterlies
  - Jet stream: narrow band of strong wind (100-200 mph) at tropopause, separates cold polar air from warm subtropical air — steers weather systems

## Cloud Types
- **High clouds** (>20,000 ft): cirrus (wispy, ice crystals, fair weather), cirrostratus (thin veil, halo around sun), cirrocumulus (small ripples)
- **Mid clouds** (6,500-20,000 ft): altostratus (gray sheet, sun diffused, light rain), altocumulus (gray puffy patches, "sheep backs")
- **Low clouds** (<6,500 ft): stratus (gray uniform layer, drizzle/fog), stratocumulus (low lumpy, some gaps), nimbostratus (thick dark, steady rain/snow)
- **Vertical clouds** (span multiple levels): cumulus (puffy cotton balls, fair weather), cumulonimbus (towering, thunderstorm, hail, tornadoes — anvil-shaped top)
  - Cumulonimbus: most dangerous cloud type — lightning, heavy rain, hail, downbursts, tornadoes. Top can reach 60,000+ ft
- **Fog**: cloud at ground level — radiation (clear night, ground cools), advection (warm moist air over cool surface), upslope (air forced up mountain), steam (cold air over warm water)

## Storms
- **Thunderstorm**: cumulonimbus cloud — lightning, thunder, heavy rain, strong winds, hail
  - Three stages: cumulus (updraft, building), mature (updraft + downdraft, lightning, rain), dissipating (downdraft dominates)
  - Lightning: electric discharge — strike temperature 50,000°F (5x hotter than sun's surface). Thunder is air expanding from heat. Count seconds between flash and thunder ÷ 5 = miles away
  - Severe thunderstorm: hail 1"+ or winds 58+ mph or tornado. Warning = take shelter
- **Tornado**: violently rotating column of air from thunderstorm to ground — most common in US (Tornado Alley: Oklahoma, Kansas, Nebraska, Iowa, Texas)
  - Enhanced Fujita Scale: EF0 (65-85 mph, minor) to EF5 (200+ mph, incredible damage)
  - Warning signs: green sky, wall cloud, rotation, roar like freight train. Safe place: basement or interior room on lowest floor, no windows, cover head
  - US averages 1,200 tornadoes/year — most are EF0-EF1. Tornadoes CAN occur outside Tornado Alley (including in cities, mountains)
- **Hurricane** (typhoon/cyclone in other regions): tropical cyclone with sustained winds 74+ mph
  - Energy source: warm ocean water (minimum 80°F). Storm surge is the deadliest hazard (not wind)
  - Categories: 1 (74-95 mph, minimal) → 5 (157+ mph, catastrophic). Saffir-Simpson scale measures wind only
  - Naming: rotating list of 21 names per year (Atlantic). Retired if storm was deadly/costly (Katrina, Sandy, Harvey, Ian)
- **Blizzard**: snow + wind ≥35 mph + visibility <¼ mile for 3+ hours — doesn't require heavy snowfall (blowing snow counts)
- **Derecho**: widespread, long-lived straight-line windstorm associated with thunderstorm complex — winds 58+ mph along line >250 miles

## Weather Forecasting
- **Models**: GFS (US global model, 13 km resolution), ECMWF (European, best global model), HRRR (US high-res, 3 km, updates hourly), NAM (North American, regional)
  - Ensemble forecasting: run model multiple times with slight variations → probability forecast (better than single deterministic run)
  - Skill decreases with time: 3-day forecast as accurate as 1-day forecast 30 years ago, 5-day now equals 3-day then
- **Tools**: weather.gov (NWS — forecast discussion, radar), weather.com, windy.com (visualizations), radarscope (phone radar app)
  - Raw model data: pivotalweather.com, tropicaltidbits.com — for weather enthusiasts
- **Reading a forecast**: "40% chance of rain" = 40% of models indicate measurable rain at that location — means probability, not coverage area or duration
- **Limitations**: beyond 10 days, forecasts have low skill. Small-scale features (individual thunderstorms) can't be predicted more than hours in advance — watch for "possible" vs "likely" wording

## Climate vs Weather
- **Weather**: what's happening now/soon (hours to days)
- **Climate**: long-term patterns (30-year averages)
- **Climate change**: global average temperature +1.2°C since pre-industrial — caused by greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O) from human activities
  - Effects: more extreme weather (heat waves more frequent/intense, heavier rainfall events, stronger hurricanes), sea level rise, droughts, ecosystem disruption
  - "Climate is what you expect. Weather is what you get." — Robert Heinlein
