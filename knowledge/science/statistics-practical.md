# Statistics Practical Guide

## Mean, Median, Mode
The mean is the arithmetic average and is sensitive to outliers. The median is the middle value and is better for skewed data like income or home prices. The mode is the most common value.

## Variability
Range shows minimum to maximum but is very sensitive to outliers. Standard deviation shows typical spread around the mean. Interquartile range shows the middle 50% and is robust for skewed data.

## Correlation Is Not Causation
Correlation means two variables move together. It does not prove one causes the other. Confounders, reverse causation, selection bias, and coincidence can create misleading relationships.

## Sampling Bias
A sample should represent the population being studied. Online polls, voluntary surveys, convenience samples, and small samples can be biased. Random sampling and high response rates improve reliability.

## P-Values
A p-value estimates how surprising the observed data would be if the null hypothesis were true. It does not measure the probability that the hypothesis is true, and it does not show effect size or practical importance.

## Confidence Intervals
A confidence interval gives a range of plausible values for an estimate under repeated-sampling assumptions. Wide intervals indicate uncertainty. Practical interpretation should consider study design, bias, and effect size.

## Base Rates
Base rates are the underlying frequency of an event. Rare events can produce many false positives even with good tests. Always ask: how common is this condition or event before testing?

## Absolute vs Relative Risk
A risk increasing from 1 in 10,000 to 2 in 10,000 is a 100% relative increase but only a 0.01 percentage point absolute increase. Reports using only relative risk can sound more dramatic than the real-world impact.

## Regression to the Mean
Extreme measurements often move closer to average on later measurement due to natural variation. This can make ineffective interventions appear helpful if they are tried at the worst moment.

## Practical Checklist
Ask: What is the sample size? Who was sampled? Is there a control group? Are outcomes meaningful? Are effects absolute or relative? Could confounders explain it? Are results replicated?
