# Seismology

## Earthquake Causes

### Plate Tectonics
- **Lithospheric plates**: Earth's surface divided into ~15 major plates (Pacific, North American, Eurasian, African, Antarctic, Indo-Australian, South American, Nazca, Cocos, Juan de Fuca, Philippine, Arabian, Caribbean, Scotia, Somali). Plates move 2-15 cm/yr (about fingernail growth rate). Driven by: mantle convection (slow flow of hot rock in asthenosphere), slab pull (dense subducting plate sinks and pulls rest of plate along — thought to be dominant driving force, ridge push (elevated mid-ocean ridges push plates apart). Plate edges = where earthquakes concentrate
- **Divergent boundaries**: plates move apart. Mid-ocean ridges (Mid-Atlantic Ridge, East Pacific Rise). Shallow earthquakes (≤20 km depth), low magnitude (typically M<6). Basaltic volcanism creates new crust. Continental rifting: East African Rift — earthquakes shallow, some moderate M<7. Normal faulting dominates
- **Convergent boundaries**: plates collide. Three types: (1) Oceanic-oceanic: older/denser plate subducts (Marianas, Tonga, Aleutians, Japan, Sumatra-Andaman, Caribbean). Deep earthquakes (up to 700 km depth — Wadati-Benioff zones: dipping plane of seismicity along subducting slab, with compression and tension patterns). Largest earthquakes occur here (M9+ — 1960 Chile 9.5, 2004 Sumatra 9.1). (2) Oceanic-continental: oceanic plate subducts under continent (Andes, Cascadia, Japan, Kamchatka). Coastal mountains, volcanic arcs (Andes, Cascades, Japan). Great megathrust earthquakes (Cascadia 1700 — M~9, Japan 2011 9.1, Chile 1960 9.5). (3) Continental-continental: collision, no subduction (Himalayas — India colliding with Eurasia, continues ~5 cm/yr). Broad deformation zone, shallow to intermediate, M<8 (Himalayan earthquakes M~8: 1934 Nepal-Bihar, 2015 Gorkha Nepal 7.8, 1950 Assam 8.6). Some of the highest seismic hazard on Earth
- **Transform boundaries**: plates slide past each other. Strike-slip faulting. San Andreas Fault (California — Pacific/NA plates ~5 cm/yr relative motion). Shallow earthquakes, M<8 typically (max along San Andreas: 1906 San Francisco 7.9). Dead Sea Transform, North Anatolian Fault (Turkey — 1999 Izmit 7.6), Alpine Fault (New Zealand), Motagua Fault (Guatemala). Along oceanic transforms: ridge-ridge transforms in the Atlantic and Pacific produce smaller earthquakes

### Fault Types
- **Normal fault**: hanging wall moves DOWN relative to footwall. Tensional stress (plates pulling apart). Divergent boundaries, extensional regions (Basin and Range, East African Rift, Aegean Sea). Typically M<7. Fault dips 50-70°. Earthquakes: earthquakes in normal faulting regimes are typically moderate (M6-7). Associated with deep-seated isostatic processes: earthquakes in Basin & Range main shock/aftershock sequences are moderate but frequent
- **Reverse (thrust) fault**: hanging wall moves UP relative to footwall. Compressional stress. Convergent boundaries. Low angle thrust (<30° = megathrust subduction zone interface faults). Shallow dip. Generate largest earthquakes (M9+ on subduction megathrusts). Thrust faults in fold-and-thrust belts (Himalayas, Zagros, Taiwan). Blind thrust faults: don't break surface (1994 Northridge 6.7 — caused $20B damage in LA, no visible surface rupture)
- **Strike-slip fault**: horizontal motion along fault line. Right-lateral (San Andreas — as you face fault, opposite side moves right) or left-lateral (North Anatolian). Vertical walls move laterally past each other. Stress: shear. Shallow (usually ≤20 km). M up to 8 (rare 8+). Classic example: 1906 San Andreas rupture 430 km, 7.9 Mw with up to 6 m offset → produced massive fires in San Francisco. Nearly pure strike-slip: can be vertical to near-vertical fault plane
- **Oblique-slip**: mix of dip-slip and strike-slip motion. Common in transpressional/transtensional zones (San Andreas has some compression at Big Bend, some extension at Salton Trough). Most faults have some oblique component in real-world application of these pure modes derived from Anderson's theory of faulting

## Seismic Waves
- **Body waves**: travel through Earth's interior
  - **P waves** (primary/compressional): fastest (~5-7 km/s near surface, up to 13 km/s in deep mantle and core). Push-pull motion (compressional/dilatational) parallel to wave direction. Travel through solids, liquids, gases. Arrive first. Sound wave in rock. P-wave shadow zone: 103°-143° from epicenter — no direct P waves → evidence for liquid outer core. P-wave velocity varies due to rock composition, temperature, pressure, water saturation. Used for tomography: creating 3D images of Earth's interior
  - **S waves** (secondary/shear): slower (~3-4 km/s near surface, up to 7 km/s in mantle). Perpendicular motion (shear). Only through solids (cannot pass through liquid outer core). S-wave shadow zone: >103° from epicenter → indicates liquid outer core. S waves are more destructive to buildings than P waves (larger amplitude, longer period, and more horizontal motion shaking)
  - Particle motion: P waves: alternating compression and dilation parallel to wave propagation. S waves: must be polarized into SH (horizontally polarized) and SV (vertically polarized) components for seismological analysis
- **Surface waves**: travel along Earth's surface. Slower than body waves but larger amplitude and longer duration. Cause most earthquake damage because of large ground displacement and longer shaking duration
  - **Love waves**: horizontal shear motion (side-to-side). Faster than Rayleigh waves. Only on layered Earth (requires velocity increase with depth). Most destructive to building foundations. Can travel through ocean basins as well
  - **Rayleigh waves**: rolling motion (vertical + horizontal, elliptical retrograde — like ocean wave, "ground rolls"). Slower than Love. Produces most of the "shaking" felt. Can be strongly amplified in soft soil (sedimentary basins). Longer-period Rayleigh waves (>20 sec) can travel globally and circle Earth multiple times
- **Wave interactions**: reflection (bounces off layers), refraction (bends — Snell's law applies in the same way as light waves), diffraction (bends around obstacles — important around core-mantle boundary), scattering (small-scale heterogeneities), attenuation (energy loss — geometrical spreading + anelastic attenuation → Q value). Seismic tomography uses wave travel times to create 3D images of Earth interior, analogous to CT scanning

## Magnitude Scales
- **Richter local magnitude (ML)**: original scale (Charles Richter 1935, Caltech). Based on maximum amplitude on Wood-Anderson seismograph at 100 km. Logarithmic: each unit = 10x amplitude, ~31.6x energy. Limited: saturates above ~M6.5, accurate only for Southern California, shallow, <600 km distance. Not used for large earthquakes or deep events
- **Moment magnitude (Mw)**: now standard for all earthquakes. Based on seismic moment (M₀ = μ × A × D): shear modulus × fault area × average slip. Mw = ²/₃ log₁₀(M₀) − 10.7 (Hanks & Kanamori 1979). Does NOT saturate (works for all sizes). Directly related to physical properties of fault rupture. Used by USGS and global agencies. Most published large earthquake magnitudes are Mw
- **Other scales**: mb (body wave magnitude, uses P-wave amplitude, good for teleseismic distances, saturates ~M6-7). Ms (surface wave magnitude, uses 20-sec surface waves, saturates ~M8-8.5). Md (duration magnitude). mB (broadband body-wave, less saturation, from IRIS). Energy magnitude (Me): based on radiated seismic energy (not just moment) — distinguishes "slow" earthquakes, tsunamigenic (more energy = larger tsunami) from "fast" energetic normal earthquakes
- **Energy comparison**: Mw 5 = small atomic bomb (WWII). Mw 6 = Hiroshima bomb. Mw 7 = ~20x M6 (32x energy per unit, but 32x scaling means each unit increase corresponds to about 32x energy, not 10x). Mw 8 = ~1000x M6. Mw 9 = ~32,000x M6. Largest recorded: 1960 Chile 9.5 Mw. Monthly: ~1 M8, ~13 M7, ~130 M6 per year globally

## Intensity — Modified Mercalli Intensity (MMI)
- **Subjective measure**: based on observed effects on people, structures, ground surface. Roman numerals I-XII. Does NOT require instruments. Used for historical earthquakes (pre-instrumental), damage assessment, public communication. ShakeMaps convert instrumental records to estimated MMI for public safety
- **MMI Scale**:
  - I (Not felt): detected by instruments only
  - II-III (Weak to Weak/light): feels like passing truck, hanging objects swing, some people inside feel it. Many unaware
  - IV (Light): felt indoors by many, dishes/windows rattle, parked cars rock, felt like heavy truck striking building
  - V (Moderate): felt by nearly everyone, sleepers waken, small objects displaced, doors swing, liquids spill
  - VI (Strong): felt by all, difficult to stand, furniture moved, plaster cracks, damage slight in well-built structures but considerable in poorly built
  - VII-VIII (Very strong to Severe): damage slight to moderate in well-built buildings, considerable in poorly built structures, chimneys fall, difficult to stand, ground cracks, cars affected in steering
  - IX (Violent): general panic, damage great in well-built structures (frames shift, foundations crack), ground cracks conspicuously, underground pipes break
  - X-XII (Extreme to Catastrophic): most masonry structures destroyed, ground badly cracked, rails bent, landslides, objects thrown into air, total destruction with visible wave in ground surface
- **Intensity vs magnitude**: one earthquake has ONE magnitude but MANY intensities (decrease with distance). Modified Mercalli scale is site-specific: reflects local geology, building quality, distance from fault. MMI VII typically corresponds to felt area radius which defines general potential for damage

## Earthquake Prediction vs Early Warning
- **Prediction**: specific time, place, magnitude. Currently NOT possible for individual earthquakes. No reliable precursors identified despite decades of research. Short-term prediction remains elusive (foreshocks not reliable — only ~5% of earthquakes have identifiable foreshocks; animal behavior and radon emissions not reproducible). Parkfield prediction (1985-1993) failed. Better understanding: statistical recurrence models: earthquake likelihood ≠ prediction. Probability forecasts for time windows of years/decades — e.g., USGS Working Group on California Earthquake Probabilities (UCERF3): 30-year probability for specific faults (≥5 on San Andreas segments with 22% probability of M≥6.7 by 2036)
- **Long-term forecasting**: (years-decades): based on paleoseismology (trenching to find past earthquake evidence in fault offsets, radiocarbon dating of organic material in paleosols — e.g., Wrightwood, CA — plus satellite geodesy/GPS to measure strain accumulation and known seismic gaps. Seismic gap hypothesis: faults sections that haven't ruptured in longest time are due for next event. Applied successfully for: 1978 Oaxaca earthquake (Ohtake et al.), 1995 Kobe earthquake (partially successful gap hypothesis) and 2004 Sumatra-Andaman (along Sunda Trench). Unsuccessfully applied for: 1989 Loma Prieta (partial segment along San Andreas, odd angle), 2014 Iquique northern Chile gap (gap filled by 8.2 foreshock then 8.1 mainshock same week — debate whether gap was "filled")
- **Early warning systems**: detect initial P-wave (fast, non-destructive) before S-wave (slower, destructive). Provides seconds to minutes of warning. Japan: JMA system (2007) — detects near epicenter via dense Hi-net seismograph network (700+ stations). Sends alerts via TV, radio, cell broadcast, railroads for automatic train braking — Shinkansen bullet trains stop automatically (in 2004 and 2011 systems worked, decelerated before shaking). Mexico: SASMEX (1991) — for Guerrero Gap, Mexico City warning (60 sec before S-waves for distant earthquakes). US: ShakeAlert (West Coast, 2019 public launch) — delivery via Wireless Emergency Alerts, apps (MyShake, QuakeAlertUSA). Limitations: blind zone near epicenter (no warning for those closest), network density issues, false alarms, magnitude underestimation for large events early in process. Threshold-based: systems robustly warn when M>5, but M6 initial detection may shake before warning. Can't predict — only detect
- **Potential actions**: 10-60 sec warning — drop, cover, hold on; open doors (prevent jamming), stop traffic (trains, cars), move away from hazards (glass, heavy objects, shelves), gas shutoff, PPE, initiate emergency protocols, send alerts. Shinkansen example: every Japanese high-speed train automatically brakes within this P-wave detection window. Tohoku 2011: earliest warnings issued 15 sec before strongest shaking (useful time for evacuation, but damage was extreme in tsunami). System limitation: blind zone for those within 30-50 km of epicenter

## Building Codes for Seismic Safety
- **Key principles**: ductility (ability to deform without brittle failure — steel reinforcement), strength (resist lateral forces), stiffness (limit drift/deformation), redundancy (multiple load paths), energy dissipation (absorb shaking energy), regularity (simple symmetric shapes reduce torsion). Regularity = avoiding soft stories, mass irregularities, vertical setbacks that induce stress concentrations. A building designed with good ductility can survive many times more ground acceleration than brittle structure
- **Base isolation**: flexible bearings (rubber, lead-rubber or steel-reinforced elastomeric bearings, or friction pendulum bearings) between building and foundation — decouples from ground motion, building moves as rigid body. Period shift (increases building fundamental period from ~0.1-0.5 sec to 1-3 sec → away from earthquake dominant energy). Used in: Japan (many buildings), US West Coast (City Halls, hospitals — San Francisco City Hall, LA City Hall, SLAC, Utah State Capitol), NZ (Wellington — Te Papa museum, Parliament). Pendulum buildings (Taipei 101: 660-ton tuned mass damper, visible to public floors 87-92). Number of buildings: >10,000 in Japan, >1,000 in US and Russia, >200 in NZ
- **Seismic dampers**: energy dissipation devices. Viscous dampers (fluid-filled cylinders, like car shock absorbers), friction dampers, hysteretic dampers (steel yielding), viscoelastic dampers. Reduce shaking by converting kinetic energy to heat. Installed in frame structures: diagonal braces or walls. Use in high-rises: ~500 buildings globally have damper-based protection systems (typically larger in new high-rises in Tokyo, Shanghai, San Francisco, LA). Some buildings also use rocking frames or self-centering systems (tendon-stressed rocking concrete walls). Leader in passive damping application: Japan (also active mass dampers as active tuned mass dampers in many skyscrapers — Yokohama Landmark Tower uses two active mass dampers tuned by control systems)
- **Retrofit**: strengthening existing buildings (very expensive). Methods: add shear walls/concrete walls, steel bracing (frame replacement with moment frame), column jacketing (wrap with steel or fiber-reinforced polymer FRP), base isolation (elevate and insert isolators — expensive, done for historic buildings: LA City Hall), soft-story retrofit (vulnerable ground floor parking/residential — many unreinforced masonry buildings or soft-story apartment buildings in San Francisco, LA, and other west coast cities). Unreinforced masonry (brick, concrete block, stone) most dangerous — no reinforcement for tension/bending, collapses easily. Seismic retrofitting: required in many areas by law (SF mandatory soft-story retrofit ordinance for wood-frame buildings: 2013 → 5,000+ buildings retrofitted)
- **Building codes by country**: Japan (world's strictest — 1981 Building Standard Law major revision, 2000 revision for performance-based design — not typically cost-driven), New Zealand (1992, post-2010-2011 Canterbury earthquakes major strengthening of heritage buildings nationwide), Chile (excellent performance in 2010 M8.8 Maule earthquake — enforced code, ductile reinforced concrete with continuous confinement, most modern structures performed well), California (Title 24 / California Building Code, based on IBC-with amendments — post-1994 Northridge earthquake reform for welds, post-1971 San Fernando required hospital base isolation and lateral force upgrade, post-1989 Loma Prieta changes). IBC/ASCE 7 provides US seismic design maps with risk-targeted ground motions. Most modern building codes based on International Building Code (IBC) and ASCE 7 standards. Seismic design categories (SDC): A (lowest) to F (highest) based on risk
- **Performance-based design**: design for specific performance levels (immediate occupancy, life safety, collapse prevention) at different earthquake probabilities (50% in 50 years, 10% in 50 years, 2% in 50 years). Next-generation beyond prescriptive codes: ensure predictable building response across range of shaking levels, realistic estimates of inelastic deformation, damage distribution. Approach used for: very tall buildings (San Francisco, LA, Seattle, Portland, Vancouver, some Tokyo super-tall and complex geometries), critical facilities (hospitals, fire stations, emergency response facilities), and performance-based seismic retrofit (Los Angeles 2028 Olympics preparedness). ASCE 41 state-of-practice for existing buildings retrofit

## Tsunami Generation
- **Causes**: ~80% from submarine earthquakes (vertical displacement of seafloor). Requirements for tsunamigenic earthquake: Mw > ~7.5 (usually M8-9). Shallow focus (<50 km depth). Thrust faulting (subduction megathrust). Vertical seafloor displacement of meters over hundreds of km. Triggers displacement of entire water column (seawater) generating surface waves. 10% from submarine landslides (fairly common locally: 1958 Lituya Bay, Alaska — wave 524 m high, 1998 PNG tsunami 15 m >2000 deaths). 5-10% from volcanic collapse/eruption (Krakatoa 1883, Anak Krakatoa 2018, Hunga Tonga-Hunga Ha'apai 2022 — atmospheric pressure wave also generated global tsunami). <5% from asteroid impact (rare, infrequent on human timescales)
- **Wave physics**: in deep ocean: wavelength 100-500 km, amplitude <1 m, period 5-60 min, speed = √(g×d) (g = gravity, d = water depth) — up to 800 km/hr in deep ocean (like jet plane). In shallow water, slows (speed reduces), wavelength reduces, amplitude increases dramatically. In shallow water (near shore): wavelength ~10-20 km, amplitude up to 10-30 m+. Energy flux conserved: as depth decreases and speed drops, amplitude increases accordingly. NOT a single wave — series of waves (wave train). First wave not always largest (2011 Japan: largest was 5th wave). Tsunami can also arrive as a rapidly rising tide (bore) or as a sudden recession of the shoreline (drawdown — sometimes first sign: water pulls out far from shore before first wave hits minutes later)
- **Warning systems**: DART buoys (Deep-ocean Assessment and Reporting of Tsunamis — bottom pressure recorders detect pressure change from passing tsunami wave, transmit data via surface buoy and satellite to warning centers). Network: ~60 stations globally (US: 39). Seismic detection → initial warning (fast but can be wrong magnitude or non-tsunamigenic event) → DART confirmation (accurate amplitude, arrival time estimation) → targeted evacuation warnings. Pacific Tsunami Warning Center (PTWC, Hawaii) — serves Pacific Ocean. West Coast/Alaska Tsunami Warning Center (WC/ATWC, Alaska — NTWC). Indian Ocean Tsunami Warning System (established after 2004 — now IOC/UNESCO intergovernmental coordination group: IOTWMS). Travel times: tsunami crossing Pacific Ocean takes ~15 hrs. Local tsunamis: arrive in minutes — need natural warning signs. Natural signs: strong earthquake (hard to stand) → head to high ground immediately. Receding ocean. Unusual animal behavior
- **Evacuation**: vertical evacuation (sturdy concrete buildings, tsunami evacuation towers) in low-lying areas where high ground miles away. Maps: tsunami inundation zone maps (USGS/NOAA, local emergency managers — based on worst-case scenario modeling). Signage: blue/white tsunami evacuation route signs in many coastal communities (Japan - widely deployed; US - West Coast, Hawaii; NZ; Chile). Don't wait for official warning if felt strong shaking >20 sec near coast. Go immediately to high ground (≥100 ft elevation or several miles inland). Tsunami waves can last hours — don't return until official all-clear. Multiple waves: largest can arrive hours after first. Japan 2011: first major evacuation based on earthquake warning and initial 3m tsunami warnings saved thousands but underestimation of 9.1 Mw's near worst-case tsunami on many coasts deaths near ~20,000 people (mostly ~60% from Sendai plain who didn't evacuate in time due to underestimation) vs warnings reduced deaths among those moving to high ground
- **Famous tsunamis**: 2004 Indian Ocean (Mw 9.1, ~230,000 deaths, 14 countries impacted, worst death toll in recorded tsunami history, generated by 1200 km of fault rupture along Andaman-Sumatra subduction zone; waves up to 50 m in Banda Aceh, Indonesia where 167,000 died). 2011 Tohoku (Mw 9.1, ~20,000 deaths, Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster due to failed seawall of 5.7 m vs tsunami surge nearly 15 m, produced overtopping and flooding of backup generators, leading to catastrophic meltdown, hydrogen explosions, and radiation release. Advanced tsunami modelling, ~$300B economic damage. Over 400,000 buildings destroyed). 1960 Valdivia/Chile (Mw 9.5, largest recorded earthquake, tsunami crossed Pacific → killed ~60 in Hawaii, ~200 in Japan, ~1,000 in Chile), 1883 Krakatoa volcanic collapse tsunami (36,000 deaths). 1700 Cascadia (Mw ~9.0 — confirmed by Japanese tsunami records — tsunami that struck Japan coast ~9 hrs after mainshock off North America coast, cause uncertain until 1996 when OR/WA/BC paleoseismic records confirmed great earthquake and tsunami. Recurrence ~300-500 years. Next major Cascadia earthquake may occur anytime within next several hundred years)
