# Art History

## Prehistoric Art
- **Paleolithic**: cave paintings (Lascaux France ~17,000 BCE — bulls, horses, deer. Lascaux closed to public since 1963/closed for preservation, replica available. Chauvet France ~30,000 BCE — oldest known cave paintings. Altamira Spain ~15,000 BCE — bison)
  - Materials: charcoal, ochre, manganese — applied with fingers, brushes, blow through hollow bone
  - Purpose: hunting rituals, shamanism, storytelling — not "decoration" (no one lived in caves deep enough for paintings)
  - Venus figurines (Willendorf, ~25,000 BCE — exaggerated female form, fertility symbol. Carved from limestone, red ochre)
- **Neolithic**: Stonehenge (England, 2500 BCE), megaliths, pottery, weaving — art becomes functional + symbolic. Çatalhöyük (Turkey) wall paintings: earliest known landscapes

## Classical Art (Greece & Rome)
- **Greek (800-146 BCE)**: idealized human form, balance, proportion — Kouros (archaic, stiff frontal), Classical (contrapposto — weight shifted, naturalistic: Spear Bearer/Polykleitos, Discobolus/Myron), Hellenistic (dynamic, emotional: Laocoön, Winged Victory of Samothrace. The Dying Gaul = defeated enemy portrayed with dignity — new)
  - Three orders: Doric (plain top/Parthenon), Ionic (scroll top/Erechtheion), Corinthian (acanthus leaves/Temple of Olympian Zeus)
  - Pottery: black-figure (silhouettes incised with detail) → red-figure (detailed anatomical drawing. Naturalistic humans)
- **Roman (146 BCE-476 CE)**: practical, realistic, narrative — portraits (warts and all — verism. Roman secular portraiture). Frescoes (Pompeii — landscapes, still lifes, mythological scenes, erotica). Mosaics (tiny tesserae — scenes of daily life, geometric, mythological)
  - Architecture: arches, concrete aqueducts (Pont du Gard, Nîmes France — carried water 50 km by gravity, gradient 1 in 3000), Colosseum, Pantheon (concrete dome, oculus — still largest unreinforced concrete dome in world), Hadrian's Wall

## Medieval Art
- **Byzantine (330-1453)**: religious icons, mosaics (Hagia Sophia, Ravenna — Ravenna mosaics of Justinian + Theodora). Gold backgrounds, flat figures (spiritual not naturalistic) — front-facing, staring. Iconoclasm (726-787, 814-842) — religious images destroyed/ banned in East
- **Romanesque (1000-1150)**: rounded arches, thick walls, small windows, barrel vaults — pilgrimage churches (St. Sernin Toulouse, Santiago de Compostela). Sculpture: tympanum above portal (Christ in Majesty, Last Judgment — Saint-Foy Conques)
- **Gothic (1150-1400, starting in France)**: pointed arches, ribbed vaults, flying buttresses, stained glass — let in light (Notre Dame, Chartres, Reims, Sainte-Chapelle Paris, St. Denis Paris)
  - Stained glass: Chartres has 170 windows (12th-13th c), deep blue "Chartres blue" lost formula
  - Giotto (Italian, 1267-1337): broke from Byzantine flatness — naturalism, emotion, perspective. Arena Chapel fresco cycle (Padua): Scrovegni Chapel. "Father of Renaissance"

## Renaissance (1400-1600)
- **Early Renaissance (Italy)**: perspective (Brunelleschi, Masaccio, Ghiberti/Baptistery Doors Florence, Donatello/ David first free-standing nude since classical). Linear perspective invented by Brunelleschi, codified by Alberti
  - Botticelli: Birth of Venus (c. 1485, tempera on canvas. First non-religious female nude since antiquity), Primavera (allegorical)
- **High Renaissance (1490-1527)**: Leonardo, Michelangelo, Raphael — peak of balance, harmony, naturalism
  - Leonardo: Mona Lisa (Paris, Louvre. sfumato = gradual transitions between colors/shadows, soft hazy effect. No visible brushstrokes. Most famous painting in world), The Last Supper (Milan, refectory of Santa Maria delle Grazie. Clarity of emotion through composition: grouping disciples in 3s)
  - Michelangelo: Sistine Chapel ceiling (Vatican — 300+ figures, 4 years on back, later (last judgment) altar wall), David (Florence Accademia — 17 ft marble, perfect youthful athlete contrapposto)
  - Raphael: School of Athens (Plato + Aristotle, perspective, harmony of classical wisdom — Vatican), Madonnas (serene, gentle — Sistine Madonna with famous two cherubs)
- **Northern Renaissance**: Van Eyck (Arnolfini Portrait Ghent — oil paint detailed, mirror reflects entire room. Ghent Altarpiece — 24 panels, "Adoration of the Mystic Lamb", restored 2010-2020), Dürer (engravings — Melencolia I, rhinoceros), Bosch (Garden of Earthly Delights — fantastical, surreal before surrealism), Bruegel the Elder (peasant life, winter landscapes — Hunters in the Snow)
  - Northern painters mastered oil painting before Italians: thin translucent glazes create luminous realism

## Baroque & Rococo
- **Baroque (1600-1750)**: drama, movement, emotion, chiaroscuro (strong light-dark contrast), tenebrism (extreme shadows)
  - Caravaggio (Italian): dramatic lighting, ordinary people as religious figures (The Calling of Saint Matthew — beam of light breaks darkness, Conversion of Saul). Bernini (sculpture, architecture — Ecstasy of St. Teresa, St. Peter's Baldachin in St. Peter's Basilica, Rome. Fountains: Trevi, Four Rivers). Velázquez (Spanish court painter — Las Meninas Philips IV + daughter + painter himself includes us, Rokeby Venus). Rembrandt (Dutch — self-portraits throughout life (like visual biography — 40+ self-portraits), The Night Watch, use of golden light. Etchings as important as paintings). Vermeer (quiet interior scenes, use of light — Girl with a Pearl Earring "Mona Lisa of the North", View of Delft)
- **Rococo (1720-1770)**: light, playful, ornate, pastel colors, aristocratic leisure — Fragonard (The Swing — playful, risqué, classical statue warning, lush garden), Watteau (fêtes galantes — aristocratic pastoral pleasure scenes), Boucher (mythological, decorative)
  - Reaction against Baroque heaviness — decorative arts flourish (furniture, porcelain, interior design)

## 19th Century
- **Neoclassicism (1750-1820)**: reaction to Rococo — back to Greek/Roman ideals, serious, moral, political — David (Oath of the Horatii — stoic patriotism, The Death of Marat French Revolution martyr, Napoleon crossing the Alps), Ingres (La Grande Odalisque Turkey elongated — beauty over anatomy)
  - Prompted by: excavation of Pompeii (1748), Winckelmann's writings on Greek art
- **Romanticism (1790-1850)**: emotion, individualism, nature, sublime (terror + awe), imagination — Géricault (Raft of the Medusa — contemporary shipwreck 1816, political), Delacroix (Liberty Leading the People July Revolution 1830, color + emotion), Goya (The Third of May 1808 — brutal execution, Disasters of War prints, "black paintings" — Saturn Devouring His Son). Turner (sea, storms, light. The Fighting Temeraire, Rain Steam and Speed — almost abstract) Caspar David Friedrich (Wanderer Above Sea of Fog, contemplative sublime nature)
- **Realism (1840-1880)**: depicting everyday life honestly (not idealized) — Courbet (The Stone Breakers road workers(peasants breaking rocks) destroyed WWII, Burial at Ornans ordinary people unheroic), Daumier (political caricature, social commentary — Third Class Carriage), Millet (The Gleaners — peasants gathering leftover grain)
- **Impressionism (1860-1890)**: capturing fleeting light + color, often en plein air (outdoors), visible brushstrokes (not smooth — at close range just dabs of paint). Against academic finish + historical/sacred subjects — preferred everyday Parisian scenes
  - Monet: Rouen Cathedral series (30+ views of same cathedral at different times, light + atmosphere observations), Water Lilies (Givenchy/Orangerie), Impression, Sunrise (gave movement name — critics derided "impression")
  - Renoir: people at leisure (Luncheon of the Boating Party, Dance at Le Moulin de la Galette — Paris dance hall scenes, dappled light)
  - Degas: ballet dancers, bathing women — pastels, candid/odd angles (L'Absinthe, Little Dancer of Fourteen Years — sculpture)
  - Cassatt (American): mothers + children, domestic scenes. Impressionism's few female artists (The Child's Bath, The Boating Party)
- **Post-Impressionism (1880-1905)**: subjective expression, structure — Seurat (pointillism — tiny dots: A Sunday on La Grande Jatte). Cézanne (bridge to Cubism — Mont Sainte-Victoire, still lifes: fractured planes). Van Gogh (thick impasto, emotional color — Sunflowers, Starry Night (exaggerated, swirling sky, cypress), The Bedroom, self-portraits. Mental health struggles. cut off ear Gauguin. Sold 1 painting in his lifetime). Gauguin (Tahiti, symbolic color, primitivism — Where Do We Come From? What Are We?)

## Modern Art (20th Century)
- **Fauvism (1905-1907)**: wild color — Matisse (Woman with a Hat, Dance, The Red Studio, The Joy of Life)
- **Expressionism (1905-1925)**: emotion over realism — Munch (The Scream — anxiety universal, modern dread). German: Die Brücke (Kirchner, Heckel — street scenes, harsh color), Der Blaue Reiter (Kandinsky (first abstract — no subject beyond color + music + spirituality), Marc (blue horses, animals as spiritual pure))
- **Cubism (1907-1917)**: multiple perspectives simultaneously — Picasso (Les Demoiselles d'Avignon — African mask influence, explicit sexuality. Guernica 1937 — anti-war, bombing of Spanish town, universal symbol of suffering). Braque (violins, still life, houses)
  - Analytical: brown/gray, fragmented objects everywhere (like constant overhead view of pieces). Synthetic: later brighter, collage (newspaper, wallpaper glued on: Still Life with Chair Caning)
- **Futurism (1909-1916, Italy)**: speed, technology, violence — Boccioni (Unique Forms of Continuity in Space bronze = figure striding like wind). Balla (dynamism of a dog on a leash — barking + tail wagging in one image)
- **Dada (1916-1924)**: anti-art, absurd, protest against war — Duchamp (Fountain — urinal signed "R. Mutt" as art, questioned "what is art?"), ready-mades. Man Ray (photography, rayographs)
- **Surrealism (1924-1950)**: dream, unconscious, irrational — Dalí (The Persistence of Memory — melting clocks, morphing/dreamlike, obsessive detail. Paranoiac-critical method: self-induced paranoiac state to create double images). Magritte (The Son of Man — man with apple obscuring face, pipe=not a pipe). Kahlo (self-portraits, pain, Mexican identity — The Two Fridas, Self-Portrait with Thorn Necklace and Hummingbird)
- **Abstract Expressionism (1940s-50s)**: New York School — action painting (Pollock drip paintings, splatter on canvas on floor, performative), color field (Rothko — large rectangles of color, spiritual contemplation). de Kooning (Woman series — aggressive brushwork)
- **Pop Art (1950s-60s)**: popular culture as art — Warhol (Campbell's Soup Cans, Marilyn Diptych — silkscreen, mass production, consumer culture. 15 minutes of fame quote from 1968). Lichtenstein (comic book dots — Whaam! Drowning Girl). Jasper Johns (flags, targets), Claes Oldenburg (soft sculptures of everyday objects: giant clothespin, giant hamburger)
- **Minimalism (1960s)**: extreme simplicity — Judd (stacked boxes, industrial materials), Flavin (fluorescent light tubes), Stella (black stripe paintings "what you see is what you see"), Eva Hesse (organic, fragile minimalist sculpture using latex, rope, fiberglass)
- **Conceptual Art (1960s-70s)**: idea over object — Kosuth (One and Three Chairs — real chair + photo + dictionary definition of chair). Burden (Shoot — had friend shoot him in arm). "The art is the idea"

## Contemporary (1970s-present)
- **Postmodernism**: irony, appropriation, mixing high + low culture — Sherman (Untitled Film Stills — herself as stock female film characters, 69 b+w self-portraits). Kruger (text over photos, feminist: "Your gaze hits the side of my face"). Koons (kitsch — balloon dog, Michael Jackson + Bubbles)
- **Installation art**: immersive environments — Eliasson (The Weather Project — sun + mist, Tate Modern Turbine Hall). Turrell (light + space — Roden Crater, Arizona, you see sky aperture). Kusama (Infinity Mirror Rooms). Yayoi Kusama (polka dots, pumpkins, infinity rooms — mental health advocate, voluntarily lives in psychiatric hospital)
- **Street art**: Banksy (anonymous, political stencils — Girl with Balloon shredding in frame. Documenta napkin with shredder, film. Exit Through the Gift Shop 2010 — about street art + art world itself). Basquiat (neo-expressionist, graffiti-inspired, skull, crown motif)
- **Digital & new media**: video art (Nauman, Viola). Internet art (net.art). Generative art (AI, algorithms, an NFTs in 2020s — Beeple sold Everydays: The First 5000 Days for $69M NFT. Hirst's The Currency and his NFT + physical works. Gallery shows globally)
