# Architecture & Design

## Architectural History

### Ancient
- **Egyptian**: pyramids (Great Pyramid Giza ~2560 BCE — 2.3M stone blocks, each 2-5 tons. Aligned to cardinal points with remarkable precision. Took ~20 years. Largest for 3800 years until Lincoln Cathedral (modern spire). Mortuary temples, obelisks, post-and-lintel construction (stone columns + stone beam on top). Sphinx (guardian figure, human head + lion body, eroded missing nose: contrary to myth, Napoleon's troops didn't shoot it off — erosion suggests much earlier damage. Drone shots show current state). Valley of the Kings: underground tombs, hidden to prevent looting (most robbed anyway)
- **Classical Greek**: temples (Parthenon 447-432 BCE Athens — Doric, perfect proportions, optical refinements: columns bulge slightly at midpoint (entasis) to correct optical illusion. Not straight lines — subtle curves make it look straight from distance). Theaters, agora (public gathering/market), stoa (covered walkway). Orders: Doric (plain — masculine, Parthenon), Ionic (scroll top — feminine, Erechtheion), Corinthian (acanthus leaves — ornate, Temple of Olympian Zeus)
  - Refinements: columns tilt inward, corners thicker, stylobate (floor) curves upward — all to counteract visual distortions. The Parthenon's curvature would form an arc 7" high; if continued, a sphere with radius = 2.5 miles
- **Roman**: arches, vaults, concrete (Roman concrete/opus caementicium — secret ingredient? volcanic ash/pozzolana from Pozzuoli — makes concrete stronger than modern; self-healing properties via lime clasts). Colosseum (80 CE, 50,000 seats, 100 days inaugural games. Vomitoria = exit tunnels, not vomit — fast crowd dispersal. Hypogeum underground complex: elevators, trapdoors, animal cages). Pantheon (126 CE — largest unreinforced concrete dome in world, 43.3m diameter, oculus 9m. Coffers reduce weight. Marble interior, original bronze roof tiles removed by later popes to cast Bernini's baldacchino). Aqueducts, basilicas (law courts, became model for early Christian churches), baths (Caracalla, Diocletian — social centers with libraries, gyms, gardens)

### Medieval
- **Byzantine**: central plan, domes, mosaics, Hagia Sophia (Istanbul 537 CE — Emperor Justinian. 32m diameter dome on pendentives. Clerestory windows around base: dome appears to float on light. 4 minarets added after Ottoman conquest in 1453, converted to mosque. Mosaics uncovered by restoration 1930s. Built in 5 years — incredibly short)
- **Romanesque** (Europe 1000-1200): rounded arches, thick walls, small windows, barrel vaults/groin vaults. Dark, solid, fortress-like. Pilgrimage churches (St. Sernin Toulouse, Santiago de Compostela Spain — scallop shell symbol of pilgrimage). Durham Cathedral (England, 1093 — earliest surviving pointed transverse arches, but still mainly round. Largest Romanesque in Europe)
- **Gothic** (France 1144-1500s): pointed arches (distribute weight more efficiently), ribbed vaults, flying buttresses (transferred lateral forces to external supports, allowing thin walls + tall windows), verticality ("aspire to Heaven"), large stained glass windows. Chartres (1194-1260 — survived fires. Blue glass, 170 windows. "Bible for the illiterate": stained glass tells stories. Two spires: 500 years apart in style — mismatched). Notre Dame de Paris (1163-1345. Rose windows. Flying buttresses: 2019 fire destroyed spire + roof + wood frame. Restoration ongoing with original materials)
  - Gothic spread: England (Canterbury, Salisbury, Westminster Abbey, King's College Chapel Cambridge), Germany (Cologne — 157m, tallest facade. Started 1248, only completed 1880 to original plans), Italy (Milan, Siena — Italian Gothic less vertical, more horizontal + marble + inlay. Florence Cathedral = Gothic exterior, Renaissance dome)

### Renaissance & Baroque
- **Renaissance** (Italy 1400-1600): return to classical forms (columns, pediments, arches), symmetry, proportion, perspective, human scale — not vertical to God, but horizontal to humans
  - Brunelleschi: Florence Cathedral dome (1436 — largest masonry dome built since Pantheon — 42m across, self-supporting, herringbone brick pattern, no scaffolding (wood frame raised, built without centering). Invented linear perspective. Two shells: inner structural, outer beautiful. Lantern at top: still being built when he died — craftsmen not strong enough to hoist heavy stones that high: Brunelleschi designed a new hoist mechanism). Foundling Hospital (first Renaissance building)
  - Alberti: treatise "De Re Aedificatoria" (first architectural treatise since Vitruvius, laid down classical rules and proportion)
  - Bramante: Tempietto (San Pietro in Montorio, Rome — perfect small Renaissance building, circular with tiny Doric columns — perfect proportional ideal). St. Peter's original ground plan
  - Palladio (Villa Rotonda, Vicenza — symmetrical all four sides identical, each with portico. Influenced Thomas Jefferson's Monticello + US Capitol)
- **Baroque** (1600-1750): drama, movement, curves, grandeur — Bernini (St. Peter's Baldachin — 95ft bronze canopy over altar, bronze taken from Pantheon. St. Peter's Square — oval, majestic, "motherly arms of the church". Cornaro Chapel — Ecstasy of St. Teresa — theatrical, natural light, performance). Borromini (San Carlo alle Quattro Fontane — complex geometry, undulating walls, oval dome). French: Palace of Versailles (King Louis XIV's power statement, Hall of Mirrors 73m, gardens by Le Nôtre. Hard to heat, 700 rooms. Cost 25% of France's annual budget? No, likely a myth: 2-3 years revenue? Still huge)

### Modern (19th-20th c)
- **Industrial Revolution**: new materials (iron, steel, glass) → new building types (train stations, factories, department stores (Garnier's Printemps, Paris Arcades, Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II Milan — oldest active shopping arcade), Crystal Palace (London 1851 — prefab glass + iron, 92,000 m², built in 9 months, destroyed by fire 1936). Eiffel Tower (1889 — temporary for World's Fair, tallest structure at 300m. Resented by Paris artists, now beloved. Radio antenna added later made it 330m)
  - Chicago School (1880s-90s): first skyscrapers (Home Insurance Building 1885 — first steel-frame, 10 stories, burned down 1931. Sullivan: "form follows function" — Carson Pirie Scott department store, Chicago. Burnham: Flatiron Building Chicago, NYC's Flatiron's predecessor)
- **Modernism** (early 20th c): "less is more" (Mies van der Rohe — Barcelona Pavilion 1929, Seagram Building NYC 1958. "God is in the details." Simplicity of structure: I-beam exposed on exterior for expression of steel skeleton). Le Corbusier (5 points of architecture: pilotis/columns, roof garden, free plan (non-load-bearing walls), ribbon window, free facade. Villa Savoye 1931. Unité d'Habitation — "machine for living in" housing block. Later: brutalist concrete — Notre Dame du Haut Ronchamp chapel sculptural organic). Frank Lloyd Wright (Fallingwater 1939 — cantilevered over waterfall, first house with underfloor heating, structural failure threatened. Guggenheim Museum NYC — continuous spiral ramp. Organic architecture: building + site unified. Usonian houses: affordable, horizontal, prairie style). Bauhaus (Gropius — school combining craft + fine arts + function. Modernist principles: clean, universal, affordable. School under Nazis: politics, relocated to Chicago, reforming as "New Bauhaus")
- **Postmodernism** (1960s-80s): "less is a bore" (Venturi). Reaction to modernism's sterility — historical references, decoration, wit, color. Venturi: Learning from Las Vegas — "a sign is more important than a building" — the Strip = valid form of urban design. Robert Venturi: Vanna Venturi House (his mother's house — broken pediment breaks modernist dogma). Michael Graves (Portland Building 1982 — first major postmodern skyscraper. Colored squares top). Philip Johnson (AT&T/Sony Building NYC 1984 — Chippendale top, granite). Frank Gehry (Guggenheim Bilbao 1997 — titanium, curvilinear forms, crumpled/twisted facades — "Bilbao effect" city revitalization through architecture. Computer-aided modeling enabled complex shapes)

## Architectural Elements
- **Column orders**: Doric (simplest, no base, fluted shaft, plain capital). Ionic (scroll volutes on capital, fluted shaft, base). Corinthian (acanthus leaf capital, fluted shaft, base). Tuscan (simplest unfluted Doric-like). Composite (Corinthian + Ionic volutes combined — later Roman invention). Gigantic/colossal order: columns spanning 2+ stories
- **Arches**: round/barrel (Romanesque). Pointed/lancet (Gothic). Horseshoe (Islamic, Spain). Ogee (S-curve, Gothic, Islamic). Segmental (flat-ish). Flat/post-and-lintel (Egyptian, Greek)
- **Vaults**: barrel (semi-circle tunnel, heavy). Groin (intersecting two barrel vaults = lighter). Rib (pointed arch ribs + thin panels = Gothic). Fan (England, intricate, branching ribs). Dome (Pantheon, Hagia Sophia, Brunelleschi's). Vaulting is about distributing weight — weight of stone ceilings: barrel = massive (needs thick walls with small windows), Gothic system: ribs + flying buttresses = thin walls with structural elegance + huge windows
- **Trusses**: triangle-based roof frame (triangles = only shape that can't collapse under stress). King post truss (simple, small spans, one vertical). Queen post truss (two verticals, larger span + attic). Howe/Pratt truss (diagonals in tension: Howe vs compression: Pratt — railroad bridges). Scissor truss
- **Curtain wall**: non-load-bearing outer wall (usually glass + metal) hanging from steel frame. Modern skyscraper essential

## Sustainable Architecture
- **Passive house (Passivhaus)**: ultra-low energy building standard — super insulation (triple-pane windows, no thermal bridging heat loss), airtight (0.6 air changes per hour at 50Pa), heat recovery ventilation (HRV), passive solar gain
  - Standard: annual heating/cooling <15 kWh/m²/year = 90% less energy than typical code building. Often 3-6" thick in wall insulation (vs code minimum ~ R-20)
- **LEED** (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design): rating system (Certified, Silver, Gold, Platinum). Measures: energy, water, materials, indoor environmental quality, site, innovation
- **Net zero energy**: building produces as much energy as it uses annually (solar panels, geothermal, heat pumps). "Zero carbon" also accounts for embodied carbon in materials
- **Green roofs**: vegetated roof — insulation, stormwater management, urban heat island mitigation, habitat, reduced energy. Extensive (thin, sedum, low maintenance) vs Intensive (thick, full garden, high maintenance, structural reinforcement often needed). Roof podiums
- **Solar orientation**: passive design — south-facing windows maximize winter sun (northern hemisphere). Overhangs shade summer sun. Thermal mass (concrete/brick) stores heat. Natural ventilation to avoid mechanical cooling

## Urban Design
- **Garden City** (Ebenezer Howard 1898): self-contained communities surrounded by greenbelts — reaction to industrial city overcrowding. Radburn, NJ; Welwyn, UK. Influenced New Towns movement
- **Radiant City** (Le Corbusier): high-density towers in parks, separation of uses, "streets in the sky" — influential but also criticized (separated people from street life; highrises in open space = isolating/unsafe. Jane Jacobs championed mixed use, dense street life, eyes on the street instead)
- **New Urbanism**: walkable neighborhoods, mixed-use (homes + shops + offices), traditional street grid, front porches, alleys, public spaces. Seaside Florida 1980 (first planned, made famous by The Truman Show), Celebration Florida (Disney, but less charming)
- **Jane Jacobs**: The Death and Life of Great American Cities (1961) — eyes on the street, mixed uses, short blocks, density, aged buildings — bottom-up city planning vs top-down. She organized to stop Robert Moses' Lower Manhattan Expressway (destroy 2,000 buildings including SoHo, Washington Square Park — protest succeeded: "This is a woman's city." Jacobs arrested 1968, quit NYC with her two sons and moved to Toronto where she died in 2006). Her principles observed across successful neighborhoods worldwide — Greenwich Village, North End Boston, West Village NYC

## Famous Architects
- **Frank Lloyd Wright** (1867-1959): 1,000+ designs, 532 built. Fallingwater, Guggenheim, Robie House, Unity Temple, Taliesin homes. Organic architecture: "No house should ever be on a hill or on anything. It should be of the hill. Belonging to it. Hill and house should live together." 70-year career; three women associated with his complex personal life
- **Louis Kahn** (1901-1974): monumental, poetic, light — Salk Institute (La Jolla — concrete perfection, single linear courtyard, central water channel leads eye to ocean. No right angles in design, all cut to "savor" line. "What do you want, brick?" Brick says "I like an arch"). Kimbell Art Museum (Fort Worth — vaulted galleries, natural light slit). National Assembly Building (Bangladesh, Dhaka — national monument, geometry of light)
- **Zaha Hadid** (1950-2016): first woman to win Pritzker Prize (2004). Parametric design, fluid forms — MAXXI Museum (Rome), Heydar Aliyev Center (Baku — flowing no angles, curves), Beijing Galaxy Soho. "Feminine" ≠ "curvy": just her complex geometries, no right angles. Died 2016 (heart attack in Miami hospital)
- **Tadao Ando** (1941-): concrete + light + nature — Church of the Light (Japan — cross-shaped cut in concrete wall), Pulitzer Arts Foundation (St. Louis), Naoshima museums (art island, Japan). T-shaped plan, cross beams and lights from slit. Concrete as "teetonic blank canvas"
- **I.M. Pei** (1917-2019): modern, geometric — Louvre Pyramid (Paris, 1989 — controversial initially, now beloved. Glass + steel, 673 panes). Bank of China Tower (Hong Kong). East Building National Gallery (DC). John F. Kennedy Library (Boston)
