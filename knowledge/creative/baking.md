# Baking Basics

## Key Differences: Baking vs Cooking
- Baking is chemistry — precise measurements matter (cooking is flexible, baking is not)
- Use WEIGHT (grams) not volume (cups) for consistent results — $20 kitchen scale is the best investment
  - 1 cup all-purpose flour = 120g (scoop and level). 1 cup granulated sugar = 200g
  - Measuring by volume can vary 20%+ depending on how you scoop
- Temperature matters: oven thermometers are cheap and essential (oven dials are often off by 25-50°F)
- Altitude adjustments: above 3,000 ft need adjustments (more flour, less sugar, higher temp)

## Essential Ingredients
- **Flour**: all-purpose (most baking), bread flour (more protein = more gluten = chewier), cake flour (less protein = tender), pastry flour, whole wheat (absorbs more water)
  - Gluten: protein network that gives structure — developed by mixing/kneading
  - Too much gluten = tough (overmixed muffins/pancakes). Too little = crumbly, dense
- **Sugar**: granulated (sweetness, structure, browning), brown sugar (moisture + molasses flavor — light or dark), powdered/confectioners (icing, no grittiness)
  - Sugar is hygroscopic (attracts moisture) → keeps baked goods soft, but also crisps in high heat (caramelization)
- **Fat**: butter (flavor, water content affects texture), oil (moisture, no water = tender — good for cakes), shortening (pure fat, no water = flaky pie crust)
  - Room temperature butter (65-68°F) creams with sugar → traps air = rise
  - Cold butter = flaky (pie crust, biscuits). Melted = dense, moist (brownies)
- **Eggs**: structure (protein), leavening (air when beaten), moisture, fat (yolk), emulsification, browning
  - Room temperature eggs emulsify better — place cold eggs in warm water 5 min
  - Separating: clean bowl (no yolk in whites = won't whip). Fat inhibits egg white foaming
- **Leaveners**: baking soda (needs acid — buttermilk, yogurt, lemon, brown sugar, cocoa), baking powder (contains acid + base, double-acting = reacts when mixed + when heated), yeast (biological, slow, flavor)
  - Too much baking soda = soapy taste (needs enough acid to neutralize)
  - Baking powder loses potency over time (test: put in hot water — should bubble vigorously)
- **Dairy**: milk (whole = better flavor/texture, low-fat = fine), buttermilk (acid + tang), cream (richness), yogurt/sour cream (tangy, tender)
- **Salt**: enhances flavor, strengthens gluten (small amount), controls yeast (don't let yeast touch salt directly)

## Techniques
- **Cremaing**: beat butter + sugar until light and fluffy (3-5 min) — traps air bubbles → rise
  - Butter must be room temperature (65-68°F) — too cold won't cream, too warm loses structure
- **Muffin method**: whisk dry ingredients, whisk wet ingredients separately, gently fold together until JUST combined — lumpy batter is OK, overmixing = tunnels + tough texture
- **Biscuit/scone method**: cut cold butter into flour until pea-sized, add liquid, knead gently — butter pieces create steam pockets = flakiness
- **Folding**: gentle over-under motion to incorporate without deflating (egg whites, whipped cream into batter)
  - Use rubber spatula, cut through center, scrape bottom, turn over — repeat
- **Kneading**: develop gluten — fold, push with palm, turn 90°, repeat. 8-10 min by hand, 5-7 min stand mixer
  - Windowpane test: stretch dough thin — should see light through without tearing (proper gluten development)
- **Proofing**: yeast dough rises — warm place (75-85°F), covered, until doubled (1-2 hours)
  - Under-proofed: dense, tight crumb. Over-proofed: flat, large holes, collapses
  - Cold proofing (fridge overnight): more flavor (enzymatic breakdown), easier to handle

## Classic Recipes (Ratios)
- **Pound cake**: 1:1:1:1 — flour, sugar, butter, eggs (by weight). Add 1 tsp vanilla, bake at 350°F
- **Cookie (sugar)**: 1 part sugar, 2 parts flour, 1 part butter, 1 egg (per ~200g flour). Chill dough before baking
  - Drop cookies: more butter/sugar = spread more, more flour = less spread. Chill dough to reduce spread
  - Brown sugar = chewier, white sugar = crisper. More egg yolk = chewier, more white = crisper
- **Pie crust**: 3:2:1 — flour:fat:water (by weight). 300g flour, 200g butter, 100g ice water. Keep everything cold
  - Blind bake: line with foil + pie weights (or beans), bake at 375°F 15 min, remove weights, bake 10 min more
- **Biscuits**: 2:1:2/3 — flour:fat:milk. Cut cold butter into flour, add milk, fold 5-6 times, cut, bake at 425°F 12-15 min
- **Bread (basic)**: 5:3 — flour:water + yeast (~1% of flour weight) + salt (~2%). 500g flour, 300g water, 5g yeast, 10g salt
  - Hydration % = water weight / flour weight. 60-65% standard bread, 70-80% artisan (wetter = harder to work, more open crumb)

## Troubleshooting
- **Cake sunk in middle**: oven door opened too early, too much leavener, underbaked, too much liquid
  - Don't open oven in first 20 min of baking (cold air causes collapse)
- **Cake dry**: overbaked, too much flour (measure by weight!), not enough fat/sugar
- **Cookies spread too much**: butter too warm, dough not chilled, too much sugar, too little flour, old baking soda
- **Bread dense**: not kneaded enough (underdeveloped gluten), over-proofed, too much flour, yeast dead (test: water + sugar + yeast → 10 min should foam)
  - Water too hot kills yeast (above 130°F). Too cold slows it down. Ideal: 105-115°F
- **Pie crust tough**: overworked (too much gluten), too much water, wrong type flour (use pastry/all-purpose, not bread flour)
- **Burned bottom, raw middle**: oven too hot, wrong rack position, dark pans absorb more heat (reduce temp 25°F for dark pans)
- **Not browning**: oven too cool, sugar content low, no egg wash (brush with egg + water for golden crust)

## Equipment
- **Essential**: scale, oven thermometer, mixing bowls, measuring spoons, rubber spatula, whisk, baking sheets, cake pans, cooling rack, parchment paper
- **Stand mixer** (KitchenAid): worth it if you bake frequently — makes creaming, kneading, whipping much easier
- **Food processor**: pie crust (pulse butter into flour), nut flours
- **Rolling pin**: tapered (traditional, more control) vs straight (even thickness)
- **Bench scraper**: dividing dough, cleaning work surface, lifting cut dough
- **Pastry brush**: egg wash, buttering pans. Silicone (dishwasher safe) > natural bristle (sheds)

## Pans & Materials
- Light metal (aluminum): best heat conductor, browns evenly, most common
- Dark/non-stick: absorbs heat faster → reduce oven temp 25°F, can over-brown
- Glass/Pyrex: heats slower, holds heat longer (good for custards, not cookies)
- Silicone: flexible, non-stick, doesn't brown well — good for muffins, NOT cookies or bread
- Cast iron: holds heat, great for cornbread, pie (crisp bottom crust)
- **Prep pans properly**: grease with butter/flour (Pam works but can leave residue), parchment paper for cookies (reusable!, makes cleanup instant)
