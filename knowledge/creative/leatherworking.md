# Leatherworking

Cutting, stitching, and finishing leather goods.

## Leather Types
- **Full-grain**: Top layer with natural grain intact — strongest, most durable, develops patina. Used for belts, wallets, boots, saddles
- **Top-grain**: Top layer sanded to remove imperfections — slightly thinner, more uniform, less expensive. For bags, upholstery
- **Genuine leather**: Split layers from full-grain — lower quality, less durable. Inexpensive goods
- **Bonded leather**: Scraps + polyurethane, least durable, flakes over time. Avoid for projects
- **Vegetable-tanned**: Tanned with tree bark tannins — stiff, toolable/embossable, ages beautifully. Best for carving/stamping
- **Chrome-tanned**: Tanned with chromium salts — soft, stretchy, many colors. Most common for garments, upholstery. Stains easily from water
- **Wet-blue**: Chrome-tanned but undyed — used for shoes, industrial applications
- **Splits**: Inner layers after top grain removed — suede (napped finish) or rough-out. Used for linings, soft goods

## Essential Tools
- **Cutting**: Rotary cutter (straight lines, curves), utility knife / craft knife (detail), curved blade knife (clicker knife - follows templates), self-healing cutting mat
- **Stitching**: Diamond chisels (punch stitching holes — 2mm to 6mm spacing), harness needles (blunt, rounded), awl (for additional holes, sewing through thick stack), stitching pony/clam (holds work while stitching)
- **Edge finishing**: Edge beveler (rounds sharp edges), edge creaser (decorative groove), slicker (grooved wood/plastic — burnishes edges with water/gum tragacanth)
- **Hardware**: Rivet setter, snap setter, grommet setter, hole punch (rotary or individual sizes), strap cutter (for consistent belt/strap width)
- **Misc**: Bone folder (creases smooth, burnishes), stitching groover (grows groove along edge for recessed stitching), dividers (mark consistent distance from edge), modeling tools (for tooling/carving)

## Cutting Leather
- **Grain side up**: Cut from grain side (top) — slicing action, not sawing. Replace blades frequently (dull blades drag)
- **Templates**: Cardstock / acrylic templates — trace around with awl (not pen — marks permanent). Cut slightly outside line, then sand to exact size
- **Thickness**: Wallets 2-3oz (0.8-1.2mm), bags 4-6oz (1.6-2.4mm), belts 8-10oz (3.2-4mm), tooling leather 4-6oz
- **Skiving**: Thin edges where leather folds or overlaps — use skiving knife or French edger. Critical for clean folded edges, reduces bulk at seams

## Stitching
- **Saddle stitch**: Two needles, one thread — needle goes through each hole from opposite sides, loop crosses each other in middle. Stronger than machine stitching (if one stitch breaks, others hold)
- **Thread**: Waxed polyester (Tiger Thread, Ritza 25) — braided, strong. Choose thickness: 0.6mm for wallets, 0.8mm for bags, 1.0mm for belts
- **Stitch length**: Holes 3-4mm apart, stitch length about 2x leather thickness. Closer stitches = more refined but weaker per stitch (more holes)
- **Backstitching**: Stitch back 3-4 holes at start and end, glue thread, hammer flat — prevents unraveling
- **Thread tension**: Even tension on both needles — too tight puckers leather, too loose shows gaps. Practice: consistent pull angle (45 degrees) and force

## Edge Finishing
- **Bevel**: Run edge beveler along all edges after cutting — removes sharp corners
- **Burnish**: For veg-tan only — wet edge with water or gum tragacanth, rub vigorously with slicker (wood or canvas). Creates smooth, shiny, hard edge. Sand between coats (320 → 600 → 1000 grit)
- **Edge paint**: For chrome-tan or color-matched edges — Fiebing's Edge Kote, Uniters, Vernis. Multiple thin coats with sanding between. Masking tape keeps paint off body
- **Burnishing process**: Sand 400 grit → bevel → sand 600 grit → wet + burnish → sand 1000 grit → apply edge paint (if desired) → final burnish

## Leather Carving & Stamping
- **Wet leather**: Soak veg-tan until fully saturated (dark uniform color), then dry surface slightly (back to light tan, cool touch). Re-wet as it dries during carving
- **Basic stamps**: Swivel knife (cuts lines — most essential tool — learn proper grip, pull toward you with consistent depth), beveler (sinks background), pear shader (rounded texture), cam tool (veins/ curves), seeder (small circles for grapes/berries), background tool (textured fill)
- **Pattern transfer**: Trace pattern onto leather with tracing film + stylus while leather is case (damp). Don't press hard enough to damage surface
- **Figure carving**: Cut with swivel knife first → bevel around cuts → texture/smooth → background → apply dye

## Dyeing & Finishing
- **Dyes**: Alcohol-based (Fiebing's Pro Dye, Angelus — fast-drying, deep penetration), oil-based (more time to work, builds gradually), water-based (eco-friendly, less deep)
- **Application**: Wool dauber (even coverage), airbrush (smooth, professional), sponge (distressed look), resist (apply before antique for two-tone effect)
- **Finishes**: Resolene (satin or gloss — protective, synthetic), beeswax (natural, soft sheen), acrylic resolene (water-resistant). Test on scrap first
- **Conditioning**: Neatsfoot oil, mink oil, leather balm (Auburn Leather Conditioner) — keeps leather supple, prevents cracking
