# Jewelry Making

Techniques for creating metal, beaded, and wire jewelry.

## Metal Techniques
- **Sawing**: Use jeweler's saw — blade teeth pointing down toward handle. Pierce metal (drill small hole for interior cuts). Lubricate blade with beeswax. Frame tension is critical (blade should ring like guitar string)
- **Filing**: Needle files — cut in one direction (not back and forth — dulls teeth). Cross-file (perpendicular) to remove material, draw-file (parallel) for smooth finish
- **Soldering**: Join metal with solder (alloy with lower melting point than base metal). Torch (butane/ propane-oxygen), flux (borax-cone or paste — prevents oxidation), pickling (acid bath removes fire scale). Three grades: hard, medium, easy (different melting temps — use hard first)
- **Annealing**: Heat metal to soften (work-hardened metal becomes brittle). Quench after annealing copper/silver (not steel). Pickle to clean
- **Forging**: Hammer metal to shape — on steel anvil or stake. Work hardens — anneal as needed. Texturing with different hammer faces (ball peen, planishing, chasing)
- **Cold connection**: No soldering — rivets (tube, wire, standard), tabs (fold over to secure), wire wrapping, sewing with fine wire. For materials that can't be soldered (mixed metals, leather, acrylic)
- **Texturing**: Hammer (ball peen for dimples, cross-peen for lines), rolling mill (impress paper/fabric/texture into soft metal), etching (acid resist — ferric chloride for copper, nitric acid for silver)

## Beading
- **Stringing materials**: Beading wire (Soft Flex, Beadalon — nylon-coated stainless steel, better than thread for heavy beads), silk thread (pearls — knot between each), elastic (stretch bracelets), leather cord, memory wire
- **Clasps**: Lobster claw, spring ring, toggle (T-bar + ring), magnetic, box clasp, barrel screw, hook + eye
- **Crimping**: Flatten crimp bead with crimping pliers (two-step: squeeze in U-shape then fold over) — secures strand to clasp
- **Bead weaving**: Loom weaving (warp threads + weft needle — patterns), off-loom (peyote, brick stitch, herringbone, right-angle weave). Peyote stitch: bead between beads, staggered rows
- **Bead types**: Seed beads (size 15/0 smallest to 6/0 largest — 11/0 standard), Czech vs Japanese (Japanese more consistent), Delica (cylindrical — perfect for patterns), glass, semi-precious stone (jasper, agate, turquoise), crystal (Swarovski), lampwork (handmade glass beads)

## Wire Wrapping
- **Wire gauges**: Higher gauge = thinner. 20g (earrings, delicate), 18g (pendants, supports), 16g (bracelet structure, heavy), 14g (rings, thicker bangles). Dead soft (easily shaped) vs half-hard (holds shape better)
- **Wire types**: Copper (cheap, practice), sterling silver (precious, tarnishes), gold-filled (gold layer bonded to brass, better than plated), brass, bronze, niobium (hypoallergenic, colorful when anodized)
- **Basic techniques**: Wrapped loop (secure join — wrap wire around itself at base of loop), eye pin (loop at end, open/close like jump ring), spiral (coiled flat), coil (tight turns around mandrel), cage (enclose stone in wire framework), tree of life (wrapped wire branches)
- **Jig work**: Wire forming jig — move pegs to create repetitive patterns (earring pairs, symmetrical shapes)

## Casting
- **Lost wax (investment)**: Carve wax model → attach sprue → invest in plaster-like material → burn out wax in kiln → molten metal centrifuged or vacuum-cast into cavity → break investment → cut sprue → polish
- **Open-back vs bezel**: Bezel (metal strip wrapped around stone, burnished against it) — simplest stone setting. Open back allows light through stone, reduces weight
- **Stone setting**: Bezel (secure, easiest), prong (4-6 metal claws, more light), channel (stones in row between walls), pavé (small stones set close, held by tiny beads of metal)

## Finishing & Polishing
- **Files**: Cut file (coarse), smooth file, needle files (small detail). Hand files for shaping, machine files (flex shaft with burrs) for detail
- **Sandpaper**: Progress through grits 220 → 400 → 600 → 1000 → 2000. Use wet/dry paper with water for final grits
- **Polishing compounds**: Tripoli (medium cut — removes scratches), rouge (fine polish — brings shine). Apply with buffing wheel on flex shaft or bench motor
- **Patina**: Liver of sulfur (lump or gel) — dip or paint on, creates black/brown/blue sulfides on silver/copper. Polish raised areas, leave patina in recesses (antiquing effect)
- **Tumbling**: Rotary tumbler with steel shot + water + burnishing compound — polishes and work-hardens. Runs hours to overnight

## Tools
- **Must-haves**: Flat nose pliers, round nose pliers, wire cutters (flush cut), jeweler's saw + blades, needle files (set of 6), bench pin (for sawing on), flex shaft (Dremel-like rotary tool but variable speed + foot pedal)
- **Nice-to-have**: Soldering torch + fire brick, pickle pot (slow cooker), rolling mill, tumbler, ring mandrel, triblet (tapered mandrel), third hand / helping hands

## Safety
- **Metal dust**: Sawing, grinding, sanding produce fine metal dust — N95 mask, ventilate, damp clean
- **Solder flux**: Boron/ fluoride compounds — avoid inhaling fumes, ventilate soldering area
- **Pickle**: Acid (sodium bisulfate or Sparex) — use only copper/brass/silver tongs (steel contaminates). No aluminum in pickle (creates copper plating)
- **Lung protection**: Fine metal particles, polishing compounds, patina chemicals — respirator with appropriate cartridges
