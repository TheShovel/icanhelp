# Photography Basics

## Exposure Triangle
- **Aperture (f-stop)**: f/1.4 (large opening, more light, shallow DOF) → f/22 (small, less light, everything in focus)
  - Lower f-number = blurry background (portrait)
  - Higher f-number = everything sharp (landscape)
- **Shutter speed**: 1/1000 (freezes motion) → 30 sec (blurs motion)
  - 1/60 minimum for handheld (slower = tripod)
  - Fast (1/500+): freeze sports, birds
  - Slow (1/30-): blur waterfalls, light trails, intentional motion
- **ISO**: 100 (clean, bright light) → 6400+ (noisy, low light)
  - Always use lowest ISO possible
  - Modern cameras handle ISO 3200-6400 well

## Composition
- **Rule of thirds**: divide frame into 3x3 grid, place subject on intersections or lines
- **Leading lines**: roads, fences, rivers lead eye to subject
- **Framing**: use arches, windows, branches to frame subject
- **Symmetry**: centered composition for architecture, reflections
- **Negative space**: empty area around subject emphasizes it
- **Fill the frame**: get closer, eliminate distracting elements
- **Golden hour**: first/last hour of daylight — warm, soft light
- **Blue hour**: twilight — moody, cool tones

## Camera Modes
- **Auto**: camera decides everything (avoid for creative control)
- **P (Program)**: auto exposure, you control flash/ISO/white balance
- **A/Av (Aperture Priority)**: you set aperture, camera sets shutter — most popular for portraits/landscape
- **S/Tv (Shutter Priority)**: you set shutter, camera sets aperture — action/wildlife
- **M (Manual)**: you set both — full creative control, useful for consistent lighting (studio, events)

## Phone Photography Tips
- Clean lens (pocket lint ruins photos)
- Tap to focus, slide to adjust exposure
- Shoot in grid mode (rule of thirds)
- Don't zoom digitally (crops and degrades) — walk closer
- For low light: brace phone, use timer, lean against something
- Edit: adjust exposure, contrast, saturation before posting
- Burst mode for action: hold shutter
- Portrait mode: good for people/pets, check edges for bad cutouts

## Lighting
- **Soft light**: diffused (cloudy day, window with curtain, softbox) — flattering for portraits
- **Hard light**: direct (noon sun, bare bulb) — harsh shadows, dramatic
- **Rembrandt light**: 45° angle, triangle of light on cheek — classic portrait
- **Backlight**: subject lit from behind, edge glow (use fill light or expose for face)
- **Golden hour**: warm, low angle, long shadows
- **Flash**: direct (harsh) vs bounce (off ceiling/wall, soft)
- Three-point lighting: key (main), fill (opposite, less power), back (rim light, separates from background)

## Types of Photography
- **Portrait**: focus on eyes, aperture f/1.4-f/4, eyes sharp, shallow DOF
- **Landscape**: small aperture f/8-f/16, tripod, foreground interest, leading lines
- **Street**: candid, fast shutter, storytelling, respect privacy, check local laws
- **Macro**: extreme close-up, specialized lens or extension tubes, steady tripod, focus stacking
- **Long exposure**: 30 sec+, tripod + remote shutter, ND filter for daytime, silky water/clouds
- **Astro**: wide lens f/1.4-f/2.8, 500/focal length = max sec before star trails, tripod
- **Product**: controlled lighting, clean background, consistency (catalog)

## Gear
- **DSLR**: optical viewfinder, big battery, massive lens selection (Canon, Nikon)
- **Mirrorless**: electronic viewfinder, smaller/lighter, newer tech (Sony, Fuji, Canon RF/Nikon Z)
- **Lens types**: prime (fixed focal, sharper, wider aperture), zoom (versatile, heavier, slower)
  - 35mm: street, environmental portrait
  - 50mm: "nifty fifty" — versatile, cheap, great for beginners
  - 85mm: classic portrait
  - 70-200mm: events, sports, compression
  - 16-35mm: wide angle, landscape
- Tripod: carbon fiber (light, expensive) vs aluminum (heavy, cheap)
- Memory cards: SanDisk/Sony SD cards are reliable — 64-128GB for most needs
- Back up photos: 3-2-1 rule (3 copies, 2 media, 1 offsite)

## Editing Basics
- Start with: exposure, contrast, white balance, crop/straighten
- Then: highlights (- for detail), shadows (+ to recover), clarity (+ for texture), vibrance (smarter than saturation)
- Color grading: adjust shadows/midtones/highlights hue separately
- Avoid: over-saturation, crushed blacks (no detail), halo from too much clarity/sharpening
- Tools: Lightroom (industry standard), Capture One, Darktable (free), RawTherapee (free)
- Shoot RAW (vs JPEG): more editing flexibility, retains detail in highlights/shadows
