# Calligraphy

The art of beautiful handwriting — tools, scripts, and techniques.

## Pen Types
- **Broad-edge nib**: Flat tip — for gothic, uncial, italic, foundational. Hold at consistent angle (30-45°). Thick thins from nib width, not pressure. Mitchell nibs (interchangeable sizes), Speedball C-series
- **Pointed nib**: Flexible tines — for copperplate, Spencerian, modern. Thin upstrokes, thick downstrokes (pressure on downstrokes spreads tines). Hunt 101, Brause EF66, Zebra G
- **Brush pen**: Flexible felt/ synthetic tip — for modern calligraphy, Chinese/Japanese. Pentel Fude Touch, Tombow Dual Brush, Ecoline. Varying pressure creates line variation
- **Fountain pen**: Convenient for practice — Pilot Parallel (broad-edge, comes in sizes 1.5-6.0mm), Lamy Joy, TWSBI with stub nib
- **Dip pen holder**: Oblique holder (copperplate — angled flange), straight holder (broad-edge/other). Flange (metal piece holding nib) should fit snugly

## Inks
- **Higgins Eternal**: Black, waterproof, good for broad-edge
- **Sumi ink**: Deep black, glossy, works with pointed nibs. Can be thinned with water (distilled)
- **Walnut ink**: Brown, easy to use, great for practice — crystals (add water) or liquid. Non-waterproof, erasable
- **India ink**: Shellac-based — waterproof, but can clog/fray nibs. Not for fountain pens
- **Gouache**: Opaque watercolor — mix to desired consistency (thick cream). Any color possible. Winsor & Newton Designers Gouache
- **Iron gall ink**: Historical — permanent, fades to brown over time. Bleeds less on paper. Needs pen cleaning after use

## Paper
- **Smooth and non-absorbent**: Coated papers (HP — hot pressed watercolor paper, Rhodia, Tomoe River, Canson Marker) — prevent feathering (ink spreading along fibers)
- **Practice paper**: HP LaserJet paper (smooth side), Rhodia dot/grid pads
- **Guidelines**: Slant lines for copperplate (55° angle), waistline/x-height, ascender/descender lines. Printable templates, light box, or pre-printed pads
- **Avoid**: Absorbent papers (newsprint, construction, rough watercolor) — bleeding and feathering ruin crisp edges

## Scripts
- **Copperplate (English Roundhand)**: Pointed nib — elegant, flowing, slanted (55°). Ovals, shaded downstrokes, hairline upstrokes. Main strokes: entrance/exit strokes, ovals, underturns, overturns, compound curves
- **Spencerian**: Pointed nib — less contrast than copperplate, more angular, smaller x-height. American business hand (19th century). Very light hairlines, delicate
- **Italic**: Broad-edge, slanted (5-10°). Lively, compact — the most legible script. Wedge-shaped serifs, diamond dots. Easy to learn, good starting point
- **Foundational**: Broad-edge, upright. Based on 10th century English Carolingian. Large x-height, round letters. Good for learning broad-edge pen control
- **Gothic (Blackletter)**: Broad-edge — dense, angular, vertical. Textura Quadrata (most formal), Fraktur (more flourishes, German), Rotunda (rounded gothic)
- **Uncial**: Broad-edge, uppercase only (majuscule). Rounded, open — medieval book hand. Good beginner broad-edge script
- **Modern Calligraphy**: Unstructured, personalized variations — mix scripts, flourishes, bounce (letters rise above x-height). Less rules, more expression

## Basic Strokes
- **Pointed pen**: Entrance stroke (hairline up from baseline), overturn (hairline up, thick down), underturn (thick down, hairline up), compound curve (both curves), oval (starts slightly left of top, thin → thick → thin), ascender loop (hairline up, loop, thick down)
- **Broad-edge**: Hairline (pen on corner, pull down from corner), straight stroke (full nib width), diagonal, curve (push/pull maintaining angle). Build letters from combinations of straight and curved strokes

## Practice Methods
- **Warm-ups**: Ovals, underturns, overturns, curves — fill a page of each before letter practice
- **Drills**: Repetitive strokes to train muscle memory — aim for smooth, consistent shapes
- **Letter families**: Group by similar shapes — copperplate: a/c/d/e (ovals), i/u/w (underturns), n/m/v (overturns), b/p (ascender/descender loops)
- **Rhythm**: Letters should be evenly spaced and consistent — "white space" between and within letters should be visually equal
- **Arm movement**: Move from shoulder, not wrist — especially for larger scripts (uncial, foundational). Forearm stays on table, shoulder moves

## Troubleshooting
- **Missing ink**: Nib too dry — clean nib (flame + wipe or use gum arabic/ potato/ ammonia water to remove manufacturing oils), thicker ink, slower movement
- **Blobs**: Too much ink on nib, ink too thin, or hesitating while pen is on paper. Wipe nib more frequently (every 2-4 words)
- **Scratches**: Nib tines misaligned — check with loupe, adjust gently (push tine up/down). Paper too rough. Nib too small for writing speed
- **Feathering**: Wrong paper (too absorbent) or ink too thin. Change paper or add gum arabic to thicken ink
