# Theatre Arts & Performance

## Acting Techniques

### Stanislavski System
- **Given circumstances**: specifics of the character's world (time, place, relationships, social context). "Magic if": what would I do IF I were this character in these circumstances. Imagination: add details to text (what character had for breakfast? what's their room like?)
- **Objectives + super-objective**: what does character want IN EACH SCENE (objective) vs in the ENTIRE PLAY (super-objective). Actor's main task is to play the objective through the scene not as statement of mood but action: "to get my husband to confess." Not just "angry" — use active verbs (to beg, to threaten, to seduce)
- **Units + beats**: every scene changes when character achieves something or new information enters. Each unit = one character objective. Beat change = shift in tactic or new stimulus
- **Emotion memory**: NOT "relive trauma." Recall sensory details (smell, sound, touch) to trigger organic emotional response. Modern teachers prefer substitution (replace emotion with a personal memory but stay in scene) or "affective memory" (recall a parallel experience). Affective Memory method (Strasberg) involves selecting a past emotional memory with sensory detail, not reliving trauma to perform
- **Ensemble**: each character exists in relationship to others. Listen + respond (acting is reacting). "The play's the thing." Not better than other actors — serve the story together

### Meisner
- **Repetition exercise**: two actors repeat phrase from observation ("You have blue eyes" / "I have blue eyes"). Focus on partner's behavior — pick up changes in how they say it. Don't act: respond truthfully to partner's behavior in the moment. Break the scripted performance habit — make it spontaneous. From this simple exercise: you learn to work off your partner, not your pre-planned choices
  - "Acting is living truthfully under imaginary circumstances." — Sanford Meisner. Goal: abolish "acting" as performing, replace with authentic moment-to-moment experience
- **Independent activity**: do a simple task (folding laundry, fixing a lamp) that requires concentration. Partner observes. The observation creates a real impulse to respond — not pre-planned. This becomes foundation for scene work

### Method Acting (Strasberg)
- **Relaxation**: eliminate physical tension that blocks impulse. Sensory exercises: experience objects without naming them (paper cup as texture, not "cup"). Sense memory: recall physical sensations (sun on face, weight of raincoat). Affective memory (emotional recall — controversial, possibly harmful for traumatized)
- **Substitution**: replace scene partner with someone from your life to generate real emotion. "The person you're in love with is not the person across from you — she is the you of the scene. So think of someone else from your life that it'd be easier to be hurt by or love"
- **Criticism**: can lead to emotional distress (boundary blurring). Not all roles require "living as the character" (Daniel Day-Lewis retired—likely not method-related). Many great actors don't use method (Meryl Streep, Ian McKellen). Better: engagement without sacrificing mental health

### Viewpoints (Anne Bogart)
- Movement-based: time (tempo, duration, kinesthetic response, repetition). Space (shape, gesture, architecture, spatial relationship, topography). Good for devised theatre (ensemble-created) + building physical awareness. Improv: "Yes, and" — accept offers, build scenes without blocking. Moment to moment, active listening, trust your impulses

## Directing
- **Interpretation**: what is the play REALLY about? Theme, through-line for each character, world of the play (stylized? realistic?). Central metaphor: what is the "heart" of the play? Find overarching idea: "Waiting for Godot = the existential need for meaning in an absurd universe"
- **Blocking**: where actors move + why. Motivated by character objective, not choreography. Don't block arbitrarily: "cross to window because you hear a noise," not "cross to window because director says so." Every movement must serve story. Develop blocking from character psychology: power dynamics, status, intimacy
- **Casting**: type (physical appearance, energy) vs against-type (stretch, risk). Diversity + representation matter. Chemistry read: do the leads have romantic/living connection? "The best actor for the role" is whoever best fits the emotional + physical requirements, not necessarily who you already know
- **Rehearsal process**: table read (hear text) → exploration (experiment, find moments) → blocking → runs (tech: integrate lights + sound + sets, dress: costumes, final run: full tech + preview + opening) → performance → closing
- **Collaboration**: designer communication (set, lights, costumes, sound) — collaborate, not dictate. Give designers freedom within concept. Stage manager = communication hub + rehearsals manager + calling the show

## Playwriting
- **Structure**: exposition → inciting incident → rising action → climax → falling action → denouement. Act I: set up characters + conflict. Act II: complications + escalation. Act III: climax + resolution
- **Dialogue**: subtext (what characters DON'T say matters more than what they do). Characters reveal themselves through action + speech. Why do they say this NOW? — not forced but arising from dramatic moment. Everyone thinks they're the hero of their own story
- **Character**: each character has a want + need (want = what they think they need, need = what they actually need for emotional resolution). Flaw that prevents them from getting it (fatal flaw: pride, fear, doubt). Arc: how they change (flat, positive, negative). Stage: character must change or choose
- **Format**: character name centered or above dialogue. Stage directions: brief, only crucial action, not novelistic. Readable at first glance
