# Baking & Pastry (Advanced)

## Doughs & Batters

### Laminated Dough
- **Croissant/puff pastry**: butter enclosed in dough, folded + rolled multiple times → many thin layers of butter + dough separated when baked by steam → flaky, airy
  - Detrempe: basic dough (flour, water, salt, yeast for croissant; no yeast for puff pastry). Beurrage: butter block (cold, pliable, 82-84% butterfat best — European style, less water. Plugrá, Kerrygold, Lurpak). Lock-in: envelop butter in dough
  - Turns: fold dough into thirds (like a letter) = 1 turn. Croissant: 3 turns (single folds). Puff pastry: 6 turns (double folds: fold in fourth for extra layers). Rest 30-60 min between turns in fridge (prevents butter melting into dough + allows gluten relaxation). Temperature critical: butter stays cold (55-60°F), dough stays cool, not too cold (butter cracks into shards)
  - After final turn: chill before rolling to final thickness. Use ruler for even thickness. Rest shaped croissants 2-3 hours at 72-75°F before egg wash + baking at 375-400°F
  - Troubleshooting: butter leaking = too warm during lamination. No rise = yeast dead or butter melted into dough. Uneven layers = uneven butter block. Dough tearing = insufficient rest, gluten too tight

### Sourdough (Advanced)
- **Hydration**: water % of flour weight. 60-65% = stiff, easy to handle (sandwich bread). 70-75% = standard artisan (slightly sticky, open crumb). 80-90% = very wet (ciabatta, focaccia — hard to handle, large holes). 100%+ = extremely high hydration, requires experience + technique
  - Higher hydration = more open crumb, harder to shape. Starter hydration should match dough hydration for best results (or close). For beginners: start at 70% hydration
- **Autolyse**: mix flour + water only, rest 20-60 min before adding starter + salt. Promotes gluten development (enzymes activate, flour absorbs water). Reduces kneading. Better extensibility
- **Bulk fermentation**: after mixing all ingredients, rest dough with stretch-and-folds every 30 min for first 2-3 hours (4-6 sets, depending on dough strength). Then let rise until 50-75% increase (not double — over-fermentation = slack dough + collapse). Time: 4-8 hours at 72-78°F (cold = slower). Use aliquot jar (small glass of same dough, track rise)
  - Signs of well-fermented: domed top, bubbles visible, jiggly, slight smell of yogurt. Under: flat, no bubbles, dense. Over: sticky, spread out, sharp/vinegary smell
- **Shaping**: pre-shape (round) → bench rest 20-30 min → final shape (boule/bâtard/batard/loaf). Tension: create surface tension by pulling dough across counter toward you (bench scraper), pinching seam, place seam side up in well-floured banneton
- **Cold retarding**: shaped loaves in fridge 8-48 hours. Slows fermentation (develops flavor — enzymes + bacteria produce complex sour notes), makes scoring easier (dough cold + stiff). Not essential but improves flavor, shelf life, and crust
- **Baking**: preheat oven + Dutch oven (cast iron, combo cooker) at 500°F for 45+ min. Flip loaf onto parchment, score (lame or sharp knife — 1/4-1/2" deep, 45° angle), place in hot Dutch oven with lid, reduce to 450°F, bake 20-25 min covered, remove lid, bake 15-25 min until deep golden/chestnut (internal 206-210°F). Cool completely (2+ hours) before cutting — or crumb is gummy. Good crumb: the bread squishes when squeezed but springs back
  - Steam: Dutch oven traps steam = best crust (crispy, glossy, blistered). For open-baking: lava rocks + water pan for steam, or spray oven + loaf with water at start. Score: allow expansion. Classic pattern: single slash (bâtard), cross (boules), wheat stalk (decorative). For best rise: deep score at 45° angle
- **Common problems**: dense crumb = under-fermented OR over-proofed OR weak starter. Flat bloom = not enough tension OR over-proofed OR scored incorrectly. Gummy texture = cut too early or underbaked. Blisters on crust = cold ferment with moisture on dough surface. Burnt bottom: place baking sheet on rack below Dutch oven

### Choux Pastry
- **Pâte à choux**: flour + water/milk + butter → cook on stovetop (dried to remove moisture) → add eggs → pipe → bake → hollow center for filling (cream puffs, éclairs, profiteroles, gougères). Unique: uses double cooking (stovetop + oven)
  - Drying stage: butter + liquid bring to boil, add all flour at once, stir vigorously until dough pulls away from pan (film forms on bottom — 1-2 min). Cool slightly (reduce to ~140°F) before adding eggs (one at a time, mix until smooth). Consistency: pipeable, holds shape without spreading. Test: press finger into piped paste — should leave distinct trough
  - Tips: pipe immediately (don't let piped shapes sit). Bake at 425°F 10 min, then reduce to 375°F 15-20 min (no peeking!). Piercing with skewer after baking releases steam — prevents sogginess. Freeze baked shells, re-crisp in 350° oven 5 min before filling. If choux lacks rise: not enough eggs? Or oven temp too low at start. Fallen after baking: underbaked or steam didn't escape fast enough (quick-open oven to vent before final browning)

## Custards & Creams

### Crème Anglaise (pouring custard)
- Eggs + sugar + milk/cream, cooked to 170-180°F (coats spoon). Temper eggs (slowly add hot milk to beaten egg+sugar while whisking constantly to prevent scrambling). Use vanilla bean (split, scrape seeds) for best flavor. Strain before chilling (to remove chalaza/stringy bits). Don't exceed 185°F or scrambles. Float ice cube over top + plastic wrap on surface to prevent skin
  - Uses: dessert sauce, ice cream base, trifle soak

### Crème Brûlée
- Custard (egg yolks + cream + sugar + vanilla), baked in water bath (low + slow = smooth, not curdled). Oven temp 300-325°F, water 2/3 up side of ramekin. Bake until just set (jiggly center — edges set but center still wobbly when gently shaken, ~30-45 min). Chill 4+ hours (or overnight) before torching sugar
  - Torching: thin, even layer of sugar (turbinado/Demerara or white). Use kitchen blowtorch (SUPER-hot, not butane torch with sooty flame don't use pocket lighters). Caramelize evenly, let harden 1 min before serving. Water bath: prevents curdling from high heat spiking — crucial

### Pastry Cream (Crème Pâtissière)
- Milk + egg yolks + sugar + flour/cornstarch (thickener). Cook until thickened (~6-8 min, vigorous boil for 30 sec to activate starch). Vanilla. Butter whisked in at end (for richness + shine/satiety). Chill covered with plastic on surface to prevent skin. Thicker than anglaise — can pipe, fill éclairs, cakes, fruit tarts. Store 3-5 days in fridge

### Italian Meringue
- Sugar syrup (240-250°F/soft ball) streamed into whipping egg whites → stable, glossy meringue. Cooks egg whites (the hot syrup reaches 240°F — hot enough to cook whites without scrambling). Swiss meringue: sugar + whites heated over simmering water to 140-160°F, then whipped (stabilizes before whipping). French meringue: just whisking egg whites + sugar, least stable — must bake immediately
  - Degrees of doneness: soft peaks (flop), firm peaks (stand straight), stiff peaks (dry, separated). Over-whipped: grains, weeping separated water. Italian meringue: cooked sugar syrup to 245°F

## Meringue-Based Desserts
- **Pavlova**: crisp meringue shell + soft marshmallow center + whipped cream + fruit. Add cornstarch + vinegar to meringue for soft center (the starch + acid creates soft center + crunchy crust). Low oven (225°F) for 60-90 min, then cool in oven (door cracked). Top with cream + kiwi + strawberries + passionfruit
- **Baked Alaska**: ice cream + cake + meringue, quickly browned in hot oven or torched. Meringue insulates ice cream. Freeze cake + ice cream base solid before adding meringue topping. Hot oven (450-500°F) 3-5 min. Torch works too

## Chocolate Work
- **Tempering**: heating + cooling chocolate to specific temperatures → stable crystal form (beta V) → shiny, snappy, smooth, no bloom (gray streaks on surface). Bloomed chocolate: still edible, just ugly — gray from melted + re-hardened fat (unstable crystals)
  - Dark: melt to 120-130°F, cool to 82°F, reheat to 88-90°F (working temp). Milk: melt to 110-120°F, cool to 80°F, reheat to 86-88°F. White: melt to 110°F, cool to 78°F, reheat to 84-86°F
  - Methods: seeding (add finely chopped solid chocolate to melted until temp drops. Most common, easiest). Tabling (pour 2/3 onto slab, work back + forth with spatula, return to bowl). Microwave (heat in short bursts, stir to seed). Ice bath (quick chill with constant stirring — risk of uneven crystalization + clumps). Verify temper: dip lip of paper in, check set: streaks shine at room temp? If bloom appears, re-temper
- **Ganache**: chocolate + cream. 1:1 ratio (truffle filling — soft, creamy, pipes). 2:1 chocolate:cream (firm, cut-able, for truffle centers). 3:1 (firm — holds shape for molding, bonbon filling)
  - Pour hot cream over chopped chocolate, let sit 1 min, gently stir from center outward. Don't whisk vigorously (incorporates air bubbles). For flavorings: add after ganache emulsifies — liqueur, extracts, spices, nut pastes
