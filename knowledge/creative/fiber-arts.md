# Basket Weaving & Fiber Arts

## Basket Materials
- **Natural fibers**: reed (rattan core, most common — comes in flat, round, oval. Soak 10-30 min before use). Cane (rattan outer layer, thin, used for chair seats + small baskets). Willow (traditional European basketry — fresh or soaked green, strongest when green). Seagrass (strong, rough, casual outdoor). Pine needles (coiled baskets, soft, aromatic). Raffia (palm leaf, soft, decorative). Hemp (strong, sustainable). Broomcorn (dyed, used for decorations)
  - All natural fibers absorb/shrink. Soak until flexible, work wet, let dry. Never rush drying or it cracks
- **Tools**: pruning shears (cut reed, willow). Awl (poke holes, tighten weaves). Tape measure. Clothespins/binder clips (hold work in progress). Spray bottle (moisten reed). Weighted base (hold basket while weaving. Paring knife — trim ends. Rapping iron — tap rows tight against each other

## Basic Weaves
- **Twining**: weave two weavers twisted around each vertical stake — strong, decorative. Cross each weaver over each other, change direction at each stake. Used for: rims, handles, transitions, basket walls alternating patterns. Start: wrap one weaver behind stake, twist, bring front, twist, behind next stake
- **Coiling**: bundle of material (center) wrapped around with stitches to previous coil. Used for: pine needle, raffia, grass baskets, floor mats, trivets. Slow but easy to pick up: stitch over previous coil, then wrap around + through same hole. Figure-8 stitch: continuous. Lazy squaw stitch: spacing between stitches
- **Plaiting**: over-under weave (like hair braiding, 3 strands). Flat, ribbon-like. Papyrus matting. Maori and Polynesian techniques (wider/flat vs round fibers). Cat's cradle: alternating over-under weave pattern
- **Random weave**: free-form, each weaver goes over-under different number of stakes. Rustic, decorative, fast

## Basket Shapes & Structures
- **Base**: round (start with slotted wooden base, or weave from center: 4 spokes, then 8, 16... add continuously. Twill or plain weave). Oval (split spokes added). Square/rectangular (wooden base with holes, stake insertion)
- **Sides/upsweep**: bend stakes up at 90° to form basket walls. Soak stakes well first to prevent cracking (heat/moisture). Tie tops loosely to hold while weaving. Gradually slope in/out for taper. Stakes can be forced inward (taper) or outward (bowl shape)
- **Rim (border)**: final step — finishing the top edge. Types: three-rod wale (weave 3 rods around each stake, tie, trap edge). Pack row ends under. U-shape binding. Rolled rim: fold stakes over, weave back down through weave. Track rim: two pieces around top, whip-stitched covered with thin cane/flat reed
- **Handle**: twisted bundle of reed/cane, wrap with thin cane for decorative finish. Insert into sides during rim step. 2-3 stakes thick. Secure: wrap handle ends into basket weave, tie tightly

## Other Fiber Arts
- **Macrame**: knotting cord into patterns. Knots: square knot (most basic, alternating), half hitch (diagonal, horizontal), lark's head (attachment knot), double half hitch (filled patterns — top horizontal line, then wrapping to fill). Materials: cotton, jute, hemp, nylon, paracord, silk. Projects: plant hangers, wall hangings, bags, jewelry
- **Spinning**: twist fibers into yarn. Fibers: wool (easiest), cotton (challenging, needs short staple), flax (linen — long, requires retting + processing), silk, alpaca, llama, synthetic. Tools: drop spindle (simple, cheap, portable) or spinning wheel (faster, for larger quantities). Draw: short forward (worsted = smooth, strong) vs long draw (woolen = fluffy, warm). Skein: twist yarn into loop, tie off. Wash to set twist, then wind into ball
- **Felting**: mat fibers together (wool only). Wet felting (hot water + soap + agitation — wool fibers lock together, can be shaped into hats, slippers, bags, 3D forms). Needle felting (barbed needle pokes dry wool — creates sculpture, flat pieces, 2D pics) — much slower but intricate
- **Weaving**: interlacing warp (vertical, tensioned) + weft (horizontal). Frame loom (beginner, small pieces, bracelets, mug cozies). Rigid heddle loom (medium, scarves, wider pieces, more pattern options). Floor loom (large, complex patterns, large fabric widths). Plain weave (over-under). Twill (over 2, under 1 = diagonal). Satin (float yarns over many warp threads, smooth surface). Tapestry weaving: weft only covers width colors, warp hidden at warp's full width
