# Photography Advanced

## Camera Systems Deep Dive

### Sensor Size Comparison
- **Full frame (35mm)**: 36×24mm. Best dynamic range + low-light + depth of field control. Camera size + lens size = biggest + heaviest. Common: Sony A7 series, Canon EOS R, Nikon Z, Panasonic S5II. Lens ecosystem: can be expensive but offers best selection
  - "One stop" advantage over APS-C in noise at same ISO. Larger pixels = better light gathering per pixel. But large lenses: 24-70mm f/2.8 is a statement — heavy, expensive, bulky
- **APS-C**: ~22-24×15-16mm (varies: Canon 1.6x crop, Nikon/Sony 1.5x). Great value, smaller system, excellent image quality. Crop factor 1.5x/1.6x: 50mm lens = 75-80mm equivalent field of view. Fuji X series, Sony a6000 series, Canon EOS R10/R50/R7. Best for many: travel, street, hobbyist
  - Better telephoto reach: 70-200mm lens = 105-300mm equivalent (good for wildlife/sports)
- **Micro Four Thirds (MFT)**: 17.3×13mm, 2x crop factor. Smaller, lighter, cheaper lenses. Excellent video (Panasonic GH series). Olympus/OM System strong IBIS. Smaller sensor = more depth of field (harder to blur background, but easier for macro)
  - Best for: video, travel, wildlife (2x crop = 100-400mm equivalent reach in smaller kit). IBIS excellent for handheld video/long exposures without tripod
- **Medium format**: larger than full frame (~44×33mm, 1.7x crop). Hasselblad, Fuji GFX series, Phase One. 50-100+ megapixels. Stunning image quality, colors, dynamic range. HUGE files. Slower operation. Very expensive (camera + lenses — GFX 100S ~$6k, lens ~$2.5k)

### Lenses by Focal Length
- **Ultra-wide <24mm**: landscape, architecture, interior, astro. Distortion (can be corrected). 14-24mm f/2.8 is common pro zoom. Focal length below 20mm = dramatic perspective, exaggerated close objects
- **Wide 24-35mm**: environmental portraits, street, landscape. 35mm: classic street/ documentary length (full frame). 28mm: popular for travel all-around prime. 24mm: wide enough for drama without too much distortion
- **Standard 40-60mm**: normal perspective (50mm ~human field of view). "Nifty fifty": f/1.8 or f/1.4, cheapest lens with best image quality ($100-200 for f/1.8, $300-600 for f/1.4). 50mm f/1.8 = best first lens. 40mm = slightly wider version, classic "standard" for full frame
- **Short tele 70-135mm**: portraits (85mm classic — flattering compression, background separation at f/1.4-f/2). 105mm macro. 135mm: preferred portrait length for headshots
- **Telephoto 135-300mm**: sports, wildlife, events. 70-200mm f/2.8 = event/wedding workhorse. 200-600mm for wildlife
- **Super tele 300mm+**: wildlife, birds, sports. Big, heavy, expensive ($5k-15k+ for fast 400-600mm f/4-2.8). Great with teleconverter (1.4x/2x extender — adds focal length at cost of aperture + sharpness)

### Aperture Explained
- **f-stop**: f/1.4 → f/2 → f/2.8 → f/4 → f/5.6 → f/8 → f/11 → f/16 → f/22 (each stop = half/double light). Lower number = larger opening = more light, shallower DOF. Sharpness sweet spot: f/5.6-f/8 for most lenses (diffraction at f/16+ for full frame, f/11+ for crop sensors)
  - Wide open (f/1.4-f/2) = softer, vignette, chromatic aberration (purple fringing — more common in wide apertures + cheaper lenses). Stopped down 1-2 stops = sharpest
- **Depth of field (DOF)**: determined by aperture + focal length + distance to subject + sensor size. Wide aperture + long focal length + close subject = shallow DOF. Same: 85mm f/1.8 at 5ft = very shallow. 18mm f/3.5 at 10ft = everything in focus
  - Hyperfocal distance: focus point where everything from half that distance to infinity is acceptably sharp. Useful for landscape photography. Hyperfocal distance = lens_focal_length^2 / (aperture × circle_of_confusion)
- **Bokeh**: quality of out-of-focus blur. Aperture blade count (more blades = rounder circles). Lens design: aspherical elements affect quality. Smooth, creamy, non-distracting. "Nisen bokeh" = double-line effect on specular highlights (bad). Leica/Zeiss/high-end Nikon/Canon lenses tend to have pleasing bokeh; cheaper 50mm f/1.8 can still produce acceptable bokeh

## Advanced Composition
- **Negative space**: empty area draws attention to subject. Minimalist. "Less is more." Use sky, empty wall, blank field. Creates calm, emphasizes loneliness/isolation
- **Patterns & texture**: fill frame with repeating elements — stairs, windows, tiles, bricks, leaves. Break pattern with one different element = strong focal point
- **Leading lines**: roads, rivers, fences, shadows, arms, stairs. Lead eye through frame. Better: leading FROM bottom corners TOWARD subject. S-curves most pleasing, diagonals dynamic
- **Frame within frame**: use foreground elements to frame subject (arch, window, branches, tunnel, door). Adds depth, context, tells story. Can darken frame edge to emphasize subject
- **Layering**: foreground + midground + background. Each layer tells part of story with depth. Gives 3D feel. Example landscape: flower foreground (close), tree midground (middle), mountain background (far). Stack to create perspective
- **Symmetry**: architectural and reflection images. Architectural: centered, straight, perfect symmetry. Strong impact. Try also asymmetry for contrast. Perfect mirror reflection on water
- **Golden ratio**: The rule of thirds is a simplified version. Fibonacci spiral guides placement. 1:1.618. Photo composition grid overlays in camera/editing apps: many have golden spiral overlay. Use dynamic symmetry

## Lighting (Advanced)
- **Hard vs soft light**: hard = small light source relative to subject (noon sun, bare bulb, small flash). Harsh shadows, texture, drama. Soft = large light source (overcast sky, window, softbox). Smooth transitions, flattering portraits
  - Soft light created by: diffuser between light + subject ($20 5-in-1 reflector, thin fabric, or scrim — large panel). Softbox: softens, shapes, controls light spread. Umbrella: bounce (soft, broad) or shoot-through (soft, more forward). Diffusion materials: gaffer's opal, bedsheet, shower curtain
- **Light direction**: front (flat, no shadows — passport/mugshot). Side (dramatic, texture, Rembrandt triangle). Back (silhouette, rim light, separation). Top (gull wing shadows under eyes — unflattering. But can create mood with gobos/modifiers). Bottom (spooky, fill shadow, product). Loop lighting: light slightly to side + above = classic portrait
- **Color temperature**: 3200K (tungsten/warm), 5600K (daylight/cool). Mixed lighting: tricky WB (use gels to match flash to ambient)
  - Gels: color correction gels on flash (CTO: green to warm CTO orange, CTB: blue). CTO = color temperature orange (turns flash warm to match tungsten) — full CTO ~3200K. CTB = color temperature blue (turns flash cool to match daylight — direction opposite)

## Editing Workflow (Advanced)
- **RAW editing**: NOT optional for serious work (records all sensor data, 12-14 bits per channel vs 8-bit JPEG. Massive headroom for exposure ±3-4 stops, white balance correction). Color depth: 12-bit = 4096 levels, 14-bit = 16,384 levels per channel. 8-bit JPEG = 256. For large prints or major edits, RAW essential. Raw Therapee, Darktable (free competitive with Lightroom)
- **Dodging & burning**: local exposure adjustments — lighten (dodge) or darken (burn) specific areas. Emphasize subject, create depth, guide eye. Non-destructive layers/ masks. In Lightroom: adjustment brush. In Photoshop: Curves adjustment layer with mask painted black/white
- **Color grading**: Lift (shadows), Gamma (midtones), Gain (highlights) color wheels — adjust hue + saturation per tonal range. Teal/orange (orange skin, teal shadows = Hollywood blockbuster look). Split toning for mood (warm highlights + cool shadows)
  - Color wheel: Hue (color), Saturation (intensity), Luminance (brightness). LUT (Look Up Table): Load looks from presets, adjust. LUT: 3D math to transform color. Can download or create your own. Use LUT as starting point for grading, then adjust master curves
- **Noise reduction**: luminance noise (grain, monochrome) vs chroma noise (color speckles). Luminance: grayscale detail — use sparingly or image goes plastic/waxy. Chroma: more aggressively reducible without losing detail. Lightroom, Topaz Denoise AI, DxO PureRAW, ON1 NoNoise
- **Sharpening**: unsharp mask (radius 0.5-1.5 pixels, amount 50-150%). Output sharpening for specific display (screen vs print). Don't oversharpen (halos). Better: Photoshop Smart Sharpen with lens blur removal for ideal sharpening

## Studio Photography
- **Background**: seamless paper (white, gray, black, colors — most popular). V-flat: large white/black panels for bounce/flag. Cloth, painted wall, outdoor
- **Portrait lighting patterns**: Rembrandt (triangle of light on shadowed cheek, 45° high key + fill). Loop (small shadow under nose, slight off axis). Split (half face lit, half dark, dramatic, moody). Butterfly (key high above camera, shadow under nose, glamour). Broad vs short lighting (broad = light on the side closer to camera, short = lit on the far side)
- **Flash vs continuous**: flash power (guide number) vs continuous (LED panel, 100W+ for daylight fill). Flash power: watt-seconds (WS). Studio strobe: 300-1000Ws. Guide number (GN): GN60 = at ISO 100 f/5.6 at 10 feet? Flash sync speed: camera limit usually 1/200–1/250, high-speed sync available
  - TTL flash: auto exposure through lens. Manual flash: full control once dialed in, consistent from shot to shot
