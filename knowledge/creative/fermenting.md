# Fermentation & Preserving

## Fermentation Basics
- **What it is**: controlled microbial growth (bacteria, yeast, mold) that transforms food — extends shelf life, creates new flavors, improves digestibility, adds probiotics
  - Lactic acid fermentation (vegetables, yogurt, sourdough): Lactobacillus bacteria produce lactic acid → tangy, preserves
  - Alcoholic fermentation (beer, wine, cider): yeast converts sugar → ethanol + CO₂
  - Acetic acid fermentation (vinegar): Acetobacter bacteria convert ethanol → acetic acid
  - Koji fermentation (miso, soy sauce, sake): Aspergillus oryzae mold breaks down starches/proteins
- **Salt**: critical for vegetable ferments — inhibits spoilage bacteria, allows Lactobacillus to thrive, controls texture
  - Ratio: 2-3% salt by weight of vegetables (20-30g salt per kg veg)
  - Salt types: non-iodized (iodine can inhibit fermentation), no anti-caking agents (same reason). Pickling/kosher salt (pure NaCl)
- **Anaerobic environment**: keep vegetables SUBMERGED below brine — oxygen allows mold/kahm yeast. Use weights (glass weights, ziploc bag filled with brine), airlock lids

## Vegetable Ferments

### Sauerkraut
1. Shred cabbage (~1 head)
2. Add 2% salt by weight (20g per 1kg cabbage)
3. Massage until water is released (10-15 min) — cabbage should be submerged in its own brine
4. Pack tightly into jar, pressing out air bubbles
5. Weigh down to keep submerged (cabbage leaf or weight)
6. Cover with cloth/airlock, keep at 65-72°F for 1-4 weeks
7. Taste after 1 week — longer = more sour. Refrigerate when ready (slows fermentation)

### Kimchi
- Base: napa cabbage (salt/soak 2 hours, rinse), daikon radish, scallions, chives
- Paste: gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes), garlic, ginger, fish sauce (or soy sauce for vegan), fermented shrimp (jeotgal) optional
- Mix paste with veggies, pack tightly into jar, leave at room temp 2-5 days (bubbling = active), then refrigerate
  - The paste is key — adjust spice level, add asian pear/apple for sweetness

### Pickles (lacto-fermented, not vinegar)
- Cucumbers: use pickling cucumbers (small, firm), NOT salad cucumbers (get mushy). Add grape leaves/horseradish leaf/tannin source (keeps crunchy)
- Brine: 3-4% salt (30-40g per L water). Add: garlic, dill, peppercorns, bay leaf, mustard seed, chili flakes
- Ferment at 65-72°F for 1-4 weeks. Check daily — skim any surface scum
- Tip: slice blossom end off (contains enzymes that soften pickles)

### Kombucha
- **SCOBY** (Symbiotic Culture Of Bacteria and Yeast): rubbery pancake of cellulose + bacteria + yeast
- **Recipe**: black or green tea + sugar + starter tea (from previous batch or store-bought kombucha) + SCOBY
  1. Brew tea (1 Tbsp loose leaf or 4 bags per 1L water), dissolve 1/4 cup sugar
  2. Cool to room temperature (important! hot tea kills SCOBY)
  3. Add starter tea (1-2 cups), add SCOBY
  4. Cover with cloth + rubber band, ferment 7-14 days at 72-80°F
  5. Taste after 7 days — should be tangy, less sweet. Longer = more sour
  6. Bottle with fruit/puree for second fermentation (2-3 days, carbonation builds — burp bottles daily to avoid explosions)
- **Scope**: new SCOBY forms on top each batch (pellicle). Remove, share, compost, or feed to chickens. Save extra with "SCOBY hotel" (jar of kombucha in fridge, feed monthly)
- **Problems**: mold (fuzzy, colorful = toss everything), kahm yeast (white wrinkled layer = skim, probably fine), vinegar taste = too long, not sweet enough = too long or not enough sugar

### Sourdough Starter
- **Day 1**: mix 50g whole wheat/rye flour + 50g water (non-chlorinated). Cover loosely
- **Days 2-7**: discard half (or use for discard recipes), feed 50g flour + 50g water daily
  - Keep at 72-80°F (warmer = faster). After 5-10 days: should double in 4-8 hours after feeding, smell fruity/tangy, not like acetone
- **Maintenance**: feed 1:1:1 ratio (starter:flour:water). Keep in fridge (feed weekly) or counter (feed daily)
  - If in fridge: take out, feed, wait for 2x rise (4-8 hours) before baking
- **Baking**: use bubbly starter at peak. 20% starter by weight of flour (100g starter for 500g flour)
  - No commercial yeast needed — the starter provides leavening + flavor
- **Discard recipes**: pancakes, waffles, crackers, pizza dough, quick breads, cookies

## Canning
- **Safety**: canning is about botulism prevention (Clostridium botulinum — soil bacteria that produces deadly toxin)
  - Botulism toxin is one of the most toxic substances known — 1 gram can kill millions
  - Botulism spores: heat-resistant, need specific conditions to grow (low acid, anaerobic, low salt, low sugar)
  - Canned food that might have botulism: looks normal (no smell/taste), but can kill — "when in doubt, throw it out" (don't taste test!)
- **Water bath canning**: for HIGH-ACID foods (pH < 4.6) — jams, jellies, pickles, fruit, tomatoes (add acid if needed)
  - Submerge jars in boiling water. Processing time varies (10-60 min depending on food + jar size + altitude)
  - Altitude: processing time increases 20% per 1,000 ft above sea level
- **Pressure canning**: for LOW-ACID foods (pH > 4.6) — vegetables, meat, poultry, fish, soups, beans
  - MUST use pressure canner (NOT pressure cooker!) — reaches 240°F (116°C) to kill botulism spores
  - Pressure depends on altitude: 10 PSI up to 1,000 ft, 15 PSI above 1,000 ft
- **Jars**: Mason jars (Ball/Kerr/Weck), 2-piece lids (flat + band). Inspect for cracks/nicks. New flat each time, band reusable
  - Fill: leave 1/2" headspace for most foods (1" for starchy). Wipe rim clean (any residue = seal failure)
  - Process: water must cover jars by 1-2". Don't tighten bands too much (air needs to vent). Cool 12-24 hours
  - Check seal: lid should be concave (curved down), no flex when pressed. Remove bands, store without them (band off = can see broken seal). Label with date + contents
  - Unsealed: refrigerate and use within 2 weeks, or re-process (if within 24 hours, re-sterilize, new lid, same processing time)
- **Altitude matters**: water boils at lower temperature at altitude — must adjust processing time/pressure
  - At 5,000 ft: water boils at 202°F vs 212°F at sea level — can't rely on boiling alone for preservation, need longer times

## Dehydrating
- **Drying**: removes moisture so spoilage organisms can't grow
  - Dehydrator ($40-200) > oven (hard to hold low temp, poor airflow)
  - Herbs: lowest temp (95°F), 1-4 hours
  - Fruits: 135°F, 6-12 hours (pretreat with lemon juice/vinegar to prevent browning)
  - Vegetables: 125°F, 4-10 hours (blanch first for better quality)
  - Meat (jerky): 165°F marinade + dehydrate 4-6 hours. Use lean meat (fat goes rancid)
- **Storage**: airtight container, cool/dark/dry. Most dried foods last 6-12 months (longer if vacuum sealed + oxygen absorber)
  - Condition: store dried fruit in jar 1 week, shake daily — if moisture condenses, needs more drying
- **Fruit leather**: puree fruit + sweetener (optional), spread thin on dehydrator sheet, dry at 135°F until not sticky (4-8 hours)

## Freezing
- **What freezes well**: fruits (berries, bananas, mango slices — individually frozen on tray then bagged), vegetables (blanch first — boiling water 1-3 min, ice bath, dry, freeze), soups/stews (leave headspace for expansion), bread, cookie dough, butter, cheese (hard cheese freezes better than soft)
  - Most vegetables need blanching (enzyme inactivation) before freezing — otherwise they deteriorate in flavor/texture
- **What doesn't freeze well**: lettuce/cucumber (cell walls break → mushy), cream/milk sauces (separate), eggs in shell (explode), cooked pasta (texture changes), potatoes in stews (get grainy), mayonnaise (separates)
- **Technique**: freeze in single layer on tray first (fruit, cookie dough balls), then bag — prevents clumping
  - Exclude air: vacuum sealer (best), press out air from freezer bags, use straw to suck air out
  - Label: contents + date — frozen food looks identical after 3 months
  - Shelf life: vegetables/fruit 8-12 months (quality), meat 4-12 months (varies), cooked meals 2-4 months, frozen foods remain safe past quality date (texture/taste degrades)
