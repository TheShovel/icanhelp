# Screenwriting

Formatting, structure, character development, and industry standards for film and TV writing.

## Format
- **Industry standard**: Courier New 12pt font (1 page ≈ 1 minute of screen time). Screenwriting software (Final Draft, Fade In, WriterSolo, Highland 2) handles formatting automatically
- **Scene heading (slugline)**: INT. or EXT. → LOCATION → TIME OF DAY (or CONTINUOUS). Example: "INT. COFFEE SHOP - DAY"
- **Action**: Present tense, concise, only what can be seen/heard. No internal thoughts. Use prose sparingly — white space is your friend
- **Character name**: CAPS, centered above dialogue. Character introduction: CAPS in action ("JOHN (30s, rugged)...")
- **Dialogue**: Character name centered, dialogue lines below, ~3.5" wide. Parentheticals (wrylies): (beat), (sarcastic), (whispering) — use sparingly
- **Transitions**: CUT TO:, FADE IN:, FADE OUT. Less used in modern screenwriting — usually only FADE IN at start and FADE OUT at end
- **Dual dialogue**: Two characters talking at once (side by side). Rare — use only for overlapping arguments, simultaneous reactions

## Three-Act Structure
- **Act 1 (Setup)**: ~25% of screenplay. Introduce protagonist, their world, the problem. Inciting incident (call to adventure) by page 10-15 — event that disrupts normal world. First plot point / act break: protagonist makes decision to engage (can't go back)
- **Act 2 (Confrontation)**: ~50%. Rising action, obstacles, complications. Midpoint: major revelation or reversal (either protagonist's fortunes change or stakes increase). "All is lost" moment near end of act 2 (dark night of the soul). Often the protagonist's greatest fear realized. Second plot point: protagonist finds renewed determination
- **Act 3 (Resolution)**: ~25%. Climax (final confrontation with antagonist/problem) — protagonist must actively solve problem (not happen to them). Resolution / denouement: show the new normal (what changed?). 1-5 pages

## Beat Sheet (Save the Cat — Blake Snyder)
- Opening Image (1) — snapshot of protagonist before the journey
- Theme Stated (5) — hint of what protagonist needs to learn
- Set-Up (1-10) — establish protagonist's world, relationships, flaws
- Catalyst (12) — inciting incident
- Debate (12-25) — protagonist questions whether to act
- Break into Two (25) — protagonist chooses to act
- B Story (30) — subplot (romance, mentor, friend)
- Fun and Games (30-55) — "promise of the premise" — the fun of the concept
- Midpoint (55) — stakes raised, false victory or defeat
- Bad Guys Close In (55-75) — obstacles multiply, pressure builds
- All Is Lost (75) — "whiff of death" — momentary defeat
- Dark Night of the Soul (75-85) — protagonist wallows, then finds renewed resolve
- Break into Three (85) — plan for final confrontation
- Finale (85-110) — protagonist applies lesson learned, defeats antagonist
- Final Image (110) — shows how protagonist has changed

## Character Development
- **Flawed protagonist**: Perfect characters are boring. Give protagonist a flaw that the plot forces them to confront. Growth = overcoming (or accepting) the flaw
- **Want vs Need**: What character thinks they want (external goal) vs what they actually need (internal growth). Their arc is realizing the need. Example: "I want to win the race" (want) → "I need to accept myself" (need)
- **Character arc types**: Change (protagonist fundamentally changes — most common), Growth (core stays same but learns lesson — hero's quest), Fall (decline into darkness — tragedy), Flat (unchanging, changes world around them — action heroes, James Bond)
- **Character voice**: Each character sounds different — word choice, sentence length, vocabulary, tics. Read dialogue aloud — does it sound like this specific character?
- **Subtext**: What characters say vs what they mean. Dialogue = 10% of communication. The other 90% = what they hide, avoid, deflect. Avoid "on the nose" dialogue (characters saying exactly what they feel)

## Writing Process
- **Logline**: 1-2 sentence summary of your screenplay — protagonist + goal + obstacle + stakes. "When [event], a [character] must [action] or else [stakes]" — test your concept: if logline isn't compelling, outline won't fix it
- **Outline/Beat Sheet**: Scene-by-scene breakdown before writing. 1-2 sentences per scene. Saves massive rewriting. Industry pros spend 30-50% of writing time on outline
- **Treatment**: 5-15 page prose summary — sells producers/execs on the story before you write script. Includes key scenes, character arcs, tone
- **First draft**: Write without editing — "shut up and write." Perfectionism kills momentum. You can fix a bad page but not a blank page
- **Rewriting**: Expected — "Writing is rewriting." Read aloud to catch awkward dialogue. Coverage: analyze scenes for purpose (does this scene advance plot, reveal character, or both? If neither, cut it)
- **Feedback (table reads)**: Listen without defending. Multiple people having same issue = it's real. You don't have to agree with all notes but consider them

## Industry Standards
- **Spec script**: Unproduced screenplay written on speculation — no attachments, no guarantee. Standard for breaking in. Must be exceptional to get noticed
- **Pitch**: Verbal summary of story — logline + key scenes + character + tone. 5-10 min. Often to development execs. "Sizzle" = make them feel the movie
- **Query letter**: 1 page to agents/managers — logline + brief bio + comparables ("It's Die Hard meets Groundhog Day"). Personalize to recipient. Keep professional
- **Option**: Producer pays small fee ($500-$10k) for exclusive right to buy the script later. Advances apply against purchase price. 12-18 months typical
- **WGA**: Writers Guild of America — minimum compensation, residuals, credit arbitration. Signatory companies required for professional production
