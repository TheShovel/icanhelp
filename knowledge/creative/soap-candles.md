# Soap & Candle Making

## Cold Process Soap Making
- **Ingredients**: oils (fat) + lye (sodium hydroxide) + water (or milk, beer, puree). Lye: CAUSTIC ALKALI (NaOH) — wear goggles + gloves, work in well-ventilated area, add lye to water (NOT water to lye — can erupt like volcano). Mix until dissolved, let cool to 100-110°F
  - Oils: coconut (hard, cleansing, high lather but drying). Olive (mild, moisturizing, soft bar). Palm (hard bar, creamy lather). Shea butter (moisturizing, creamy). Castor (bubbly lather). Almond (gentle). Rice bran (creamy). Lye calculator: "SoapCalc" online — enter oil amounts, get required lye + water quantity. Each oil has saponification value (SAP) = how much lye needed to turn oil into soap
- **Process**: heat oils to 100-110°F. Mix lye water into oils. Stick blend to "trace" (thin pudding consistency — dripping from blender leaves visible trail). Add fragrance (essential oils or fragrance oils, 0.5-1 oz per lb of oils). Color: natural clays, oxides, micas (use cosmetic-grade), activated charcoal. Pour into mold (silicone loaf mold). Insulate 24 hrs. Unmold, cut, cure 4-6 weeks (bars harden, pH drops to safe ~9-10). Cold process soap is NOT safe until lye is completely saponified — test with pH paper (want 9-10 or lower)
- **Hot process**: cook in slow cooker after trace (accelerates saponification). Cook until "vaseline stage" (transparent gel). Add fragrance at the end. Can use same day (but still better to cure 1-2 weeks for harder bar). Less smooth aesthetic — rustic appearance
- **Melt and pour**: pre-made soap base (glycerin, shea, goat's milk). Melt, add fragrance + color, pour. Easiest method — no lye handling. Great for kids/ beginners. Not "from scratch" but perfectly good soap

## Candle Making

### Wax Types
- **Soy**: natural, clean burn, biodegradable, less soot, lower melt point (melts in sun). 100% soy = can have mottled/frost appearance (rustic look). Good for containers (tarts, votives, pillars need harder wax blend). Soy wax is a soft wax
- **Paraffin**: most common, cheap, strong scent throw (binds fragrance oil best). Blended with soy or additives. Can be harder (pillars, tarts). Petroleum byproduct
- **Beeswax**: natural, honey scent, dripless (best for taper pillars), burns longer, expensive $10-20/lb vs $2-8/lb for soy/paraffin. Clean air-purifying negative ions (claims: beeswax releases negative ions). Doesn't need fragrance — natural honey aroma
- **Coconut**: creamy, excellent scent throw, smooth tops, expensive. Mixes with soy for better container performance
- **Palm**: crystalline (feather-like pattern), hard, good for pillars, can be eco-sourced. Some issues with orangutan habitat (imported palm oil)—buy sustainable
- **Gel wax**: clear, rubbery (for decorative embedded objects — sand, shells, marbles). Burns longer, burns differently than traditional wax — don't use with normal candle wicks. Requires special high melt wick + gel-specific wick

### Supplies
- **Wicks**: size matters (too small = tunneling, too big = soot + smoke). Pre-waxed wicks. Types: cotton (most common — for container/ pillar/tart/taper). Wood (crackles, trendy, needs specific melting). HTP (heat treated — good for soy). CD (candle making, zinc core — clean burn, self-trimming). ECO (clean burning, natural). Size chart: varies for wax type + container diameter. General: 2-3" container → size CD-10 to CD-14 or LX-12 to LX-20; 3-4" container → CD-16 to CD-20. Test before production batches
- **Fragrance oil**: candle-safe (not essential oils — some may not bind with wax and can be flammable). Max fragrance load: 6-10% of wax weight (1 oz per pound). Too much = sweating, wick clog, poor burn, fire hazard. Fragrance load: check supplier's max recommended for each wax type
- **Containers**: heat-resistant glass (canning jars, tumblers). Don't use thin glass (wine glasses) — can break. Metal tins (heat-resistant painted). Ceramic with melt temp under 275°F could crack. Tempered glass is best

### Process
1. **Melt wax** to recommended temp (soy: 170-180°F, paraffin: 160-180°F). Use double boiler (don't overheat — wax is flammable). Digital thermometer essential — wax can catch fire above manufacturer's flash point
2. **Add fragrance oil** at recommended temp (soy: 160°F — when wax off heat). Stir gently (avoid air bubbles) for 2 min
3. **Cool to pouring temp** (soy: 120-140°F, paraffin: 135-155°F). Over-hot = separation, wet spots; under-hot = rough top, sinkholes
4. **Secure wick** in container (wick sticker dot, centered). Use wick centering tool (pencil with hole, wick bar, clothespin on top)
5. **Pour wax** slowly, avoid splashing. Leave 1/2" headspace from rim. Tap container to release bubbles (use heat gun for plastic containers). Pour at 125-135°F for soy
6. **Let set** 24-48 hours. Don't disturb (no moving). Keep away from drafts (uneven cooling = surface cracks, sinkholes)
7. **Trim wick** to 1/4" before first burn (prevents mushrooming + soot). First burn: allow melted wax to reach container edge (full melt pool, ~1 hour) to prevent tunneling

## Candle Safety
- **Burn time**: 3-4 hours max per session (longer = wick mushrooms, glass overheats). Extinguish if flame gets too high
- **Never leave unattended**. Keep away from drafts, pets, kids, flammable items. Trim wick to 1/4" each burn
- **Container**: stop burning when 1/2" wax remains at bottom (glass can shatter). For tins: don't burn to bottom (hot tin on surface)
- **Fire safety**: candle=open flame. Never leave a burning candle alone (not even for a "quick" errand). Keep out of reach of children + pets. Burn away from anything flammable (curtains, books, decorations). On heat-resistant surface
