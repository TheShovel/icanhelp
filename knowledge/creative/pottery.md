# Pottery & Ceramics

Handbuilding, wheel throwing, glazing, and firing techniques.

## Clay Types
- **Earthenware**: Low-fire (cone 06-04, ~1900°F), porous after firing, red/brown/white. Needs glaze for waterproofing. Common for flowerpots, terracotta, beginner work
- **Stoneware**: Mid-fire (cone 6, ~2200°F) and high-fire (cone 10, ~2300°F). Vitreous (waterproof without glaze). Durable — dinnerware, mugs, functional ware. Most common studio choice
- **Porcelain**: High-fire (cone 10, ~2350°F). White, translucent when thin, very strong. Finicky — prone to warping, slumping. Advanced material
- **Paper clay**: Clay + paper pulp (10-30%) — lighter, stronger dry strength. Good for sculpture, repairs (paper burns out in firing)

## Handbuilding
- **Pinch pots**: Start with ball of clay, insert thumb, pinch walls evenly while rotating. Oldest pottery method
- **Coil building**: Roll coils (rope of clay), stack and blend (score + slip). Can make large forms, any shape. Blend interior for strength
- **Slab building**: Roll flat slab (uniform thickness with rolling guides — two sticks of same height), cut shapes, assemble when leather-hard. Boxes, plates, geometric forms
- **Score and slip**: Scratch lines on joining surfaces + apply slip (liquid clay) = strong mechanical bond. Essential for all handbuilding joins

## Wheel Throwing
- **Centering**: Most critical step — clay spinning on wheel, apply even pressure to center against centrifugal force. If not centered, pot wobbles
- **Opening**: Create hole in center of centered ball, leave 1/2" floor thickness
- **Pulling walls**: Evenly pull clay upward — inside hand supports, outside hand pulls. Multiple pulls, each 1/2" taller
- **Shaping**: Use ribs, sponge, hands — curve inward for bottle, outward for bowl
- **Trimming**: After clay firms to leather-hard — trim foot ring (bottom of pot). Trim upside-down on wheel
- **Common forms**: Cylinder (mug, cup), bowl, plate, bottle/vase, teapot

## Drying
- **Slow and even** — cover with plastic (slow), open slightly (medium), remove (fast). Cracking occurs from uneven drying
- **Stages**: Wet → Cheese-hard (can hold shape but still soft) → Leather-hard (firm, can trim/handle) → Bone-dry (room temp, no moisture, fragile — can be bisqued)
- **Cracking repair**: For leather-hard: score + slip + press. For bone-dry: paper clay slip (stronger). Don't water down — creates weak spot

## Glazing
- **Glaze components**: Silica (glass former), alumina (stiffener — prevents running), flux (melts at lower temp). Color from metal oxides
- **Application**: Dipping (even coat), pouring (controlled dripping), brushing (3 coats), spraying (even, fine mist), wax resist (keep areas unglazed — foot ring)
- **Glaze fit**: Glaze must compress slightly on cooling — if glaze shrinks more than clay, crazing (fine cracks). If less, shivering (glaze flakes off)
- **Food safety**: Lead-free glazes only for dinnerware. Some low-fire glazes not food-safe (lead, cadmium). Test with lemon slice on fired glaze (acid test)
- **Common defects**: Crawling (glaze won't stick — dusty clay, apply more), pinholes (air escaping through glaze), blistering/ bubbling (too thick/ fast), running (too much flux)

## Firing
- **Bisque firing**: Cone 06-04 (~1900°F) — removes chemical water, makes clay porous for glazing. Slow ramp (prevent cracking). Unglazed bisque is called "bisque"
- **Glaze firing**: Cone 6 (~2200°F) or Cone 10 (~2350°F) — melts glaze into glassy surface. Atmosphere affects results
- **Kiln types**: Electric (most common, oxidation atmosphere — clean colors), Gas (reduction atmosphere — earthy, variegated effects), Wood (long firing, ash glaze, labor intensive), Raku (remove at red heat + reduction in combustible material — dramatic crackle)
- **Cone system**: Pyrometric cones measure heat work (temperature + time). Small triangle melts at specific point. Orton cones standard — 022 (lowest) to 14 (hottest)

## Studio Safety
- **Silica dust**: From dry clay, sanding bisque — wear N95 mask, wet-mop floors, no dry sweeping
- **Glaze toxicity**: Lead, barium, cadmium in some glazes — no food contact, wash hands after handling, no spraying without ventilation
- **Kiln fumes**: Vent outside — carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, metal fumes from glazes
- **Ergonomics**: Core strength for centering, wrists straight when throwing, stretch hands regularly
- **Slip hazard**: Wet floor from throwing — wear rubber-soled shoes, mop spills immediately
