# Singing & Vocal Technique

## Breath Support
- **Diaphragmatic breathing (belly breathing)**: diaphragm descends, lungs expand downward — belly pushes out, shoulders stay still. The foundation of all good singing. Opposite of shallow chest breathing
- **Exercise**: lie on back, hand on belly. Inhale → belly rises (balloon inflating), exhale slowly with "sss" — feel belly fall. Master this before adding pitch. Goal: 15-20 sec steady exhale
- **Appoggio (Italian technique)**: engage lower abs/core during inhalation to resist diaphragm's natural recoil — creates steady, controlled airflow. The "breath suspension" feeling, not locking
- **Support vs pressure**: support = controlled airflow from core engagement. Pressure = forced, tight air from throat/chest — causes strain, cracking. If throat feels tight, you're using pressure, not support
- **Quick inhale**: breathe like surprised — silent, fast, low. Practice: gasp and hold (singers call this "the sip"). Mark breaths in sheet music

## Resonance
- **Resonance = amplification**: vocal cords produce fundamental tone (buzzy); resonance cavities (throat, mouth, nasal passages, sinuses, chest) shape and amplify it. This is what gives a voice "carry" without effort
- **Places of resonance**: Chest (lower frequencies, full sound), Pharyngeal (throat — cutting, bright), Nasal (bright, "twangy" — useful for mix voice, excessive = nasal quality), Head/mask (forward, ring — face vibrations around nose/cheeks), Mask resonance is key for projection without strain
- **Exercise**: hum, feel vibration on lips/nose. Open to vowel — keep same buzz/placement.
- **Resonance vs volume**: don't push air to be louder — find the resonant "sweet spot" where the tone rings effortlessly.

## Vowel Shaping
- **Pure vowels (English)**: EE (feet), IH (sit), AY (say), EH (bed), AH (father), UH (but), AW (law), OH (goat), OO (boot), U (book). Singing requires pure vowels — diphthongs (gliding vowels like "I" = ah-ee) held on first vowel
- **Modification**: vowels must adjust in higher register (e.g., "AH" toward "UH," "EE" toward "IH") to maintain resonance without strain. Called "covering" or "vowel migration" — essential for passaggio navigation
- **Italian vowels (the standard)**: A (ah), E (eh), I (ee), O (oh), U (oo). Practice legato on single vowel across scales
- **Vowel uniformity**: keep consistent mouth shape for same vowel throughout phrase. Jaw drops for open vowels (AH), closer for closed (EE). Tongue position: high front (EE), low back (AH)

## Registers
- **Chest voice**: thick vocal cord closure, lower pitch range, feels in chest — speaking voice, belt, powerful lower notes. Full, rich
- **Head voice**: thinner cord closure, higher range, feels in head/face. Lighter, more flexible. Not the same as falsetto (falsetto has air leakage — breathy, disconnected)
- **Mixed voice**: blend of chest + head — allows belting higher without straining. The holy grail of contemporary singing. Thin edge of vocal cords approximates while keeping cord closure — feels like "zipping" up
- **Falsetto**: CT muscle dominant, TA relaxes — floppy cords, air escapes. Airy, light. Useful for style, but can't build power here
- **Passaggio**: transition points between registers (primo = chest→mix, secondo = mix→head). Smoothing the passaggio = major technical goal. Exercises: sirens, lip trills, nasal consonants help bridge the break
- **Belting**: singing in chest/mix above comfort zone (C5+). Requires strong support, open throat, forward resonance. Misused belting causes damage — must be taught carefully

## Vocal Warm-Ups
- **Daily routine (10-15 min)**: (1) gentle hum (glides, 5-note scales) — 2 min. (2) lip trills / tongue trills (bbbb, rrrr) across range — 3 min. (3) sirens (lowest to highest and back) — 2 min. (4) scales on vowels (doh-re-mi patterns) — 3 min. (5) arpeggios (1-3-5-8-5-3-1) — 2 min. (6) agility runs (fast scales or staccato) — 2 min
- **Lip trills**: blow air through relaxed lips (brrr). Easiest warm-up — relaxes vocal tract, builds breath control. Do on scales, slides, siren
- **Humming**: gentle vibration without full phonation — less impact on cords. Vary pitch, feel vibrations in mask. "Mmmm" up and down scales
- **Straw phonation**: sing through a stirring straw into water or air. Reduces vocal fold collision force — excellent for rehab and warm-up. Do scales, arpeggios, songs through straw for 3-5 min
- **Don't**: push through pain, strain, or hoarseness. Warm up gently and gradually increase intensity. Hoarseness = STOP

## Range Extension
- **Range vs tessitura**: range = all notes you can physically produce. Tessitura = where you sound best / sing comfortably (85% of performance). Focus on tessitura, not extreme range
- **Lower extension**: relax, open throat (yawn feeling), more air, chest engagement. Avoid pushing downward (creates fry or strain). Sirens descending gently, sighs on "huh"
- **Higher extension**: lighten, more head voice, smaller vowel modification, breath support (not pressure!). Lip trills ascending, octave slides, "guh/guh/guh" staccato climbs
- **Daily range practice**: 5 min of gentle siren/glide across full range, then specific exercises at edges. Never brute-force high notes — build with proper technique over months
- **Vocal breaks**: everyone has them. Smoothing the break = consistent work on mixed voice, vowel mod, and support through the passaggio

## Ear Training for Singers
- **Pitch matching**: sing a note, check against piano/tuner app. Close eyes, feel the note in your body. Essential skill — most pitch issues are hearing/feedback, not vocal
- **Interval recognition**: practice common intervals (perfect 5th = "Twinkle Twinkle", major 3rd = "When the Saints", minor 2nd = "Jaws"). Sing intervals over drone
- **Solfège**: do-re-mi-fa-so-la-ti-do. Movable do (do = tonic of key) standard for singers. Practice scales, arpeggios, sight-singing melodies
- **Active listening**: listen to singers and analyze: breath points, register shifts, vowel choices, vibrato, dynamics. Transcribe phrases by ear

## Microphone Technique
- **Dynamic vs condenser**: dynamic (SM58) for live — rugged, handles SPL, rejects feedback. Condenser for studio — sensitive, captures detail, needs phantom power
- **Distance**: 2-6 inches typical. Closer = more bass (proximity effect — warmer, intimate). Farther = thinner, more ambient. Adjust distance for dynamics (pull back on loud, move in for soft/personal)
- **Angle**: slightly off-axis (not directly into grille) reduces plosives (P, B, T puffs) and sibilance (S, SH). On-axis = brightest, most present. Off-axis = warmer, less harsh
- **Handling noise**: hold base of mic, not the grille. Avoid rubbing cable. Use shock mount in studio. Wind pop filter for studio recording
- **Live vs studio**: live = project, consistent distance, handle feedback. Studio = subtler dynamics, closer, multiple takes, microphone technique for timbre not just volume

## Vocal Health
- **Hydration**: drink water throughout the day (aim for 2L). Not just before singing — vocal folds need systemic hydration. Room-temperature water during practice. Avoid: dairy (mucus), caffeine/alcohol (dehydrate), spicy foods before performing (acid reflux risk)
- **Rest**: vocal folds are muscles — need recovery after heavy use. 10 min voice rest per hour of singing. If hoarse, rest until speaking feels normal. Sleep 7-9 hrs — vocal folds repair during sleep
- **Avoid strain**: throat clearing (drink water instead), whispering (more strain than quiet speaking), yelling, excessive coughing. If you must clear throat, do gentle "huh huh" breath instead
- **Acid reflux (GERD/LPR)**: silent reflux damages vocal folds. Symptoms: chronic cough, lump in throat, hoarseness mornings. Avoid eating 3 hrs before bed, elevate head, limit trigger foods (tomato, citrus, spicy, chocolate, caffeine, alcohol)
- **Warming down**: gentle hums, lip trills, sirens descending after performance. Helps transition from high-intensity singing to speaking — prevents blood pooling in vocal cords
- **When to see an ENT**: hoarseness lasting >2 weeks, sudden voice loss, chronic pain while singing, blood in phlegm, loss of range after illness. Possible issues: nodules, polyps, Reinke's edema, laryngitis
