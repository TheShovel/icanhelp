# Origami

The Japanese art of paper folding — techniques, bases, and models.

## Paper Types
- **Kami**: Standard origami paper (thin, square, colored one side, white other). Best for beginners. 15cm x 15cm standard size
- **Tant**: Thicker than kami, both sides colored — for geometric models, modular origami
- **Washi**: Traditional handmade Japanese paper — long fibers, thin, strong. Holds creases well, expensive
- **Foil paper**: Paper + aluminum foil — holds shape, good for complex models, doesn't hold crease as sharply
- **Tissue foil**: Tissue paper + foil — can be made at home (tissue pasted onto foil), very thin for complex designs
- **Elephant hide**: Thick, durable — for models handled frequently. Tends to crack if folded sharply
- **Paper size**: Larger paper = easier for beginners. Standard 15cm (6"), miniature 3-5cm for tiny models, 25-35cm for complex designs

## Basic Folds
- **Valley fold**: Fold toward you — crease forms a V shape. Most basic fold
- **Mountain fold**: Fold away from you — crease forms an inverted V. Often dashed-dotted line in diagrams
- **Reverse fold**: Open paper and fold inside between layers — in-and-out fold. Used for legs, heads, beaks
- **Squash fold**: Open a flap and squash flat while aligning with center crease
- **Petal fold**: Lift up, squash sides inward to create elongated point. Found in crane base
- **Pleat fold**: Series of parallel folds (like accordion) — alternating valley and mountain
- **Crimp fold**: Two reverse folds simultaneously — shorten a flap or change direction
- **Sink fold**: Push tip inside model — very advanced fold (open sink, closed sink). Flatten interior point into model

## Major Bases
- **Waterbomb base**: Valley folds in both diagonals, mountain on both axes — collapse into triangle with two flaps. Base for inflatable models (ball, bunny)
- **Preliminary (Square) base**: Waterbomb base inverted — valley on axes, mountain on diagonals. Collapse into square with two flaps. Starting point for crane, iris
- **Bird base**: Preliminary base → petal fold on all four sides + two flaps become wings/body. For crane, flapping bird
- **Frog base**: Bird base → additional squash folds → eventually forms frog. More complex, more folds
- **Fish base**: Specific fold sequence creating two points. Base for fish, whale, some animals
- **Blintz base**: Fold all four corners to center. Base for many modular units, some traditional models
- **Preliminary + blintz** (blintz base on top of preliminary): Common for complex models

## Traditional Models
- **Crane (Orizuru)**: Most iconic origami model — from bird base. Fold wings up, head down, tail up, blow into hole to inflate. Japanese tradition: folding 1000 cranes grants a wish
- **Masu box**: Traditional Japanese wooden box — from waterbomb base. Has lid variation. Perfect for storing small items
- **Lily (Iris)**: From bird base — petals fold down. Classic flower model
- **Jumping frog**: From frog base — push back of head and release to make it jump. Children's favorite
- **Paper balloon (waterbomb)**: Waterbomb base → six-sided pyramid → inflate by blowing through small hole. Can be filled with water (throw at targets)
- **Samurai helmet**: Simple model from half-square paper. Fold top corners, fold up bottom. Wearable

## Modular Origami
- **Sonobe module**: Simple parallelogram unit — connect units to form polyhedra. 6 units = cube, 12 = octahedron, 30 = icosahedron
- **Kusudama**: Decorative balls made from modular units (often glued). Traditional for potpourri, medicine balls. Flower kusudamas are popular
- **Wireframe**: Open-frame polyhedra — edges visible, hollow interior. Takes fewer units than solid models
- **Unit locking**: Modules connect by inserting flaps into pockets (no glue needed for well-designed units). Tension holds structure together

## Tools & Techniques
- **Bone folder**: Bone or Teflon tool — creases sharper than fingers, prevents paper damage
- **Tweezers**: For small, precise folds in complex models (miniature origami)
- **Paper clips, clothespins**: Hold layers temporarily while working on other parts
- **Wet folding**: Dampen paper slightly (with spray bottle or damp cloth) — paper becomes soft, moldable, holds 3D curves instead of sharp creases. For organic models (animals, human figures). Thick paper works best (140lb watercolor)
- **Scoring**: Not traditional — modern technique for very thick paper (elephant hide, cardstock). Score with empty ballpoint pen + ruler on backside, then fold

## Reading Diagrams
- **Standard notation**: Valley fold (dashed line), mountain fold (dash-dot-dash), existing crease (thin solid), hidden fold (gray/green dashed), turn over (curved arrow), rotate (circular arrow), zoom/cutaway (inside messy area)
- **Step-by-step**: Most diagrams use numbered illustrations. Don't skip steps — many creases depend on previous folds
- **YouTube**: Slow-motion tutorials helpful for complex sequences — pause and mimic

## Community & Resources
- **Origami USA**: Largest origami society — publishes convention books, magazine
- **Origami Tanteidan** (Japan): Japanese Origami Academic Society — technical diagrams for complex models
- **Robert Lang**: Pioneer of computational origami — crease pattern design software (TreeMaker), complex insect/bird models
- **Akira Yoshizawa**: Grandmaster — developed wet folding, Yoshizawa-Randlett diagram system. 50,000+ models, published few diagrams
- **Satoshi Kamiya**: Known for extreme complexity — Ancient Dragon (200+ steps), Phoenix, Ryujin
