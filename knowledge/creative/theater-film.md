# Theater, Film & Performance

## Theater History
- **Greek (6th-4th c. BCE)**: origin of Western theater — festivals of Dionysus, plays in amphitheaters (perfect acoustics — Epidaurus seats 14,000, actor's whisper carries to back row). Three great tragedians: Aeschylus (Oresteia — trilogies, introduced second actor), Sophocles (Oedipus Rex, Antigone — 3-actor rules, perfect plot structure), Euripides (Medea, Bacchae — psychological realism, flawed heroes, women+outsiders)

  - Comedian: Aristophanes (Lysistrata, The Frogs — political satire, slapstick, sex jokes. Lysistrata = sex strike to end Peloponnesian War)
  - Elements: chorus (commentary, narration, 12-15 people), masks (exaggerated expressions - helped "type" cast characters across gender + played multiple roles), three-actor rule, deus ex machina ("god from machine" — crane lowered actor playing god to solve plot. Now means contrived ending)
- **Roman**: borrowed from Greek, more spectacle, less philosophical — Seneca (violent tragedies, influenced Renaissance revenge tragedies), Plautus (comedies of mistaken identity, clever slaves — inspired Moliere, Shakespeare, A Funny Thing Happened...). Decline with Rome's fall
- **Medieval**: religious — mystery plays (Bible stories, trade guilds performed. Each guild responsible for scene: carpenters = Noah's Ark, bakers = Last Supper. Various locations: pageant wagons on street, simple staging), morality plays (Everyman — allegorical, protagonist meets abstract figures like Death, Good Deeds, Knowledge — leads to salvation)
- **Renaissance**: Commedia dell'arte (Italy — improvised comedy, stock characters (Harlequin, Pantalone, Colombina), masks). Spanish Golden Age: Lope de Vega (hundreds of plays), Calderón (La Vida es Sueño/Life is a Dream)
  - Shakespeare (England 1564-1616): 38 plays — tragedies (Hamlet, Macbeth, King Lear, Othello, Romeo and Juliet), comedies (Midsummer Night's Dream, Twelfth Night, Much Ado), histories (Henry IV, Richard III), late romances (The Tempest, The Winter's Tale). Globe Theatre — "groundlings" (stand), "heavens" (roof painted). Iambic pentameter (10 syllables/line: da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM), soliloquies (thinking aloud to audience), asides (direct to audience)
  - Marlowe (Dr. Faustus — man sells soul for knowledge).
- **Modern revolution** (late 19th-20th c): Ibsen (A Doll's House — recognized as first modern realism play: ordinary people, social issues, controversial ending). Chekhov (The Cherry Orchard, Three Sisters, Uncle Vanya, The Seagull — subtext, "comedy of desperation", the famous "gun" rule: if you show a gun in act 1 it must fire by act 3 — Chekhov's own rule. Naturalistic acting by Moscow Art Theatre/Stanislavski). Brecht (Epic theater — alienating effect/Verfremdung: break fourth wall, songs, placards, don't let audience sink into emotion — make them THINK, not feel. Threepenny Opera, Mother Courage); Miller (Death of a Salesman — common man tragedy, Expressionist techniques for internal flashbacks). Williams (A Streetcar Named Desire, The Glass Menagerie — poetic realism). Beckett (Waiting for Godot — Theatre of the Absurd, nothing happens "Nothing happens, nobody comes, nobody goes, it's awful" — critic said). Wilson (Fences — African American experience)
- **Contemporary**: musicals (Hamilton, Wicked, Book of Mormon, Dear Evan Hansen, Hadestown, SIX, The Lion King). Immersive theater (Sleep No More — audience walks through space, 3-hour loop. Then She Fell). One-person shows. Devised theater (ensemble-created, no single playwright)

## Film History
- **Pre-film**: zoetrope, magic lantern, Eadweard Muybridge (1878 — galloping horse photos proved all four hooves leave ground at once)
- **Silent era (1895-1927)**: Lumiere brothers (first public film show 1895 Paris — Workers leaving the factory, Arrival of a Train. Short actualities). Méliès (A Trip to the Moon 1902 — first narrative, special effects (stop-motion, double exposure). Famous image: rocket in moon's eye)
  - Griffith (Birth of a Nation 1915 = racist propaganda + innovative technique: close-ups, cross-cutting, tracking shots). Chaplin (The Kid, City Lights, Modern Times, The Great Dictator — physical comedy genius, tramp character, pathos. First actor to appear on Time magazine cover)
- **Classic Hollywood (1927-1960s)**: The Jazz Singer (1927, first talkie — part-silent, part-synced dialogue. Studio system = big studios (MGM, Warner Bros, Paramount, RKO, 20th Century Fox) owned production + distribution + theaters)
  - Golden Age stars: Bogart (Casablanca, Maltese Falcon — hardboiled noir, "Here's looking at you, kid" — line improvised while she was actually cold and shivering?). Hepburn (The Philadelphia Story, Bringing Up Baby, Woman of the Year). Grant (Notorious, North by Northwest, His Girl Friday). Film Noir: Double Indemnity, The Big Sleep, Sunset Boulevard — dark, cynical, femme fatale, expressionist shadows
  - Musicals: Singin' in the Rain, Wizard of Oz. Westerns: Stagecoach, The Searchers. Hitchcock (Psycho, Vertigo, Rear Window, North by Northwest — master of suspense, signature cameo appearances in his films)
- **Post-war & foreign**: Italian Neorealism (Bicycle Thieves 1948 — non-professionals, real locations, post-war poverty). French New Wave (Breathless 1960 Godard — jump cuts, handheld, breaking rules — Jean Seberg + Jean-Paul Belmondo cool. Truffaut's 400 Blows — freeze frame ending). Kurosawa (Seven Samurai, Rashomon — multiple perspectives, influential on Westerns + Star Wars)
  - Fellini (La Dolce Vita, 8½ — surreal). Bergman (The Seventh Seal — chess with Death, Wild Strawberries). Kubrick (2001: A Space Odyssey, A Clockwork Orange, The Shining, Dr. Strangelove — meticulous, each shot painterly. 2001: most realistic (for its time) space film ever made)
- **New Hollywood (1967-1980s)**: Bonnie and Clyde (1967 — graphic violence, antihero). The Godfather (1972 — changed filmmaking, studio respect, masterpiece). Jaws (1975 — first summer blockbuster). Star Wars (1977 — franchise, special effects revolution). Apocalypse Now (1979 — madness of Vietnam, heart of darkness). Taxi Driver (1976), Raging Bull (1980)

  - American New Wave: Easy Rider, Five Easy Pieces, The Graduate, McCabe & Mrs. Miller — directors as auteurs, smaller films, personal vision. Scorsese, Coppola, Altman, Ashby, Bogdanovich
- **Modern (1980s-present)**: blockbusters (Steven Spielberg: ET, Jurassic Park, Schindler's List, Saving Private Ryan. Indiana Jones, Star Wars original trilogy). Independent film rise (Tarantino: Reservoir Dogs, Pulp Fiction, Kill Bill — non-linear, pop culture dialogue, homage. Coen Brothers: Fargo, Big Lebowski, No Country for Old Men). Digital age: Avatar (2009 — 3D, motion capture), Marvel Cinematic Universe (2008-present — franchise model, interconnected. Has changed cinema: more IP-based films). Streaming era (Netflix originals: Roma, Irishman, Marriage Story — changed distribution, film release & theater windows. COVID 2020 accelerated this)

## Acting Techniques
- **Stanislavski system**: "the method" — emotional memory (recall own emotion for role), given circumstances (specifics of the world), magic "if" (what would I do IF I were this character?), objectives (what does character want IN each scene). Units + beats (each scene section = one character objective). "Super-objective": what character wants throughout entire play
- **Meisner**: repetition exercise (two actors repeat phrase, focusing on partner's behavior, picking up changes). "Acting is living truthfully under imaginary circumstances." Listening + responding honestly > imposing intended result. Don't plan performance — be spontaneous based on partner
- **Method acting** (Strasberg): affective memory (recalling personal emotions — deeper than Stanislavski's emotional memory), sense memory (recreating physical sensations — weight, temperature, texture). Danger: can be psychologically intense, not for everyone, can cause harm. Better: Stanislavski/Meisner
- **Practical Aesthetics** (David Mamet, William H. Macy, playwright+actor): analyze scene in four steps: literal (what happens), want (what does character want from other), action (what VERB is character doing to get it — "to beg" vs "to threaten"?), as-if (how is it like my life?). Simple, practical, no emotional recall

## Stagecraft Terms
- **Blocking**: movement on stage (blocking notation: stage left/right, upstage/downstage, center stage. "Upstage" = toward back of stage — upstage used because raked stage slopes down toward audience ("downstage"), so upstage literally higher). "Stage right" = actor's right when facing audience
- **Fourth wall**: imaginary wall between stage and audience — actors pretend audience isn't there. Breaking fourth wall = acknowledging audience (Brecht, Shakespeare, modern comedies, House of Cards)
- **Tech theater**: lighting (spots, floods, gobos, color gels, LED), sound (body mics, speakers, sound effects), set (scenery, props), costumes. Fly system: ropes + pulleys above stage to raise/lower scenery
- **Stage manager**: most important person in theater — runs rehearsals, calls cues (sound, light, curtain), maintains script, keeps show consistent. Stage manager is the one calling "places" and show start
