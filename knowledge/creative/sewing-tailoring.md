# Sewing & Tailoring (Advanced)

## Stitches & Seams

### Hand Stitches
- **Running stitch**: basic in-and-out, even spacing (1/8-1/4"). For gathering, basting (temporary), mending. The foundation stitch
- **Backstitch**: strong, looks like machine stitching (forward 1/4", back 1/8", repeat). For seams, repairs, areas of strain. Hand-sewn equivalent of straight machine stitch
- **Slip stitch/ ladder stitch**: nearly invisible. Pick up thread from fold, then from other side, alternate. For hemming, closing pillow openings, finishing linings. The "invisible stitch" — important skill for tailored garments
- **Whip stitch**: overcast edge, diagonal. For seam finishing, appliqué, edge joining. Close spacing = neat: 1/16-1/8" apart for fine fabrics
- **Blind hem**: pick up one thread from garment, then fold hem edge. Alternative to slip stitch for heavier fabrics. Almost invisible on right side
- **Buttonhole stitch**: closely spaced stitches around cut edge. For hand-worked buttonholes on fine garments. Place knot at the buttonhole's end to protect the edge
- **Catch stitch/ herringbone**: criss-cross stitch. For hemming knits (stretches with fabric). Use for garment sewing where more stretch is needed than a whip stitch allows

### Machine Stitches
- **Straight stitch**: basic, adjustable length (2.5mm standard). Use for: seams, topstitching, edge stitching. Basting stitch: 4-5mm length for temporary
- **Zigzag**: width + length variable. Use for: seam finishing (overcast edge), buttonholes, stretch fabrics, appliqué, satin stitching. Edge seam: width 3, length to width ratio near 1:1
- **Stretch stitch**: narrow zigzag with some stretch. For knits, jersey. Many machines have a lightning bolt or stretch specific stitch
- **Blind hem stitch**: for hemming with nearly invisible stitch. Roll fabric so hem fold and garment show alternating zigzag. Stitch catches 1-2 threads of the garment on each pass. Best for heavier fabrics

### Seam Finishes
- **French seam**: enclosed, tidy — good for sheer fabrics, blouses. Sew seam wrong sides together at 3/8", trim, press. Fold right sides together, sew again at 1/4" enclosing raw edges. Clean finish — no serger needed. For delicate blouses, fine cotton, linen
- **Flat-felled seam**: strong, durable — jeans, shirts. Sew seam, press to one side, trim one seam allowance, fold other over, topstitch. You see two rows of stitching on right side
- **Overlock/serged**: fastest, professional. Clean-finished edge, stretchy. Requires serger (cut + sew simultaneously). Only one needle? Typically 2-5 threads, 3-4 most common for garment sewing. Serger blades trim while stitching. Ideal for knits
- **Zigzag edge**: simple, okay for stable fabrics not under stress. Width 3-5, stitch on edge so threads overhang slightly. For home sewing where finished appearance not critical

## Pattern Adjustment (Fitting)

### Common Adjustments
- **Full bust adjustment (FBA)**: for larger cup sizes (C cup+). Slash and spread pattern at bust point to add width + length. Add to side front piece. For woven garments: must add both width and length (needs darts to shape to body). Knits: often just need width
  - Standard pattern draft size: B cup. For each cup size above: spread 1/2" at bust = 1" total added per cup. Need to add width to both front pattern pieces (half to side, half to center). Swayback adjustment
- **Swayback adjustment**: for lower back curve (excess fabric pooling at small of back). Pinch out horizontal wedge from center back at waist level (take 1/2-1" from back length). Transfer to pattern by cutting across back waist and overlapping the dart
- **Shoulder slope**: too-square shoulders = wrinkles from shoulder to bust. Pinch out diagonal from neck to mid-shoulder. Remove from pattern at neckline / armhole intersection. Forward shoulder: needed for rounded shoulders — add to pattern at shoulder back, remove at front
- **Lengthen/shorten**: at designated adjustment lines on pattern (not just hem). For arm length: above elbow average vs below. For inseam: at crotch, along leg depending on short/long body. Add/subtract proportional amount both above and below any adjustment lines to keep balance
- **Grading between sizes**: if bust = size 12, waist = 10, hip = 14, blend between sizes. Draw smooth new cutting line. For bodice: grade across side seams. For pants: blend at side + inseam

### Taking Measurements
- **Bust**: fullest part, tape parallel to floor, not too tight (over bra). High bust: above bust, under armpits. Difference between high bust + full bust = cup size (2" = B, 3" = C, 4" = D, 5" = DD). Use high bust for pattern size, then do FBA if needed
- **Waist**: natural waist (narrowest point, below ribcage). Don't suck in. Typically above belly button
- **Hip**: fullest part (7-9" below waist, depends). Also hip depth = waist to hip length
- **Back length**: from prominent bone at base of neck to waist. Shoulder to shoulder: across back, not too tight
- **Inseam**: crotch to floor (barefoot). Also outseam = waist to floor (side)

## Fabric Guide

### Fabric Types
- **Woven**: non-stretch (unless bias cut). Cotton (broadcloth, poplin, chambray — easy, beginners). Linen (wrinkles, crisp, cool, challenging for construction because slips). Denim (heavy, durable). Silk (slippery, fray-prone). Wool (various weights — worsted for suiting, flannel for shirts). Challis (rayon: drapey, soft, fray-prone, stretch in one direction). Taffeta (stiff, shiny, holds shape)
- **Knit**: stretchy. Jersey (T-shirt fabric, 2-way stretch or 4-way). Interlock (stable, doesn't curl). Rib knit (cuffs, neckbands). Sweater knit (bulky). Spandex/lycra (high stretch, for activewear). How to work: ballpoint/ jersey needle, reduced tension, twin needle for hems
- **Specialty**: denim (heavy needle, topstitching thread). Corduroy (nap must be same direction, avoid pressing too hard — collapses pile). Velvet (crush-prone, sew in direction of nap, use microfibers walking foot or roller foot). Satin (slippery, use fine pins + tissue paper under presser foot). Lace (delicate, seam finishes hidden)
- **Lining**: Bemberg rayon (most used for garments — breathable, smooth, static-free). Polyester lining (cheap, less breathable, can be hot). Silk (luxury lining, expensive). For jackets, skirts, dresses

## Tailoring

### Suit Jacket
- **Canvas** (chest piece): between outer fabric + lining — gives shape, structure. Fused (glued on = cheap + stiff, bubbles over time) vs sew-in (floating canvas = traditional, molds to body, breathes, expensive). Lapels: notch (standard), peak (formal), shawl (tuxedo)
  - Pockets: flap (standard), besom (slot), patch (casual). Working buttonholes (cuff buttons that actually unbutton, a sign of quality tailoring). Sleeve head: roll of padding at top of sleeve for smooth shoulder line
- **Trousers**: waistband (curved to match body shape, interfaced). Pleats (flat front more modern, pleats for classic/drape). Cuff (1-1.5" hem, adds weight for drape, optional)
- **Hem types**: blind hem (invisible). Topstitched (visible, casual). Fused hem (hem tape — iron-on, quick, doesn't lie as flat in many cases). Deep hem (1.5-2", heavy fabrics so they hang well)

## Mending & Alterations

### Common Repairs
- **Hole in knit**: duplicate stitch (Swiss darning) — mimic knit stitch with matching yarn. Weave needle in and out following existing stitches. Trim or secure loose ends with thread. For small holes
- **Torn seam**: machine stitch over existing holes, matching thread. Reinforce beginning + end with backstitch. For larger repairs: patch on inside
- **Patch**: iron-on (quick, fuses, may come off in wash). Sew-in (better, but raw edges must be finished). Denim: use denim patch + heavy thread + zigzag
- **Button replacement**: shank (for thicker fabric — yarn/thread stem under button for buttonhole space) vs sew-through (flat against fabric). Use thread doubled, 6-8 passes through each hole, wind thread around shank area 3-4 times to create stand. Use button gauge (toothpick on top) for shank spacing
- **Zipper replacement**: seam ripper to remove old. New zipper: baste in place first (hand stitch), then machine (zipper foot, 5/8" seam for most dress zippers, 1/2" for pants). Invisible zipper: special foot, press coils flat before sewing. Lapped zipper: common on pants, side of zipper hidden behind one side. Exposed zipper: trendy, both sides visible
- **Hemming**: turn up desired amount (1-2" for pants, 2-3" for skirts, 5" for formal dresses). Press fold, baste, try on, adjust. Finish edge (overlock, zigzag, or hem tape). Hand stitch blind hem or machine stitch. Drop hem: stitch deeper to allow letting down for growing children?

## Tools & Equipment
- **Sewing machine**: computerized (many stitches, can maintain consistent speed, better for beginners) vs mechanical (simpler, less to go wrong, cheaper). Key features: adjustable stitch length + width, reverse, free arm (sleeve/ pant hemming). Brands: Brother (beginner-friendly), Janome (quality mid-range), Bernina (luxury, high precision, professional). Viking, Singer vary widely
  - Feet: zipper foot, buttonhole foot, walking foot (for knits, quilting — feeds top layer evenly, prevents stretching), blind hem foot, 1/4" piecing foot. A walking foot is essential for attaching knits without stretching and keeping two layers of any fabric feeding together
- **Serger/overlocker**: cuts + sews + finishes edge. For knits, garment construction, fast seam finishing. 4-thread for durability, 3-thread for rolled hem. Differential feed: for knits (prevents wavy seams on stretch). Juki, Brother, Babylock, Janome
- **Pressing tools**: iron (steam, hot), pressing ham (curved surfaces), sleeve roll (narrow tubes), seam roll (thin), clapper (wool — holds heat, pounds seams flat), tailors ham for shaping curved garments. Pressing is essential — don't skip (press each seam open as you sew: press, not iron — lift and set, don't slide). Use spray starch for crisp finishing, but don't spray on wool
- **Measuring**: tape (60" soft, sturdy). Seam gauge (6" metal ruler with slider). Clear gridded ruler (6x24 for pattern drafting). French curve (curve drawing, armhole fitting)
