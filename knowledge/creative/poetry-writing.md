# Poetry Writing

Forms, techniques, and guidance for writing poetry.

## Poetic Forms
- **Sonnet**: 14 lines, iambic pentameter. Types: Shakespearean (3 quatrains + couplet, rhyme ABAB CDCD EFEF GG — turn/twist in final couplet). Petrarchan/Italian (octave + sestet, rhyme ABBAABBA CDECDE — volta after octave). Spenserian (ABAB BCBC CDCD EE). Modern sonnets can be loose with meter/rhyme
- **Haiku**: Japanese form — 3 lines, 5-7-5 syllables. Focus on nature, a moment of perception, juxtaposition of two images with cutting word (kireji). Seasonal reference (kigo). Modern haiku in English often looser — essence is concision and present-moment awareness
- **Villanelle**: 19 lines: 5 tercets + 1 quatrain. Two refrains alternate: A1 (line 1) + A2 (line 3) → A2 A1 (quatrain end). Highly structured, obsessive repetition. Famous: Dylan Thomas "Do Not Go Gentle Into That Good Night", Bishop "One Art"
- **Free verse**: No fixed meter or rhyme scheme. Most common in contemporary poetry. Relies on: line breaks, rhythm (natural speech patterns), imagery, sound devices. Not "anything goes" — every line break should be intentional
- **Blank verse**: Unrhymed iambic pentameter. Closest to natural English speech rhythm. Used by Shakespeare (his plays), Milton (Paradise Lost), Wordsworth (The Prelude), Frost (many poems)
- **Sestina**: 6 stanzas of 6 lines each + 3-line envoi. Same 6 end-words repeated in specific rotating pattern. Extremely challenging. Word pattern: 123456 → 615243 → 364125 → 532614 → 451362 → 246531. Envoi: all 6 words, 3 per line
- **Limerick**: 5 lines, AABBA rhyme. Anapestic meter (da-da-DUM da-da-DUM). Humorous, often bawdy. "There once was a man from Nantucket..."

## Poetic Devices
- **Metaphor**: Direct comparison without "like/as" — "Hope is the thing with feathers" (Dickinson). Extended metaphor = conceit (develops throughout poem). Implied metaphor: "I've barked commands at my life" (dog training metaphor)
- **Simile**: Comparison using "like" or "as" — "My love is like a red, red rose" (Burns). More explicit than metaphor, less assertive
- **Personification**: Giving human qualities to non-human — "The wind stood up and gave a shout" (Thomas). "Because I could not stop for Death, He kindly stopped for me" (Dickinson)
- **Imagery**: Sensory language (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell). Concrete and specific — not "flower" but "marigold with its acrid perfume, orange as a traffic cone". Imagery > abstraction — show don't tell
- **Alliteration**: Repeated initial consonant sounds. "Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers." Creates rhythm, emphasis, musicality
- **Assonance**: Repeated vowel sounds. "The rain in Spain falls mainly on the plain." Creates internal rhyme, mood
- **Consonance**: Repeated consonant sounds (not just initial). "The black sack cracks in the back." Gives texture
- **Onomatopoeia**: Words that sound like what they mean — buzz, hiss, crack, whisper, murmur. Creates sonic immediacy
- **Enjambment**: Line breaks mid-sentence — carries meaning across line break. Creates tension, surprise, multiple meanings. "I carried his body / of work home" — "body" first seems to mean corpse, then modified by "of work"
- **Caesura**: Pause within line — by punctuation or natural breath. Creates emphasis, rhythm variation. "To err is human, — to forgive, divine"

## Sound & Rhythm
- **Meter**: Pattern of stressed/unstressed syllables. Iamb (unSTRESSED stressed — da-DUM) — most natural English rhythm. Trochee (STRESSED un — DUM-da). Anapest (da-da-DUM). Dactyl (DUM-da-da). Spondee (STRESSED STRESSED). Pyrrhic (un un)
- **Iambic pentameter**: 5 iambs per line (10 syllables). "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?" Natural speech rhythm. Variations (inversion, extra syllable) keep it from being mechanical
- **Rhyme**: Perfect (night/light — identical ending sounds). Slant/near (worm/storm — similar but not identical). Eye (love/move — look same, sound different). Internal rhyme (within line). End rhyme (line ends). Masculine (one stressed syllable: home/alone). Feminine (two syllables: dying/flying)
- **Rhythm in free verse**: Created through: line length variation, syntactic parallelism, repetition, word choice for duration (long vowels slow, short vowels quick). No meter but not without rhythm

## Writing Process
- **Observation**: Poetry comes from attention — notice details others miss. Keep notebook (or phone notes) for images, phrases, overheard conversations, sensory experiences. "What did you see today that surprised you?"
- **Starting points**: Interesting overheard phrase, striking image, strong emotion, question, word you love, prompt from another poem. Write first without worrying about form — shape later
- **Revision**: Most of poetry is revision. Steps: (1) Write without editing. (2) Read aloud — mark awkward places. (3) Cut clichés, adjectives, abstractions. (4) Check every word — is it the right word? (5) Examine line breaks — do they serve meaning? (6) Examine sound — does it sing? (7) Let it sit for days/weeks. (8) Revise again
- **Kill your darlings**: Cut favorite lines that don't serve the poem. If it can stand alone as great line, it might belong in different poem. A poem is a system: every part serves the whole
- **Workshop**: Read poems aloud to trusted readers. Ask: "What do you hear happening here?" (not "What do you think?"). Receive feedback without defense. You don't have to accept every suggestion but listen for patterns

## Reading Poetry
- **Read widely**: Old and new, across cultures. Classic: Dickinson, Frost, Yeats, Hopkins, Bishop, Stevens, Auden. Contemporary: Ocean Vuong, Ada Limón, Ross Gay, Terrance Hayes, Mary Oliver, Claudia Rankine
- **Read aloud**: Poems are sonic experiences. Mouth and ear catch things eye misses. Read other poets aloud to internalize rhythm
- **Annotation**: Mark words you love, images, surprises, questions. Ask: "Where does the poem turn?" "What's the tension?" "What's left unsaid?" Write in margins — engage actively
- **Memorization**: Memorize poems you love. Internal rhythm and language become part of your writing voice. Recite while walking, driving, showering

## Publishing
- **Literary magazines**: Hundreds publish poetry — The New Yorker, Poetry Magazine, Ploughshares, Rattle, Beloit Poetry Journal, many online at Submittable. Read the magazine first (submit to places you actually read)
- **Submission etiquette**: Submit 3-5 poems, include cover letter (brief: name, previous pubs if relevant, thank you). Simultaneous submissions OK (notify if accepted elsewhere). Rejection = normal part of process (famous poets were rejected hundreds of times). Respond to acceptance with professionalism
- **Chapbook**: Small collection (20-30 pages). Usually focused on one theme or project. Many contests and small presses publish them. Self-publishing viable with ISBN and distribution via Amazon KDP
