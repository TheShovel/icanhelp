# Home Brewing (Beer)

## Equipment
- **Essential**: brew kettle (5+ gallon, stainless steel or enamel), fermenter (food-grade bucket with lid/airlock or glass carboy 5-6.5 gallon), airlock (fermentation CO₂ escape without letting air in), siphon + tubing (racking cane, auto-siphon recommended), bottles + capper (crown caps + capper tool, or swing-top/grolsch bottles. Sanitizer (Star San = no-rinse acid sanitizer, PBW = cleaner. Two different steps: Clean THEN Sanitize. Starsan = contact time 1-2 min, foam OK, 2 oz makes 5 gallons). Thermometer, hydrometer (measure specific gravity — OG original, FG final = ABV calculation)
- **Nice**: wort chiller (immersion copper — rapid cooling = clearer beer, less off-flavors. 25 ft for 5 gal batch). Auto-siphon (sanitary transfer). Bottle tree + vinator (sanitize bottles fast). Temperature control (fermentation temp matters: too high = fusel alcohols/off flavors, too low = stuck fermentation. Fridge + inkbird controller for precise control, ~$60)
- **Sanitization is EVERYTHING**: anything that touches cooled wort/beer must be sanitized (not just clean). Starsan = foam on contact for 30 sec-1 min. Don't fear the foam (Starsan foam harmless, beneficial — don't rinse! Rinsing re-contaminates)

## Ingredients
- **Water**: filtered/spring > tap (chlorine/chloramine = off-flavors — campden tablet 1/4 tab per 5 gal removes chlorine compounds instantly, treat before adding grain/extract). Mineral content affects beer style
  - Water profile: adjust minerals for style (Burton on Trent = hard water = IPAs light, Pilsen = soft = delicate Pilsners). For beginners: start with spring or filtered water
- **Malt**: base malt (2-row, pilsner, pale ale — ~80% of grain bill). Specialty (crystal/caramel = sweetness/color, roasted = dark/stout/porter, Munich/Vienna = malty/bready body). Extract (LME = liquid malt extract, DME = dry malt extract)
  - All-grain vs extract: all-grain = more control, better quality, cheaper per batch, more equipment + time. Extract = simpler, faster, good first batches. Partial mash = hybrid
- **Hops**: bittering (high alpha acids, boil 60 min — Warrior, Magnum, Nugget. Provide bitterness — bittering isomerization (alpha acids converted to iso-alpha acids in boil). Flavor (15-30 min — Cascade, Centennial, Willamette). Aroma (0-15 min, flameout/whirlpool, dry hop after fermentation — Mosaic, Citra, Simcoe, Galaxy, El Dorado, Nelson Sauvin)
  - AAU/IBU: bitterness units. 0 IBU (no hops), ~100 IBU (very bitter IPA). Bitterness is IBU × alphas, but use brewing calculator
  - Hops should be stored: vacuum sealed, freezer (oxygen + heat degrade quickly). Use within 6 months for quality, 1-2 years for bitterness (still OK for bittering)
- **Yeast**: ale (top-fermenting, warmer 60-75°F, faster, fruity esters — many IPA/pale ale). Lager (bottom-fermenting, colder 45-55°F, slower, cleaner, crisper, need lagering time)
  - Liquid yeast (White Labs, Wyeast — more variety, fresher, needs starter for high gravity). Dry yeast (Fermentis/Saflager — cheaper, more stable, pitch more packets for high gravity. US-05 = clean American ale, S-04 = English ale, K-97 = German ale/Kolsch. SafLager W-34/70 = excellent lager yeast, forgiving at ale temps). Always pitch enough yeast: underpitching = stress = off-flavors (stall, incomplete fermentation, diacetyl, fusel alcohols)

## Process
1. **Brew day**: steep grains (extract: specialty grains bag, 150-170°F, 20-30 min), bring to boil, add extract + bittering hops (60 min), flavor hops (15 min), aroma hops (last minute/flameout), cool quickly (wort chiller or ice bath — cool to 70°F within 20-30 min, rapid cooling = clean beer, less DMS/skunk)
   - All-grain: mash + sparge (mash at specific temp for 60 min: 148°F = thinner/drier beer, 154°F = fuller/ more body). Sparge = rinse grains to extract sugars
2. **Fermentation**: transfer to sanitized fermenter, aerate (shake/splash/oxygenate vigorously — yeast needs oxygen for growth. Wort out of boil has no dissolved oxygen). Pitch yeast at proper temp, add airlock, ferment at temp appropriate for yeast strain (stable temp, use swamp cooler or fridge)
   - Primary fermentation: 5-14 days (bubbles slow, krausen drops). Rack to secondary = optional (helps clarity, reduces trub contact = cleaner beer for long aging or dry hopping). For most beers: 2-3 weeks primary is fine, skip secondary
3. **Bottling**: clean + sanitize bottles, prepare priming sugar (corn sugar/dextrose boiled in water — 4.5-5 oz per 5 gal for ~2.5 vol CO₂ for most styles. Table sugar works same: 5 oz. Less for bottling bucket — dissolve and rack beer onto it). Siphon beer into bottling bucket with priming solution, fill bottles (wand fills from bottom — leave 1" headspace for future homebrew carbonation level), cap
   - Alternative to bottling: Kegging (Corny keg, CO₂ tank, regulator, kegerator/dispensing system. Advantages: can force carbonate in 24 hours instead of 2 weeks, no bottle cleaning, more control, easier serving)
4. **Condition**: let bottles carbonate at room temp, 2 weeks minimum (3-4 weeks better, lagers longer). Chill, drink, enjoy!

## Common Off-Flavors
- **Green apple/butterscotch** (acetaldehyde): young/under-ripe — age beer, pitch more yeast next time, ferment at correct temp. Diacetyl: buttery/butterscotch = diacetyl not cleaned up by yeast — warm up beer after fermentation ends (diacetyl rest) for 2-3 days
- **Band-aid/medicinal** (chlorophenols): chlorinated water — use campden tablet (remove chloramine/chlorine) or filtered water
- **Paper/cardboard/stale** (oxidation): oxygen exposure after fermentation — minimize splashing siphoning, fill bottles to proper headspace, reduce headspace in carboy. #1 homebrew complaint
- **Skunk/cat pee** (light-struck): UV light — use brown bottles, store in dark. Clear/green bottles skunk in MINUTES in sunlight
- **Banana/clove** (esters/phenols): usually yeast character (Hefeweizen = desirable, German wheat), but could be too warm fermentation — ferment cooler for cleaner beer
- **Solvent/fusel alcohol/lacquer thinner**: too high fermentation temp — ferment at correct temp range, pitch enough yeast to avoid stressed fermentation. Common with underpitched yeast + high temp
- **Sulfur/rubber**: yeast stress or certain strains (lager yeast = some sulfur normal, ages out). Clean, good sanitation, proper pitch rate
- **Mold/caramel/cider/acetic/sour/vinegar**: infected — dump batch, clean everything, sanitize better. Replace plastic components (scratched plastic harbors bacteria). Glass/stain steel easier to sanitize

## Beer Styles (Common for Homebrew)
- **American Pale Ale**: moderate 30-50 IBU, citrus/pine hops (Cascade, Centennial), clean US yeast (US-05), OG 1.045-1.060
- **IPA (India Pale Ale)**: high 40-70+ IBU, heavy hops (Citra, Mosaic, Simcoe, Amarillo, Galaxy, Nelson Sauvin). West Coast = clear, bitter, dry, piney. New England (NEIPA) = hazy (unfiltered + biotransformation = look like orange juice), low bitter, heavy dry hop, juicy, full-bodied
- **Stout/porter**: dark, roasted malt, chocolate/coffee flavors, 30-50 IBU. Dry Irish Stout (Guinness-style, low ABV ~4.2%), Imperial Stout (high ABV 8-12%)
- **Wheat beer**: Hefeweizen (German, banana/clove), Witbier (Belgian, orange/coriander — Blue Moon-style). American wheat: clean, light, easy-drinking. OG 1.040-1.055
- **Lager**: Pilsner (crisp, hoppy, Czech/German), Helles (German pale, malty), Marzen/Oktoberfest (rich, malty), Bock/Doppelbock (strong, malty). Clean, crisp, more difficult (need temp control)
