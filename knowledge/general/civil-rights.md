# Civil Rights & Law

## Constitutional Rights — Key Amendments
- **1st Amendment**: freedom of religion (establishment + free exercise), speech, press, assembly, right to petition government. Not absolute — restrictions allowed for: incitement to violence (Brandenburg), defamation, obscenity (Miller test), true threats, fighting words, time/place/manner restrictions. Speech on government property (public forums) gets highest protection; non-public forums less
- **2nd Amendment**: right to keep and bear arms. District of Columbia v. Heller (2008): individual right for self-defense in home. McDonald v. Chicago (2010): applies to states via 14th Amendment. Regulations allowed: background checks, mental health restrictions, felons, concealed carry permits, assault weapon bans (upheld in some circuits). NYSRPA v. Bruen (2022): must be "consistent with historical tradition" — stricter scrutiny applied
- **4th Amendment**: protection from unreasonable searches/seizures. Warrants require probable cause, particular description (place + items), oath/affirmation. Exceptions: plain view, consent, search incident to arrest, exigent circumstances, automobile exception, stop-and-frisk (Terry stop — reasonable suspicion, not PC). Exclusionary rule: illegally obtained evidence inadmissible (Mapp v. Ohio 1961). No warrant needed for: border searches, school searches (students), parole/probation searches, abandoned property
- **5th Amendment**: grand jury indictment (federal), double jeopardy (can't be tried twice for same offense), self-incrimination (right to remain silent — Miranda v. Arizona 1966), due process (federal), eminent domain (just compensation required). Miranda rights: police must inform of right to silence and attorney before custodial interrogation. Invocation must be unambiguous ("I want a lawyer" vs "Maybe I should get a lawyer"). Failure to Mirandize → statements suppressed, but not automatically case dismissed
- **6th Amendment**: speedy trial, public trial, impartial jury, notice of charges, right to confront witnesses (cross-examination), compulsory process (subpoena witnesses), right to counsel (Gideon v. Wainwright 1963 — states must provide attorney if can't afford one). Ineffective assistance of counsel → violation (Strickland v. Washington 1984: performance deficient + prejudiced outcome)
- **14th Amendment**: Citizenship Clause (born in US = citizen). Equal Protection Clause (no state may deny equal protection — applies to governments, not private actors). Due Process Clause (applies bill of rights to states via incorporation). Key for: civil rights, LGBTQ+ rights (Obergefell v. Hodges 2015 — same-sex marriage), affirmative action, voting rights. Substantive due process: protects rights not explicitly listed (privacy, marriage, contraception, abortion — Dobbs v. Jackson 2022 overruled Roe, returning abortion to states)

## Equal Protection
- **Standards of review**: (1) Strict scrutiny (fundamental rights, suspect classes — race, national origin, alienage). Government must show: compelling interest + narrowly tailored + least restrictive means. Usually fatal. (2) Intermediate scrutiny (quasi-suspect — gender, legitimacy). Important government interest + substantially related. (3) Rational basis (everything else — age, wealth, disability). Legitimate government interest + reasonably related. Almost always upheld. Bostock v. Clayton County (2020): Title VII's "sex" discrimination includes sexual orientation and gender identity

## Due Process
- **Procedural due process**: fair procedures before government deprives life, liberty, or property. Elements: notice, opportunity to be heard, neutral decision-maker. What process is due depends on: nature of right, risk of error, government interest (Mathews v. Eldridge test). Applies to: hearings before welfare termination, student suspensions, parole revocations
- **Substantive due process**: certain fundamental rights protected from government interference even with fair procedures. Historical examples: marriage (Loving v. Virginia), procreation (Skinner v. Oklahoma), contraception (Griswold v. Connecticut), private sexual acts (Lawrence v. Texas). Dobbs (2022) rejected substantive due process for abortion — now determined by states

## Discrimination Law
- **Employment**: Title VII of Civil Rights Act (1964): prohibits discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex (includes pregnancy, sexual orientation, gender identity), national origin. Applies to employers with 15+ employees. Also: ADEA (age 40+, 20+ employees), ADA (disability, 15+ employees), Equal Pay Act (gender wage gap). File claim with EEOC (180 days federal, 300 in state agencies). Remedies: back pay, reinstatement, damages, attorney fees
- **Housing**: Fair Housing Act (1968): prohibits discrimination in sale, rental, or financing based on race, color, religion, sex, familial status, national origin, disability. Applies to most housing (exemptions: owner-occupied with ≤4 units, religious orgs limiting to members, private clubs). File with HUD. Testing (paired testers) is common enforcement method
- **Public accommodations**: Title II of Civil Rights Act: no discrimination in places of public accommodation (hotels, restaurants, theaters, etc). Covered: race, color, religion, national origin. NOT explicitly sex (but some states add). Applies to private businesses serving the public
- **Disability**: ADA (1990) — prohibits discrimination in employment (qualified individual with a disability, reasonable accommodation unless undue hardship), public services (state/local government), public accommodations (businesses open to public), telecommunications. "Disability" = physical/mental impairment substantially limiting major life activity OR record of OR regarded as. Reasonable accommodation: modified schedule, assistive tech, physical access changes. Undue hardship: significant difficulty/expense (case-by-case)

## Police Interactions / Rights
- **Traffic stop**: you must stop, provide license/registration/insurance if asked. You have right to remain silent ("I invoke my right to remain silent"). Don't consent to search ("I do not consent to a search"). If officers search anyway, don't physically resist — repeat "I do not consent" and fight in court. In most states, you must give name if ID'd as suspect (HI stop laws vary)
- **Arrest**: you have right to lawyer (Ask: "I want my lawyer"). Right to remain silent (don't answer questions after arrest). You'll be read Miranda rights (when in custody + interrogation). If not Mirandized, your statements may be inadmissible, but charges can proceed. You have right to phone call (within reasonable time). Do NOT lie to police (can be charged separately)
- **On street**: police need reasonable suspicion to stop/frisk (Terry stop). Mere refusal to answer questions is NOT reasonable suspicion. You can ask "Am I free to leave?" If yes → walk away. If no → you're detained. Don't run. Don't physically resist
- **Photography/video**: you have First Amendment right to record police in public (inside buildings sometimes restricted). Officers may not delete photos/video. If told to step back for "scene security," comply but continue recording. Many states prohibit interfering with law enforcement — know your state law
- **Immigration status**: generally don't need to answer questions about citizenship/immigration status. Non-citizens: have right to remain silent and talk to lawyer/consulate. Police can't detain solely for immigration status in most states (some "show me your papers" laws require it at stops)
- **Non-citizens**: carry immigration documents if required. Do NOT sign anything without lawyer. Most non-citizens have right to contact consulate

## Voting Rights Act
- **Voting Rights Act of 1965**: Section 2 bans racial discrimination in voting nationwide (any practice that results in dilution/denial). Section 4(b) originally defined covered jurisdictions. Section 5 required preclearance (federal approval before changing voting laws) for jurisdictions with history of discrimination. Shelby County v. Holder (2013) struck down Section 4(b) formula → Section 5 effectively dead. Congress hasn't passed new formula
- **Impact after Shelby**: states previously covered (TX, NC, AL, GA, etc) passed voter ID laws, purged voter rolls, closed polling places, changed districts. Courts struck some down as intentionally discriminatory. VRA still protects against intentional discrimination (Section 2): plaintiffs must show discriminatory effect + causal link — harder than preclearance
- **Other voting rights**: 15th Amendment (race), 19th (sex), 24th (no poll tax), 26th (18+). National Voter Registration Act (motor voter). Help America Vote Act (voting machines, provisional ballots). Voting accessibility for elderly/disabled (AVAA 1984)
- **Current issues**: gerrymandering (partisan — Rucho v. Common Cause 2019 says federal courts can't hear partisan gerrymandering claims, but racial gerrymandering still illegal), voter purges, polling place closures, mail voting restrictions, felony disenfranchisement (2/3 of states allow voting after sentence, some require completion of probation/parole)

## Landmark Supreme Court Cases
- **Marbury v. Madison (1803)**: established judicial review — courts can strike down laws violating Constitution
- **Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857)**: African Americans not citizens. Overruled by 14th Amendment
- **Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)**: "separate but equal" constitutional. Overturned by Brown v. Board
- **Brown v. Board of Education (1954)**: school segregation unconstitutional. Overturned Plessy
- **Mapp v. Ohio (1961)**: exclusionary rule applies to states (illegally obtained evidence)
- **Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)**: right to counsel for criminal defendants in state courts
- **Miranda v. Arizona (1966)**: rights warning required before custodial interrogation
- **Loving v. Virginia (1967)**: interracial marriage bans unconstitutional
- **Roe v. Wade (1973)**: constitutional right to abortion. Overruled by Dobbs (2022)
- **Bush v. Gore (2000)**: halted Florida recount, effectively decided presidential election
- **District of Columbia v. Heller (2008)**: individual 2A right to gun ownership for self-defense
- **Citizens United v. FEC (2010)**: corporate/union political spending = speech, unlimited independent expenditures
- **Obergefell v. Hodges (2015)**: same-sex marriage constitutional right nationwide
- **Shelby County v. Holder (2013)**: struck down VRA preclearance formula
- **Bostock v. Clayton County (2020)**: Title VII prohibits LGBTQ employment discrimination
- **Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health (2022)**: abortion not constitutional right, returned to states
- **Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard/UNC (2023)**: race-based affirmative action in college admissions unconstitutional
