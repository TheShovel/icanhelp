# Economics (Advanced)

## Macroeconomics Deep Dive

### Business Cycle
- **Phases**: Expansion (growth, employment up, inflation moderate). Peak (top of cycle, inflationary pressure builds). Contraction/recession (GDP decline 2+ quarters, unemployment rises). Trough (bottom, resources underutilized). Recovery (growth resumes, unemployment still high initially)
  - Average expansion: ~5 years (since 1945. 2009-2020 expansion = 128 months, longest in record). Average recession: ~11 months (shortest 2020 COVID = 2 months, longest 2008 Great Recession = 18 months)
  - NBER: official arbiter of recession start/end. Based on depth, diffusion, duration of decline in many indicators (not just GDP)
- **Indicators**: Leading (predict economy: stock market, building permits, consumer confidence — maybe okay), Coincident (match current: employment, GDP production, personal income), Lagging (follow: unemployment rate, corporate profits, inflation, prime rate, average duration of unemployment). Yield curve (inverted = short-term rates > long-term = recession predictor — has predicted every recession since 1968 with few false alarms. Usually inverts 12-18 months before recession)

### Monetary Policy Tools (Detailed)
- **Open market operations**: most common — Fed buys/sells Treasury securities to affect reserves. Buying = injects money, lowers rates. Selling = withdraws money, raises rates. OMOs conducted by New York Fed every day through primary dealers (large banks, 31 currently)
  - Overnight reverse repo facility (ON RRP): Fed borrows from non-bank institutions, sets floor on short-term rates at IOER rate
- **Discount rate**: rate Fed charges banks for emergency borrowing. Usually above federal funds rate — used as ceiling for short-term rates. "Lender of last resort." Banks rarely borrow from discount window (stigma)
- **Interest on reserves (IORB)**: Fed pays interest on bank reserves held at Fed. Since 2008 (post-crisis). Gives Fed tool to influence rates with large reserve supply
- **Quantitative easing (QE)**: buying longer-term securities (Treasuries, MBS) to lower long-term rates. Expands Fed balance sheet ($900B pre-2008 → $4.5T 2014 → $9T 2022 after COVID). Stimulates borrowing, investing, asset prices. Used: 2008 financial crisis, 2020 COVID
- **Quantitative tightening (QT)**: opposite — let securities mature without reinvesting or sell them outright. Shrinks balance sheet. Raises long-term rates. Slows economy

### Fiscal Policy Details
- **Automatic stabilizers**: programs that automatically increase spending or cut taxes during recession without legislation — unemployment insurance (UI: expansion triggered automatically when jobless claims exceed normal thresholds). Food stamps (SNAP). Income taxes (progressive — lower income = less taxed during recession). Corporate profit taxes collapse as profits fall
  - Estimated to offset ~50% of GDP fluctuations in first 3 years of a downturn (automatic stabilizers effect). Much smaller in developing countries with weaker safety nets
- **Multiplier effect**: $1 government spending → >$1 GDP increase (recipients spend → other businesses income increases → those businesses hire more, people spend more = multiplied)
  - Multiplier = 1/(1-MPC). MPC = marginal propensity to consume (0.8 average in US → multiplier = 5). But: crowding out (higher rates reduce private investment), Ricardian equivalence (people save more assuming future taxes = if they think spending must be paid back), lags. Estimated real multiplier: 0.5-1.5 for government purchases per CBO. Temporary vs permanent effect: temporary
  - Best: direct transfers to low-income households (highest MPC). Government investment (infrastructure, green energy, education) has high long-run growth multiplier. Tax cuts: smaller multiplier for high-income households (lower MPC)

### Debt & Deficits
- **US national debt**: $34T (2024, ~120% of GDP). ~$27T held by public, ~$6.5T intra-governmental (Social Security trust funds, Medicare, federal retirement). Growing ~$1-2T/year. Interest payments: ~$870B/year (2024, growing — could top $1T in 2025)
  - Japan comparison: national debt ~250% of GDP but sustained (low interest rates + most domestically owned + home safe haven + deflation). US: can sustain high debt as long as interest rates stay low relative to growth (r < g: government can outgrow its debt). But with current rates > growth, debt ratio grows (primary deficit rises)
  - When is debt a problem? When interest payments crowd out spending (military, safety net, infrastructure). Some traditional reckoning: rising interest rates + debt + r > g = unsustainable unless primary deficit shrinks. No hard threshold — financial markets' confidence matters (silent "sudden stop" risk). Argentine debt default crisis 2001: r > g for long enough, creditors stop buying
- **Deficit vs debt**: deficit = annual gap (spending > revenue). Debt = accumulated deficits — sum of all borrowing. Debt held by the public as % of GDP = best metric

### International Economics
- **Balance of payments**: current account (trade in goods/services + income + transfers). Capital account (financial assets: stocks, bonds, real estate). Financial account (investment flows, FDI). By definition: current account deficit = capital account surplus (foreign investment in US). US runs chronic current account deficit — foreign central banks accumulate dollars to keep exchange rates low against USD, which fuels US Treasury demand and low interest rates (the "exorbitant privilege")
  - IMF: coordinates global monetary cooperation. Special Drawing Rights (SDRs): quasi-international reserve asset, value based on basket (USD 43%, EUR 29%, RMB 12%, JPY 8%, GBP 8%)
- **Exchange rate regimes**: floating (market determines — US, EU, Japan, Canada, UK). Managed float (central bank intervenes occasionally — China, India, Singapore). Fixed/pegged (to USD or a basket — Saudi Arabia, Hong Kong, many Caribbean (pegged to USD) or to Euro (Denmark, CFA Franc zone). Dollarization: use USD directly (Ecuador, El Salvador, Panama)

### Economic Schools of Thought
- **Keynesian**: demand drives economy — during recession, government should spend (deficit spending) to stimulate demand, even if it means running deficits. Multiplier effect, fiscal policy. Dominated 1945-1970. John Maynard Keynes (1883-1946), general theory
  - Critique: can't time stimulus precisely, leads to inflation, government overreach. Stagflation 1970s (high inflation + high unemployment = impossible in Keynesian Phillips curve) discredited naive version
- **Monetarist** (Milton Friedman): money supply growth = key to inflation control. "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." Rule-based monetary policy (steady money growth rate). Criticized for ignoring velocity (money turnover rate) shifts in 1980s-90s
  - Friedman: advocated for central bank to set a rule (increase money supply by ~3%/year) instead of discretionary policy. Used by Volcker Fed to break inflation (raised Fed funds rate to 20% 1981)
- **Austrian** (Hayek, Mises): free markets, no government intervention, sound money (gold standard). Business cycles caused by central bank credit expansion (artificially low rates → malinvestment → bust). No central bank or fractional reserve. Influence limited (not part of mainstream academic economics)
- **Supply-side**: lower taxes + deregulation → increase production (supply) → economic growth. Laffer curve: tax revenue vs tax rate (revenue increases at moderate rates, decreases if rates too high). Influenced Reagan tax cuts 1981. Used in tax policy debates but not a full economic theory

## Microeconomics Deep Dive

### Market Failures
- **Public goods**: non-rival (one person's use doesn't reduce availability) + non-excludable (can't prevent use) = market won't provide enough. Examples: national defense, fireworks, lighthouse, clean air, street lighting, basic research. Solution: government provision, taxes, subsidies
- **Externalities**: costs/benefits to third parties not reflected in price. Negative: pollution (carbon tax, cap-and-trade, regulation). Positive: education (government schooling, subsidies, student grants). Coase theorem: with clear property rights + low transaction costs, private bargaining can solve externalities — regardless of who has property rights (bargaining corrects externality). In practice: high transaction costs, complex property rights
- **Natural monopoly**: one firm can serve entire market at lowest cost (declining average cost over relevant range). Water, electricity, gas grid, railways. Solutions: public ownership, regulated monopoly (price caps), competitive bidding for franchise
- **Information asymmetry**: one party has more info than other. Used car market (lemons problem — sellers know defects, buyers can't tell, so good cars leave market). Health insurance (sick people buy more → adverse selection). Labor market (signaling: education as signal of ability, not just human capital). Solutions: regulation (disclosure), warranties, screening, signaling. Nobel 2001: Akerlof, Spence, Stiglitz

### Game Theory
- **Prisoner's dilemma**: two prisoners, both rat = both serve 5 years. Both stay silent = 1 year each. One rats = rat goes free, other serves 10. INDIVIDUALLY rational choice: RAT (dominant strategy). Collectively: both benefit from cooperating (stay silent). Resolution needs: repeated interaction + punishment/reputation
  - Applied to: oligopoly pricing (firms have incentive to collude, but each has incentive to cheat). Arms races. Climate change (each country benefits from others reducing emissions). Advertising wars. Tragedy of the commons
- **Nash equilibrium**: each player chooses best strategy given others' choices. No one can improve by changing alone. Prisoner's dilemma Nash: both confess (dominant strategy). Not necessarily Pareto efficient (mutual silent = better for both but violates individual incentives). John Nash (Beautiful Mind). Also: applied to bargaining (Nash bargaining solution: split surplus equally)
- **Zero-sum vs non-zero**: zero-sum = one's gain is another's loss (poker). Non-zero = both can win/lose (trade, economic growth). Much of life is non-zero-sum
- **Common use in economics**: auctions (first price sealed bid, second price (Vickrey): winner pays second-highest bid = encourages truthful bidding = strategically equivalent to English/ascending. First-price sealed: dominate strategy). Bargaining. Oligopoly competition (Cournot = quantity, Bertrand = price). Mechanism design — reverse game theory: design rules for desired outcomes
