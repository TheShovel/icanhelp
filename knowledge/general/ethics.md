# Ethics & Moral Philosophy (Advanced)

## Meta-Ethics
- **Cognitivism vs non-cognitivism**: Are moral statements truth-apt (can be true/false)? Cognitivism: yes — "murder is wrong" states a fact. Non-cognitivism: no — it expresses emotion (emotivism: "Boo murder!") or a command (prescriptivism: "Don't murder!"), not a proposition with truth conditions
- **Moral realism**: moral facts exist objectively, independent of what anyone thinks. Moral truths are discovered, not invented. Challenges: how to account for moral disagreement? How do moral facts fit into a naturalistic world? (Queerness argument: morality would require weird non-natural properties — J.L. Mackie argued this)
- **Moral anti-realism**: no objective moral facts. Error theory (Mackie): moral statements try to state facts but are all FALSE because no moral properties exist — but we should keep moral talk for practical reasons. Relativism: moral truths are relative to culture/individual. Subjectivism: moral statements express individual attitudes. Expressivism/non-cognitivism: moral language expresses emotions/commitments, doesn't describe reality
- **Moral naturalism**: moral facts are natural facts (e.g., "good = maximizes well-being"). Reduces "good" to natural properties. Compatible with science. Impressive: Cornell realism (Boyd, Sturgeon): moral properties = natural properties that we can observe scientifically via our sensitivity to them
- **Moral non-naturalism**: moral facts are sui generis, not reducible to natural facts. Intuition (G.E. Moore's "open question argument": if good were defined as "pleasure," asking "Is pleasure good?" would be incoherent — but it's a meaningful open question, so goodness can't be identical to any natural property). Moral intuition: we grasp basic moral truths directly (like math)
- **Moral particularism**: no moral principles — each situation judged on its own merits. What's right depends on context, not general rules. The right thing in one case isn't rule-based

## Normative Ethics (Theories of How to Act)

### Deontology
- **Kant**: act only according to maxims that could be universal law (Formula of Universal Law). Treat persons as ends in themselves, never merely as means (Formula of Humanity). Motive matters, not consequences. Good will is the only thing good without qualification
  - Perfect duties (never lie, never kill, never steal, keep promises). Imperfect duties (develop talents, help others — not always required but must be adopted as goals). The shopkeeper who gives correct change because it's good for business acts according to duty but NOT from duty (no moral worth)
  - Problem: what if a murderer asks where your friend is hiding? Kant says: never lie, even then. Most find this implausible (Benjamin Constant objected, Kant replied in "On a Supposed Right to Lie from Philanthropy")
  - Formula of Universal Law: test by contradiction — can I rationally will everyone to false promise when in need? No — the practice would be undermined if everyone did it. Formula of Humanity: never treat rational beings as merely instruments (you can treat as means, but never as ONLY means — e.g., when you hire a plumber, you treat them as a means to fix pipes, but simultaneously respect their status as rational agent by paying them, not cheating them)
- **W.D. Ross**: prima facie duties (fidelity, reparation, gratitude, justice, beneficence, self-improvement, non-maleficence). No absolute hierarchy — must judge which duty is weightiest in context. When duties conflict, we have an "actual duty" after considering trade-offs

### Consequentialism
- **Classical/hedonic utilitarianism** (Bentham): maximize pleasure, minimize pain. "Greatest happiness principle." All pleasures equal (pushpin as good as poetry). Felix calculus: intensity, duration, certainty, propinquity, fecundity, purity, extent
- **Mill's qualitative utilitarianism**: higher pleasures (intellectual, aesthetic) are more valuable than lower (bodily) — "better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied." Proof: those who have experienced both prefer higher pleasures
- **Rule utilitarianism**: follow rules that maximize overall well-being (even if a particular act doesn't). Avoids problem of framing someone for a crime even if it would maximize happiness (because the RULE against framing maximizes happiness in the long run — but rule utilitarian must justify rule by appeal to consequences)
- **Preference utilitarianism** (Singer): maximize satisfaction of preferences, not just pleasure. Ethical implications: animals with preferences count (hence Singer's animal liberation). Abortion: Singer argues that because fetuses can't have preferences, abortion is permissible (controversial)
- **Negative utilitarianism**: minimize suffering > maximize happiness. If happiness and suffering are symmetrical, we have stronger reason to prevent suffering than to create happiness. The "asymmetry" argument (Popper, Schopenhauer)
- **Problems**: demandingness (should I give all spare money to charity?). Calculation (impossible to calculate all consequences). Justice (could justify framing an innocent to stop riots). Integrity (acts that violate personal integrity)

### Virtue Ethics
- **Aristotle**: eudaimonia (flourishing) achieved by cultivating virtues (character traits). Virtue = Golden Mean between extremes (courage between cowardice + recklessness; generosity between stinginess + profligacy). Practical wisdom (phronesis): know how to apply virtue in specific situations
  - What would a virtuous person do? Develop good character through practice (habit), not just reasoning. Example: a generous person gives the right amount to the right people at the right time — determined by phronesis, not by a rule. The virtuous person sees what is called for in each situation
- **Modern virtue ethics** (Hursthouse, Foot, Annas): right action = what a virtuous agent would do. Virtues are human excellences — traits needed to flourish as a human being (given our nature as social, rational animals). Objection: circular (how do we know who is virtuous without knowing what's right?). Reply: we learn virtue by practice and exemplars, not by definition
- **Agent-based virtue ethics** (Slote): agent's motives matter more than actions. An action is right if it expresses a virtuous motive (benevolence, care, etc.)

### Pragmatist Ethics
- **Dewey**: moral decisions are like scientific hypotheses — test them in experience, revise based on outcomes. No fixed moral rules — morality evolves as society evolves. Experimental, fallibilist
- **James**: truth is what works in practice. Moral truth is what leads to human flourishing when acted upon. Pluralist: there is no single answer to all moral questions; different values may be incommensurable
- **Rorty**: anti-foundationalist — we reason from within our shared moral practices, not from universal principles. Solidarity (expanding our sense of "us") is the basis of moral progress, not universal truth. Moral progress: expand the circle of who we include in "people like us." Language shapes what experiences are possible

## Applied Ethics

### Animal Ethics
- **Singer** (utilitarian): sentient beings count morally (capable of suffering). Speciesism is bias for our species (like racism/sexism). Factory farming causes immense suffering for trivial human pleasure — morally indefensible. Must become vegetarian/vegan (or drastically reduce animal products)
- **Regan** (deontological): animals are "subjects of a life" — have inherent value (like humans). Rights-based approach: not just equal consideration, but the right not to be harmed. Abolition of animal agriculture, not just reform. Problem: if rights are equal, does a rat's right to life equal a human's?
- **Nussbaum** (capabilities approach): every sentient being is entitled to flourish according to its species' typical form of life. Animals have entitlements to basic capabilities: life, bodily health, play, etc. Beyond sentience to capabilities

### Medical Ethics (Four Principles)
- **Autonomy**: respect patient's right to make their own decisions (informed consent). Exceptions: when patient lacks capacity (dementia, unconscious, severe mental disability). Advanced directives (living will, healthcare proxy)
- **Beneficence**: act in patient's best interest. Do good. Sometimes conflicts with autonomy (patient refuses treatment that doc thinks best). Tension between respecting autonomy and doing what's best
- **Non-maleficence**: do no harm. Also: avoid unnecessary treatment, consider side effects. Primum non nocere ("first, do no harm")
- **Justice**: fair distribution of scarce resources (ventilators, vaccines, organs). Equal access to care. Not all needs can be met → prioritization criteria
  - Examples: informed consent (autonomy), withdrawing life support (beneficence vs non-maleficence), resource allocation (justice — triage: who gets the last ICU bed?), confidentiality vs duty to warn (Tarasoff case: therapist has duty to warn potential victim)
- **Trolley problem variants**: doctor harvest organs from one healthy person to save five dying patients — wrong (even though utilitarian reasoning says save five?). Most say wrong (sanctity of life, violating the "don't kill" rule). What about aborting a fetus to save the mother? (less often trolley problem)

### Environmental Ethics
- **Anthropocentrism**: only humans have intrinsic value. Environment has instrumental value (for human needs). Stewardship: we should care for nature because it's good for us + future generations. Conservation: resource management for long-term human use
- **Biocentrism**: all living things have intrinsic value. Taylor: "respect for nature" — each living thing has its own good. Humans not superior, just one species among many. Problem: if all life equally valuable, starve if you eat plants? Usually: need hierarchy with some interests over others
- **Ecocentrism** (Leopold, Naess, Deep Ecology): ecosystems, species, and processes have value (not just individuals). "A thing is right when it tends to preserve the integrity, stability, and beauty of the biotic community." Deep ecology: interconnectedness of all life; anti-consumerism; biodiversity as intrinsic value. Land ethic (Leopold): humans are citizens, not conquerors of the land community
- **Climate ethics**: who bears responsibility for climate change? Historical responsibility (developed countries caused most emissions). Polluter pays principle. Ability to pay principle. Future generations: do we owe them a livable planet? Non-identity problem (Parfit): different people will exist depending on our choices — is "X will harm future individuals who wouldn't otherwise exist" coherent? Our choices affect WHO exists, not just how they live. But: we can still owe them certain quality of life conditions regardless
