# Linguistics & Language

## What is Linguistics
- Scientific study of language (not about learning many languages)
- **Subfields**: phonetics (sounds), phonology (sound patterns), morphology (word structure), syntax (sentence structure), semantics (meaning), pragmatics (context/use), sociolinguistics (language and society), historical linguistics (language change), psycholinguistics (language and brain)

## Phonetics & Phonology
- **Phoneme**: smallest unit of sound that distinguishes meaning — /p/ vs /b/ in "pat" vs "bat"
  - English has ~44 phonemes (depending on dialect). Languages range from 11 (Rotokas, Papua New Guinea) to 112 (!Xóõ, Khoisan language with clicks)
- **Allophone**: variant of a phoneme that doesn't change meaning — the /p/ in "spin" (unaspirated, no puff) vs "pin" (aspirated, puff) — same phoneme in English, different in Hindi
- **IPA** (International Phonetic Alphabet): standardized system for representing all human speech sounds
  - Each symbol represents exactly one sound — across all languages
  - Consonant chart: place of articulation (where in mouth: bilabial, dental, alveolar, palatal, velar, glottal) × manner (how: stop, fricative, affricate, nasal, liquid, glide)
  - Vowel chart: tongue position (front/central/back) × height (high/mid/low)
- **Suprasegmentals**: pitch, stress, tone, intonation — affect meaning beyond individual phonemes
  - Tone languages: pitch changes word meaning (Mandarin: ma = mother/hemp/horse/scold depending on tone)
  - All languages use intonation (statement vs question: "You're going." vs "You're going?")

## Morphology & Syntax
- **Morpheme**: smallest unit of meaning — "unbreakable" = un- (negation) + break + -able (capable of)
  - Free morpheme: can stand alone (break, cat). Bound morpheme: must attach (un-, -s, -ed, -ing, -able)
  - Derivational: changes meaning or part of speech (happy → unhappy → happily). Inflectional: adds grammatical info without changing core meaning (cat → cats, walk → walked)
- **Morphological types**:
  - Isolating/analytic: each word = one morpheme (Chinese, Vietnamese) — word order rules
  - Synthetic: words can have multiple morphemes. Agglutinative: each morpheme has one meaning (Turkish, Swahili, Japanese). Fusional: morphemes encode multiple meanings at once (Spanish: -o = 1st person singular present indicative; Russian)
  - Polysynthetic: words can contain as much info as an entire sentence (Inuit, Mohawk)
- **Syntax**: rules for combining words into sentences
  - Constituency: phrases are natural units ("the big dog" = noun phrase, not "big dog the")
  - Word order typology: SVO (English, Mandarin, Spanish — 35% of languages), SOV (Japanese, Korean, Turkish, Hindi — 41%), VSO (Arabic, Welsh, Tagalog — 7%), VOS/OVS/OSV (rare)
  - Universal Grammar (Chomsky): humans have innate capacity for language — all languages share basic structure. Controversial — competing theories emphasize learning

## Semantics & Pragmatics
- **Semantics**: meaning of words and sentences
  - Synonym: same meaning (big/large). Antonym: opposite (hot/cold). Hyponym: specific (dog is hyponym of animal). Hypernym: general (animal is hypernym of dog)
  - Polysemy: one word, multiple related meanings (mouse = animal + computer device)
  - Homonym: same word, unrelated meanings (bank = river bank + financial institution)
  - Semantic roles: agent (doer), patient (receiver), instrument (tool used), experiencer (one who feels), location, etc.
- **Pragmatics**: meaning in context — what speaker intends vs what words literally say
  - Implicature: implied meaning ("Can you open the window?" = request, not question about ability)
  - Presupposition: background assumption ("The king of France is bald" presupposes there IS a king of France)
  - Speech acts: using language to DO things (promising, warning, betting, apologizing, naming)
  - Grice's maxims: quality (truthful), quantity (informative as needed), relevance, manner (clear, brief, orderly) — we assume these in conversation

## Language Acquisition
- **Critical period hypothesis**: first language acquired best before puberty (about age 7-12). After: grammar and accent usually non-native
  - Genie (isolated child found at 13): learned vocabulary but never mastered grammar
  - Deaf children: must be exposed to sign language early — without it, never achieve native fluency
- **Stages of acquisition** (universal across languages):
  1. Cooing (6 weeks-3 months): vowel-like sounds
  2. Babbling (6-10 months): consonant-vowel syllables (ba-ba, da-da). Deaf babies babble in sign!
  3. One-word stage (12-18 months): holophrastic ("dog" = "I see a dog")
  4. Two-word stage (18-24 months): telegraphic speech ("more milk", "mommy go")
  5. Telegraphic speech (2-3 years): longer but missing function words ("daddy go store")
  6. Complex sentences (3-5 years): grammar approaches adult level, errors show rule-learning ("I goed to the store" = applying regular past tense rule productively)
- **Overregularization**: "I runned", "two foots" — NOT mistakes, they're OVERAPPLYING rules (evidence they LEARNED the rule)
- **Universal stages**: all children, in all languages, go through these same stages at similar ages — supports innate language capacity

## Language & Brain
- **Broca's area**: left frontal lobe — speech production, grammar. Damage → Broca's aphasia (can understand, can't produce fluent speech — "telegraphic speech")
- **Wernicke's area**: left temporal lobe — language comprehension. Damage → Wernicke's aphasia (can produce fluent speech, but it's nonsense — can't understand)
- **Arcuate fasciculus**: connects Broca's and Wernicke's. Damage → conduction aphasia (can understand and produce but can't repeat)
- **Right hemisphere**: prosody (tone, intonation, emotion), metaphor, humor, discourse-level processing
- **Bilingualism**: languages share some neural resources but distinct in some regions
  - Bilinguals have better executive function (attention, switching, inhibition) — lifelong cognitive advantage
  - Never "too late" to learn second language (vocabulary can be learned at any age, but native-like phonology is unlikely after childhood)

## Language Change & Variation
- Historical linguistics: how languages change over time
  - Sound change: systematic (Great Vowel Shift in English, 1400-1700 — all long vowels shifted: "name" pronounced "nah-muh" in Chaucer's time)
  - Grammaticalization: content word becomes grammar marker (English "will" = originally "to want/wish")
  - Cognates: words from common ancestor (English "mother", Latin "mater", Sanskrit "matr")
  - Comparative method: reconstruct proto-languages by comparing cognates (Proto-Indo-European — spoken ~6000 years ago, ancestor of most European + many Asian languages)
- **Language families**: Indo-European (3.2B speakers — English, Spanish, Hindi, Russian, etc.), Sino-Tibetan (1.4B — Mandarin, Cantonese, Tibetan), Niger-Congo (600M — Swahili, Yoruba, Zulu), Afro-Asiatic (350M — Arabic, Hebrew, Amharic, Somali), Austronesian (350M — Indonesian, Tagalog, Hawaiian, Maori), Dravidian (200M — Tamil, Telugu, Kannada)
  - Some languages are isolates: Basque, Korean, Ainu — not related to any other known language
- **Dialect continuum**: neighboring dialects are mutually intelligible, distant ones not (Scandinavian languages, Arabic-speaking world)
  - "A language is a dialect with an army and navy" (political distinction, not linguistic)
- **Language death**: half of world's ~7,000 languages will disappear this century — one dies every ~2 weeks
  - When last fluent speaker dies, the language dies with them
  - Language revitalization: Hebrew revived (from liturgical to spoken), Hawaiian, Māori, Welsh — possible with effort
- **Pidgin vs Creole**: pidgin = simplified contact language (no native speakers), creole = native language that develops from pidgin (Hawaiian Creole, Haitian Creole)

## Sociolinguistics
- **Dialect**: regional/social variation of language — NOT inferior to "standard" language (linguistically, all dialects are equally complex and rule-governed)
  - Non-standard dialects (African American Vernacular English, Appalachian, Cockney) have consistent grammatical rules — just different from standard
- **Code-switching**: alternating between languages/dialects in conversation — NOT deficiency, it's skilful linguistic choice
- **Diglossia**: two varieties of same language used in different contexts (Standard Arabic for formal, local dialect for everyday; Swiss German vs High German)
- **Sapir-Whorf hypothesis**: language influences thought — strong version (determinism, controversial) vs weak version (influence, widely accepted)
  - Color terms: languages divide color spectrum differently, affects color perception (Russian has distinct words for light blue/ dark blue → faster discrimination)
  - Grammatical gender: speakers of gendered languages think of objects with associated gender traits (German "bridge" = feminine → "beautiful", Spanish "bridge" = masculine → "strong")
- **Politeness**: strategies to save face (positive politeness = include, negative politeness = don't impose). Different cultures prioritize differently
