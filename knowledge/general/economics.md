# Economics Basics

## Microeconomics

### Supply & Demand
- **Demand**: quantity consumers WILLING to buy at each price — downward sloping (higher price → less demand)
  - Law of demand: price↑ → quantity demanded↓ (all else equal)
  - Shifts: income changes, preferences, price of related goods (substitutes and complements), expectations, population
- **Supply**: quantity producers WILLING to sell at each price — upward sloping (higher price → more supply)
  - Law of supply: price↑ → quantity supplied↑ (all else equal)
  - Shifts: production costs, technology, number of sellers, taxes, expectations
- **Equilibrium**: price where quantity demanded = quantity supplied (market clears)
  - Surplus: price > equilibrium (too much supply). Shortage: price < equilibrium (too much demand)
  - Market tends toward equilibrium — surpluses cause prices to fall, shortages cause prices to rise
- **Elasticity**: how responsive quantity is to price changes
  - Elastic: consumers sensitive to price (luxuries, many substitutes — soda, restaurant meals)
  - Inelastic: consumers need it regardless (necessities, few substitutes — insulin, gasoline, electricity)
  - Perfectly elastic: infinite sensitivity (identical products). Perfectly inelastic: zero sensitivity (life-saving drug)
  - Elasticity determines who bears tax burden (more inelastic side pays more of the tax)

### Market Structures
- **Perfect competition**: many small firms, identical products, no market power, price takers (farming, commodities)
  - Zero economic profit in long run (price driven down to cost)
- **Monopoly**: single seller, unique product, high barriers to entry, price maker — charges higher prices, produces less than competitive market
  - Natural monopoly: one firm can serve entire market at lower cost than multiple (utilities, water, electricity grid)
  - Regulated: government allows monopoly but controls price (public utilities)
- **Oligopoly**: few large firms, strategic interdependence (each firm's decision affects others), high barriers — car industry, airlines, cell phone carriers
  - Collusion (firms cooperate to raise prices) is illegal in US (antitrust). Tacit collusion (signaling, follow-the-leader) is common
  - Game theory (Prisoner's Dilemma): individual incentives lead to worse collective outcome
- **Monopolistic competition**: many firms, differentiated products, some market power — restaurants, clothing, hair salons
  - Differentiate through: branding, quality, location, service, advertising

### Externalities & Public Goods
- **Positive externality**: benefit to third parties (vaccination, education, research)
  - Under-provided by market → government subsidy (grants for research, free public education)
- **Negative externality**: cost to third parties (pollution, smoking, loud music)
  - Over-provided by market → government intervention (carbon tax, pollution permits, bans)
- **Public goods**: non-rival (one person using doesn't reduce availability) and non-excludable (can't prevent use)
  - Market won't provide (free rider problem) → government must (national defense, street lighting, clean air, lighthouses)
- **Tragedy of the commons**: shared resource overused because no one has incentive to preserve it (overfishing, air pollution, public grazing land)
  - Solutions: property rights, regulations, collective governance (Elinor Ostrom's Nobel Prize research)

## Macroeconomics

### GDP & Economic Growth
- **GDP** (Gross Domestic Product): total value of all goods/services produced in a country in a period
  - C + I + G + (X-M) = Consumption + Investment + Government + Net Exports
  - Nominal GDP: current prices. Real GDP: adjusted for inflation (true growth)
  - GDP per capita: better measure of living standards than total GDP
- **GDP growth**: 2-3% per year is healthy for developed economies. Negative growth for 2+ quarters = recession
  - Recession = output gap (actual GDP < potential GDP). Boom = actual > potential (inflation risk)
- **Limitations of GDP**: ignores inequality, unpaid work (childcare, volunteering), environmental damage, leisure time, quality of life

### Inflation
- **Inflation**: general increase in prices (decrease in purchasing power)
  - Mild inflation (2-3%) is normal and healthy (encourages spending vs hoarding cash)
  - Hyperinflation: >50%/month (Zimbabwe 2008, Germany 1923, Venezuela 2018) — destroys economy
  - Deflation: prices falling — sounds good but causes people to delay purchases, reduces spending, causes recession (Japan "Lost Decade")
- **Causes**: demand-pull (too much money chasing too few goods), cost-push (production costs rise), expectations (self-fulfilling — people expect inflation so they demand higher wages)
  - Money supply: more money in economy → inflation (Milton Friedman: "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon")
- **Measuring**: CPI (Consumer Price Index — basket of typical goods), PPI (Producer Price Index), PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures — preferred by Fed)
  - CPI tends to overstate inflation (doesn't fully account for quality improvements, substitution)
- **Winners/losers**: borrowers benefit (pay back with cheaper money), savers lose (money buys less). Fixed income (pensioners) hurt most

### Unemployment
- Types: frictional (between jobs, normal ~2-3%), structural (mismatch between skills and available jobs — automation, global competition), cyclical (recession — demand insufficient)
- **Natural rate of unemployment**: frictional + structural (always present, ~4-5%)
  - Full employment: unemployment at natural rate (NOT 0%) — some unemployment is healthy (friction allows labor market flexibility)
- **Phillips curve**: inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation (historically) — broke down in 1970s stagflation
- **Costs of unemployment**: lost output (GDP gap), lost skills, psychological harm, social problems (crime, family breakdown, substance use)

### Fiscal Policy
- **Government revenue and spending** to influence economy
- **Expansionary**: more spending/lower taxes → stimulates economy (used in recessions)
  - Can cause deficits (spend more than revenue). Deficit: annual shortfall. Debt: accumulated deficits over time
- **Contractionary**: less spending/higher taxes → cools economy (used when inflation too high)
- **Multiplier effect**: $1 of government spending → >$1 of GDP (recipients spend money, who spend money...)
  - Multiplier = 1/(1-MPC). MPC = marginal propensity to consume (if people save 20%, MPC=0.8, multiplier=5)
- **Automatic stabilizers**: unemployment insurance, progressive taxation — automatically increase spending/decrease taxes in recession without legislation
- **National debt**: US national debt ~$34 trillion (2024, ~120% of GDP). Higher than 100% GDP is concerning but manageable if interest payments are affordable and economy grows
  - Japan: >250% debt/GDP but low interest rates, most owned domestically — no crisis (yet)
  - Debt matters when: foreign-owned, rising interest rates, slow growth, primary deficit (deficit before interest payments)

### Monetary Policy (Central Banks)
- **Federal Reserve** (US Fed): manages money supply, interest rates, banking system
  - Dual mandate: maximum employment + stable prices (2% inflation target)
- **Tools**:
  - Federal funds rate: interest rate banks charge each other — affects all other rates. Raise = cool economy (tighten). Lower = stimulate (ease)
  - Open market operations: buy/sell government bonds (buy = increase money supply, lower rates; sell = decrease)
  - Reserve requirements: how much banks must hold (rarely changed now)
  - Quantitative easing (QE): buying large amounts of bonds to lower long-term rates (after 2008, 2020 COVID)
  - Quantitative tightening (QT): opposite — selling bonds or letting them mature (reducing balance sheet)
  - Forward guidance: communicating future policy intentions to influence expectations now
- **Interest rates affect**: borrowing costs (mortgages, car loans, credit cards), business investment, stock market (lower rates = higher stock prices), exchange rates (higher rates = stronger currency), inflation
- **Federal Reserve Chair**: Jerome Powell (2018-2026). Independent from politics (designed that way — long terms, policy decisions not subject to congressional approval)

### International Trade
- **Comparative advantage**: countries should specialize in what they produce relatively most efficiently, then trade (David Ricardo)
  - Even if one country is better at EVERYTHING, both benefit from trade (focus on comparative, not absolute advantage)
- **Trade barriers**: tariffs (tax on imports), quotas (limit quantity), subsidies (to domestic producers), non-tariff barriers (regulations, standards)
  - Protect domestic industries but raise prices for consumers, reduce efficiency, can trigger trade wars
- **Trade deficit**: imports > exports — NOT necessarily bad (US has had trade deficit for decades, still world's largest economy)
  - Trade deficit = capital surplus (foreigners invest in US). As long as investments are productive, deficit is sustainable
- **Globalization**: increased trade, capital flow, migration, information exchange — lifted billions out of poverty (especially China, India), but created winners (skilled workers, capital owners) and losers (manufacturing workers in developed countries)
- **Exchange rates**: determined by supply/demand for currencies
  - Strong dollar: good for imports, bad for exports (American tourists love it, American manufacturers hate it)
  - Purchasing power parity (PPP): exchange rate that equalizes price of same basket of goods across countries — Big Mac Index (informal measure by The Economist)
