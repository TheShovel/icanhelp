# Indigenous Cultures & History

## Pre-Columbian Americas

### North America
- **Mississippian culture (800-1600 CE)**: Cahokia (near St. Louis) — largest pre-Columbian city north of Mexico, population 10,000-20,000+ at peak (1250 CE). Monk's Mound: 100 ft tall, 14 acres base — largest earthen structure in the Americas. Platform mounds, chiefdom, extensive trade networks (shells from Gulf, copper from Great Lakes, mica from Appalachians, obsidian from Wyoming). Declined before European contact (probable causes: drought, deforestation, resource depletion, internal conflict)
- **Ancestral Puebloans (Anasazi, 100-1300 CE)**: Southwest (Mesa Verde, Chaco Canyon, Canyon de Chelly). Cliff dwellings (Mesa Verde: 600 rooms carved into cliff face). Kivas (underground ceremonial chambers). Sophisticated architecture: Chaco Canyon great houses aligned to solar/lunar cycles. Advanced water management (check dams, canals). Vanished by 1300 CE — likely drought (1276-1299 megadrought), deforestation, social collapse
- **Iroquois Confederacy (Haudenosaunee)**: Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, Tuscarora (after 1722). Great Law of Peace (oral constitution — estimated 1146-1650, maybe earlier). Grand Council: 50 sachems. Influence on US Constitution? Yes, possibly — Franklin acknowledged knowing Iroquois political structure, "joining the colonies in a confederation might be wise" (Albany Plan 1754). Women held significant power: clan mothers appointed sachems, could remove them
- **Plains Indians**: Lakota (Sioux), Cheyenne, Comanche, Blackfoot, Arapaho. Nomadic following bison herds. Horse culture ~1700 (Spanish horses spread north from Mexico). Teepee (portable, 12-16 hide poles). Buffalo utilized for everything: food (meat, organs), clothing (hides), shelter (teepee covers — needs 10-15 hides), tools (bones), fuel (dried dung — buffalo chips), thread (sinew). Sun Dance ceremony. Plains sign language used across tribes as trade language-of-signs. Wounded Knee Massacre (1890): ~300 Lakota killed by US 7th Cavalry, marked end of Indian Wars

### Mesoamerica
- **Olmec (1500-400 BCE)**: "Mother culture" of Mesoamerica. Gulf Coast Mexico. Signature: colossal basalt heads (up to 9 ft tall, 20-40 tons, transported over 60 miles — likely by raft on rivers). First writing (cascajal block, uncertain), first calendar, first ballgame. Jade working. Feathered serpent deity (later Quetzalcoatl). Abandoned ~400 BCE — unknown cause
- **Maya (250-900 CE classic period)**: City-states (Tikal, Palenque, Copán, Chichén Itzá). NOT an empire — independent cities sharing culture/writing/calendar/religion. Achievements: writing (Maya script = logosyllabic — nearly deciphered. 800+ glyphs), mathematics (base-20, concept of zero), astronomy (Venus cycle, solar year 365.2420 days — more accurate than Gregorian 365.2425), architecture (step pyramids, palaces, observatories, ballcourts. El Castillo pyramid Chichén Itzá: 91 steps per side × 4 sides + 1 top = 365 days, equinox snake shadow effect). Human sacrifice (flayed gods, bloodletting. NOT as common as Aztec — but some for important events). "Classic Maya collapse" (800-900 CE) — complex, still debated. Likely: drought, overpopulation, deforestation, soil depletion, political fragmentation. Not total collapse: Maya cities abandoned but people continued in North Yucatan (Chichén Itzá, Mayapán) — Spanish arrival 1500s found Maya civilization with cities, writing, social hierarchy
- **Aztec (Mexica, 1325-1521)**: Tenochtitlán (Mexico City — island in Lake Texcoco, 200,000+ population at peak, larger than any European city at the time). Chinampas (floating gardens — raised beds in shallow lake, extremely productive, 7 harvests/year possible for some crops). Triple Alliance (Tenochtitlán + Texcoco + Tlacopan). Tribute empire (not direct rule — conquered territories paid tribute in goods, food, slaves, sacrificial victims). Religion: Huitzilopochtli (sun/war god) — massive human sacrifice needed according to Aztec religion (thousands/year, up to 20,000 at temple dedication 1487 — numbers debated). War for both conquest/captives (flower wars: ritual battles with Tlaxcala to capture prisoners for sacrifice). 1519: Hernán Cortés + 600 Spanish + native allies (Tlaxcala, Totonac — enemies of Aztecs). Smallpox killed 50%+ of population. 1521: Tenochtitlán fell after 93-day siege. Malintzin/Malinche (Doña Marina) = translator/strategist for Cortés — complex historical figure (traitor or survivor? — considered traitor in Mexico, but she was enslaved Maya woman, likely acting to survive). Destruction of temples, imposition of Christianity, colonial caste system

### South America
- **Caral (Norte Chico, 3000-1800 BCE)**: oldest civilization in the Americas (contemporary with pyramids of Egypt!). Coastal Peru. No pottery! No writing! But monumental architecture (6 large pyramids, largest platform mound 460x500 ft base, 60 ft high). Quipu (knot records — cords with knots as numerical recording, possibly language encoded). Subsistence: anchovies + sardines + squash/beans (irrigation). Earliest known civilization in Western hemisphere. Caral itself: 6 pyramid mounds, 2 plazas, elite residences, amphitheater. 30+ sites around area. No fortifications or weapons found — suggests peaceful society with collective governance? Ceremonial center with no warfare
- **Chavín (900-200 BCE)**: Andean highlands (Chavín de Huántar, Peru). Influential religious cult across Peru. Jaguar/human hybrid imagery (staff god, later spread to other Andean cultures: Wari, Tiwanaku, Inca). Underground galleries + labyrinth at Chavín. Stelae with carved jaguar + human forms. Shell trumpets. Influence spread across Andes through pilgrimage + trade networks
- **Moche (100-800 CE)**: north coast Peru. Master metalsmiths (gold-working, metallurgy, tumi ceremonial knives, jewelry, alloyed copper-gold-silver). Mural art (Huaca del Sol — largest pyramid in Peru? Highly decorated with Moche pantheon and ritual scenes). Ceramic portrait vessels (Moche pots — lifelike faces; each individual distinct — possible portraits of rulers or mythical figures). Sacrificial practices (recent evidence: mass sacrifice of warriors, teeth marks on bones, consumption). Sophisticated irrigation canals (Ascope Canal: 113 km long). No writing system — but iconography tells stories. Pollen evidence: filled with ritual offerings, maize, and marine resources
- **Tiwanaku (500-1000 CE)**: Lake Titicaca (Bolivia/Peru). Elevated fields (raised fields + canal systems: prevented frost, increased agricultural yields. Warm water irrigation extended growing season). Gate of the Sun (stone monolith, carved deity with staffs influenced by older Chavín). Akapana pyramid (terraced, 60 ft tall). Tiwanaku expansion controlled altiplano trade routes. Collapse ~1000 CE: drought
- **Inca (1438-1533)**: largest pre-Columbian empire (4,000+ km along Andes, 12M population). Cusco center (shaped like puma). Quechua (official language). Quipu (knot records — numerical + possibly more, possibly a 3D binary narrative). Roads: 25,000+ mile network (Qhapaq Ñan — royal road system. Chasqui runners: relay messengers could travel 240 km/day). Agriculture: terraces (thousands of hectares of terraced mountainsides, stabilizing slopes, preventing erosion, extending growing season by a few weeks), freeze-dried potatoes (chuño — freeze at night, press out water, sun-dry, store for years. Crucial for Inca expansion — could supply armies with long-lasting food)
  - Machu Picchu (built 1450, abandoned ~1572, rediscovered by Hiram Bingham 1911, never found by Spanish — because it wasn't on Spanish maps. Not a typical Inca city, more likely royal estate for Pachacuti). Stonework: dry-stone walls with precise jointing — no mortar required (the stones fit perfectly with joints thinner than a credit card, using hammerstones, shaped by grinding. How? Still debated — hammerstones slightly cupped to shape each stone). Each stone individually shaped to fit perfectly against neighbors. Survived earthquakes: walls built with slight inward tilt + trapezoidal doorways
  - Religion: Inti (sun god), Viracocha (creator god). Mummies of past emperors kept in Cusco. Provincial: Pachamama (earth mother) still worshipped today in Andean communities
  - Conquest: 1532 — Pizarro (168 Spanish, 37 horses). Civil war: Atahualpa (north) vs Huáscar (south) just ended — weakened Inca. 1532 captured Atahualpa at Cajamarca (8,000 Inca vs 168 Spanish, thousands killed — surprise + horses + steel + guns). Ransom: room filled with gold (once filled, they murdered him anyway). 1533-1572: resistance Manco Inca → Vilcabamba (last Inca stronghold). Smallpox: killed 50-90% of native population in first century (no immunity to Old World diseases). By 1570: population collapsed from 12M to <1M in coastal areas

## Australia & Oceania
- **Aboriginal Australians**: oldest continuous culture on Earth — 65,000+ years (genetic + archaeological evidence. First human migration out of Africa about 65,000 years ago — reached Australia by boat). Songlines (Dreaming tracks across landscape — oral maps + stories + songs encoding survival information: water sources, edible plants, navigation. Songs describe topography and seasons — could cross continent by song). No writing, but complex oral tradition (500+ languages before colonization, ~150 remaining today)
  - Didgeridoo (yidaki): wind instrument from northern Australia (~1,500 years old). Not used across all Aboriginal groups — specific to Arnhem Land. Boomerang: returning (throwing stick shaped for return flight — hunting/play) vs non-returning (killing stick — heavier, used for hunting)
  - Colonization: 1788 British colony at Sydney Cove. ~1,500 people initially. Frontier conflict, disease (smallpox — speculation it was intentionally spread, but smallpox outbreak 1789 preceded intentional attempts; debated), forced removal from land. Stolen Generations (1905-1970s): Aboriginal children removed from families to be raised in white institutions — "assimilation" policy. Apology: 2008, PM Kevin Rudd. Native Title Act 1993: first recognition of Aboriginal land rights (Mabo decision overturned terra nullius — legal fiction that land was "empty" before colonization). Today ~3% of population, many remote communities facing severe health/economic gaps
- **Māori (New Zealand)**: arrived from Polynesia ~1300 CE (by waka/hourua — double-hulled ocean-going canoes, navigating by stars + ocean swells + bird migration). Oral history: "Great Fleet" theory revised — more likely deliberate migration over many generations. Haka (war dance, challenge. Performed before battle, also for ceremonies, funerals — not just scary face, it's about identity and pride. Used also for welcoming guests). Ta moko (traditional tattooing — chisels cut grooves into skin, not punctured. Face tattoos: genealogy, rank, achievements). Colonization: Treaty of Waitangi (1840) — English + Māori versions had differences (ceded sovereignty/ governance? Cession of sovereignty vs cession of governance. Māori version: "kawanatanga" = governance, not sovereignty = arguably different). Land Wars (1845-1872). Today: Treaty settlements ongoing — apologies + compensation for land confiscations. Waitangi Tribunal: reviews Crown breaches since 1975. Māori language (te reo) revitalization (immersion preschools kohanga reo + schools kura kaupapa + Māori TV)
