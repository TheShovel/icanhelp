# Philosophy & Ethics

## Major Branches
- **Metaphysics**: what is real? (reality, existence, time, free will, mind-body problem)
- **Epistemology**: what is knowledge? (what can we know? how do we know it? what is truth?)
- **Ethics**: what is right and wrong? (how should we live? what is the good life?)
- **Logic**: what is valid reasoning? (deductive, inductive, fallacies)
- **Aesthetics**: what is beauty? (art, taste, beauty)
- **Political philosophy**: what is justice? (rights, government, liberty, equality)

## Western Philosophy Timeline
- **Ancient**: Socrates (questioning, Socratic method), Plato (Theory of Forms, The Republic, allegory of the cave), Aristotle (logic, virtue ethics, Golden Mean, metaphysics, biology)
  - "The unexamined life is not worth living" — Socrates
  - Stoicism (Epictetus, Seneca, Marcus Aurelius): focus on what you control, accept what you don't, virtue is the only good
  - Epicureanism: pleasure (absence of pain) is the highest good, simple life, friendship, don't fear death
- **Medieval**: Augustine (original sin, City of God), Aquinas (synthesis of Christianity + Aristotle, natural law), Avicenna and Averroes (Islamic philosophy preserved + expanded Greek thought)
- **Modern** (1600s-1800s): Descartes ("I think therefore I am", mind-body dualism), Hume (empiricism, skepticism, problem of induction), Kant (categorical imperative, phenomenal vs noumenal), Mill (utilitarianism, liberty), Nietzsche (master-slave morality, will to power, "God is dead"), Marx (class struggle, alienation, dialectical materialism)
- **20th century**: Existentialism (Sartre: "existence precedes essence", Camus: absurdism, de Beauvoir: feminism), Wittgenstein (language, meaning-as-use), Rawls (justice as fairness), Foucault (power, knowledge, social institutions), Chomsky (linguistics, critique of power)

## Major Ethical Theories
- **Utilitarianism** (Jeremy Bentham, John Stuart Mill): greatest good for the greatest number
  - Act utilitarianism: judge each action by its consequences
  - Rule utilitarianism: follow rules that maximize overall well-being
  - Problem: can justify harming a minority for majority benefit
- **Deontology** (Immanuel Kant): act according to duty and universal moral rules
  - Categorical imperative: act only according to rules that could be universal law
  - Treat people as ends, NEVER merely as means
  - Problem: absolute rules can lead to bad outcomes (can't lie even to save a life)
- **Virtue ethics** (Aristotle): focus on character (virtues) rather than rules or consequences
  - Golden Mean: virtue lies between extremes (courage between cowardice and recklessness)
  - What would a virtuous person do? Develop good character through practice
- **Care ethics**: emphasizes relationships and responsibility to others (Carol Gilligan, Nel Noddings)
  - Criticizes traditional ethics as male-focused (abstract rules vs concrete relationships)
- **Contractarianism**: morality = social contract among rational self-interested people (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Rawls)
- **Pragmatism**: truth is what works in practice — ideas are tools for problem-solving (William James, John Dewey, Richard Rorty)

## Free Will vs Determinism
- **Determinism**: every event (including choices) is caused by prior events — free will is illusion (physical laws, genetics, environment)
- **Libertarianism (not political)**: we genuinely make free choices — quantum uncertainty, conscious agency create real alternatives
- **Compatibilism**: free will and determinism are compatible — free will = ability to act according to one's desires without external coercion even if those desires are determined
  - Most philosophers today are compatibilists
- **Implications**: moral responsibility (can we blame/praise if no free will?), legal punishment, meaning of life

## Meaning of Life
- **Nihilism**: life has no inherent meaning or purpose. Can be paralyzing or liberating (you create your own)
- **Existentialism**: meaning is not given — we create it through choices and actions. Life is absurd (the world doesn't give us meaning, we want meaning → conflict)
- **Absurdism** (Albert Camus): the conflict between human desire for meaning and meaningless universe — accept absurdity, live fully in spite of it (Sisyphus happy pushing rock)
- **Religious**: meaning comes from God, divine purpose, afterlife, cosmic plan
- **Humanistic**: meaning comes from relationships, love, creativity, helping others, personal growth
- **Hedonism**: pursuit of pleasure and avoidance of pain = the good life (some problems: some pleasures destructive, not all worthwhile things are pleasurable)

## Ethics in Daily Life
- **Golden Rule**: treat others as you want to be treated (found across cultures and religions)
- **Do no harm**: first check whether actions cause unnecessary suffering
- **Integrity**: align actions with stated values — trustworthy person is reliable across contexts
- **Honesty**: truthfulness but not brutal — consider timing, necessity, kindness
  - Kant says never lie. Mill says lie if it prevents greater harm. Virtue ethics says honest person knows when.
- **Fairness**: equal treatment unless relevant differences justify different treatment
- **Responsibility**: own your actions, their consequences, and your impact on others
  - Also: take responsibility for what you can influence beyond yourself (community, environment, future)

## Thought Experiments
- **Trolley problem**: runaway trolley heading toward 5 people. Pull lever to divert to side track with 1 person. Pull? (Utilitarian: pull. Deontological: don't intervene)
  - Variations: push fat man off bridge to save 5 (most say no — direct harm different from indirect?)
- **Veil of ignorance** (Rawls): design society without knowing your position in it — would you accept inequality? (Rawls: yes, only if it benefits the least advantaged)
- **The experience machine** (Nozick): plug into machine that gives perfect pleasurable experiences forever. Do you? (Most say no — we want to actually DO things, not just feel pleasure)
- **Ship of Theseus**: if you replace all parts of a ship, is it still the same ship? Identity over time — applies to personal identity (are you same person as childhood self?)

## Critical Thinking & Philosophy
- **Socratic method**: ask questions to expose contradictions, challenge assumptions, clarify thinking
  - Not about winning arguments — about getting closer to truth together
  - "I know that I am intelligent, because I know that I know nothing" — Socrates
- **Occam's razor**: simpler explanations are more likely correct (but not guaranteed)
- **Hanlon's razor**: "Never attribute to malice what can be explained by stupidity" (or ignorance, or accident)
- **Hitchens' razor**: "What can be asserted without evidence can be dismissed without evidence"
- **Popper's falsification**: scientific theories must be testable and potentially falsifiable
- **Russell's teapot**: burden of proof is on the person making the claim, not the skeptic
