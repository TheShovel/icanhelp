# Basic Logic & Critical Thinking

## Logical Arguments
- **Premise**: statement offered as evidence or reason
- **Conclusion**: statement being argued for based on premises
- **Deductive**: if premises true → conclusion necessarily true (all humans are mortal, Socrates is human → Socrates is mortal)
- **Inductive**: premises make conclusion probable (most swans I've seen are white → all swans are probably white — but black swans exist)
- **Abductive**: inference to best explanation (diagnosing illness from symptoms = most likely cause given the evidence)
- **Valid argument**: conclusion follows logically from premises (even if premises are false)
- **Sound argument**: valid + all premises are true

## Common Fallacies
- **Ad hominem**: attacking person instead of argument ("you're just saying that because you're X")
- **Straw man**: misrepresenting argument to make it easy to attack ("you want to ban cars?" when someone suggests more bike lanes)
- **False dilemma/dichotomy**: only two options when more exist ("you're either with us or against us")
- **Slippery slope**: one step leads to extreme outcome with no evidence ("if we legalize marijuana, next is heroin")
- **Appeal to authority**: "expert says" without verifying expertise is relevant or consensus exists
- **Appeal to nature**: "natural is good, chemical is bad" (arsenic is natural, penicillin is chemical)
- **Appeal to emotion**: using emotion (fear, pity, guilt) instead of evidence
- **Bandwagon**: "everyone believes it" — popularity doesn't equal truth
- **Circular reasoning**: conclusion is restated as premise ("it's true because it's in the Bible, and the Bible is true because it's the word of God")
- **Hasty generalization**: small sample → broad conclusion ("I met one rude French person, all French people are rude")
- **Post hoc ergo propter hoc**: "after this, therefore because of this" — just because A happened before B doesn't mean A caused B
- **Tu quoque**: "you do it too" — doesn't make the action right
- **Begging the question**: assuming what you're trying to prove
- **False equivalence**: comparing two things that aren't comparable ("Hitler painted art, so all artists are evil")
- **Loaded question**: question with an unjustified assumption ("have you stopped beating your spouse?")

## Cognitive Biases
- **Confirmation bias**: seeking/ interpreting information that confirms existing beliefs
- **Dunning-Kruger effect**: people with low competence overestimate their ability; experts underestimate
- **Anchoring**: first piece of information influences subsequent judgments (initial price offered in negotiation)
- **Availability heuristic**: overestimating likelihood of events that come to mind easily (plane crashes vs car crashes)
- **Hindsight bias**: "I knew it all along" — events seem more predictable after they happen
- **Sunk cost fallacy**: continuing something because you've already invested time/money, even when it's not worth it
- **Optimism/pessimism bias**: overestimating positive (or negative) outcomes
- **Negativity bias**: negative events/ information have more impact than positive
- **In-group bias**: favoring people in your group over outsiders
- **Self-serving bias**: attributing success to yourself, failure to external factors
- **Fundamental attribution error**: attributing others' actions to character, your own to circumstances

## Scientific Thinking
- **Falsifiability**: a claim must be testable and could be proven false to be scientific
  - "It's true because you can't prove it's false" = unfalsifiable (therefore unscientific)
- **Null hypothesis**: assumption of no effect — need evidence to reject it
- **Peer review**: other experts evaluate research before publication — not perfect (biases exist), but essential
- **Replication crisis**: many published studies can't be replicated — skepticism is healthy
- **Occam's razor**: simpler explanation (fewer assumptions) is more likely correct — but not always
- **Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence**: if it contradicts established science, the evidence needs to be correspondingly strong

## Decision Making Frameworks
- **Pros/cons list**: list factors for each option, weigh importance (not just count)
- **Cost-benefit analysis**: compare expected benefits against costs (monetary, time, emotional)
- **Expected value**: (probability of outcome × value of outcome) for each option, sum, compare
- **Second-order thinking**: what happens after the outcome? And after that?
- **Inversion**: instead of asking "how do I achieve X?", ask "what would prevent X?" — remove obstacles
- **Premortem**: imagine the decision failed, work backwards to identify why — catches blind spots
- **Regret minimization**: "Will I regret not doing this in 10 years?" — big decisions
- **Sleep on it**: for important decisions, give yourself overnight — emotional distance clarifies

## Probability & Risk
- **Base rate**: how common something is in general population — must consider when evaluating evidence
- **Regression to the mean**: after extreme events, outcomes tend to return to average (sophomore slump, sports)
- **Law of large numbers**: larger samples are more reliable (1000 coin flips ≈ 50/50, 10 flips might not be)
- **Gambler's fallacy**: after 5 heads in a row, coin is NOT "due" for tails — each flip is independent (50/50)
- **Bayesian thinking**: update beliefs based on new evidence — prior probability + new data = posterior probability

## Critical Questions to Ask
- What's the claim being made?
- Who is making it? What's their motive?
- What evidence supports it? Is that evidence reliable?
- Could there be alternative explanations?
- What would it take to prove this wrong?
- Is this based on data or anecdote?
- What am I assuming? What are they assuming?
- How does this fit with what I already know that's well-established?
- If this were false, what would I expect to see? Do I see that?
