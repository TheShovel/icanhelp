# Government & Politics Basics

## Forms of Government
- **Democracy**: citizens hold power directly or through representatives
  - Direct: citizens vote on policy directly (Swiss cantons, town meetings). Representative: citizens elect officials to govern (most democracies)
  - Liberal democracy: free elections, rule of law, protection of rights, independent judiciary, free press
  - Illiberal democracy: elections happen but rights/rule of law are eroded (Hungary, Turkey, Russia)
- **Republic**: head of state is elected (not monarch) — most modern democracies are republics
- **Monarchy**: single ruler (usually hereditary). Constitutional: monarch is figurehead, parliament governs (UK, Japan, Sweden). Absolute: monarch has full power (Saudi Arabia, Brunei, UAE)
- **Authoritarianism**: single leader/party with absolute power, no meaningful opposition, limited rights (China, Russia, North Korea, Belarus, Syria)
- **Totalitarianism**: state controls all aspects of life (Nazi Germany, USSR under Stalin, North Korea)
- **Theocracy**: religious leaders rule, law based on religious texts (Iran, Vatican City, Afghanistan under Taliban)
- **Oligarchy**: ruled by small wealthy/powerful group (historical: Sparta, Venice. Modern elements: Russia oligarchs, corporate influence in many countries)

## US Government Structure
- **Three branches** (separation of powers — checks and balances):
  - **Legislative** (Congress): makes laws — House of Representatives (435 members, 2-year terms, proportional by population) + Senate (100 members, 6-year terms, 2 per state)
    - Powers: tax, spend, declare war, approve treaties (Senate), confirm judges (Senate), impeach (House) and convict (Senate)
    - House: Originates revenue bills, more responsive to public (frequent elections). Speaker of the House = majority party leader
    - Senate: Filibuster (60 votes to end debate), filibuster can be abolished (nuclear option). Senate confirms presidential appointments
  - **Executive** (President): enforces laws — President (4-year term, max 2 terms), Vice President, Cabinet (15 departments)
    - Powers: veto legislation, commander-in-chief of military, appoint judges/cabinet (with Senate confirmation), pardon federal crimes, executive orders (direct agencies, can be overturned by courts)
    - Executive orders: not laws but directives to executive branch — can be reversed by next president
  - **Judicial** (Supreme Court + lower federal courts): interprets laws — 9 Supreme Court justices (lifetime appointments, nominated by President, confirmed by Senate)
    - Judicial review (established Marbury v. Madison 1803): Court can strike down laws as unconstitutional
    - Stare decisis: precedent matters — but can be overruled (Brown v. Board overruled Plessy v. Ferguson)
    - Cases: original jurisdiction (disputes between states), appellate (most cases — appeals from lower courts)
- **Federalism**: power divided between federal and state governments
  - Federal powers: defense, currency, immigration, interstate commerce, patents
  - State powers: education, police, roads, intrastate commerce, marriage/family law, elections (10th Amendment)
  - Concurrent (both): tax, courts, law enforcement, transportation
  - States as "laboratories of democracy" (Louis Brandeis) — policy experiments at state level

## Political Ideologies
- **Classical liberalism**: individual rights, limited government, free markets (18th-19th century — Adam Smith, John Locke)
- **Conservatism**: tradition, stability, authority, private property, gradual change. US: limited government, free markets, traditional values, strong national defense
- **Liberalism** (US modern): government action to reduce inequality, protect rights, regulate economy, social safety net
- **Libertarianism**: maximum individual liberty, minimal government (both economic and social). No taxes, no welfare, no regulations, no military intervention
- **Socialism**: means of production owned collectively by workers/state — reduce inequality, provide social services
  - Democratic socialism: socialist policies within democratic framework (Nordic countries — high taxes, strong welfare state, free healthcare/education, but capitalist market)
  - Authoritarian socialism / communism (Marxist-Leninist): state controls economy and society, single party (USSR, China, Cuba, North Korea)
- **Populism**: "the people" vs "the elite" — can be left-wing (anti-corporate) or right-wing (anti-immigrant, nationalist). Often anti-establishment, charismatic leader, direct connection with people
- **Fascism**: authoritarian nationalism, dictatorial power, suppression of opposition, private ownership with strong state control, often racist/imperialist (Mussolini's Italy, Hitler's Germany)
- **Anarchism**: no government — voluntary cooperation only. Rarely achieved in practice beyond small communities

## Elections & Voting
- **Electoral College** (US President): 538 electors (270 to win), each state gets number = House + Senate seats
  - Winner-take-all in most states (Maine and Nebraska split by district)
  - Criticized: candidate can win popular vote but lose election (2000, 2016). Over-represents small states
  - Faithless electors: some states penalize, Supreme Court (2020) allows states to require electors to vote as pledged
- **Gerrymandering**: drawing district boundaries to benefit one party — parties do it when they control state legislatures
  - Both parties do it, whoever's in power. "Independent redistricting commissions" reduce it (used in some states)
  - Packing (concentrate opposition voters in few districts) + Cracking (spread opposition across many districts so they lose each)
- **Primaries**: parties choose their candidate — open (any registered voter can vote), closed (only party members). Caucuses: party meetings (less common now, Iowa and Nevada used)
  - First past the post (FPTP): winner gets seat even if <50% — tends toward two-party system (Duverger's law). Runoff: if no candidate gets majority, top two face off
- **Voter turnout**: US ~50-60% in presidential, ~40% midterm, ~25% local — lowest among developed nations
  - Countries with higher turnout: mandatory voting (Australia 90%), automatic registration, election day holiday, weekend voting, mail voting
  - Who votes: older, wealthier, more educated citizens vote more → policy favors those groups
  - Voter ID laws: controversial — supported as anti-fraud (rare), criticized as voter suppression (disproportionately affects minorities, poor, elderly)

## Political Parties (US)
- **Democratic Party**: center-left. Platform: social justice, healthcare access, climate action, progressive taxation, regulation, abortion rights, gun control, immigration reform
  - Internal factions: progressive (Sanders, AOC), moderate (Biden, Manchin), establishment (Clinton, Pelosi)
- **Republican Party**: center-right. Platform: limited government, free markets (with some protectionism now), low taxes, strong military, gun rights, restrictions on abortion, traditional values, immigration enforcement
  - Internal factions: MAGA/Trump wing (populist, nationalist), establishment (McConnell, Romney), libertarian (Rand Paul), neoconservative (foreign intervention)
- **Third parties**: Libertarian Party, Green Party — rarely win but can "spoiler" (Ralph Nader 2000, Jill Stein 2016)
- **Two-party system**: structural (FPTP voting, ballot access laws, campaign finance, media coverage) — very hard for third parties to break through

## Key Concepts
- **Rule of law**: everyone (including government) is subject to the law, laws are clear, public, stable, and applied equally
- **Social contract**: people give up some freedom to government in exchange for protection of rights and order (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau)
- **Checks and balances**: each branch limits the others — no one branch dominates (Congress passes law, President can veto, courts can strike down)
- **Federalism**: power divided between national and state governments — allows local variation, experimentation, protection against central tyranny
- **Civil rights**: rights to equal treatment and freedom from discrimination (race, gender, religion, disability, sexual orientation)
- **Civil liberties**: individual freedoms protected from government (speech, press, religion, assembly, privacy, due process)
  - Different from civil rights: liberties = freedoms FROM government, rights = equal treatment BY government
  - Tension: security vs liberty (Patriot Act, surveillance, encryption backdoors)
- **Separation of church and state**: government cannot establish religion or interfere with religious practice (Establishment and Free Exercise clauses, 1st Amendment)
- **Pluralism**: multiple groups compete for influence — no single group dominates (interest groups, corporations, unions, advocacy organizations)
- **Lobbying**: trying to influence government decisions — legal, regulated, $3.5B+ spent annually in US. Concerns: money in politics, revolving door (government officials become lobbyists)
  - Citizens United (2010): corporations and unions can spend unlimited money on political speech (Super PACs)
- **Media role**: "fourth estate" — watchdog on government, inform public, provide platform for debate. Crisis in journalism: local news decline, misinformation, partisan media, economic pressure
  - Decline of local newspapers: "news deserts" increase polarization, reduce government accountability
