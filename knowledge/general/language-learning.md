# Linguistics & Language Learning

## Language Families
- **Indo-European** (3.2B speakers): includes Romance (Spanish, French, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian, Catalan — from Latin), Germanic (English, German, Dutch, Swedish, Norwegian, Danish, Icelandic, Yiddish), Slavic (Russian, Ukrainian, Polish, Czech, Serbian, Bulgarian), Indo-Aryan (Hindi, Urdu, Bengali, Punjabi, Marathi, Gujarati), Celtic (Irish, Scottish Gaelic, Welsh, Breton), Hellenic (Greek), Albanian, Armenian, Iranian (Persian/Farsi, Pashto, Kurdish)
  - Proto-Indo-European spoken ~6,000 years ago (Kurgan hypothesis: from Pontic-Caspian steppe/Ukraine-Russia, or Anatolian hypothesis: from Turkey via farming expansion). "Wheel" across all branches: descendants share "wheel" word: Sanskrit cakrá, Avestan čaxra, Greek kúklos, Old English hwēol, Old Church Slavonic kolo — suggests PIE speakers had wheeled vehicles by 4,000 BCE
- **Sino-Tibetan** (1.3B): Chinese (1.2B speakers — actually language family: Mandarin, Cantonese/Hakka/Hokkien/Wu/2 dozen+ all distinct from each other Cantonese has 9 tones). Tibetan, Burmese. Roughly 400 languages
- **Niger-Congo** (600M): sub-Saharan Africa. Swahili (80M, Bantu, 7 noun classes, Arabic loanwords), Yoruba (45M, Nigeria, tone language), Zulu (12M, South Africa, clicks from Khoisan languages). Largest African language family
- **Afro-Asiatic** (350M): Semitic (Arabic 310M, Hebrew revived from liturgical to spoken), Cushitic (Somali, Oromo), Berber, Chadic (Hausa 80M), Egyptian (Coptic = modern descendant of ancient Egyptian, liturgical only)
- **Austronesian** (350M): Indonesian/Malay (200M, national language developed from Malay trade language), Tagalog/Filipino, Javanese, Vietnamese (actually Austroasiatic! — Austronesian: Malagasy, Hawaiian, Māori, Samoan, Tongan, Fijian, Filipino/Tagalog)
  - Spread from Taiwan ~4,000 years ago to Easter Island, Hawaii, Madagascar — vast maritime expansion. Incredibly: Malagasy speakers in Madagascar = Austronesian (not African! Sailed from Borneo/Indonesia 2,000 years ago)

## Difficult Languages (for English Speakers)
- **Category I** (~24 weeks, 600 hrs of class): Spanish, French, Italian, Portuguese, Dutch, Swedish, Norwegian, Danish, Romanian — similar to English (Germanic/Romance roots, shared vocabulary, Latin alphabet)
- **Category II** (~36 weeks, 900 hrs): German (more grammar cases, word order differences), Haitian Creole, Indonesian, Swahili
- **Category III** (~44 weeks, 1100 hrs): Greek, Hebrew, Hindi, Bengali, Thai, Vietnamese, Turkish, Finnish, Polish, Russian, Serbian/Croatian/Bosnian — significant differences in writing systems (Cyrillic for some), grammar, vocabulary. Russian has 6 cases, three genders. Greek 24-letter alphabet
- **Category IV** (~88 weeks, 2200 hrs): Arabic (script right-to-left, root system, diglossia = formal + spoken different), Cantonese (6-9 tones, each character learned one at a time, no alphabet), Mandarin (4 tones, thousands of characters), Japanese (3 writing systems: kanji Chinese — 2k+ required literacy, hiragana/katakana ~50 each. Honorific language at different speech levels), Korean (Hangul alphabet = easiest writing system — invented 1443 by King Sejong "so commoner could learn" — but grammar honorific tiers, verb-final, subject-object-verb)
  - FSI ranking: Category IV languages take ~88 weeks of full-time study. BUT many reach conversational fluency much faster via immersion + active practice

## How Languages Are Learned
- **Comprehensible Input Hypothesis** (Stephen Krashen): we acquire language by understanding messages (reading + listening) at i+1 level (just above current ability). NOT by studying grammar rules or drilling. Acquisition ≠ learning
  - Input: read extensively. Listen to podcasts/radio/music — even if you don't understand everything. Gradually you do more
  - Grammar study can inform monitoring but rarely becomes acquisition. Language is acquired through use, not study, but study can speed up noticing of patterns
- **Output**: speaking/writing — pushes you to produce, but input is primary. Don't wait to "be ready" — speak from day 1 (tolerate being wrong). Conversation partner gives feedback, new input
- **Practice schedule**: daily (even 15-30 min) > cramming. Consistency > intensity. Spaced repetition (Anki or app) for vocabulary — reviews at increasing intervals [1d, 3d, 7d, 21d, 60d...] matures memory absorption
- **Age effect**: adults CAN become fluent (vocabulary learning speed is faster than children). Native-like accent is very hard after ~12 (critical period). But grammar + vocabulary? Adults learn faster! Motivation + strategy matter more than age after childhood

## Approaches to Learning
- **Immersion**: surround yourself with language — change phone to target language, read news, watch movies/shows (with TL subtitles then no subtitles), listen to music/podcasts. Live in country = full immersion but 3-months in a year of active daily study can still carry conversational ability alone. Input 10-30 hours a week
- **Tutoring/classes (iTalki, Verbling)**: 1:1 conversation practice with native speaker. Structured lessons. Less expensive than group classes ($10-30/hour on iTalki for community tutors). Grammar clarification when needed. Speaking practice pushes output
- **Apps (Duolingo, Anki, Memrise)**: good for vocabulary, not fluency. Duolingo = best for motivation + streaks, but alone won't make you conversational. Anki = most efficient vocab retention (SRS). Memrise = video clips, authentic context
- **Textbooks**: grammar reference (useful as supplement, not primary). Graded readers: books written for learners (leveled, use limited vocabulary). Assimil (French method: short dialogue + notes, build from readings. Good self-study book + audio)
- **Method**: input + interaction + spaced repetition + consistency. Example daily routine: 15 min Anki, 30 min reading (graded reader then news articles). 30 min listening (podcast/music), 15 min iTalki conversation + 15 min journal writing. Adjust time to level, increasing input as comprehension grows

## Polyglot Techniques
- **Sentence mining**: collect sentences (not isolated words) from immersion content — word in context with audio, example, picture. Anki cards: front = sentence in TL, back = translation/ definition + audio. Most efficient vocabulary acquisition
- **Shadowing**: listen to audio + speak simultaneously, copying intonation + rhythm (improves pronunciation + listening). Works best after some grammar base
- **Scriptorium**: write sentences while speaking aloud simultaneously — coordinates hand + mouth + ear + eye = deeper learning
- **Listening/reading technique**: listen to audio while reading transcript at same time. Then listen without reading. Then repeat new, unknown phrases out loud. Next day's review: recall-written phrases at natural speed
- **Staged reading**: watch first with TL subtitles, then with TL audio + TL subtitles, then without subtitles. Then try summarizing plot in target language
- **Language laddering**: learn language X through language Y (Spanish through French, not native English). Forces deeper engagement with Y, primes you for early grammar/vocabulary of X (if related)

## Writing Systems
- **Alphabet**: letters represent sounds (Latin, Greek, Cyrillic, Korean Hangul, Arabic, Hebrew). Latin: ~26 letters (most flexible + widespread, English variation: 26 letters. Roman, Cyrillic: Russian 33. Hangul Korean: ~24 letters + combined syllable blocks. Arabic: 28 consonants (vowels optional short marks), right-to-left, cursive only. Hebrew: 22 consonants (vowel marks optional), right-to-left
- **Syllabary**: symbols represent syllables (Japanese hiragana: 46 basic characters. Hiragana = grammar/ native Japanese words. Katakana = foreign loanwords. Each = 1 mora: ka, ki, ku, ke, ko. Combined: 100+ syllable sounds). Cherokee: 85 symbols. Vai (Liberia): ~200
- **Logographic**: symbols represent words/morphemes — Chinese characters (Hanzi, required 3,000-6,000 for literacy. Most complex. Simplified (China) vs Traditional (Taiwan, Hong Kong). Each character = 1 syllable + one morpheme. Kanji (Japanese adopted Chinese characters: 2,136 commonly used. Different readings = complications: 音読み on'yomi (Chinese-derived) + 訓読み kun'yomi (native Japanese)). Egyptian hieroglyphs (mixed system: logographic + alphabetic + determinative signs). Cuneiform (Mesopotamian — wedge-shaped, each sign = syllable or word)

## Endangered Languages
- ~7,000 languages spoken today. One dies every ~2 weeks. By 2100: 50-90% may be extinct (language death)
  - 3,000+ languages spoken by fewer than 10,000 people. 1,500+ languages have fewer than 1,000 speakers
- **Reasons**: globalization, education systems (monolingual national language) — colonization + cultural assimilation + migration + media dominance of large languages
- **Revitalization**: Hebrew (most successful — 0 native speakers 1880, today 5M+ native speakers). Māori (immersion preschools Kōhanga Reo, Māori radio/TV). Hawaiian (immersion schools, decreasing elder speakers, new generation rising). Welsh (Welsh-language schools, medium of life in parts of Wales. About 30% speak some Welsh, up from decline). Navajo (teaching in schools, online resources, but still declining). Irish (all school taught, but daily use limited. Gaeltacht areas, but all those under 3M speakers: fluency drops in younger generations. Most spoken daily by ~70,000)
  - Key factors: intergenerational transmission (parent-to-child) > government policy. Schools alone can't revive without home/community use
