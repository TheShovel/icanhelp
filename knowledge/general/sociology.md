# Sociology & Society

## What is Sociology
- The study of human society, social structures, institutions, interactions, and patterns
- **Microsociology**: face-to-face interactions, small groups (symbolic interactionism, ethnomethodology)
- **Macrosociology**: large-scale social structures, institutions, inequality (functionalism, conflict theory)
- **Sociological imagination** (C. Wright Mills): connecting individual experiences to larger social forces — personal troubles vs public issues (one person unemployed = personal trouble; 10% unemployment = public issue)

## Major Sociological Theories
- **Functionalism** (Durkheim, Parsons, Merton): society is a system of interrelated parts working together for stability
  - Each institution (family, education, religion, economy) serves a function
  - Manifest functions (intended, obvious) vs latent functions (unintended, hidden)
  - Dysfunction: when a part disrupts stability — social change happens when system can't adapt
- **Conflict theory** (Marx, Weber, Collins): society is conflict between groups competing for resources
  - Marx: class conflict (bourgeoisie vs proletariat). Weber: also status groups and political parties
  - Inequality is built into social structures — change comes through conflict
  - Modern applications: race (critical race theory), gender (feminist theory), global inequality (world systems theory)
- **Symbolic interactionism** (Mead, Blumer, Goffman): society is created through everyday interactions and shared meanings
  - People act based on meanings things have for them, meanings arise from social interaction, meanings are modified through interpretation
  - "Situations defined as real are real in their consequences" — Thomas Theorem
  - Dramaturgy (Goffman): life is theater — front stage (how we present ourselves) vs back stage (private self)
- **Feminist theory**: gender inequality is central to social organization — patriarchy, intersectionality (race + class + gender together), standpoint theory

## Social Stratification & Inequality
- **Social class**: hierarchical division based on economic resources, social status, power
  - Upper class (1-3%): significant wealth, political influence, inherited status. Upper-middle: professionals, managers, advanced degrees
  - Middle class (40-50%): white-collar, some college, home ownership. Working class (30%): blue-collar, hourly wages. Lower class (15-20%): poverty, unstable employment, limited education
  - Socioeconomic status (SES): composite of income, education, occupation
- **Social mobility**: movement between social classes
  - US has lower social mobility than most developed countries (Great Gatsby Curve: more inequality = less mobility)
  - Factors: education (biggest mobility driver), family wealth, race, geography, social networks
  - Absolute mobility: most people are better off than parents (economic growth). Relative mobility: your position compared to others — stagnating
- **Poverty**: absolute (lack of basic necessities) vs relative (below society's average standard)
  - US poverty line 2024: ~$31,200 for family of 4 — outdated measure (doesn't account for cost of living variations, benefits)
  - 37.9M Americans (11.5%) live in poverty. Child poverty rate: ~16% (higher than most developed countries)
  - Deep poverty: 50% below poverty line — 5% of population. Extreme poverty (global): <$2.15/day, fallen from 35% (1990) to 8% (2024) — huge progress
  - Poverty cycle: poor → less education → lower income → poor (breaking requires systemic change, not just individual effort)
  - Working poor: have jobs but below poverty line (minimum wage = $15,080/year full time — below poverty line for family)

## Race & Ethnicity
- **Race**: social construct based on physical characteristics — no biological basis (more genetic diversity within any "race" than between them)
  - Racial categories change over time and vary by country — proof it's socially constructed
- **Ethnicity**: shared cultural heritage, language, ancestry, traditions (Hispanic/Latino, Italian, Jewish, Chinese)
- **Racism**: prejudice + power — discrimination based on race, embedded in institutions
  - Individual racism: personal prejudice, acts of discrimination
  - Systemic/institutional racism: policies, practices, structures that produce racial inequality (housing discrimination, criminal justice, education funding, healthcare access)
  - Implicit bias: unconscious associations/attitudes that affect behavior — can be measured (Implicit Association Test) and changed with awareness
- **Prejudice vs discrimination**: prejudice (attitude), discrimination (behavior). Can have prejudice without discrimination and vice versa
- **Privilege**: unearned advantages based on social group membership (race, gender, class, ability, etc.)
  - Not about guilt — about awareness of advantages that are invisible to those who have them

## Gender & Sexuality
- **Sex**: biological (chromosomes, hormones, anatomy) — male, female, intersex (~1.7% of births)
- **Gender**: social, cultural, psychological — masculinity, femininity, non-binary, genderqueer, agender
  - Gender identity: internal sense of own gender (transgender: identity differs from sex assigned at birth. Cisgender: matches. Non-binary: neither exclusively male nor female)
  - Gender expression: how you present (clothing, mannerisms, name)
  - Gender roles: socially expected behaviors for each gender — vary by culture and change over time
- **Sexual orientation**: attraction pattern — heterosexual, homosexual, bisexual, asexual, pansexual
  - NOT the same as gender identity. About 5-10% of population is LGBTQ+
  - Not a choice, not changeable (conversion therapy is harmful and ineffective, banned in many states)
- **Gender inequality**: women earn ~82% of men's earnings (US, controlled for hours/occupation: 93-95%). Women do ~75% of unpaid care work globally
  - Gender pay gap: varies by race (Black women ~64%, Latina women ~57% of white men's earnings — intersectionality!)
  - Glass ceiling: invisible barriers to advancement. Glass escalator: men in female-dominated fields promoted faster

## Social Institutions
- **Family**: nuclear (parents + children), extended (multi-generational), chosen (non-biological significant relationships)
  - Functions: socialization, emotional support, economic cooperation, regulation of sexuality/ reproduction
  - Declining marriage rates, delayed childbearing, more cohabitation, more single-parent families
- **Education**: socializes children, transmits knowledge, sorts people into positions (credentialism)
  - Hidden curriculum: implicit lessons (obedience, punctuality, competition)
  - Tracking: sorting students into academic/vocational paths — reproduces inequality (poor/minority students tracked lower)
  - School funding: property tax-based → rich districts have well-funded schools, poor districts underfunded
- **Religion**: belief system about ultimate meaning, supernatural, moral codes
  - Functions: meaning, community, social control, support during crisis
  - Secularization: declining religious affiliation in developed countries (US: 20%+ unaffiliated, growing)
- **Government**: creates and enforces laws, allocates resources, represents interests
  - Bureaucracy: rational-legal authority, hierarchical, specialized, rule-based — efficient but can be dehumanizing
- **Economy**: production, distribution, consumption of goods/services
  - Capitalism: private ownership, profit motive, market competition, wage labor. Critiques: inequality, exploitation, cycles of crisis
  - Socialism: collective/state ownership, planned production, reduced inequality. Critiques: reduced efficiency, limited freedom, power concentration

## Social Change
- **Social movements**: collective efforts to promote/ resist social change — civil rights, women's suffrage, LGBTQ+ rights, environmental, labor, Black Lives Matter, MeToo
  - Stages: emergence (problem recognized), coalescence (organizing, protesting), bureaucratization (professional staff, funding), decline (success, failure, co-optation, repression)
  - Resource mobilization: successful movements need resources (money, people, media, organizations)
  - Political process: movements succeed when political system opens (allies in power, divisions among elites)
- **Technology & society**: printing press (Reformation, science), internet (information revolution, social media, polarization), AI (automation, surveillance, creativity)
  - Technological determinism: technology drives social change (somewhat true, but society also shapes technology)
  - Digital divide: unequal access to technology based on income, age, geography, education
- **Globalization**: increasing interconnectedness — economic, cultural, political, technological
  - Economic: global supply chains, multinational corporations, financial markets
  - Cultural: global media, cultural diffusion (McDonald's, K-pop, Hollywood), cultural hybridity
  - Anti-globalization: backlash (nationalism, protectionism, cultural preservation)
- **Urbanization**: more people live in cities than rural areas since ~2007 globally
  - Megacities (10M+): Tokyo, Delhi, Shanghai, São Paulo, Mumbai, Mexico City, Cairo, New York
  - Suburbanization, gentrification (wealthy move into poor urban areas → displace residents), urban decay
